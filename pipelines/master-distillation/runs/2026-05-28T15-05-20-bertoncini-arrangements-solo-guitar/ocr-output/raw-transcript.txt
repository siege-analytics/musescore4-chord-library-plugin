Preface

Hector Berlioz called the guitar a “little orchestra.” It is in the spirit of the phrase
that these arrangements Were conceived.

Anything that Is written can find Its way to the guitar, from a simple folksong, to a
operatic aria, to a big band sounding shout chorus, to an orchestral excerpt. The magic
really starts when a song calls an arranger into action and when a piece of manuscript
paper is there to work with. This starts the process of allowing an idea to take form and
to take the arranger on a journey.

Some journeys start with the melody being stated in the soprano, though some-
times the mood or message Is better served with the melody being given to the bass
voice or even nestled within the voicing. In some contexts these melodies stand alone
or are supported with spare harmonies. In other contexts, they can be given new mean-
ing through the use of the wide range of techniques available to the guitarist/arranger.
Counterpoint, cluster voicings, open-string Voicings, the special color of each string, and
many more techniques can be used to enhance the arrangement if the arranger keeps
in mind the spirit of the music. Each idea creates the impetus for the next phrase and
the journey continues.

The following arrangements were created with a loving spirit and the desire ta
bring something special to the listener.
Special thanks to Chris Ullrich, who was the major inspiration for this project and

who got the process of engraving this music started.

To Jason Ennis, who tirelessly worked to ensure the accuracy and clarity of these
manuscripts.

To Jeff Davison and to Michael Gassman who have been supportive friends and
partners in this project.

To all my friends, whose love of the quitar and whose constant encouragement
helped keep us on track,

-Gene Bertoncini, New York, March 2011

Notes about the Transcriptions and Notation:

This book falls somewhere in between a transcription book and a book of ar-
rangements. The process always began by copying Gene's original hand-written manu-
scripts. The pieces were then fleshed out by transcribing the missing material from the
recordings on Body and Soul and Quiet Now. At times, improvised solos or sections
were included in the notated arrangements. When there was a discrepancy between
the original conception of an arranged section and the recording, Gene made the call
which to include. Where possible, notes left out of a voicing or a counterpoint line on
the recorded version were included and marked with parenthesis.

Special care was taken to included a great number of Gene’s fingerings. With a
few exceptions, the fingerings have been placed to the lower right of the notes to which

they correspond.

One unique technique that Gene employs very effectively is the creative and un-
orthodox use of open sirings in his voicings. We have extracted a number of interesting
voicings and compiled them in a Glossary of Open-String Voicings included at the end

of the book.

-Jason Ennis, New York, April 2011
