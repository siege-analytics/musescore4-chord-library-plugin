JAZZ GUITAR PATTERNS & PHRASES VOLUME 1

Welcome to Jazz Guitar Patterns & Phrases Volume 1, it’s great to have you here!

In this course, you will learn classic jazz patterns that I collected over the years by transcribing solos
of jazz greats such Wes Montgomery, Charlie Parker, Grant Green, Joe Pass, Kenny Burrell, Pat
Metheny, and many more.

This course is best followed on the online version, with the ability to watch video, bookmark pages,
and ask questions.

Click here to access the online version of this eBook.

Why Learn Jazz Patterns?

I think you'll agree that practicing scales and arpeggios is an important part of learning the jazz
guitar. These tools are an essential ingredient of the jazz guitar solo, but it takes more than that to
make your solos sound like jazz.

What you need to become fluent in the jazz language is jazz vocabulary. That’s why you practice
licks and learn solos from the greats.

Studying licks is a great exercise, but it’s hard to get these licks into your improvised solos without
sounding unnatural. This is because these licks are lifted out of their context and don’t necessarily
work for your style of playing.

This is where patterns come into play. Jazz patterns are bite-sized pieces of music that work as a
bridge between technical material (scales and arpeggios) and vocabulary.

Imagine scales and arpeggios as the alphabet of the jazz language. Patterns are words you make
with this alphabet and licks are sentences you make with these words. Together you can use them
to turn your solos into stories that are interesting to listen to.

Practicing Patterns

My suggestion is to start at the beginning, with study 1. Learn how to play the study and memorize

it.

The studies aren’t meant to sound musical on their own, but work as a mnemonic aid to get as much
patterns under your fingers in a short period of time.

Once you can play study 1 fluently, have a look at which patterns you find the most interesting or fit
your style the most and start working on these patterns.

Start with learning each pattern note-by-note and practice them in different settings:

Learn the different positions of each pattern and find out which positions suit your playing style
the most.

Take a pattern through the circle of fifths to get the different positions under your fingers
(see pattern 1, pattern 15, or pattern 47 for examples).

Put on a backing track of a single chord and start improvising, starting each phrase with the
pattern you want to practice (see pattern 1 for example).

Try to personalize each pattern by changing the rhythm or adding/deleting notes from the
pattern.

Play through the different example licks and come up with some licks of your own.

Improvise over a standard of your choice and use a pattern or a combination of patterns from
time to time (see pattern 3, pattern 5, pattern 7, pattern 9, pattern 33, the 1235 technical
study, pattern 41, pattern 51, pattern 81, or pattern 85 for examples).

Pattern 1 — Cmaj7 (Enclosure)

This first pattern is a bebop classic and is often used at the beginning of a phrase.

The main part of this pattern is a diatonic enclosure around the root of the major chord.

A diatonic enclosure is when you play one note above (the 9), then one note below (the 7), and
finally your target note (the 1).

There is a wide variety of enclosures, but atm we will focus on this particular one around the root.

The second part of pattern 1 is the 1235 motif (see pattern 36).

The Bebop Scale (Pattern 58, 59, 60)

The bebop scale is an 8-note scale.

It is constructed by adding a chromatic passing note to 7-note scales such as the Mixolydian,
Dorian, and Ionan modes.

There are three kinds of bebop scales

1. The dominant bebop scale — Mixolydian + 7
2. The major bebop scale — Major scale + b6
3. The minor bebop scale — Dorian + 7

What Are the Advantages of the Bebop Scale?

e The bebop scale gives you a framework for adding chromatic notes to your jazz solos.

e The bebop scale adds a sense of tension and release to your soloing ideas.

e When you start the bebop scale on a downbeat and on a chord tone, all the following chord tones
of the scale will fall on downbeats and the other notes of the scale on upbeats.
Let me clarify that last concept with an example.

Here I play the G bebop scale descending, starting on a downbeat and on the root of the scale.

Notice how all chord notes of G7 (blue circles) fall on downbeats.

The Dominant Bebop Scale

Although there are several kinds of bebop scales, the dominant bebop scale is what is normally
referred to as “The Bebop Scale”.

The dominant bebop scale is a Mixolydian mode with a descending chromatic note between the root
and the b7.

| (Dominant) bebop scale = Mixolydian mode + 7

Here is a comparison between the Mixolydian mode and the dominant bebop scale.
I reversed the scale in the diagram because that is how the bebop scale is usually played.

The note that is added to the G Mixolydian scale is the 7 (F#).

About The Author

Hi, my name is Dirk Laukens and I teach jazz guitar.

I grew up with the music of Led Zeppelin and Black Sabbath, but soon after
my first guitar teacher (Jo Cassiers) introduced me to the jazz standards So
What and All of Me, I got hooked on jazz.

This led me to start studying jazz guitar full-time, first at the Jazz Studio
(Antwerp, Belgium) and then at the Royal Conservatory of Music, also in
Antwerp.

My guitar teachers at that time were Hendrik Braeckman and Martijn van Iterson. I also
followed guest lessons and workshops by John Abercrombie, Jesse van Ruller, Kenny Wheeler, Barry
Harris, Maria Schneider, and many more.

In 2003, I started Jazz Guitar Online as a means to document my lessons at the Conservatory.
This grew out to the biggest and most popular jazz guitar website it is today, and a thriving forum
with over 65,000 members.

Don’t hesitate to contact me if you have feedback or questions about all things guitar-related, I’m
here to help!
