READ THIS FIRST!

Jazz standards are the vehicles that jazz musicians use to improvise.
Sure, jazz musicians compose original music, but at the center of their
jazz education are jazz standards.

You can’t avoid them. They are the cornerstones of the jazz language,
and to become a great jazz improviser, you need to learn them. But
it’s not quite enough to learn them. You need to understand how they
work and how to make use of their harmonic structures.

The thing that | love about jazz music is the rich harmonic language
it is saturated in. Even musicians from outside the jazz world will dip
their feet into jazz theory to pull out some of the musical lessons it has
to offer. It’s one of the reasons you can go to college to study it. At
the end of the day, no matter what style of music you prefer, jazz can
teach you so much about music and how it works.

| wrote this book because | believe that the tunes will teach us how
to play. Yes, you can study jazz theory and other elements outside of
jazz standards. But the jazz standards themselves contain all of the
lessons and information you need.

In case you don’t know who | am, | think it’s important to introduce you
to myself. After all, you’re going to be reading a lot of my writing, so it’s
only appropriate!

My name is Brent Vaartstra. I’m a professional jazz musician (guitar-
ist) based out of New York City. | actively gig around here and have

I’m best known for being the jazz musician behind the international-

hundreds of thousands of musicians around the world, and I’m excited
to help you too!

IMPORTANT TERMS

In this book, depending on the version, there may or may not be some
terms used within musical notation that you will want to know. This is
a quick list of terms you may see that will set you up for success and
help avoid confusion.

Parent key: refers to the major or minor key as represented in the
key signature. The key the composition is based on.

Tonic: the first note of a scale degree or chord of a key center.

CA: stands for “chromatic approach.” Represents a non-diaton-
ic note approaching a diatonic note, usually by a half step.

CP: stands for “chromatic passing.” Represents a non-diatonic
note that connects two diatonic notes in either half steps or a
combination of whole and half steps.

Recorded 8va: this means that the accompanying recording of
the given exercise is one octave higher than written on the staff.
This may or may not be noted depending on your book version.

Recorded 8vb: this means that the accompanying recording of
the given exercise is one octave lower than written on the staff.
This may or may not be noted depending on your book version.

Recorded 15ma: this means that the accompanying recording of
the given exercise is two octaves higher than written on the staff.
This may or may not be noted depending on the book version.

Recorded 15mb: this means that the accompanying recording of
the given exercise is two octaves lower than written on the staff.
This may or may not be noted depending on your book version.

Opt. 8va or 8vb: this means the marked part on the chart can
be played an octave higher or lower than written in the case that
it violates the range of your instrument.
BEFORE WE START: THE PRO
GUIDE TO LEARNING JAZZ
STANDARDS

This book is centered around analyzing these ten essential jazz stan-
dards and drawing from them specific lessons that will help us be-
come better improvisers and jazz musicians in general. But before we
get into these jazz standards studies, we need to go over some im-
portant stuff first.

These jazz standards studies automatically give away some critical
information such as the chord changes. However, due to copyright re-
strictions, the melodies are not able to be included. So it’s important
to understand that these studies are not meant to be used by them-
selves to learn a jazz standard, regardless if the information is provid-
ed or not.

Music is a language, and jazz is a way of communicating. Therefore,
it must be treated as such. You can’t merely read notes or chords off
of a page and claim that you are learning these jazz standards. Much
more investigation needs to be done.

So in the next coming pages, I’m going to be talking about how to
learn jazz standards the right way. It’s important to me that you un-
derstand how to do this first because the rest of the book won't focus
as much on this process. Be sure to pay attention to this. It will set you
up for success when it comes to learning jazz standards so that you
can navigate them with confidence, and ultimately never forget them.

{EARNING BY EAR VS. GHEET Music

When it comes down to learning jazz standards the right way, how you
do it is crucial. The “how” may seem inconsequential, but it is.

The easy way is to buy some sheet music, read the melody and chords
off the page, memorize (or sometimes not) and call it good. That’s
undoubtedly the instant gratification way, but | guarantee you that’s
not the best way to learn jazz language or any piece of music.
Remember that jazz is a language. One of the most critical parts of
learning a language is mimicking. You hear something and copy it. If
you talk to anyone who is bi-lingual, they will tell you they became flu-
ent by surrounding themselves with native speakers and being forced
to listen, decipher and respond.

Jazz is not music meant to be learned from sheet music. It never was.
Back in the bebop days in the 1940’s, jazz musicians would pile into
clubs and listen to each other play. They would literally pick things up
on the bandstand, in rehearsals, and just by listening to records.

That doesn’t mean they couldn’t read music, or never read music,
but learning music by ear was always the primary method. That’s
something | want you to understand. Jazz is first and foremost
aurally learned music. So if you want to become a great jazz im-
proviser, you need to follow in the footsteps of the great jazz musi-
cians before you.

Learning music by ear is not always the easy way, and if you aren’t
used to it, you may find it quite challenging at first. But it’s the best
way hands down, and the more you do it, the easier it gets.

Don’t misunderstand me either. Sheet music is not bad. Can sheet
music play a role in your jazz education? Of course, it can. In fact,
reading is an essential part of learning a language. It can be a great
tool to conceptualize and analyze jazz language. It can also be an
excellent resource for checking the work your ear has done. If you’ve
gone through the process of learning a jazz standard by ear, you may
want to check a chord or two you are unsure about or a piece of the
melody that seems ambiguous. Also, if you’re in a bind and don’t
know a tune at a gig, there’s no harm in checking out a chord chart
on the fly.

The idea is not to throw away sheet music but to know its place. If
you genuinely want to become great jazz improviser, you are going
to need to get your face out of the pages and open up your ears
instead.
But if you’re anything like me, you don’t take action on something un-
less you know why you are doing it (especially if it requires more work).
So why should you learn jazz by ear and not sheet music? Let’s go
through a few reasons:

1. You will internalize it better.

Unless you don’t know how to read, learning music by ear tends
to be harder for most than reading it. The answers are practically
given to you. You don’t have to think as critically. But when we
use our ears to learn music, we are forced to translate what we
hear to our instrument.

Along with the struggle of learning jazz from recordings comes
repetition. You have to listen to small fragments of music at a
time and figure it out on your instrument. This naturally results
in many repetitions. In short, the harder you work for it, the more
likely you are to remember it.

2. Jazz calls for reaction.

Jazz music is based on improvisation. Improvising is one of the
core components that makes jazz, jazz. Improvisation is alll
about reacting and responding to one’s environment. Most of
the time you can’t be sure exactly what to expect, but the fun-
damental idea of improvisation is that you will react to what is
happening on a whim.

This is where sharpening your ear comes in. Let’s say you are
playing a jazz standard and a ii-V-| chord progression is coming
up in the form (in the key of C: Dmin7-G7-Cmaj7).

But suddenly you hear the piano player approach it chromati-
cally (Ebmin7-Ab7-Dmin7-G7-Cmaj7). What do you do? Do you
double check the sheet music to see if the piano player is play-
ing the “correct” chord changes, and then scold him or her for
getting it wrong? Of course not.

Jazz is not predictable. Sheet music is. When we learn jazz lan-
guage by ear, we are training ourselves to respond to the music
with our ear.
3. It helps you become an interpreter.

Jazz is not a calculated kind of music. The spirit of improvisation
is movement and change. If there are too many rules and limita-
tions attached to the music, the magic begins to wane.

In jazz, everyone has a unique voice. We may be trying to say
similar things, but ultimately it comes out a little bit different. |
may play the melody to a song one way, and you may phrase it a
bit differently. The message is still the same, but we both have
a unique way of saying it.

| rarely use a piece of sheet music to learn the melody of a jazz
standard. The only time | ever take a peek is when | can’t find
recordings that play the melody “straight,” which with some re-
search, is not hard to do. And while that can be helpful some-
times, if you learn a melody from sheet music you miss the point.

In this book, the melodies are not included. But if you do seek
out some sheet music with melodies, be warned that they will be
watered down. When notating a melody it is difficult to interpret
the phrasing and spirit of how to express it. You need to listen
to recordings as well to get the true essence. Now, it’s possible
that you may protest all of this. I’ve heard every excuse in the
book over the years from my subscribers emailing me. But trust
me, learning jazz standards by ear and not sheet music will set
you up for success every single time.

But I’m not going to leave you hanging. Let’s talk about the pro-
cess!
THE LIST PROCESS FOR LEARNING JAZZ STANDARDS BY EAR

| may or may not have convinced you that learning jazz standards by
ear is the way to go. Either way, I’m going to teach you how to do it.

If you’re anything like me, you appreciate an organized step-by-step
process of doing things. It’s helpful to have a checklist to go through
so that you know you are accomplishing something to its fullest ex-
tent.

In comes my handy little acronym for learning jazz standards and jazz
language in general: LIST.

>> THELIST PROCESS:

Listen
Internalize
Sing

Transfer

LISTEN

The first step in the process is fairly simple: listen to the jazz stan-
dard. That may seem like an obvious first move, but you would be sur-
prised how many students rush into learning a song with having barely
listened to it.

The most important thing you can do when learning jazz standards is
put your instrument away and just listen. Find as many recordings as
possible of the jazz standard you want to learn and go through all of
them. You need to become acquainted with the song, and without be-
coming acquainted with it first, you will have started off on the wrong
foot.
Only after you have identified recordings and started listening to the
jazz standard should you move on to the next step.

>> PRO TIP #1: LEARN THE LYRICS

When choosing versions to listen to, always include one with a vocalist
if possible. This is obvious to singers, but not always so obvious to in-
strumentalists.

Learning lyrics is extremely helpful for internalizing melodies. Especial-
ly in the case of many jazz standards, they are poetic, memorable, and
tell the story and essence of the song.

INTERNALIZE
This next step is a continuation of the first: more listening.

Start listening to the jazz standard everywhere you go. On your com-
mute to work or school. On your walk, run, or at the gym. Play the song
on repeat. Don’t be afraid to overdo it. But the main thing | want you
to accomplish during this step is intentional listening.

What do | mean by that? Imagine you are sitting in your living room
watching a movie. As long as it’s engaging enough for your tastes, it’s
likely you will sit still, eyes trained on the screen for two hours or more.
That’s pretty incredible if you think about it.

What if you treated music in the same way you did the movie? In gen-
eral, it’s a great practice to listen to music and give it your full atten-
tion, no distractions. But this is especially important to spend time
doing when you are trying to learn a new jazz standard.

If you give that song your full attention, you will begin to internalize
that song, and it will start to sink into your subconscious.
SING

This next step is really important. Singing is a powerful way to prove
that you have actually internalized the information you are hearing.
No, you don’t have to be a great singer, and sure, if you want, you can
whistle or hum.

The primary application of singing involves the melody. Be sure that
you can sing the melody of the standard, both along with the recording
and on your own, before you learn it on your instrument.

You may have heard the saying before, “if you can sing it, you can play
it.” Understandably, if you read that at face value it would almost
seem the suggestion is you can magically play a melody on your in-
strument just by singing. Of course, this is not the case.

>> PRO TIP #2: FINDING THE BASIC VERSION OF THE MELODY

One common complaint | get is not knowing what the “correct” melody is
because jazz musicians take so many liberties with it. Here’s something to
remember: Frank Sinatra is your friend. Frank almost always sings melo-
dies straight. So if there is a version of Frank doing it, check it out. Through-
out our studies, | do my best to include a Sinatra version when available.

If it's a less classic song, identify the original composer or artist who orig-
inally performed it. Chances are, they are going to play the melody true to
its original intent.

Do listen to, but avoid learning melodies from artists like Billie Holiday or
Miles Davis. They always mess with the melody (unless it's their compo-
sition).

What singing does is it takes away 50% of the learning process. It
proves you have internalized it and all that is left is transferring that
musical information to your instrument.

If you want to take singing a step further, try singing the bass notes.
This will help you begin to learn the harmony (chord progressions) of
the jazz standard. Listen in to the bass instrument on your recordings
and see if you can sing the roots. This is a more advanced tip, but this
will help you along with the next step.

TRANSFER

Now it’s time to actually pick up your instrument (you shouldn’t have
been touching it until now). You should be quite familiar with the song,
know the melody, and have at least a grasp on the chord changes de-
pending on where you are at in your musical abilities.

1. Transfer the melody. Start learning the melody on your instru-
ment. Again, this should only be a matter of you taking what
you can already sing and finding the notes on your instrument.
Once you find the notes, follow through with plenty of repetition
so that muscle memory starts kicking in.

2. Learn the chords. This is the most challenging part of learning
songs by ear, but | encourage you to do your best! It is incredi-
bly helpful to have a good understanding of how jazz harmony
works. We will go over that quite a bit in this book, but if you need
a more ground-up approach to chords and chord progressions,
consider checking out my book Zero to Improv.

The trick is to combine knowledge of building chord progressions
and ear training together to identify the quality of the chords
(major, minor, dominant...etc.) and how those chords function in
the chord progressions.

>> PRO TIP #3: LISTEN TO THE BASS NOTES

Like | mentioned in the Sing step, the bass note movement is very im-
portant. For example, if you can identify that the bass player is playing
a concert Eb note for the first chord, then all you have to do is figure out
what quality it is. Then you listen to the bass note of the next chord.
Let's say the following bass note is a concert C. From there you can
start to identify the chordal movement.

Now, if learning chord progressions by ear is brand new to you and you
get stumped, that’s okay. This would then be a good time to take a
look at the sheet music. But when you do, go through it while listening
to a recording, starting and stopping when appropriate to see if you
can decipher what’s going on by ear.

If |were to add one more step to the LIST process, it would be Review.
At the end of the day, you have to play these songs over and over
again to get them internalized and become more familiar with them.
Play them on your own, but be sure to play them with other musicians.

If you follow the LIST process, | guarantee you will know every jazz
standard you learn really well. You are not going to forget them (or cer-
tainly not easily), and you will be set up for success in your improvisa-
tion.

Autumn Leaves is an excellent jazz standard for us to start with be-
cause it covers some basic chord progressions and harmonic con-
cepts found in jazz. In fact, a lot of jazz musicians start out on Autumn
leaves for these very reasons.

We are going to spend a little bit of extra time on this one because
there are many small lessons we need to cover that will set us up for
success throughout the rest of the jazz standard studies.

Qutumn feawes Bie

Autumn Leaues was composed by the Hungarian-French Composer Joseph Kos-
ma with lyrics by poet Jacques Prévert. Its original title is “Les Feuilles Mortes”
which means “The Dead Leaves.” American songwriter Johnny Mercer wrote
English lyrics.

The song has an AABA form, meaning the first theme (or section) is repeated,
followed by the second theme, and ending on the original theme. It has two
common played keys: concert G minor and E minor.

Autumn Leaves is arguably one of the most popular jazz standards of all time,
and has been recorded many hundreds of times.

>> WHAT ARE GUIDE TONES?

Guide tones are notes within a chord structure that both help define a
chord, and can be used to transition to another chord melodically.

I’m going to explain that further in just a second, but first, why should
you care about guide tones and how will they help you in your jazz im-
provisation?

One of the most common questions | get is “how do | make the chord
changes come out in my solos?”

In other words, if you started improvising without accompaniment
would a listener know you were playing Autumn Leaves (assuming they
are familiar with the song)?

The key to achieving this is knowing how to outline the chord changes
in your improvisation. In future lessons, we will be digging deeper into
this, but guide tones are the perfect starting point.

In jazz, the guide tones are almost always the 3rds and 7ths of each
chord.

Blue Besse Bio

Blue Bossa was written by jazz trumpeter Kenny Dorham. It was first recorded
on Joe Henderson’s “Page One” album in 1963. It’s a blend of hard bop and
Bossa Noua. It is most commonly played in the key of concert C minor.

While Blue Bossa isn’t a classic Bossa Nova tune like the many com-
positions of Antonio Carlos Jobim, it demonstrates how important the
relationship between jazz and Bossa Nova is. As a jazz musician you
need to have some Bossa Nova’s under your belt, and Blue Bossa is a
great entry-level tune to get your feet wet.

It’s important to note that when it comes to jazz standards, many
of them are played with a swing feel, meaning the eighth notes are
swung. However, in the case of a Bossa Nova, the eighth notes are
played straight.

Blue Bossa is a great entry-level tune because the melody is relatively
simple, the form is short, and the chord changes are minimal. We can
learn a lot from this song.

IMPROV LESSON
Using the Melody in Your Felos

In our last improv lesson on Autumn Leaves we talked about the guide
tones, and how knowing what the important notes in the chords are,
combined with voice leading, will help you outline the changes.

This is definitely part of the puzzle when it comes to becoming a great
jazz improviser. But at the end of the day, we are trying to make melo-
dies. A melodic solo, regardless of chops or technique, is the ultimate
goal.

So how do we start developing melody in our improvisation? Well, we
will be covering quite a bit of this as we move through other improv les-
sons in this book, but hands down the place to start is the melody of
the song you are playing.

Often times, improvisers forget that they are improvising over a song
and not just a set of chord changes. The melody of a song is the de-
fining part. | mean, people aren’t usually singing chord changes in the
shower, right? Melodies are copyrightable, but chord changes are not.
What makes one blues different from another blues? The melody. The
chord changes are usually the same or similar.

| often get questions from my subscribers on Learn Jazz Standards.
com asking what scales they can play over different chords or chord
progressions. While that’s not a bad question to ask, the intent is usu-
ally to figure out what notes they can play over a piece of music. But
why not just look at the melody first? | think this is the best and most
musical starting point.

I’m going to teach you a great exercise to help you put this into action.
I’m also going to have a notated example of this exercise so that you
can further understand what | am getting at.

Blue Bossa is a great tune to apply this improv lesson because it has
a solid, simple melody. In this exercise, you will be improvising over Blue
Bossa using the melody as your guide. Feel free to use the backing
track provided in your download folder, a metronome, or practice with
another musician.
HERE’S THE EXERCISE:

1. The first chorus, just play the melody completely straight.
Essentially, there is no improvising happening yet. During this
chorus, you are establishing exactly what the melody is.

2. The second chorus, embellish the melody. What does it mean
to embellish? You are using the melody as a “guideline.” You can
veer off from the melody a bit, but not too far off. You want to
be clearly hearing the melody throughout the chorus, while still
taking liberties with it.

3. The third chorus, reference the melody. You can go ahead and
improvise freely and don’t necessarily have to stick so tightly
to the melody. But you will need to reference it from time to
time. Someone who just walked into your practice room should
be able to eventually pick up what song you’re playing because
you referenced a part of the melody.

To demonstrate, | have transcribed an improvised solo | took over Blue
Bossa using this exercise. The first chorus | just play the melody, the
second chorus | embellish (though maybe a little too much), and the
third chorus | reference. You can follow along with the music by clicking
the sheet music to listen to the recording.

Keep in mind, | improvised this solo on a guitar so there may be areas
where it violates the range of your instrument. However, | have marked
optional suggestions of playing a line an octave lower. This is merely a
demonstration so that you can understand how to do this for yourself.

>> WHATIS A CHORD TONE?

A chord tone is a note derived from a chords related scale and makes
up the basic structure of the chord. The basic chord tones in any 7"
chord are: Root-3-5"-7", The 3", 5" and 7" are altered depending on
the quality of the chord.

It’s vital that you understand how to build major 7, minor 7, dominant
7, half-diminished, and diminished 7 chords. For the purposes of this
book, | don’t want to go too deep into that. If you lack this basic un-
derstanding, | would encourage you to check out my jazz theory book
Zero to Improv which goes over this in much more depth.

But let’s quickly go over the formulas for these basic 7* chords.
>> 7™ CHORD FORMULAS:

Formula for a dominant 7 chord: Root-3"-5'-b7
Formula for a minor 7 chord: Root-b3-5"-b7
Formula for a half-diminished chord: Root-b3-b5-b7
Formula for a diminished 7 chord: Root-b3-b5-bb7

The good news is we already have identified the most important chord

Vractice Composing Melodies

In our Blue Bossa study, we talked about the importance of the mel-
ody and how we can use it to inform our improvisation. And though
we took a quick detour to discuss using chord tones in our last improv
lesson, | want to come back to this concept of melody.

As I've already stressed, the ultimate goal in our jazz improvisation
is to create great melodies in the moment. The goal isn’t to play the
most technical, virtuosic lines or the flashiest patterns. If you can do
so while creating great melodies, awesome, but that shouldn’t be your
primary focus.

But how do we learn to create great melodies?

A considerable part of this is listening. The more you listen to jazz and
solos you like, the more you will start to understand the language. The
more melodies of jazz standards or other kinds of songs you listen to
and learn, the more you will widen your musical vocabulary.

But there are some excellent practices for working on this and one of
my favorites is to create contrafacts.

>> WHAT'S A CONTRAFACT?

A contrafact is a melody that is written over top of an existing songs
chord changes, thus creating a new song altogether. Classic examples
of this are rhythm changes heads like “Oleo” or “Anthropology.” Those
are melodies written over the changes to Gershwin’'s “| Got Rhythm.”

You can think of contrafacts as simply borrowing the chord changes of
another song. Not a bad way to compose a song, right? You already
like the chord progression to an existing song, just write a new melody.

Why does writing contrafacts help with improvising melodies?

Improvising is often described as “composition sped up.” But if you
want to compose quickly, wouldn't it be helpful to practice composing
slowly (aka a normal pace)?

When we take the time to compose a melody, we are allowing our-
selves to dream up exactly what we want to express. Composition in
many ways is the ideal environment for creating melodies, not impro-
visation. But when we hear some of the best jazz musicians play it
seems as if they have an endless flow of extraordinary musical ideas.
We want to compose melodies at that speed and at that level.

So this improv lesson requires that you take action. The assignment is
to create a contrafact over the chord changes of It Could Happen
to You.

This doesn’t mean you have to notate it, although that can be a great
idea. But certainly compose it on your instrument, memorize it, and if
you can, record it so you can keep it logged away.

different examples of contrafacts I’ve written over these chord chang-
es. Both are different in their own way, and I'll explain that.

The first thing to point out is that this song does not start on the |

Sweet Georgia Brown starts on the VI7. |t wouldn’t seem like such a

expectedly through the cycle of 4ths to the ii chord. Except once again

Now, if you’re the kind of person that has a problem with breaking the
rules, jazz may not be the most comforting music for you to study.
Why is it possible to change the chord quality of chords within the
diatonic series?

Because why not. So far in our studies, we have observed that a minor
vi chord can be turned into a dominant VI7 chord in the context of a
I-VI-ii-V or even a iii-VI-ii-V. ’ve explained that it creates more tension
and release, therefore enhancing the voice leading movement of the
chord progression. We’ve seen a few other cases of this as well but
usually, have justified the changing of chord qualities as relating to a
different key center.

But in this case, the VI7 and the II7 are clearly relating to the parent
key center (as highlighted in blue). Don’t get too hung up on this, just
know that it is entirely normal and acceptable to turn minor chords in
the diatonic series into dominant 7“ chords.
Bars 9-12 hang on the V chord which is always dominant. So really,

>> WHAT IS A MODE?

A mode is a type of scale. ‘Mode’ comes from the Latin for ‘manner or
method’ but the names of them are Greek because that's where they
originated from. Each mode is related to its parent major scale (Ionian).
You can think of each mode as starting and finishing on a given tone of
the parent scale.

The easiest way for me to describe modes is to think of them as scales
starting and ending on different tones of their parent scale. There are
7 modes (one for each scale tone of the major scale) to be aware of.

I’m going to list them out starting with the parent key of the first chord
of So What.

Luckily, when we break this bridge section down into key centers, it

But what exactly is going on here? Is there any rhyme or reason to why
Richard Rodgers wrote the bridge this way? To our ear this sounds
good. Why is that?

The first element that gives the bridge so much strength is the
melody. I’ve already talked about how important the melody is to any
song, so this shouldn’t come as a surprise. If you listen to how the
melody is composed over these chord changes, it’s perfect. It con-

the cycle of non-diatonic key centers, it sounds completely natural.

The second element is the pattern being used. Yes, it’s a series of
ii-V-l’s which our ears recognize. But the key centers are moving in a
particular order. They are moving in 3rds.

Bb to Gb is a descending major 3" interval.
Gb to D is a descending major 3" interval.
D to Gb is an ascending major 3" interval.

So, there is some order to the madness. This pattern helps our ears
latch on to the movement.

IMPROV LESSON

How do we connect these chords together in our improvisation? That’s
the next natural question, whether you were previously familiar with
this song or not.

Throughout this book, | have been providing you with lots of tools. Let’s
go through some of them to serve as a fresh reminder:

1. Guide tones. | believe guide tones are helpful to connect chord
changes together. That’s why I’ve mapped them out for you in
every lesson.

2. Use the melody. Not sure which notes to choose? Start with the
melody, and incorporate it into what you're playing.

3. Use the chord tones. The root and 5" are up for grabs too. Map
out all of the chord tones. It can help you identify more note
choices to work with.

4. Write a contrafact. Take a step back and slow the improvisa-
tion process down. Compose your own simple melody so you can
begin to discover how to turn note choices into musical phrases.

5. Use motifs. Sometimes creating rhythmic and melodic patterns
over tricky passages can help you simplify things and develop
themes.

6. Write your own licks, or transcribe them. Break the song down
into smaller parts and compose licks over different chord pro-
gressions. Even better: learn from the best and grab some licks
from your favorite jazz musicians. Go straight to the records.
Combine these with having a solid knowledge of navigating ii-V-l’s,
and you’re on the right track.

For me, one of the most powerful practices is composing my own
solos. This is similar in concept to writing a contrafact, except this
time | am expanding from a simple melody.

My goal when composing solos is to slow the improvisation process
down and give myself the space to dream up precisely what | would
like to improvise. It’s incredible to me that when | have done this, it has
helped me solidify the jazz language | know.

I’ve given this assignment to private students and every single time |
get the same response: Wow. That really helped.

Now, if you are a beginner and new to jazz music, you may be getting
intimidated. Don’t be! Recently | stumbled across a solo | had com-
posed my freshman year of college, and | played through it. It was
rough. But the funny thing is | remember at the time feeling like | had
written a solo | wanted to take. This helped me realize how much I’ve
improved over the years.

No matter what level you are at, you can do this. It doesn’t have to
be phenomenal, and it doesn’t have to sound perfect. It just has to be
what you know right now. It will help you solidify that. You don’t have
to write it down. Just compose it on your instrument and memorize it.

Stella by Starlight was written by Victor Young for the 1944 film “The Undecid-
ed.” Ned Washington later wrote lyrics to it. At different times it was adopted
by influential jazz musicians such as Frank Sinatra, Charlie Parker, Stan Getz
and Miles Davis, making it a standard song in jazz repertoire. This song is most
commonly played in the key of concert Bb major.

This is important: listen to the original version. I’ve included a link in
the suggested listening. It’s always good to listen to original record-
ings, but in this particular case, it’s even more important.

Why? The chord changes that jazz musicians play are different.
They have the same essence, but because of the influence of mu-
sicians like Miles Davis and the advent of the Real Book, the chord
changes have evolved into more traditional jazz harmony. If you were
to learn the chord changes from the original version, you wouldn't be
playing the same stuff as other jazz musicians will on gigs and jam
sessions. That’s my disclaimer. In the chord chart, I’ve included the
first chord used in the original version, just to give you some context.

>> WHAT'S A HYBRID II-V-I?

A hybrid ii-V-I is when a minor ii-V-i is mixed with a major ii-V-l. The ii
and V are borrowed from the minor key, but they resolve to the major |.

Example: Dmin7(b5)-G7(b9)-Cmaj7

Hybrid ii-V-l’s come up in lots of other jazz standards such as the Cole
Porter songs “What is This Thing Called Love” and “I Love You.” You
definitely want to be familiar with them. This compositional technique
is used to add a sense of surprise. When you hear the minor ii-V being
played, it gives you the sense that it will resolve to a minor chord, but
then unexpectedly, it resolves to a major chord.

step back and map out the notes we have available to us. While scales
are not musical by themselves, they can help us conceptualize which

and make sense of how to connect the harmony in our lines.

WRAPPING THINGS UP

Jazz standards are the vehicles that jazz musicians use to improvise.
To become a better jazz musician, it is imperative that we study them
and explore their unique harmonic worlds. The more jazz standards you
explore, the more you will understand how to improvise over all of them.

As | said in the introduction of this book, | chose these ten jazz stan-
dards for very specific reasons. | didn’t pick them just so you could
learn them, although | hope you do. | chose them because they all con-
tain powerful harmonic and rhythmic lessons that apply to hundreds of
other standards.

My hope has been that as you have explored this book some of the
mystery of jazz harmony has been cleared up. Indeed, music is a life-
time study. There is no final arrival point. That’s part of what makes it
so intriguing and fun. | hope that these studies will serve you for many
years to come, not just at the time you are first working through them.

Finally, | want to thank you for working through this book and spend-
ing time with me! Although | haven’t been with you in the flesh, when |
write books like these, | always imagine you are in my home studio, and
lam teaching you in person.

Jazz is brilliant music and a wonderful art form to study. Keep on
working through it, enjoy the ride, and remember to have fun on your
musical journey.

-Brent Vaartstra
