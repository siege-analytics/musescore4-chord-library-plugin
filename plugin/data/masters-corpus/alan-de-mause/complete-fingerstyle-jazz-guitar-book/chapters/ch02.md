## Dotted Note Extension Rule

> A dot after a note extends the note for precisely half as long as the note itself. In 4/4
> time a half note normally receives two beats. If it were dotted, it would receive three beats, as in the
> example below.

**Significance (p. 36).** Defines foundational rule for dotted notes that governs all duration calculations.

## Dot vs Tie Distinction

> Although dotted notes and ties are both used as note extenders, there is a difference. A dot
> after a note always extends the note by half the original value of the note. A note with a tie attached
> to the proceeding note on the right extends the original note by the value of the second note. When
> music is notated, the choice between using dotted notes over tied notes is determined by the ease
> of readability.

**Significance (p. 36).** Distinguishes two easily-confused notation tools and gives rule for choosing between them.

## Self-Monitoring While Counting

> Try counting the eighth-note subdivisions aloud, especially in trouble spots. If you listen to
> yourself as you count, you will most likely spot any inaccuracies. For instance, if you listen and
> find that at a certain point you are changing the speed of your counting to match what you think
> the music is asking you to play, stop and check. The pace or tempo of the underlying basic beat
> should always be the same, of course. Pencil in the counting if necessary.

**Significance (p. 40).** Diagnostic technique for self-correcting rhythmic errors.

## Swing/Shuffle Feel Definition

> But an authentic idiomatic interpretation would require eighth notes on 1, 2, 3 and 4 to be
> slightly longer in duration, and notes on and to be proportionally shorter. In early jazz, an accent
> was placed on 1 and 3. Later the place of emphasis changed, landing on 2 and 4. For now, stay
> with 1 and 3. This long-short feeling is called shuffle, or swing. It is also the feeling underlying the
> songs of rhythm and blues. The eighth-note feel will not show up in notes with a greater duration
> than eighth notes.

**Significance (p. 42).** Defines swing/shuffle, the defining rhythmic characteristic of jazz guitar.

## Swing Tempo Limitation

> This shuffle rhythm works best for slower-to-medium blues and jazz. When the tempo is very
> fast, play eighth notes more evenly.

**Significance (p. 42).** Prevents misapplying swing feel at fast tempos, linking rhythm interpretation to musical context.
