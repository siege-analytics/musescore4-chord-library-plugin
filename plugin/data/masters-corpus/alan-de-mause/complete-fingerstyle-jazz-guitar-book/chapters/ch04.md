## Good guitar keys rule

> the most
> common solo guitar keys are C, G, D, A, and F major; A, E, B, and D minor. These keys provide
> plenty of opportunity to use the open strings, which makes the physical task of playing easier.

**Significance (p. 63).** Establishes foundational principle for key selection in fingerstyle arranging.

## Melody range minimum threshold

> tunes with a preponderance of notes lower
>
> than D, fourth string, are too low for two-part (melody and accompaniment) arranging.

**Significance (p. 63).** Concrete lower-bound rule for melody range viability.

## Brightness range rule

> if most of the notes are pitched below the second
> string, B, the arrangement will probably not sound very bright and won’t sparkle.

**Significance (p. 63).** Secondary tonal-quality criterion for register choice.

## Transposing accidentals

> When transposing to a new key, accidentals, sharps, flats, and naturals, not occurring in the
> original key must be transposed too. In measure three of Tune 2, there occurs a G sharp, not
> naturally found in the key of C. To move this accidental to the key of D, count five scale steps up (or
> use the trace down a column method) in D to A and make it sharp.

**Significance (p. 67).** Procedural rule for non-diatonic accidentals during transposition.

## Transposing chord symbols

> When transposing a melody, the chord symbols must be transposed too. Chord letter names (A,
> B, C, etc.) are transposed exactly the same as notes, by using the counting steps or column tracing
> method. The chord qualities (maj7, m7, etc.) stay the same.

**Significance (p. 67).** Rule that chord roots transpose but qualities do not.

## Notation convention stems up

> The stems of the melody notes point up making room for an accompanying part below.

**Significance (p. 68).** Core notational convention for two-voice fingerstyle.

## Beaming eighth notes for instruments

> Eighth notes and notes of smaller values in the vocal melody part are separated, and not
> beamed. This is so the singer can easily match the notes with syllables and words. However, for
> instrumentalists it is easier to read flagged notes (eighths, sixteenths, etc.) if they are beamed
> together.

**Significance (p. 68).** Practical reading-convention difference affecting source interpretation.

## Bass note from chord root

> The root of a chord is
> the important note for now. If the chord symbol is C, the root is C. For starters, just use the bass note
> with the same letter name as the chord name.

**Significance (p. 70).** Foundational rule for thumb-bass accompaniment.

## Bass rhythm vs melody density rule

> If the rhythm of the melody is busy and many-noted, you can
> complement it by playing longer notes in the bass. When the melody is made up of longer note
> values, you can play a busier bass part.

**Significance (p. 74).** Rhythmic counterpoint guideline balancing melody and bass.

## Alternating octaves bass technique

> You can even place a repeated note in
> alternating lower and higher octaves. You will find this in traditional ragtime and folk blues styles

**Significance (p. 73).** Names alternating-octave bass pattern with stylistic context.

## Alternating octaves pitch caveat

> Alternating octaves work best
> with tunes that are pitched in a higher range so that the bass note of the lower octave is not higher
> than the melody note, which would confuse the listener.

**Significance (p. 73).** Constraint preventing voice-crossing error.

## Fingering consistency rule

> Above all, do settle on a fingering, otherwise you
> will always have a moment of hesitation, of quickly sorting through the fingering possibilities when
> you play.

**Significance (p. 74).** Performance-readiness principle for fluent execution.

## Root/fifth bass interval definition

> The interval (distance) between the first
> note of a major scale and the note five scale steps up is called a perfect fifth (or just fifth, henceforth).

**Significance (p. 78).** Defines perfect fifth as theoretical basis for root/fifth bass pattern.
