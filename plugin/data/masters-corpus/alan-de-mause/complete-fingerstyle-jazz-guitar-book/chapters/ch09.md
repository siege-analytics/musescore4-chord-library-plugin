## Guitar as miniature orchestra

> Think of the guitar as a miniature orchestra where melody, rhythm, harmony and counterpoint
> are played at the same time. Lines are accompanied by other lines. Chords exist, but not so often
> as clumps of static harmony than as individual independent voices. Selection of notes and voice
> leading becomes very important, and the standard-brand barre chords we all learned early-on are
> likely to be replaced by smaller, partial chord fragments.

**Significance (p. 114).** Reframes guitar's role from chord-strummer to contrapuntal ensemble.

## Specific-to-general practice method

> To get the most value from this Section, itis important to create specific examples from general
> exercises and to make general exercises from specific examples. For instance, if a concept is
> illustrated in the context of the cycle of fifths or harmonized scale only, you should make it your
> job to select a part of it and use it in a specific spot in a familiar tune. It could be part of an intro,
> ending, bridge, fill—anything, anywhere. Conversely, if you are attracted to a specific idea ina
> tune, take the time to put that idea through various general musical permutations, such as the
> cycle of fifths, or harmonized scale. This enables you to think of using it in other keys and other
> tunes. Make the specific general and the general specific.

**Significance (p. 114).** Author's core bidirectional transfer principle.

## Scale construction half steps

## Third intervals defined

> When two notes are stacked upon each other with a skipped note in-between, a major third ot
> four half steps or a minor third of three half steps is formed.

**Significance (p. 115).** Atomic building blocks for triads and seventh chords.

## Triads from stacked thirds

> When two sets of thirds are stacked, three note chords are formed. These triads will be a
> major, minor, or diminished chord.

**Significance (p. 116).** Defines triad construction by stacking thirds.

## Jazz harmony seventh chords

> The sound of jazz harmony has come to include chords with at least four different notes.
> Counting alternate lines or spaces upward from the lowest note of scale step will produce a chord
> with these intervals: 1-3-5-7. The scale step on which a chord is built affects the numerical
> description of the intervals. If a chord is built from the first scale step, the scale spelling is 1-3-5-
> 7, but if the chord is built on the fifth step of the scale, it is spelled: 5-7-2-4.
>
> Since the top note is seven scale steps (or degrees) away from the bottom note, these chords
> are called seventh chords.

**Significance (p. 116).** Defines minimum harmonic unit of jazz with scale-degree spelling.

## Four chord types in major scale

> Chords in a major scale can be grouped into four types:
>
> Two major sevenths land IV
> One dominant seventh Vv
>
> Three minor sevenths Il, land VI
> One half-diminished seventh Vu

**Significance (p. 117).** Quick-reference summary of diatonic harmony.

## Cycle of fifths defined

> The most basic harmonic movement of jazz is the cycle of fifths. The cycle of fifths, set in the
> context of the major scale descends in units of five scale steps, a perfect fifth (or seven half steps).
> In the diagram below F is a perfect fifth below C. A perfect fifth below F is B, and so it goes until
> moving through the cycle returns to C. This cycle is often graphically drawn as a circle because the
> last leg of the key cycle ends at the starting position.

**Significance (p. 118).** Defines cycle of fifths as foundational harmonic engine of jazz.

## V7-I cycle movement

> The cycle often includes consecutive V7-| (dominant sevenths to major chords) or just V7-V7
> chords. Chords will commonly move every two or four beats, but may be any duration.
