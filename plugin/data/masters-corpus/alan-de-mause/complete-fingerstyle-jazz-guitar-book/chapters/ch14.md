## Intro last chord rule

> Intros can start on any chord. The last chord of the intro must be a V7 or V7 substitute in order to
> move into the first chord of the tune itself. Endings typically use V7-I or substitute. IV-l is also used,
> especially in soul or gospel music.

**Significance (p. 160).** This is the foundational harmonic rule governing how intros and endings must be constructed, establishing the V7 gateway principle that drives all subsequent examples.

## Last measures as intro

> To make a point of intro ordinariness, the last four- or eight measures of the tune itself can often
> be used, including the melody.

**Significance (p. 160).** This practical shortcut teaches players how to instantly generate idiomatic intros from material they already know, reducing the technique to a reuse of existing structure.

## Intro harmonic movement example

> This intro starts solidly in F but immediately moves out of key for a little surprise. The first

> to Ab7 and Gm7, cyclically to C7 and stays on the final V7 with a little sprinkle of whole-tone scale
> thrown in at the C+7.
> Finally, the low pedal tone C is a common device, used also in the example above. It says,
> “Here comes the melody.

**Significance (p. 160).** This annotated example explicitly names the compositional techniques at work (chromatic motion, cycle motion, whole-tone color, low pedal tone), teaching how to construct a harmonically varied intro step by step.

## Vamp definition and function

> A vamp is an intro that is repeated over and over until a cue is given to move into the song
> proper. It occurs in instrumental jazz, but more often is used with singers. Besides setting up the
> feeling and tempo of the tune, the guitarist is repeatedly offering the singer a chance to launch her
> song. It's definitely an ears-up situation.

**Significance (p. 161).** This defines the vamp as a distinct formal device with a specific interactive function, alerting the reader to the listening responsibility it demands.

## II-V7 within one measure

> Especially in jazz, the V7 chord is proceeded by a II chord, as it is here in the last measure. You
> can spot one in measure two, also.

**Significance (p. 161).** This reinforces the II-V7 as the core jazz harmonic unit and trains the reader to recognize it embedded within intro examples.

## 2-in-1 intro/ending trick

> There are three measures leading to a | chord in the last measure, followed by a V7, the classic
> setup chord. What if you just didn’t play the G13 on the third beat? Your intro would become an
> instant ending. It’s no trick, it’s just so. You've just multiplied your list of intros and endings by two.
>
> Another tip: Four measure intros are often two two measure units, so usually whatever you use
> for the last two measures can stand alone as a two measure intro.

**Significance (p. 163).** This gives two concrete reuse rules — omit the final V7 to convert an intro to an ending, and truncate any four-bar intro to a two-bar intro — that dramatically multiply a player's practical vocabulary.

## Endings harmonic formula

> Endings end, and usually on a | chord. V7-I, IV-1, and especially li-V7-| are standard.

**Significance (p. 163).** This concisely states the three harmonic formulas for endings, giving the player a ranked hierarchy to apply in performance.

## Turnaround definition and function

> In most jazz situations, tunes will have several choruses. Typically, the last two measures of
> the tune will set up the first measure of each following chorus with a turnaround. This is practically
> another intro, and whatever you would use as a two-measure intro of a tune you could use as a
> turnaround. Like introductions, turnarounds often start on a | chord and finish on a V7 or V7
> substitute chord.

**Significance (p. 164).** This formally defines the turnaround, links it structurally to the intro, and states its harmonic shape, establishing the conceptual equivalence that lets players share material across both contexts.

## Turnaround backing a singer

> About the only restriction is when you are backing a singer who is holding a note through the last
> two measures. You should then still be accompanying intelligently and not kicking out the jams.

**Significance (p. 164).** This is the one explicit rule limiting turnaround freedom, teaching situational restraint when the soloist's held melody supersedes harmonic improvisation.

## Turnaround with modulation rule

> If the first chord of the second chorus is other than a | chord, the two turnaround measures must
> modulate to the chord of the first measure.

**Significance (p. 164).** This states a critical structural rule that extends turnaround technique to modulating situations, a common real-world performance demand.

## Tag (coda) definition

> A tag, or coda, is a musical extension of the last out chorus of a tune. For one reason or another
> the band leader (or you alone) decides to stretch the music. A standard cue is usually given — “Tag!”
> At this point the replacement of the usual final | chord—E, here—with a V7 (or other substitute) chord
> is required. You have to listen up to hear what kind and how long of a tag it will be.

**Significance (p. 164).** This defines the tag, names its performance cue, and identifies the essential harmonic substitution (I -> V7) that initiates it, giving the player both the conceptual framework and the mechanical trigger.

## Instant modulation: V7-I

> How fast do you want to go? You can modulate as fast as two beats. Presume you have ended
> a tune on the standard | chord and have used two beats doing it. You are moving to D’. Use the
> last two beats to borrow V7 (A>7) from the upcoming new key, Db.
>
> You could even modulate just with one beat on A’7, but you would then arrive at the new key
> a beat too early.

**Significance (p. 165).** This teaches the minimum-time modulation technique, giving a precise beat-count rule and warning about the one-beat pitfall — essential practical knowledge for real-time key changes.

## Back-cycling modulation method

> Using the cycle of fifths as a method of movement, work backwards in the cycle to F7 and then
> C7. Viola! as my fiddle teacher used to say.
> From this chord outline you can create a realization that will fit the style and situation at hand.

**Significance (p. 166).** This teaches back-cycling as a systematic method for filling the harmonic gap between two keys during a modulation, directly applying the cycle-of-fifths concept to a compositional task.

## II-V7-I modulation method

> Again, we know that this one measure modulation begins with a | chord in C and ends with a
>
> setup V7 type chord of the new key, D. What to do in-between? You know from your other experi-
>
> ences that the most common jazz progression unit is Il-V7-I. Use it here then, by putting a I! of the
>
> upcoming new key in front of the new key V-7.
> This II-V7-I is part of the cycle.

**Significance (p. 166).** This teaches the II-V7-I substitution as the smoothest and most idiomatic method for bridging two keys, explicitly connecting it to the cycle and positioning it as the preferred jazz-language solution.
