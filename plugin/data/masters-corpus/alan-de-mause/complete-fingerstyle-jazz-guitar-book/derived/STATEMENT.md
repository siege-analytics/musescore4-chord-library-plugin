# Complete Fingerstyle Jazz Guitar — Statement of Outputs

## Overview

*Complete Fingerstyle Jazz Guitar* by Alan de Mause is a comprehensive method for teaching solo fingerstyle jazz guitar from first principles through finished performance. The book is structured as a carefully sequenced three-section arc: Section One (Chapters 1–4) establishes physical mechanics and foundational notation; Section Two (Chapters 5–8) builds harmonic craft through a progressive hierarchy of harmonization intervals; and Section Three (Chapters 9–15) addresses mature jazz concerns including harmonic theory, self-accompaniment devices, accompanist technique, expressive language, voice leading, structural conventions, and complete finished pieces. The method moves from the hand to the page to the harmony to the idiom, treating each layer as a prerequisite for the next.

The book's operative procedure throughout is the conversion of a lead sheet — a single-note melody plus chord symbols — into a complete fingerstyle solo guitar arrangement. Every technical system the book teaches is in service of this conversion: how to voice the melody, how to harmonize it with intervals below, how to supply and vary the bass, how to deploy harmonic motion at structural moments, and how to inflect the result with jazz's idiomatic expressive devices. The foundational source material is always the lead sheet; the complete performance is always the target.

The book's animating aesthetic principle, stated explicitly in Chapter 15 and reinforced through Chapters 6, 8, and 10, is that whatever sounds good to the ear will work, constrained only by community musical standards. This ear-first pragmatism governs every technical prescription: chord-tone matching is a test to pass, not a rule to follow mechanically; arpeggiation is abandoned when fingering demands it; voice-leading collisions can be valid arrangement solutions. The book teaches a craft in which physical mechanics, harmonic knowledge, and idiomatic listening are integrated into a single judgment the master calls, across different chapters, *effect*, *impression*, and ultimately *the jazz sound itself*.

## Systems

### Melody Harmonization Hierarchy System

The book's central system is a progressive hierarchy of harmonization devices applied to a single-note melody sourced from a lead sheet. Beginning from bare unharmonized melody, the player may add a diatonic third below, promote that third to a tenth (an octave-displaced third functioning as a bass note), substitute or augment with a sixth below, and anchor all of this with an oom-pah root/fifth bass. Each successive member adds harmonic density while following the same chord-tone matching logic — the underlying chord symbol is the arbiter of acceptability, with the ear as the final court of appeal, and melody primacy must be preserved throughout. (Chapter 5 summary; Chapter 6 summary; Chapter 7 summary; Chapter 8 summary)

**Members:**
- **Bare Single-Line Melody** — the unharmonized melody derived from a lead sheet; the entry-point member of the hierarchy
- **Thirds Below (Parallel Thirds Harmonization)** — a harmony note a diatonic third below each melody note, played with free stroke on adjacent strings
- **Tenths (Octave-Displaced Third as Bass)** — the third dropped an octave lower so it functions as an independent bass note beneath the melody
- **Sixths Below (Diatonic Sixth Harmonization)** — a harmony note a diatonic sixth below the melody, functioning as a melody shadow or bass note, with the key note kept on top
- **Root/Fifth Oom-Pah Bass** — alternating root and fifth of each chord in the bass as the foundational accompaniment underneath the melody

**Traversal Rules:**

- **Add Bass to Complete Three-Part Texture** (`_pending:voice-layer-addition`) — When thirds harmonization is present, add root/fifth bass to complete the melody-harmony-bass three-voice arrangement. "Put the bass back in. Even a simple root/root and root/fifth bass can turn the melody with thirds into a full three-part arrangement." (Chapter 6 summary, p. 92)

- **Promote Thirds to Tenths with Independent Bass Rhythm** (`VoiceMotion`) — When moving from parallel thirds to tenths, assign the tenth its own independent rhythmic pattern rather than doubling the melody rhythm. "The tenth does often function as a bass note, but only infrequently does it move parallel to the melodic rhythm. More often it has its own independent rhythm." (Chapter 7 summary, p. 98)

- **Rhythmic Counterpoint Between Melody Density and Bass Density** (`_pending:rhythmic-counterpoint`) — When the melody is rhythmically busy, use longer bass note values; when the melody is sparse, use a busier bass part. "If the rhythm of the melody is busy and many-noted, you can complement it by playing longer notes in the bass." (Chapter 4 summary, p. 74; Chapter 3 summary, p. 56)

- **Abandon Arpeggiation When Melody Leaps Unpredictably** (`TextureCycle`) — Switch away from arpeggiation texture when the melody continually leaps wildly. "If you notice that the melody of the tune you wish to arrange continually leaps up and down wildly, abandon arpeggiation for that particular tune." (Chapter 6 summary, p. 90)

- **Keep Key Note on Top When Traversing to Sixths** (`VoiceMotion`) — When applying sixth-below harmonization, the key note must remain on top to preserve major tonality. "Scales harmonized in sixths have the key note of each scale on top, with the harmony note a sixth below. If the key note were in the bottom position the ear would perceive the scale as being in a minor key." (Chapter 8 summary, p. 108)

**Modification Rules:**

- **Omit Bass Third When Melody Already Supplies It** (`OmissionAllow`) — When the melody note is itself a major or minor third of the chord, do not add a redundant third in the bass; use root or fifth instead. "When the melody is a major or minor third, the bass note is either a root or fifth, and when the melody is other than a third, the third is used in the bass to harmonically identify the chord." (Chapter 5 summary, pp. 82–83)

- **Accept Non-Chord-Tone Thirds That Pass the Ear Test** (`NCTHarmonization`) — When a third-below harmonization lands on a non-chord tone, accept it if it passes an ear test; adjust to a nearby chord tone only if needed. "Non-chord tones make for the interesting spice in an otherwise dull chord-tone-only dish. It's your ear and your decision." (Chapter 6 summary, p. 92)

- **Adjust Sixths That Imply Unwanted Harmonic Extensions** (`NCTHarmonization`) — When a diatonic sixth implies an extension (seventh, ninth) not intended by the chord symbol, optionally adjust the sixth to a strict chord tone. "The minor seventh interval is contained in the C scale, so it is not a clash. You can change the C to D if you wish to keep the sound to a simple Dm chord." (Chapter 8 summary, p. 104)

- **Sharpen or Flatten Fifth for Altered Chord Symbols** (`_pending:altered-interval-derive`) — When the chord symbol specifies an altered fifth (#5 or b5), raise or lower the bass fifth accordingly. "If the chord is C7#5, raise the fifth a half step, and if it is C7b5, lower the fifth a half step." (Chapter 5 summary, p. 77)

- **Use Mixed-Rhythm Bass to De-Emphasize Strict Oom-Pah** (`TextureCycle`) — Replace strict alternating root/fifth with quarter, half, or whole notes in mixed combinations for rhythmic variety. "Another way of de-emphasizing the oom-pah accompaniment while still using only root/fifth is to have bass notes with mixed rhythm moving in quarter, half or whole notes." (Chapter 5 summary, p. 78)

- **Prioritize Musical Effect Over Sustain Perfection** (`_pending:effect-priority`) — Accept shorter note durations in arpeggiated harmonization when fingering constraints prevent full sustain; overall effect supersedes mechanical completeness. "Go for the overall effect, and don't get crazy over the shorter duration of a single note." (Chapter 6 summary, p. 89)

- **More-Is-Less Arranging Restraint** (`DensityCeiling`) — Prefer slow-moving bass, call-and-answer sequences, and selective harmonic devices over dense layering of all available techniques simultaneously. "Here is a tune that isn't cluttered with every arranging device, but is still totally whole, missing nothing." (Chapter 8 summary, p. 111; Chapter 15 summary, p. 168)

### Jazz Harmonic Movement System

This system governs how harmonic motion is constructed and navigated in fingerstyle jazz arrangements. The book identifies three primary movement types — diatonic (scale-step), cycle-of-fifths (descending perfect fifths), and chromatic (half-step root movement) — and establishes specific structural deployment contexts (intro, ending, turnaround, tag, modulation) each with distinct harmonic requirements. The system treats harmonic movement as several simultaneously moving voices rather than block-chord substitution: the core conceptual shift, introduced in Chapter 13, is from chords as static dot-formations to harmonic motion as several moving lines. Seventh chords are the minimum harmonic unit of jazz throughout. (Chapter 9 summary; Chapter 13 summary; Chapter 14 summary)

**Members:**
- **Diatonic Harmony (Scale-Step Movement)** — harmonic movement by whole and half steps within a single key
- **Cycle of Fifths (Descending Perfect Fifth Movement)** — harmonic movement descending by perfect fifths (or ascending fourths), the most basic harmonic movement of jazz
- **Chromatic Root Movement (Half-Step Progression)** — harmonic movement in which chord roots move by half steps, with diminished seventh as the standard connector chord for ascending sequences
- **Structural Deployment Contexts** — formal positions within a performance (intro, ending, turnaround, tag, and modulation), each with specific harmonic requirements
- **Seventh Chord (Minimum Jazz Harmonic Unit)** — four-note chords built by stacking thirds (1–3–5–7); the minimum harmonic unit of jazz harmony

**Traversal Rules:**

- **Voice-Lead by Step Rather Than Root-Position Block Motion** (`VoiceMotion`) — Prefer moving individual voices by half-step, whole-step, or mixed intervals over always placing the root in the bass. "If you think of harmonic movement as several moving voices rather than a glob of dots on a chord box grid, your arrangements and improvisations will be more airy, more interesting and less clunky." (Chapter 13 summary, pp. 155–156)

- **Contrary Motion for V7–I Resolution** (`VoiceMotion`) — In V7–I progressions, accompany a descending top voice with an opposing bass movement rather than parallel motion. "As the top note descends by half step the root note has its own opposing pattern, not parallel to the top." (Chapter 13 summary, p. 156)

- **Tritone Pull Drives V7–I Resolution** (`VoiceMotion`) — The tritone interval within a dominant seventh chord creates tension that resolves by half step to the tonic chord tones. "You can just feel the tug of the unresolved Bb and E (the tritone interval of a-5) being pulled by the A and F in the resolved F chord." (Chapter 13 summary, p. 156)

- **Spread Texture: Bass Alternates Up a Fourth / Down a Fifth** (`_pending:spread-interval-alternation`) — Create a spread texture by alternating bass notes up a fourth and then down a fifth, producing a tenth interval from the melody above. "The root bass notes alternate in moving up a fourth and then down a fifth. Each new bass note is a tenth interval from the melody note above." (Chapter 13 summary, p. 157)

- **Use Chromatic Bass-Note Choice to Maintain Chromatic Continuity** (`VoiceMotion`) — When moving between chords, select the bass voicing note (root, third, fifth) that continues a chromatic line rather than jumping to the root. "The lowest note of Gmaj7 in the first measure is D, the fifth … This was done to continue the effect of chromatic movement." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 120)

- **Use Diminished Seventh as Connector for Ascending Chromatic Movement** (`_pending:connector-chord-insertion`) — When root movement ascends by half steps, insert a diminished seventh chord as the in-between connector chord. "In many cases the diminished seventh chord is the in-between, the hamburger helper of ascending harmonic movement." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 120)

**Modification Rules:**

- **Intro Gateway: Final Chord Must Be V7 or Substitute** (`_pending:structural-gateway-constraint`) — Any intro must close on a V7 or V7 substitute chord to create the harmonic gateway into the first chord of the tune. "The last chord of the intro must be a V7 or V7 substitute in order to move into the first chord of the tune itself." (Chapter 14 summary, p. 160)

- **Tag Initiation: Substitute V7 for Final I Chord** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — To initiate a tag/coda, replace the final I chord with a V7 (or substitute) chord, extending the harmonic motion. "The replacement of the usual final I chord—E, here—with a V7 (or other substitute) chord is required." (Chapter 14 summary, p. 164)

- **II–V7–I as Most Idiomatic Modulation Bridge** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — When modulating between keys, use a II–V7–I of the new key as the smoothest and most idiomatic jazz bridge. "The most common jazz progression unit is II-V7-I. Use it here then, by putting a II of the upcoming new key in front of the new key V7." (Chapter 14 summary, p. 166)

- **Reuse Song's Final Measures as Instant Intro** (`_pending:structural-reuse`) — The last four or eight measures of the tune, including its melody, can directly serve as an intro. "The last four- or eight measures of the tune itself can often be used, including the melody." (Chapter 14 summary, p. 160)

- **Drop Root/Fifth for Rootless Tritone/Fourth Voicing in Ensemble** (`OmissionAllow`) — In ensemble settings where the bassist supplies root and fifth, voice chords as alternating pairs of fourths and tritones without explicitly stating the root. "There is no explicitly stated root or fifth of the chord. If you play in a group, the bassist will cover roots and fifths." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 129)

- **Two Additional Notes Suffice for a Full-Sounding Arrangement** (`DensityFloor`) — A melody accompanied by only two inner-voice notes produces a full arrangement; do not add density beyond what the ear requires. "A melody accompanied by two additional notes is often all you need to make an arrangement sound full." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 125)

- **Deceptive Cadence bVI Substitution for Modulation** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — Insert a bVI chord after diatonic cycle movement to open alternative harmonic paths as a modulation device. "A VI (A-6) of the C scale is inserted. This deceptive cadence opens up several possibilities." (Chapter 15 summary, p. 167)

### Self-Accompaniment Texture System

This system organizes the full toolkit of self-accompaniment textures available to a solo fingerstyle jazz guitarist, arranged from simplest to most complex. Its members range from single root-movement bass notes through doubled bass, root/fifth alternation, walking bass with chromatic approach notes, arpeggiation, two-note inner voices, moving tenths (adapted from stride piano practice), and walking-bass-plus-chord conceived bass-first. Traversal rules govern when and how to move between textures based on ensemble context, melodic density, and stylistic period. Modification rules govern how individual textures are altered: applying anticipation/delay, adjusting voicing register for ensemble, and resolving potential melody/bass overlaps. The system's governing aesthetic is that musical effect and coherence outrank mechanical interval adherence. (Chapter 10 summary; Chapter 11 summary)

**Members:**
- **Root-Movement Bass** — simplest accompaniment: bass notes match chord root names directly
- **Doubled Bass Notes** — two same bass notes per measure; swing-era upright bass style
- **Root/Fifth Alternation** — bass alternates between root and fifth of each chord; folk, country, and jazz oom-pah idiom
- **Walking Bass with Chromatic Approach Notes** — jazz walking bass line using chromatic approach notes to target each chord
- **Arpeggiation** — broken-chord texture appropriate for slower tunes; p plays lower strings, i/m play higher strings with free stroke
- **Two-Note Inner-Voice Voicing** — sparse two-note accompaniment producing full texture with minimal voice count
- **Moving Tenths (Stride Piano Device)** — sustained half-note or quarter-note tenths in the bass, adapted from swing-era piano practice of Fats Waller and Art Tatum
- **Bass-First Walking-Bass-Plus-Chord** — offset bass note preceding the chord, conceived bass-first for maximum harmonic flexibility

**Traversal Rules:**

- **Conceive Bass Line Before Chord in Walking-Bass Texture** (`_pending:generative-order`) — When building a walking-bass-plus-chord texture, plan or improvise the bass line first and add chords afterward. "Think bass-plus-chord, not chord-plus-bass. It's best to conceive of a bass line first—planned or improvised—and then maybe add a chord." (Chapter 11 summary, p. 141)

- **Select Arpeggiation Texture for Slower-Moving Tunes** (`TextureCycle`) — Choose arpeggiation as the accompaniment texture when the tune moves slowly; project the melody note clearly within the arpeggio. "For slower moving tunes, arpeggiation is a natural for fingerstyle playing. Don't forget to emphasize the melody note so that it won't get lost." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 125)

- **Voice High on Top Four/Five Strings When Electric Bass Present** (`_pending:register-separation`) — In ensemble with electric bass, move all chord voicings to the upper four or five strings to avoid muddiness. "Play your rhythm part, whether constant or comping, higher on the board on the top four or at most five strings." (Chapter 11 summary, p. 135)

- **Comping Density: More in Holes, Less When Soloist Is Active** (`DensityCeiling`) — When accompanying a soloist, increase harmonic/rhythmic density during the soloist's rests and reduce it when the soloist plays busily. "Play more when the soloist is not playing, and play less when he is. Play more when there are 'holes' to be filled." (Chapter 11 summary, p. 135)

**Modification Rules:**

- **Apply Anticipation or Delay to Bass Line for Rhythmic Variety** (`_pending:rhythmic-displacement`) — Lead the bass note ahead of the beat (anticipation) or delay it behind the beat to avoid four-on-the-floor heaviness. "Leading with a bass note is a way to give variety to accompaniment instead of just chugging along with four bass notes to a measure… The opposite of leading with the lower line is trailing or delaying it. This too steers away from heaviness." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 124)

- **Prioritize Effect Over Strict Interval Pattern** (`_pending:effect-priority`) — When varying alternating bass patterns, maintaining the low-to-high alternation effect matters more than preserving the exact interval. "Keeping a strict pattern of root/seventh is not as important as long as the effect (low to high alternation) is there." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 123)

- **Reframe Melody/Bass Overlap as Valid Arrangement Solution** (`_pending:voice-overlap-permit`) — When the melody note merges with the top voice of a two-note bass accompaniment, treat this as a valid arrangement solution rather than a problem. "Often the melody may merge with and become the top part of the two note bass accompaniment. It's not a problem, it's a solution." (Chapter 10 summary, p. 126)

- **Avoid Constant Bass Note Doubling in Modern Style** (`TextureCycle`) — Unless recreating vintage jazz, do not double bass notes constantly; modern style favors more variety. "Today the bass doesn't often play four quarter notes to the bar … Unless you, as the guitarist/bassist, want to recreate vintage jazz, avoid constant doubling." (Chapter 11 summary, p. 141)
