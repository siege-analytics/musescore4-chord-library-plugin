# Chapter 1 — Section One/Part 1: Guitars, Hand Positions, Fingerstyle Strokes

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

Chapter 1 establishes the foundational posture and hand mechanics for fingerstyle jazz guitar, beginning with the principle that the guitar must be held by the right elbow, left leg, and chest so the fretting hand remains free for technique. The chapter introduces the p-i-m-a-c finger naming convention and the precise right-hand geometry—thumb inverted in a hitchhiking position, first three fingers tilted back toward the bridge—that prevents collisions and enables efficient stroke mechanics. The rest stroke emerges as the primary melody-production technique, requiring the plucking finger to move through the string and come to rest on the adjacent string, while the alternating i-m finger pattern is established as the foundational fingerstyle locomotion principle, analogous to walking. The chapter defines the one-finger-per-string scheme as an ergonomic alternative for repeated patterns and specifies that the thumb uses only free stroke (never rest stroke) to maintain volume balance subordinate to the melody fingers. Critical terminology introduced includes the E minor pentatonic blues scale as foundational melodic vocabulary, the stem-direction notation convention mapping notes to hand assignment, and the tie symbol as distinct from a slur. Practical requirements complete the chapter: damping open strings by light touch, using the metronome within 60-100 bpm to prioritize accuracy over speed, and never pre-resting plucking fingers on strings before striking.
