# Chapter 3 — Section One/Part 3: Rhythming Around

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 teaches the core two-voice fingerstyle technique of combining rest stroke on the melody (played with i, m, or a) and simultaneous free stroke on the thumb for accompaniment, with the critical rule that adjacent strings require two free strokes to avoid cancellation. The chapter introduces the standard pattern-playing finger-to-string assignment (a→1st, m→2nd, i→3rd, p→4th/5th/6th) and establishes that bass rhythm functions as both a structural and expressive element—the constant bass as a drumlike accompaniment can shift the mood or character of a piece depending on whether quarter notes or half notes are used, a principle that elevates bass-rhythm choice from mechanical drill to arranging decision. Key arranging norms include the expectation that melody notes typically appear more frequently than bass notes in a solo guitar arrangement, the recognition that a lead sheet (single-note melody plus chord symbols) contains all the information needed to create a fingerstyle jazz arrangement, and practical guidance on tune selection (avoiding blistering tempos, complex syncopation, and unfriendly keys) and on detecting modulations through accidentals in melody and chords rather than explicit key-signature changes.
