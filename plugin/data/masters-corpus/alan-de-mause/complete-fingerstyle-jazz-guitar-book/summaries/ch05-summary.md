# Chapter 5 — Section Two/Part 3: Oom-Pah Power

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches the oom-pah bass accompaniment pattern by establishing root and fifth bass notes under each chord, then extends this foundation to handle altered fifths (sharpened or flattened), mixed-rhythm bass for variation, and the critical distinction between major and minor thirds that define chord character. The chapter introduces precise methods for locating thirds using major and minor scales respectively, and establishes the chromatic scale as a universal half-step measuring tool where major thirds always equal four half steps and minor thirds three half steps. A defining characteristic of the master's approach is the stacking-thirds formula—major third plus fifth equals major chord, minor third plus fifth equals minor chord—which explains triads as two thirds stacked together and leads to the procedural rule that when melody contains a major or minor third, the bass uses root or fifth, but when melody lacks a third, the bass supplies it to harmonically identify the chord. Key terminology introduced includes root/fifth bass placement, altered fifths, mixed-rhythm bass technique, major versus minor third, chromatic scale, triads, and the melody-contains-third exception for arranging.
