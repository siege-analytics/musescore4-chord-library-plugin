# Chapter 6 — Section Two/Part 6: Harmonizing a Melody with a Third Below

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch06.md`._

Chapter 6 teaches the technique of harmonizing a melody by adding a note a third below, a familiar sound from casual sing-alongs that requires free stroke technique on adjacent strings to project the top melody line clearly. The chapter introduces the load-bearing concept of chord-tone matching, which evaluates whether harmonized thirds align with the underlying harmonic structure, and establishes a flexible decision rule: non-chord tones are acceptable if they pass the ear test and serve as interesting spice, though adjustments to nearby chord tones are optional. Characteristic of the master's practical approach, the instruction prioritizes musical effect over mechanical perfection—sustain arpeggio notes where possible but accept shorter durations when fingering constraints demand it, and abandon arpeggiation entirely if the melody leaps unpredictably. The chapter also prescribes the canonical right-hand fingering for arpeggios (p plays lower strings, i and m play middle and higher strings with free stroke) and emphasizes that thirds harmonization alone rarely carries a full arrangement, requiring bass notes to complete the three-part texture of melody, harmony, and bass.
