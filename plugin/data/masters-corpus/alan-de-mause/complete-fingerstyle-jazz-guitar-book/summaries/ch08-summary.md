# Chapter 8 — Section Two/Part 8: Harmonizing with Sixths

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch08.md`._

Chapter 8 teaches harmonizing melodies with sixths—notes a sixth scale step below the melody—which function identically to thirds and tenths in relating chord tones to melody notes: when the melody is the root, third, or fifth of a chord, the sixth below becomes the third, fifth, or seventh (or ninth) respectively. The master establishes a foundational voicing rule that sixths must keep the key note on top to preserve the sense of major tonality, and introduces the load-bearing concepts of diatonic sixths (major or minor sixths drawn from the scale) and the adjustment discipline required when sixths imply harmonic extensions like sevenths. Sixths serve dual functional roles as melody shadows or bass notes, and the chapter's characteristic philosophy—visible in the more-is-less principle—emphasizes restraint in arranging, using slow-moving bass, call-and-answer sequences, and selective harmonic devices to create shape and impression without clutter. Practice involves harmonizing familiar tunes with diatonic sixths while making necessary adjustments to avoid unintended harmonic implications.
