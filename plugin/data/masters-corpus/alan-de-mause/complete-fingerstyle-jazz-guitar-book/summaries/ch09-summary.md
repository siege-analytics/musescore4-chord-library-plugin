# Chapter 9 — Section Three/Part 1: Harmonic Background

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 reframes the guitar as a polyphonic instrument capable of playing melody, rhythm, harmony, and counterpoint simultaneously, replacing static barre chords with smaller partial chord fragments built from individual independent voices. The foundational concepts introduced are scales constructed from half-step intervals, thirds (major and minor) as atomic building blocks, triads formed by stacking thirds, and seventh chords as the minimum harmonic unit of jazz harmony. The chapter establishes three primary harmonic movement types: diatonic harmony (movement within the scale by whole and half steps), the cycle of fifths (descending by perfect fifths and including V7-I resolution patterns), and chromatic root movement (progression by half steps). A characteristic pedagogical move is the bidirectional transfer principle—converting general exercises into specific musical contexts (intros, endings, fills) and conversely generalizing specific ideas from tunes through harmonic permutations—enabling application across keys and pieces. The chapter also addresses practical voicing by introducing open-position alternatives to close-position seventh chords to accommodate typical finger spans.
