# Chapter 11 — Section Three/Part 3: Capable Accompanist Accoutrements

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Chapter 11 teaches the philosophy and practical techniques of accompaniment, establishing that good accompanying creates an environment for soloists to do their best work while remaining transparent to the ear. The master distinguishes fingerstyle accompanying from soloing by freeing guitarists from the melody line to add melodic, harmonic, and rhythmic inventions, and introduces comping—a technique evolved from stride piano's sparse punctuation—as the core vocabulary for this role. Key rules include the comping density principle (play more in holes, less when the soloist is busy), register separation when playing alongside electric bass (voice high on the top four or five strings), and the bass-first conception rule (think bass-plus-chord rather than chord-plus-bass to maximize harmonic flexibility). The chapter offers specific solutions for ensemble playing: guitar duets can divide registers or use alternate tunings (demonstrated by Carl Kress and Bucky Pizzarelli), singers require a follower's responsiveness, and single-line accompaniment—drawn from Dixieland listening—provides stylistic variety beyond chord-based approaches. Walking-bass-plus-chord structure, the chapter emphasizes, requires study of actual bass players' techniques including bigger intervals, broken time, and textural variety, with modern style favoring less constant doubling than vintage jazz.
