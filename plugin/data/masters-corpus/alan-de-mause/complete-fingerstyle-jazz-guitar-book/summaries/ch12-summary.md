# Chapter 12 — Section Three/Part 4: Expressive Devices of Jazz

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch12.md`._

Chapter 12 teaches the expressive devices that define jazz guitar language, starting with the foundational rhythmic framework: a quarter-note pulse in 4/4 time underlying melodies played as a stream of eighth notes, with swing feel notated as straight eighths but performed with triplet-based syncopation. Key techniques introduced include off-beat accents (a trademark of jazz phrasing), ghosted notes (de-emphasized through light picking or articulated via hammer-ons and pull-offs for dramatic understatement), and slides or fall-offs (produced by fingering a note and sliding with pressure, with long slides called glissandos). The chapter emphasizes that mastering jazz requires learning its idiomatic street language through listening to live and recorded music, and defines rhythmic displacement—the characteristic use of anticipation and delay to reinterpret written notes—as another defining expressive trademark of the jazz tradition.
