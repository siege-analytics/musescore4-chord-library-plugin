# Chapter 2 — Musical Scales & Intervals

_Pages 26-46._

## Diatonic scale definition

> The diatonic scale is a sequence of 7 musical notes organized in half steps and whole steps.
>     It is based on the 7 “natural” notes: C, D, E, F, G, A, B. The half steps are between the notes:
>     B, C and E, F.

**Significance (p. 7).** Defines the foundational scale and locates the two natural half steps the rest of the book relies on.

## Major scale pattern

> • W-W-H-W-W-W-H • (W = whole tone / H = half tone).

**Significance (p. 7).** The canonical interval formula used to build every major scale referenced later.

## Pattern changes from new root

> We can start the scale from any note, but the sound of the sequence will not be the same,
>     because the scale pattern will be changed. For example: The "C" major scale pattern is:
>     -W - W - H - W - W - W - H, but if we start it from the second note (D) the pattern would
>     change to - W - H - W - W - W - H - W.

**Significance (p. 7).** Explains why accidentals are required to preserve the major pattern in other keys.

## Natural minor (relative) scale

> The natural minor scale, also called relative scale, is the major scale itself started from the
> 6th degree. Therefore, if we have a "C major" scale, the minor scale will be "A minor".

**Significance (p. 8).** Establishes the relative-minor relationship the book uses throughout.

## Relative minor pattern

> •W-H-W-W-H-T-T•

**Significance (p. 8).** Gives the interval formula for the natural minor scale.

## Fretboard memorization steps

> Step 1:
>      Memorize the names of the open string notes.
>
>      Step 2:
>      Memorize the names of the natural notes on the 6th and 5th strings (Note: "natural note"
>      means notes without any accidentals). Notes containing accidentals will consequently be
>      visualized. Remember that the 1st and 6th strings are the same (E).
>
>      Step 3:
>      Notes belonging to the 4th, 3rd string can be visualized using the interval of octaves from
>      the 5th and 6th strings.

**Significance (p. 13).** Prescribes the book's specific octave-based method for learning fretboard notes.

## Second string via fourth string octave

> Step 4:
>                                    68
>      The second string notes can be visualized using an octave from the 4th string. So, before
>      trying to visualize the 2nd string notes, make sure you know the 4th string notes very well.

**Significance (p. 13).** Handles the B-string anomaly via a derived-octave shortcut from the 4th string.

## Shapes vs intervals warning

> However, we must be careful when learning
>      a scale, because if we memorize it only by shape without knowing the intervals, eventually we
>      will have to come back and learn the intervals, otherwise it would be impossible to go to the
>      next step. Therefore, both must be learned at the same time.

**Significance (p. 15).** Core methodological rule: never learn shape without interval understanding.

## Guide notes on 5th and 6th strings

> The secret to visualizing the guitar
>      fretboard is to learn everything having two guide points:
>      a) guide note on the 6th string
>      b) guide note on the 5th string
>      That guide note is what we call ROOT or 1, and all the intervals we play will be related with
>      it, even the most distant ones.

**Significance (p. 15).** Names the book's two-guide-point system used for every interval and chord shape.

## Melodic vs harmonic intervals

> a) MELODIC & HARMONIC INTERVALS : Melodic intervals are notes played one after the
>      other, and harmonic intervals are notes played simultaneously.

**Significance (p. 17).** Foundational distinction needed before any chord/voicing discussion.

## Simple vs compound intervals

> b) SIMPLE & COMPOUND INTERVALS : Simple intervals are intervals within one octave,
>      and compound intervals are those separated by more than one octave.

**Significance (p. 17).** Defines the simple/compound boundary that justifies labels like M9, b9, M2 vs M9.

## Diatonic vs chromatic intervals

> d) DIATONIC & CHROMATIC INTERVALS : Diatonic intervals are those determined
> by notes in the diatonic scale (major scale), and chromatic intervals are those determined by
> modified notes of the diatonic scale.

**Significance (p. 18).** Distinguishes scale-derived intervals from altered ones, prerequisite to chord alterations.

## Enharmonic intervals

> f) ENHARMONIC INTERVALS: These are two notes notated or written differently, but with
> the same sound.

**Significance (p. 18).** Defines enharmonic equivalence used throughout chord-spelling decisions.
