## Circle of 4th / 5th definition

> The circle of fourth (or circle of fifth) is the relationship among the major and the minor keys
> existing on the Western chromatic scale. It is important to understand that everything starts with
> the “C” Natural Major Scale: C, D, E, F, G, A, B, C which has the pattern W-W-H-W-W-W-H
> (W = whole tone / H = half tone ).

**Significance (p. 28).** Establishes the foundational major scale interval pattern that every other key derivation in the chapter depends on.

## Preserving major-scale sound

> To have the same sound of “C” major in other scales, we have to modify some notes to keep the
> melodic pattern W-W-H-W-W-W-H.

**Significance (p. 28).** Explains why sharps and flats exist in other keys, framing accidentals as preservation of an interval pattern rather than arbitrary marks.

## Sharp and flat circles enumerated

> The Circles of 4th and 5th are defined according to the order that Sharps and Flats appear on
> the scales. Below is the list of all major scales: C, G, D, A, E, B, F#, C# (circle of fifth or sharp
> circle) and C, F, Bb, Eb, Ab, Db, Gb, Cb (circle of fourth or flat circle).

**Significance (p. 28).** Lists the canonical sequences of keys for both circles, the load-bearing reference data for the chapter.

## Order of sharps and flats

> SHARP ORDER: F, C , G , D , A , E , B                      FLAT ORDER: B , E , A , D , G , C , F

**Significance (p. 31).** Memorization anchor; the two ordered sequences underlie every key-signature identification rule taught next.

## Identifying key from sharps

> Memorizing the order of sharps and flats helps us realize quickly how many of them exist in
> every key. The secret of sharps is that the last one is a half step below from the major key.

**Significance (p. 31).** Gives the practical rule for naming a sharp key from its signature, a core sight-reading skill.

## Sharp-key identification example

> • What is the key that has three sharps? If we know their order, everything is easy - F#, C#, G#.
> The last sharp is " G# ", which is a half step below from A. Therefore, the key will be A major.

**Significance (p. 31).** Worked example showing how the half-step-below rule is applied to identify a sharp key.

## Identifying key from flats

> But the secret of flats is that the one before the last will be the key. Example:
>
> • What is the key that has 3 flats? The order is Bb, Eb, Ab. "Eb" is the one before the last flat,
> therefore it is the key.

**Significance (p. 31).** The mirror rule for flat keys, paired with a worked example; both rules together cover all key-signature reading.

## Key signature placement

> The key signature is placed on the staff after the clef and before the time signature. It
> determines the key of the song.

**Significance (p. 31).** Defines where key signatures live on the staff, a notational rule the reader needs before interpreting any score.

## Canceling a key change

> However, if there is a change of key in a song, we should cancel the sharps or flats on the
> previous key using the natural sign, and soon after, put the new key signature.

**Significance (p. 31).** Specifies the notational procedure for mid-piece key changes, a concrete rule for writing or reading modulations.

## Major vs minor and relative key

> A song can be in a major key (happy sound) or a minor key (sad sound) with some exceptions
> that we will learn later. We call the relative key those that sound "sad", and it is the VI degree
> in relation to the major key.

**Significance (p. 32).** Defines relative minor as the VI degree of the major, the key concept linking each major signature to its minor counterpart.
