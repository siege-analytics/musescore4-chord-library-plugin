# Chapter 4 — Chord Structures

_Pages 52-195._

## Fretboard as matrix; chord-first method

> The guitar fretboard is a kind of matrix that we must visualize very well. In order to master
>  it, the <irst step is to learn as much as we can about many different chords in different zones.
>  These chords will guide us to learn everything we need to know, including arpeggios, scales
>  and phrases.

**Significance (p. 52).** States the book's organizing thesis: chord visualization across the fretboard is the gateway to scales, arpeggios, and phrases.

## Four root-string chord groups

> a) Chords starting from the 6th string
>  b) Chords starting from the 5th string
>  c) Chords starting from the 4th string
>  d) Chords starting from the 3rd string (works only with triads)

**Significance (p. 52).** Defines the four-zone organizational scheme that structures the entire chapter and book.

## Three chord groups

> Chords can be divided into three speci7ic groups:
>  Triads (3 notes), Tetrads (4 notes) and Extended Chords (more than 4 notes).

**Significance (p. 52).** Establishes the top-level taxonomy used to organize all chord material.

## Root position vs inversion

> When we say root position, we are
>  referring to chords that have the root note as the bass note. Inverted chords are chords that
>  have another note , the 3rd or the 5th , in case of a triad, as the bass note.

**Significance (p. 52).** Defines the inversion vocabulary used throughout the chapter.

## Tertian harmony definition

> Any chord is built based on the notes of a given scale, and the vast majority of them are
>      formed by superimposition of thirds. The chords formed with this interval pattern are
>      called tertian chords and are part of what we call tertian harmony.

**Significance (p. 54).** Defines the theoretical scaffolding (stacked thirds) that justifies the chord-construction logic.

## Four triad types formulas

> 1) major triad = root, major 3rd, perfect 5th.
>      2) minor triad = root, minor 3rd, perfect 5th.
>                                         68
>      3) diminished triad = root, minor 3rd, dim 5th.
>                                 46
>      4) augmented triad = root, major 3rd, augmented 5th.

**Significance (p. 54).** Compact reference for the four triad qualities by interval formula.

## Triad inversion labels

> a ) Root Position = 1,3,5
>      b) First Inversion = 3,5,1
>      c) Second Inversion = 5,1,3

**Significance (p. 54).** Names the three triad positions by chord-tone order, the framework reused for tetrads.

## Slash chord notation

> Chord inversions are indicated by a slash plus the letter of the bass note, which will
> indicate the inversion to be performed. We call these chords, slash chords.
> Example: C major in the 7irst inversion is written: C/E, which reads C over E.

**Significance (p. 55).** Defines slash-chord symbol conventions used throughout the rest of the book.

## Chord Skeletons concept

> So let's memorize some pre-set shapes containing the basic notes to
> build a chord: Root, 3rd, and 5th, starting from the 6th, 5th and 4th strings. These simple
> shapes will be called “Chord Skeletons”. Basically we can have 2 types of chord skeletons,
> one using intervals going to the left side of the root, and another one using intervals going
> to to the right side of the root.

**Significance (p. 57).** Introduces the book's signature 'Chord Skeleton' construct - the central visualization device.

## Right/left side definition

> Note: When we say “right and left side”, we are referring to the perspective of the person
> holding the guitar.

**Significance (p. 57).** Disambiguates the spatial vocabulary that the skeleton system depends on.

## Left-side shape caveat

> It is important to understand that some chords using the left side shape might not work
>      properly because they are almost impossible to play. The 5th string/left side shape, see
>      diagram below, works better than the 6th string/left side shape, which we do not use with
>      a minor chord.

**Significance (p. 58).** Codifies a load-bearing playability rule about which skeletons to avoid.

## Visualizing 4th-string skeleton

> The best way to visualize skeleton shapes starting on the 4th string is to always visualize
>      the root of this chord on the 6th string.

**Significance (p. 60).** States the cross-string anchoring rule for 4th-string visualization.

## Diminished triad shape rationale

> the diminished triad will use a left shape using 3 consecutive strings. Actually
>      this type of diminished shape is not as common as the “real” diminished chord. We use
>      this shape to create phrases (licks) when improvising, or even when we play a solo guitar
>      piece.

**Significance (p. 64).** Explains why the diminished triad gets a non-standard shape and its idiomatic use.

## Symmetric chords - no inversion

> There is no inversion to diminished or augmented chords, because they are symmetric.
> Although we will learn later about symmetrical chords and scales, let’s clarify this idea:
> Symmetric chords are those that repeat themselves in a predetermined intervallic cycle.
> The diminished chord, for example, repeats itself each minor 3rd, and the augmented
> chords repeat themselves every major 3rd.

**Significance (p. 75).** Defines symmetric chords and the rule excluding them from inversion treatment.

## Superimposition of thirds

> If we look closely, the superimposition of thirds from the major scale is a sequence of odd
> intervals R, 3, 5, 7, 9(2), 11(4), 13(6) that will help us Find all chord families.

**Significance (p. 81).** States the foundational stacking-thirds principle that generates every chord family in the book.

## Tetrad subgroups

> After learning the Triad Family, it is time to learn the second chord family, which we call
> Tetrads. However, the term tetrad actually consist of a group of many chord families,
> which contain 4 notes. See below:
>
> a) Seventh Chords
> b) Sixth Chords
> c) Added Tone chords

**Significance (p. 81).** Defines the tetrad super-family and its three sub-families.

## Major 7 default symbol rule

> (The major 7th should always be indicated. The 7th without indication will always be minor)

**Significance (p. 82).** Captures a critical chord-symbol convention used throughout the book.

## Sixth-chord/seventh-chord equivalence

> Although we have 4 sixth chords, two of them are exactly equal to two seventh chords in
> the first inversion: "C6" is exactly the same as "Am7" first inversion and Cm(b6) is exactly the
> same as "Abmaj7" first inversion. We can call them by both names, but depending on the
> harmonic situation one of them will work better.

**Significance (p. 83).** Names a load-bearing enharmonic-chord identity that lets one shape serve two harmonic functions.

## Drop 2 and Drop 3

> a) Drop 2: we drop the second highest note of the tetrad to the bottom note of the tetrad.
>      b) Drop 3: we drop the third highest note of the tetrad to the bottom note of the tetrad.
>
>      It is very important to understand that drop chord voicings can only be used with tetrads,
>      leaving behind the tensions 9th, 11th and 13th that will be used later with the extended
>      chords.

**Significance (p. 84).** Defines the Drop 2 / Drop 3 voicing transformations and their tetrad-only scope.

## Smallest movement voice-leading

> The choice of notes for a chord
>      sequence, most of the time must keep the smallest possible movement between the notes
>      from one chord to another. Certainly, sometimes there may be deliberate leaps, but the
>      smallest movement will almost always be the best way.

**Significance (p. 84).** States the voice-leading rule that governs voicing choices throughout the book.

## Flat-9 between bass and top to avoid

> Note: For major and minor chords, we should avoid intervals of a flat 9th between the bass note
> and the top note of the chord.

**Significance (p. 77).** A characteristic load-bearing voicing rule the author repeats when building 3rd-inversion shapes.

## Suspended chord definition

> Suspended chords are those where the 3rd is replaced by the 4th
>      or 2nd. We call these chords "sus2" and "sus4”. Although the two instances exist, the most
>      common is to replace the 3rd by the 4th.

**Significance (p. 108).** Defines the sus-chord family precisely as 3rd-replacement (not 3rd-omission-plus-add).

## Sus chord visualization tip

> Note: In order to visualize this type of chord properly, it is recommended to
> consider them as dominant 7th chords using some different tension notes. In other words, for the
> "sus" chords visualization, we will use the simple dominant 7th chord shapes we already know
> to guide us.

**Significance (p. 109).** Tells the player to anchor sus shapes to known dominant-7 shapes - a characteristic Benson/Farrell move.

## Sus9 / Sus13 chord-symbol semantics

> • Gsus9: means that we have chord tones: root, perfect 4th, minor 7th and major 9th.
>
>
>      •Gsus13: means that we have chord tones: root, perfect 4th, minor 7th and major 13th.
>       (It can also mean: root, perfect 4th, minor 7th major 9th and major 13th).

**Significance (p. 108).** Decodes the jazz-style sus9 / sus13 symbols into precise chord-tone content.

## 4th-string chords derived from 5th/6th

> The extended chords will be learned by rooting them on the 5th and 6th strings. The 4th
> string will be used to play the 6th and 5th string chords without the bass note. Usually, a
> 4th string chord is derived from a 6th string chord. It will start from the 7th or 6th of the
> chord.

**Significance (p. 127).** Explains the derivation method that lets the player generate 4th-string extended voicings from known root-string shapes.

## Dominant 7 to diminished/half-dim/maj7#4 web

> Almost every 4th string chord can be used and played on the 5th string. By doing this we will
>   see some relationships between many different types of chords. As we have already learned the
>   connection between major and minor chords, it is time to concentrate on dominant 7th chords,
>   which have a strong relationship with diminished, half diminished and major 7th (#4) chords.

**Significance (p. 128).** Names the family of enharmonic substitutions that drive the chapter's altered-dominant material.

## Guitar must omit notes; not piano

> It is important to remember that many of the chords on the guitar have to be edited. This
>      is totally different from the piano which is a complete harmonic instrument, where we can have
>      many chord voicing possibilities without having to omit any note.

**Significance (p. 112).** States the load-bearing instrument-specific constraint that drives voicing choices throughout the chapter.
