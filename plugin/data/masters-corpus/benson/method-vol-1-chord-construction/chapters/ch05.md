## Harmonic Field definition

> As we know, the majority of the chords are built from the superimposition of 3rd 's, and in this
>   chapter we will apply that concept over the Natural Major Scale. In other words, we will build
>   chords from each note from that scale forming what we call: diatonic chords. As we have 7
>   notes, we will have 7 chords as well.

**Significance (p. 177).** Defines how the harmonic field is constructed via tertian stacking on each scale degree, yielding seven diatonic chords.

## Roman numeral degrees

> Each chord, using the same order that they appear (triad or tetrad), is represented by a
>   roman numeral (upper-case numerals represent major chords, and lower-case numerals
>   represent minor chords): I, IIm, IIIm, IV, V, VIm, VIIm. Now that we organized the chords
>   into roman numerals, we will call them “Degrees".

**Significance (p. 177).** Establishes the upper/lowercase Roman numeral convention used throughout the book for diatonic analysis.

## The 7 Modes

> If we start the natural major scale each time from different note, we will have 7 different scales.
> We call them “The 7 Modes”. Each mode will receive a specific name and has its own
> harmonic characteristics.

**Significance (p. 178).** Defines modes as rotations of the natural major scale, anchoring the book's modal framework.

## Modes drive harmony and melody

> It is important to understand that from now on we will use the modes for everything, including
> harmony and melody, because they can show us all the possible tensions for each chord and
> also which is the correct scale for each situation. So, you must memorize the names and the
> scales notes from each one of the modes.

**Significance (p. 178).** States the book's central rule that modes determine both chord tensions and scale choice.

## Types of Notes in the Modes

> a) Chord Notes:
>                        46
>    - Triads (1, 3, 5) or Tetrads (1, 3, 5, 7).
>
> b) Extended Notes:
>   - Tension notes or coloring notes (9, 11, 13).
>
> c) Avoid Notes:
>   - Scale notes that cause harmonic conflict with the chord notes. Melodically they can sound
>     interesting in the middle of a melody, but never as an ending note.
>
> d) Characteristic Notes:
>   - Scale notes that give the mode it's characteristics.

**Significance (p. 178).** Four-category taxonomy of scale notes used to analyze every mode in the book.

## Avoid notes rule

> Scale notes that cause harmonic conflict with the chord notes. Melodically they can sound
>     interesting in the middle of a melody, but never as an ending note.

**Significance (p. 178).** Practical rule for avoid notes: usable mid-phrase, never as a resting tone.

## Relative major/minor sharing chords

> Summarizing, we will use the Natural Major Harmonic Field to organize the
>   “happy sounding songs” and the Natural Minor Harmonic Field to organize the “sad sounding

**Significance (p. 179).** Key conceptual rule: major and minor fields share chords; only the analytic frame differs.

## Extended notes usage rule

> Not all extended notes of a chord must be in it.
>
> Many of them should only be played as the passing tones of a melody on the chord.

**Significance (p. 182).** Key rule clarifying that listed tensions are melodic options, not mandatory voicing components.
