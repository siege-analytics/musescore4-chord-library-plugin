## Study Devices for Harmonic Fields

> a) Play the harmonic /ields in the same key until you feel comfortable.
>   b) Always study with rhythm (funk, swing, etc). It helps you to memorize things faster.
>   c) Before you change or add a new key, make sure you know the key you practiced previously.
>   d) To make visualization easier, choose chords that are close to each other, using only the 6th
>   and 5th string chords. The 4th string chords should be studied only after you are comfortable
>   with the others.

**Significance (p. 187).** Lays out the four-rule practice protocol that governs how the entire harmonic field chapter must be drilled.

## Visualization via bass note

> The best way to not get lost on the guitar fretboard, is to 5irst visualize the bass note of the
>   chord. However, different from scales, we must visualize the root of the chord (bass note)
>   only using the 5th and 6th strings.

**Significance (p. 187).** Defines the chord-root visualization rule that distinguishes chord study from scale study on the fretboard.

>   Cmaj7/E or even C/E, which are the same chords. However, as a phrygian chord, Em7 can

## IV degree lydian #4 rule

> We do not have to put the (#4) in the chord all the time, but in a few cases it might work. In

## V degree dominant sus substitution

> We can replace the V chord (dominant) with a sus chord. Example: G7 can be replaced by
>   Gsus9 or Gsus13. In theory, every Dm/G can be considered as a mixolydian chord even
>   without the note B, which is the 3rd of G.

**Significance (p. 213).** Defines the V-to-sus substitution and the Dm/G mixolydian reinterpretation, central to dominant function harmony.
