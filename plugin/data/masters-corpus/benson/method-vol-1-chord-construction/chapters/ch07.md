# Chapter 7 — Leading Tone & How Other Scales Appeared

_Pages 242-311._

## Leading tone definition

> A leading tone
>   is what we call the note that resolves (or leads to) another tone a half step above or a half step
>   below it. However, functionally speaking it is the VII degree of the diatonic scale (Natural
>   Major Scale). Therefore, the note "B" is the leading tone of the "C" note which gives us the
>   feeling of tension and preparation for this tone.

**Significance (p. 223).** The whole chapter's framework rests on this functional definition of leading tone as the VII degree producing tension-and-resolution.

## Origin: Natural Minor had no leading tone

> One of the greatest musical problems for the church
>   choir at the time was to be able to sing the Natural Minor Scale in tune and this happened for
>   one reason: the Natural Minor Scale had no leading key.

**Significance (p. 223).** Historical premise that motivates the entire derivation of harmonic/melodic minor scales.

## Birth of Harmonic Minor Scale

> So, to end this problem, the VII degree of the Natural Minor Scale was changed half a step up,
>   forming a scale as complete as the Natural Major Scale because now it has a Leading Tone. That

**Significance (p. 223).** Defines the construction rule that produces the Harmonic Minor Scale from the Natural Minor.

## Birth of Melodic Minor Scale

> In addition to the Harmonic Minor Scale, another scale was created: the Melodic Minor Scale.
>   Initially, that scale was used only in its ascending order, because the descending order was
>   difficult to sing in tune, as it sounded a lot like the Natural Major Scale, and contained a minor
>   3rd at the end.

**Significance (p. 223).** Explains the historical asymmetric (ascending-only) origin of melodic minor.

## Bach Scale / modern melodic minor

> Currently the Melodic Minor Scale is considered by most modern authors as being equal to the
> Bach Scale, meaning that it has only one form, both ascending and descending.

**Significance (p. 224).** Establishes the book's working definition of melodic minor as a single symmetric scale.

## Origin of Harmonic Major Scale

> In the mid-twentieth century, the Russian composer Nicolai A. Rimsky-Korsakov, named
> another variation of the Natural Major Scale: Harmonic Major Scale

**Significance (p. 224).** Attributes and names the third new scale system the chapter introduces.

## Three new scale families generate modes

> Just like the natural major and natural minor scale, each one of these three new scales generate
> seven different modes, creating three groups of seven scales each. In theory, we could also
> consider them harmonic fields, however in order to avoid confusion with names, we will call
> them"scale modes".

**Significance (p. 224).** Defines the organizing principle of the chapter: three families x seven modes, called scale modes not harmonic fields.

## Function of derived chord families

> It is important to understant that we cannot use the chord families derived from these three
> groups of scale modes to guide us through songs, we can only use them to add more "colors" to
> the chords.

**Significance (p. 224).** Critical rule: these derived chord families are color/tension devices, not structural progressions.

## Harmonic Minor Im mode intervals

> Im Harmonic Minor: Root /maj 2nd /min 3rd /perf 4th /perf 5th /min 6th /maj 7th

**Significance (p. 225).** Canonical interval spelling for the Harmonic Minor Im mode (the parent scale).

## Avoid/characteristic notes for Am(maj7)

> Avoid notes: minor 6th when the chord is Am(maj7)
> Characteristic notes: major 7th

**Significance (p. 226).** Pattern example of how each derived mode is specified by avoid + characteristic notes.

## Rule: extensions as passing tones

> VERY IMPORTANT:
>
>       Not all extended notes of a chord must be in it.
>
>       Many of them should only be played as the passing tones of a melody on the chord.

**Significance (p. 227).** Stated as VERY IMPORTANT across all three scale families - extensions inform melody, not necessarily voicings.

## Visualization device: bass note on 5th/6th

> The best way to not get lost on the guitar fretboard is to /irst visualize the bass note of the
> chord. However, different from scales, we must visualize the root of the chord (bass note)
> only using the 5th and 6th strings.

**Significance (p. 228).** Core fretboard visualization rule used to navigate each scale's chord family.

## Melodic Minor Im mode intervals

> Im Melodic Minor: Root /maj 2nd /min 3rd /perf 4th /perf 5th /maj 6th /maj 7th

**Significance (p. 251).** Canonical interval spelling for the Melodic Minor Im mode (the parent scale).

## Altered Scale intervals (VII)

> VII Altered : Root /min 2nd /aug 2nd /maj 3rd /aug 4th /min 6th /min 7th

**Significance (p. 251).** Defines the Altered Scale (G7alt) as the VIIth mode of Melodic Minor - load-bearing for jazz dominant theory.

## bIII Lydian Augmented intervals

> bIII Lydian Augmented: Root /maj 2nd /maj 3rd /aug 4th /aug 5th /maj 6th /maj 7th

**Significance (p. 251).** Canonical spelling of Lydian Augmented as bIII of Melodic Minor - signature jazz major7#5 source.

## IV Lydian Dominant intervals

> IV Lydian Dominant: Root /maj 2nd /maj 3rd /aug 4th /perf 5th /maj 6th /min 7th

**Significance (p. 251).** Defines Lydian Dominant (7#11) as the IV of Melodic Minor - core jazz dominant device.

## Harmonic Major I mode intervals

> I Harmonic Major: Root /maj 2nd /maj 3rd /perf 4th /perf 5th /min 6th /maj 7th

**Significance (p. 277).** Canonical interval spelling for the Harmonic Major parent scale - major scale with b6.

## Harmonic Major bVI Lydian Augmented #2/#5

> bVI Lydian Augmented (#2/ #5): Root /aug 2nd /maj 3rd /aug 4th /aug 5th /maj 6th /maj 7th

**Significance (p. 277).** Defines the doubly-augmented mode that emerges from Harmonic Major bVI - distinct from the Melodic Minor Lydian Augmented.

## Harmonic Major VIIm Locrian bb7

> VIIm Locrian (bb7): Root /min 2nd /min 3rd /perf 4th /dim 5th /min 6th /dim 7th

**Significance (p. 277).** Defines the fully diminished 7th mode of Harmonic Major - source for o7(b13) and (11) chord colors.

## Study device: same-key practice

> a) Play the harmonic /ields in the same key until you feel comfortable.

**Significance (p. 228).** First and primary practice rule applied identically to all three scale families.

## Study device: rhythm-based practice

> b) Always study with rhythm (funk, swing, etc). It helps you to memorize things faster.

**Significance (p. 228).** Pedagogical rule: rhythmic context aids memorization of the chord families.

## Study device: chord proximity for 6th/5th strings

> d) To make visualization easier, choose chords that are close to each other, using only the
>    6th and 5th string chords. The 4th string chords should be used as soon as you get
>    confortable with the others.

**Significance (p. 228).** Method rule constraining how the learner approaches each new scale family on the fretboard.
