# Book-level distillation — method-vol-1-chord-construction

_Aggregated from per-chapter summaries under `summaries/`._

## Overall Arc

The George Benson Method, Vol. 1 is structured as a strict bottom-up curriculum that moves from notation literacy to a working theory of diatonic harmony on the guitar. Chapter 1 establishes the notation vocabulary: the staff, the treble/bass/C clef family (with the treble G-clef privileged for guitar), and the grand staff. Chapter 2 builds the pitch and interval system on top of that notation - the diatonic and chromatic scales, the W-W-H-W-W-W-H major formula, the relative minor as the 6th-degree rotation, and a full interval taxonomy (melodic/harmonic, simple/compound, diatonic/chromatic, enharmonic). Chapter 3 turns those scales into a system of key relationships via the Circle of Fourths and Fifths, with explicit memorization anchors (orders of sharps and flats) and mirror-image identification rules. Chapter 4 - the densest chapter - constructs the chord universe by tertian stacking. Chapter 5 organizes those chords into harmonic fields and the seven modes, Chapter 6 prescribes how to practice those harmonic fields, and Chapter 7 extends the field idea to Harmonic Minor, Melodic Minor, and Harmonic Major as color sources rather than progression engines. The pedagogical sequence is itself a thesis: notation, then scales, then key relationships, then chords, then fields, then practice method, then chromatic color.

## The Fretboard-as-Matrix System

The book's load-bearing system is a chord-first visualization of the fretboard. Chapter 4 declares the fretboard a matrix to be navigated chord-first, with arpeggios, scales, and phrases derived from chord knowledge rather than the reverse. Chord shapes are organized into four root-string zones - 6th, 5th, 4th, and (for triads only) 3rd string - and Chapter 2 supplies the spatial scaffolding for this: the Root splits the fretboard into left and right sides, the fretboard repeats at the 12th fret, and two Guide Notes on the 5th and 6th strings serve as the Root reference for every interval and chord shape. Chapter 2 also issues the methodological rule that shapes and intervals must be learned together, never separately. Chapters 6 and 7 then restate a universal practice rule across every harmonic field and every derived family: locate the chord's bass-note root on the 5th and 6th strings first, and defer 4th-string voicings until those are mastered.

## The Chord-Family System (Chapter 4)

Chapter 4 partitions the chord universe into three families - Triads, Tetrads, and Extended Chords - all justified by tertian harmony (R, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13). The signature device is the Chord Skeleton: pre-set Root/3rd/5th shapes on the 6th, 5th, and 4th strings with right-side and left-side variants. Triads have four qualities and three inversions (root, first, second), with the rule that diminished and augmented chords have no inversions because they are symmetric. Tetrads expand to Seventh, Sixth, and Added-Tone subfamilies with a 4-position inversion framework; an unmarked 7 is minor by convention (major 7 must be explicitly indicated); Drop 2 / Drop 3 voicing transformations are restricted to tetrads; and enharmonic identities are formalized (C6 = Am7 first inversion; Cm(b6) = Abmaj7 first inversion). Extended Chords (9, 11, 13) codify the #11-versus-natural-11 rule (use #11 on major and dominant chords to avoid clashing with the major 3rd; minor chords take the natural 11 freely), define altered chords as dominants with more than one altered extension, and note that the 5th is almost always omitted in 13ths. Voice leading is governed by a smallest-movement rule, and a specific voicing prohibition forbids a flat-9 between bass and top note on major or minor chords. Suspended chords are defined strictly as 3rd-replacement (sus2, sus4) and are taught by anchoring to known dominant-7 shapes. A load-bearing instrument constraint runs throughout: unlike the piano, the guitar must edit and omit notes, and this drives nearly every voicing choice.

## The Harmonic Field System (Chapters 5-6)

The harmonic field is the book's organizing frame for harmony in practice. Chapter 5 builds it by stacking thirds on each degree of the natural major scale, yielding seven diatonic chords in Roman numerals (I, IIm, IIIm, IV, V, VIm, VIIm), and pairs each degree with one of the seven modes (Ionian through Locrian) as rotations of the parent scale. The book's central rule here is that modes drive both harmony and melody by revealing each chord's tensions and the correct scale choice. Chapter 5 then introduces a four-category taxonomy of scale notes that becomes load-bearing for the rest of the book: Chord Notes (1-3-5(-7)), Extended Notes (9, 11, 13 tensions), Avoid Notes (usable mid-phrase but never as ending tones), and Characteristic Notes (those that give the mode its identity). It distinguishes the Natural Major and Natural Minor Harmonic Fields as the 'happy' and 'sad' frames sharing chords but differing in analytic vantage, and clarifies that listed extensions are melodic options, not mandatory voicing components. Chapter 6 then prescribes how to study these fields: a four-rule Study Devices protocol (one key at a time, always with a rhythmic groove, mastery before key changes, 6th/5th-string voicings before 4th-string), the Visualization via bass note principle, and a degree-by-degree substitution catalog (e.g., the V dominant-to-sus substitution G7 -> Gsus9/Gsus13, the IIIm phrygian Em7(b6) and slash voicings, the VIIm locrian recast of Bo as G7(9)/B with the 9th forbidden because it produces a b9 avoid note).

## Color Scales and the Leading-Tone Argument (Chapter 7)

Chapter 7 grounds the rest of the book's chromatic vocabulary in a single functional definition: the leading tone is the VII degree of the Natural Major Scale, the half-step tension the Natural Minor lacks. This historical absence motivated raising the natural minor's VII to produce the Harmonic Minor Scale, then the Melodic Minor Scale (treated per the Bach Scale as one symmetric form rather than the older ascending-only convention), and finally Rimsky-Korsakov's Harmonic Major Scale. Each parent scale generates seven scale modes - the book deliberately calls these scale modes rather than harmonic fields, because the rule is that their derived chord families are not progression-guiding structures but coloristic devices layered over existing chords. Signature derived modes are spelled out: from Melodic Minor, the VII Altered Scale, bIII Lydian Augmented, and IV Lydian Dominant; from Harmonic Major, bVI Lydian Augmented (#2/#5) and VIIm Locrian (bb7), the latter sourcing o7(b13) and (11) colors. A recurring VERY IMPORTANT rule restates that extended notes need not sit in the voicing - many belong only as passing tones in the melody over the chord.

## The Method as a Unit

Taken together, the volume teaches an integrated method with four interlocking commitments. First, terminology is fixed and load-bearing: harmonic field, diatonic chords, Degrees, the seven mode names, the Chord/Extended/Avoid/Characteristic note categories, Chord Skeletons, Guide Notes, Drop 2/Drop 3, leading tone, scale modes, Bach Scale. Second, the fretboard is taught chord-first with bass-note visualization on the 5th and 6th strings, 4th-string voicings deferred, and shapes-with-intervals learned as a single unit. Third, practice is rule-governed: one key at a time, always with a rhythmic groove, mastery before modulation, and proximity-based chord selection. Fourth, the analytic frame separates structural diatonic harmony (the natural major and minor harmonic fields of Chapters 5-6) from chromatic color (the derived scale-mode families of Chapter 7), with explicit rules - the smallest-movement voice-leading rule, the #11-versus-11 rule, the no-flat-9-between-bass-and-top rule, the sus-as-3rd-replacement rule, the no-inversions-for-symmetric-chords rule, the VII-9 forbidden in Locrian, and the omit-the-5th-in-13ths convention - constraining every voicing choice. The instrument constraint that the guitar must edit and omit notes, stated in Chapter 4, sits behind the whole method and explains why visualization, families, and bass-note anchoring carry the pedagogical weight that piano-centric theory would assign to literal voice-leading.
