# Chapter 2 — Musical Scales & Intervals

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch02.md`._

Chapter 2 lays the theoretical and notational groundwork the rest of the book depends on, beginning with the diatonic scale built on the seven natural notes with half steps between B-C and E-F, the major-scale formula W-W-H-W-W-W-H, the natural (relative) minor taken from the 6th degree with its W-H-W-W-H-T-T pattern, and the 12-note chromatic universe. It then codifies the book's diagram conventions: left-hand finger numbering 1-4, the X (muted) and O (open) symbols, fret-number indicators tied to the nearest finger, arched-line barres with repeated-number notation for non-index barres, and the rule that scale diagrams show intervals over a chosen root rather than fingerings. A spatial framework is introduced in which the Root splits the fretboard into left and right sides, the fretboard repeats at the 12th fret, and notes are memorized in steps using open strings, naturals on the 6th and 5th strings, and octave shortcuts to the 4th, 3rd, and 2nd strings (with the B-string anomaly handled via a 4th-string octave). A core methodological rule is stated explicitly under the Shapes vs intervals warning - shapes and intervals must be learned together, anchored by two Guide Notes on the 5th and 6th strings that serve as the Root reference for every interval and chord shape. The chapter then defines the load-bearing interval vocabulary: melodic vs harmonic, simple vs compound, diatonic vs chromatic, and enharmonic intervals, preparing the reader for later chord-spelling and alteration work. Ear training is prescribed via the song-reference method, with complete ascending and descending interval tables (24 songs total, from Isn't She Lovely through Willow Weep for Me) and a full inversion table mapping each ascending interval to its descending complement, foundational for chord inversions and voice leading.
