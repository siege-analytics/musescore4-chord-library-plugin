# Chapter 3 — Circle of Fourth / Circle of Fifth

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 teaches the Circle of Fourths and Circle of Fifths as the system of relationships among major and minor keys on the Western chromatic scale, anchored in the C Natural Major Scale and its W-W-H-W-W-W-H interval pattern. Under "Preserving major-scale sound," it frames sharps and flats not as arbitrary accidentals but as modifications required to preserve that pattern in other keys, and then enumerates the canonical sequences: C, G, D, A, E, B, F#, C# for the sharp (fifth) circle and C, F, Bb, Eb, Ab, Db, Gb, Cb for the flat (fourth) circle. The chapter drills the order of sharps (F, C, G, D, A, E, B) and flats (B, E, A, D, G, C, F) as a memorization anchor, then gives two mirror-image identification rules with worked examples: the last sharp is a half step below the major key (three sharps ending on G# yields A major), while for flats the one before the last is the key (three flats ending on Ab gives Eb major). It also covers notational mechanics under "Key signature placement" and "Canceling a key change" — the signature sits between clef and time signature, and modulations are written by naturalizing the old accidentals before introducing the new signature. Finally, under "Major vs minor and relative key," it introduces the load-bearing term relative key, defined as the VI degree of the major and characterized as the "sad" minor counterpart to each major signature.
