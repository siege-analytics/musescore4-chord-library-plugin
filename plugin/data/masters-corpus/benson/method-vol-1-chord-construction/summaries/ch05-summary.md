# Chapter 5 — Harmonic Fields & Scale Modes

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches how the harmonic field is built by stacking thirds on each degree of the natural major scale, yielding seven diatonic chords labeled with upper/lowercase Roman numerals (I, IIm, IIIm, IV, V, VIm, VIIm), with accidentals like bII, bIII, #IV available when needed. It defines the 7 Modes as rotations of the natural major scale (Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Aeolian, Locrian), giving the exact note sets in C and declaring the book's central rule that modes drive both harmony and melody by revealing each chord's tensions and correct scale choice. The chapter introduces a four-category taxonomy of scale notes -- Chord Notes (triads/tetrads 1-3-5(-7)), Extended Notes (9, 11, 13 tensions), Avoid Notes (usable mid-phrase but never as ending tones), and Characteristic Notes (those that give the mode its identity) -- and provides reference cards for Ionian, Lydian (with its #11), Mixolydian (dominant-7th profile with avoid 4th), and Locrian (half-diminished with avoid b2). It distinguishes the Natural Major and Natural Minor Harmonic Fields, framing them as the 'happy' vs. 'sad' organizing systems that share the same chords but differ in analytic frame, and tabulates the natural minor degrees Im, IIm(b5), bIII, IVm, Vm, bVI, bVII. A clarifying rule notes that listed extended notes are melodic passing-tone options, not mandatory voicing components. The load-bearing terminology introduced includes harmonic field, diatonic chords, Degrees, the seven mode names, and the Chord/Extended/Avoid/Characteristic note categories.
