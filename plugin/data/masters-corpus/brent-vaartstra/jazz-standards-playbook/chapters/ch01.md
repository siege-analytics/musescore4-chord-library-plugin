## Jazz as aural language

> Jazz is first and foremost
> aurally learned music. So if you want to become a great jazz im-
> proviser, you need to follow in the footsteps of the great jazz musi-
> cians before you.

**Significance (p. 12).** This is the book's foundational pedagogical rule — ear-first learning over sheet music — which governs every subsequent method recommendation.

## Sheet music proper role

> The idea is not to throw away sheet music but to know its place. If
> you genuinely want to become great jazz improviser, you are going
> to need to get your face out of the pages and open up your ears
> instead.

**Significance (p. 12).** This clarifies the prescriptive rule: sheet music is a checking tool, not a learning primary, establishing the hierarchy students must internalize.

## Ear learning: internalization benefit

> You have to listen to small fragments of music at a
> time and figure it out on your instrument. This naturally results
> in many repetitions. In short, the harder you work for it, the more
> likely you are to remember it.

**Significance (p. 13).** This explains the cognitive mechanism behind ear-first learning — effortful retrieval via repetition — giving students a reason to adopt the harder method.

## Ear learning: reaction/improvisation benefit

> This is where sharpening your ear comes in. Let’s say you are
> playing a jazz standard and a ii-V-| chord progression is coming
> up in the form (in the key of C: Dmin7-G7-Cmaj7).
>
> But suddenly you hear the piano player approach it chromati-
> cally (Ebmin7-Ab7-Dmin7-G7-Cmaj7). What do you do? Do you
> double check the sheet music to see if the piano player is play-
> ing the “correct” chord changes, and then scold him or her for
> getting it wrong? Of course not.

**Significance (p. 13).** This concrete example of a chromatically substituted ii-V-I demonstrates why ear training is non-negotiable for real-time jazz performance.

## LIST process overview

> >> THELIST PROCESS:
>
> Listen
> Internalize
> Sing
>
> Transfer

**Significance (p. 15).** This is the book's central procedural framework — the four-step acronym that structures all subsequent ear-learning instruction.

## Listen step: instrument away

> The most important thing you can do when learning jazz standards is
> put your instrument away and just listen. Find as many recordings as
> possible of the jazz standard you want to learn and go through all of
> them.

**Significance (p. 15).** This gives the operative rule for the Listen step — deliberate, instrument-free listening across multiple recordings before any playing begins.

## Pro tip: learn the lyrics

> >> PRO TIP #1: LEARN THE LYRICS
>
> When choosing versions to listen to, always include one with a vocalist
> if possible. This is obvious to singers, but not always so obvious to in-
> strumentalists.
>
> Learning lyrics is extremely helpful for internalizing melodies. Especial-
> ly in the case of many jazz standards, they are poetic, memorable, and
> tell the story and essence of the song.

**Significance (p. 16).** This is a concrete, actionable technique — including vocal versions and memorizing lyrics — for deepening melodic internalization that instrumentalists typically skip.

## Internalize step: intentional listening

> What do | mean by that? Imagine you are sitting in your living room
> watching a movie. As long as it’s engaging enough for your tastes, it’s
> likely you will sit still, eyes trained on the screen for two hours or more.
> That’s pretty incredible if you think about it.
>
> What if you treated music in the same way you did the movie? In gen-
> eral, it’s a great practice to listen to music and give it your full atten-
> tion, no distractions.

**Significance (p. 16).** This analogy defines 'intentional listening' operationally — undivided, distraction-free attention — distinguishing passive from active listening as a learnable skill.

## Sing step: proof of internalization

> The primary application of singing involves the melody. Be sure that
> you can sing the melody of the standard, both along with the recording
> and on your own, before you learn it on your instrument.

**Significance (p. 17).** This sets a concrete pass/fail criterion — sing it alone before touching the instrument — making the Sing step a gate, not merely a suggestion.

## Pro tip: finding straight melody

> >> PRO TIP #2: FINDING THE BASIC VERSION OF THE MELODY
>
> One common complaint | get is not knowing what the “correct” melody is
> because jazz musicians take so many liberties with it. Here’s something to
> remember: Frank Sinatra is your friend. Frank almost always sings melo-
> dies straight. So if there is a version of Frank doing it, check it out. Through-
> out our studies, | do my best to include a Sinatra version when available.
>
> If it's a less classic song, identify the original composer or artist who orig-
> inally performed it. Chances are, they are going to play the melody true to
> its original intent.
>
> Do listen to, but avoid learning melodies from artists like Billie Holiday or
> Miles Davis. They always mess with the melody (unless it's their compo-
> sition).

**Significance (p. 17).** This gives specific, named artist guidance for sourcing a reliable reference melody — a practical rule that solves a common student obstacle.

## Sing step: 50% rule

> What singing does is it takes away 50% of the learning process. It
> proves you have internalized it and all that is left is transferring that
> musical information to your instrument.

**Significance (p. 17).** This framing positions singing as the conceptual midpoint of the entire learning process, clarifying its functional role in the LIST sequence.

## Singing bass notes for harmony

> If you want to take singing a step further, try singing the bass notes.
> This will help you begin to learn the harmony (chord progressions) of
> the jazz standard. Listen in to the bass instrument on your recordings
>
>
> and see if you can sing the roots.

**Significance.** This extends the Sing step into harmonic ear training by isolating the bass line as a method for learning chord progressions aurally.

## Transfer step: melody then chords

> 1. Transfer the melody. Start learning the melody on your instru-
> ment. Again, this should only be a matter of you taking what
> you can already sing and finding the notes on your instrument.
> Once you find the notes, follow through with plenty of repetition
> so that muscle memory starts kicking in.
>
> 2. Learn the chords. This is the most challenging part of learning
> songs by ear, but | encourage you to do your best! It is incredi-
> bly helpful to have a good understanding of how jazz harmony
> works.

**Significance (p. 18).** This defines the two-sub-step sequence within the Transfer stage, establishing melody-first as the operative order and naming chord learning as the hardest part.

## Pro tip: bass note identification

> >> PRO TIP #3: LISTEN TO THE BASS NOTES
>
> Like | mentioned in the Sing step, the bass note movement is very im-
> portant. For example, if you can identify that the bass player is playing
> a concert Eb note for the first chord, then all you have to do is figure out
> what quality it is. Then you listen to the bass note of the next chord.
> Let's say the following bass note is a concert C. From there you can
> start to identify the chordal movement.

**Significance (p. 18).** This gives a step-by-step ear-training technique for identifying chord progressions by isolating root motion first, then determining chord quality — a concrete method for beginners.

## Chord quality identification by ear

> The trick is to combine knowledge of building chord progressions
> and ear training together to identify the quality of the chords
> (major, minor, dominant...etc.) and how those chords function in
> the chord progressions.

**Significance (p. 18).** This defines the cognitive combination required for aural chord learning — theoretical knowledge plus active ear training — establishing it as a skill pair, not a single ability.
