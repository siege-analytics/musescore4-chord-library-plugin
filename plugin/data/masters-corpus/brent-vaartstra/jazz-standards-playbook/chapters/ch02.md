> ¥ The top line shows the note names spelling out a C major scale.

> Vv The second line shows the quality of chord created when a 7*

> ¥ The fourth line shows the roman numerals for each scale degree.

## Minor Diatonic Series caveat

> Minor Diatonic Series of 7th Chords
> is different from the Major Diatonic Series in that it more so rep-
> resents common chord choices in the minor tonality than an actual
> harmonized scale. It is much more complicated to come up with
> a true scale to harmonize because there are the three different
> minor scales to consider. This becomes problematic when trying
> to provide only one set of roman numeral scale degrees. An entire
> chapter could be dedicated to this, but for now for simplicities sake,
> understand there are borrowed tones from the different scales, re-
> sulting in an imperfect harmonization

**Significance (p. 28).** Corrects a common theoretical misconception by flagging that the minor diatonic chart is a practical convention, not a strict harmonization, preventing misapplication of the framework.

## Relative keys definition

> Major and minor keys that share the same key signature are called rel-
> ative keys. Essentially, they share the exact same notes as each other.

**Significance (p. 32).** Defines relative keys, the central harmonic concept around which the entire Autumn Leaves analysis is built.

## Finding relative key trick

> From the minor key you can either memorize its relative key or use this
> trick: think a major key a minor 3% up from the minor key (or vise-versa).

**Significance (p. 32).** Gives a practical interval-based rule for locating any relative major from a minor key, making the concept immediately actionable on any instrument.

## Guide tones definition

> Guide tones are notes within a chord structure that both help define a
> chord, and can be used to transition to another chord melodically.

**Significance (p. 36).** Defines guide tones precisely, introducing the primary improvisational tool taught in this chapter's improv lesson.

## Guide tones are 3rds and 7ths

> In jazz, the guide tones are almost always the 3rds and 7ths of each
> chord.

**Significance (p. 36).** Specifies exactly which chord tones function as guide tones in jazz, giving students a concrete and immediately applicable rule.

## 3rds and 7ths define chord quality

> The 3rds and 7ths define the chords in this progression. 7“ chords are
> built: Root-3'9-5%-7". The root is never altered, and the 5“ is never al-
> tered unless it is a half-diminished or fully diminished 7% chord. Even
> still, the 5% is not a strong note in the chord. But the 3% and 7* are
> pivotal in defining whether a chord is major, minor, dominant, half-di-
> minished, or fully diminished.

**Significance (p. 37).** Explains why 3rds and 7ths are the guide tones by showing they are the only chord members that change to differentiate chord quality, grounding the rule in music theory.

## Voice leading definition

> Voice leading is the smooth melodic movement of notes (or voices)
> from one chord to the next.

**Significance (p. 37).** Defines voice leading as a companion concept to guide tones, establishing the principle of stepwise resolution that makes guide-tone lines musical.

## 7th resolves to 3rd in ii-V-I

> Notice how the 7* of each chord resolves to the 3 of the next chord.
> It happens to work perfectly this way because the chords are cycling
> in 4ths. But the basic concept is the closest step-wise motion you can
> make from the 7‘ of a chord in this ii-V-l, is the 3 of the next. That’s
> voice leading.

**Significance (p. 37).** Describes the specific voice-leading mechanism — 7th resolving by step to the 3rd of the next chord — that students must internalize to connect guide tones into a melodic line.

**Significance (p. 38).** Prescribes the concrete practice method for internalizing guide tones — isolated half or quarter notes through the full form — giving students a repeatable exercise template.
