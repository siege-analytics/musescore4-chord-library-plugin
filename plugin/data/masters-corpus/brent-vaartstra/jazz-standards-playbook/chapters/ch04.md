## chord function depends on context

> To understand the function of a chord, you must look at both the
> chord that comes before it and after it.

**Significance (p. 61).** This is the chapter's central harmonic principle, establishing that chord analysis is relational rather than absolute.

## secondary dominant definition

> A secondary dominant is when a dominant 7 chord acts as a V chord
> of a diatonic chord other than the tonic. We call this “tonicization.” This
> means the chord the secondary dominant precedes now sounds like a
> new tonic to the listener.

**Significance (p. 62).** Defines secondary dominants and tonicization as new vocabulary essential to understanding the harmonic analysis of this and future chapters.

## 7th chord formulas

> Formula for a major 7 chord: Root-3"-5"-7%
> Formula for a dominant 7 chord: Root-3"-5'-b7
> Formula for a minor 7 chord: Root-b3-5"-b7
> Formula for a half-diminished chord: Root-b3-b5-b7
> Formula for a diminished 7 chord: Root-b3-b5-bb7

**Significance (p. 64).** Provides the explicit interval formulas that underpin all chord tone identification and improvisation work throughout the book.

## guide tones as primary chord tones

> The good news is we already have identified the most important chord
> tones, which are the guide tones. Continue to let the 3rds and 7ths of
> each chord be your guide. But let’s start utilizing these other ones as
> well, because ultimately over and above scales we want to be impro-
> vising off of these.

**Significance (p. 64).** Establishes the pedagogical hierarchy that chord tones—not scales—are the primary material for improvisation, bridging the guide tone concept from earlier chapters.

## chromatic approach note

> In some cases, you will see a “CA” over a note. This stands for “chro-
> matic approach,” meaning the note in question does not represent a
> basic chord tone, extension or alteration, but is simply chromatically
> approaching a diatonic note. In some cases, they are connecting two
> diatonic notes together.

**Significance (p. 65).** Introduces the chromatic approach as a named technique, distinguishing it from structural chord tones and providing a framework for understanding non-chord-tone melodic movement.

## chord tone arpeggio voice leading rule

> The chord tones are played as arpeggios, meaning they always move
> in order. However, the first chord tone of a given chord may not always
> be the root. | have written this exercise using voice leading as a rule of
> thumb. Whatever the last note played in a particular bar was, | move
> to the nearest chord tone of the next chord. You'll notice that often
> this note is even the same note as the previous bar.

**Significance (p. 65).** Defines the voice-leading rule governing the chord tone exercise, teaching smooth arpeggio connection across chord changes as a concrete technique.

## arpeggio order rule

> Regardless of which chord tone is started on, they always go in order.
> Root-3-5%-7*, or 3rd-5%-7%-Root, or 5%-7%-Root-3"...etc.

**Significance (p. 65).** States the invariant ordering rule for arpeggios regardless of starting note, which is the structural constraint that makes the chord tone exercise work as a mapping tool.

## iv minor chord caution

> Don’t get too hung up on the iv minor chord. In your improvisation, you
> will simply want to consider the difference in the chord quality. Don’t
> be playing major thirds or major 7ths!

**Significance (p. 63).** Gives a concrete negative rule for improvising over the iv minor chord, warning against the most common quality-mismatch error.
