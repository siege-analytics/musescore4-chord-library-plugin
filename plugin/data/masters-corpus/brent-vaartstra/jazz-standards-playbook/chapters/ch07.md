## modal harmony definition

> So What is one of the most classic examples of modal harmony. This
> means that the harmonic language is based on the modes of a given
> key center.

**Significance (p. 102).** Defines modal harmony as a concept distinct from functional harmony, establishing the theoretical foundation for the entire chapter.

## modal vs functional harmony

> Modal harmony is not based on “functional harmony.” So far in this
> book, every jazz standard has used harmonic movement based on ei-
> ther the Major or Minor Diatonic Series of 7“ chords. We have seen ii-
> V-| chord progressions, and I-vi-ii-V chord progressions, among others,
> all of which are derived from functional harmony.

**Significance (p. 102).** Draws an explicit contrast between modal and functional harmony, orienting the student to why So What requires a different improvisational approach.

## guide tones omitted for modal tunes

> Because So What is based on modal harmony, there is no need to
> identify the guide tones. This is because there is little to no harmon-
> ic movement actually happening. There are no chords to connect to-
> gether (other than one key change), and because the best way to im-
> provise over modal harmony is by using modes, guide tones aren’t so
> necessary.

**Significance (p. 103).** States the rule that guide tones are unnecessary in modal contexts and gives the reason, directing students to use modes instead as the primary improvisational tool.

## seven modes listed

> The easiest way for me to describe modes is to think of them as scales
> starting and ending on different tones of their parent scale. There are
> 7 modes (one for each scale tone of the major scale) to be aware of.

**Significance (p. 107).** States the rule that there are seven modes, one per scale degree, giving students the complete scope of the modal system.

## Dorian mode formula

> Intervallic formula: W-H-W-W-W-H-W
> Scale tone formula: 1-2-b3-4-5-6-b7
> D Dorian: D-E-F-G-A-B-C

**Significance (p. 108).** Gives the precise intervallic and scale-tone construction of the Dorian mode, the primary mode used throughout So What.

## Dorian as parent scale degree 2

> So what is the parent major
> scale, or lonian, of D Dorian? C major. So a D Dorian is simply a C ma-

**Significance (p. 109).** Concretely explains how to derive D Dorian from its parent key, giving students the practical formula for applying modal thinking to So What.

## Eb Dorian key center change

> For these brief 8 bars it moves to Eb Dorian, which means the parent
> key is Db major. This is just a half step up from the previous key center.
> So the two parent keys we are dealing with in So What are C major
> and Db major.

**Significance (p. 110).** Identifies the only harmonic shift in the tune — a half-step key center change — and names both parent keys, giving students the complete harmonic map for improvisation.

## modes as note choices, not scales

> As |’ve mentioned already with scales, this doesn’t mean you should
> play the scales like scales. You should just use them as note choices,
> and sometimes thinking of them in the context of different modes can
> be helpful.

**Significance (p. 111).** States the critical rule distinguishing idiomatic modal improvisation from mechanical scale-running, shaping how students should apply all the modal exercises.

## modal arpeggio exercise principle

> In this exercise, | have connected each arpeggio with the nearest chord
> tone of the proceeding chord. As you'll notice, this means the arpeggio
> of each chord doesn’t always start on the root. But that’s a good thing
> because you should be able to play a chord starting on any chord tone.

**Significance (p. 112).** Establishes the voice-leading rule for connecting arpeggios — targeting the nearest chord tone rather than always starting on the root — a core technique for smooth modal melodic lines.

## modal pattern exercise in 3rds

> We are using a descending and ascending pattern connecting the
> modes together. In this case, the pattern moves in 3rds and connects
> to the next mode in either a half step or a whole step.

**Significance (p. 113).** Defines the specific intervallic mechanism of the modal pattern exercise, teaching students how adjacent modes are linked through 3rd-based motion and half/whole-step connections.

## Miles Davis style characteristics

> v He uses lots of space. He doesn’t feel the need to play lots of
> notes.
>
> ¥ He plays a phrase, leaves some space, and then expands upon
> that phrase.
>
> v¥ He generally plays within the Dorian mode, with exceptions of
> course and occasional chromatic approach notes and such.

**Significance (p. 114).** Distills Miles Davis's modal improvisation style into three actionable rules — space, phrase-then-expand, and Dorian-with-chromatic exceptions — giving students a model to emulate.
