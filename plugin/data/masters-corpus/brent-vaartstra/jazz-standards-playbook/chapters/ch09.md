## Diminished chord as dominant substitute

> The
> F#tdim/7 chord is a substitute for the dominant VI7 chord.

**Significance (p. 141).** Establishes the core harmonic function of the passing diminished chord, grounding what might seem like a chromatic anomaly in conventional dominant-substitution theory.

## Diminished 7 as rootless dominant b9

> F#dim7: F#-A-C-Eb
> D7(b9): D-F#-A-C-Eb
>
> Do you see the similarities in these two chords? They are virtually the
> same except the root in the D7(b9). You can think of the F#dim7 as a
> rootless D7(b9).

**Significance (p. 141).** Gives the student the concrete pitch-level proof that dim7 and dominant b9 share all tones except the root, making the substitution relationship audible and analyzable.

## Chromatic passing function of diminished

> Now, on a compositional level the F#dim7 is functioning as more than
> just a substitution. It’s also a creating a chromatic passing note, in this
> case the bass note, to connect the | chord with the ii chord.

**Significance (p. 142).** Distinguishes two simultaneous functions of the diminished chord—harmonic substitution and voice-leading bass motion—giving students a dual analytical framework.

## Bridge key centers move in 3rds

> The second element is the pattern being used. Yes, it’s a series of
> ii-V-l’s which our ears recognize. But the key centers are moving in a
> particular order. They are moving in 3rds.
>
> Bb to Gb is a descending major 3" interval.
> Gb to D is a descending major 3" interval.
> D to Gb is an ascending major 3" interval.

**Significance (p. 144).** Identifies the structural logic (cycle of major 3rds) behind the bridge's rapid key changes, directly linking this song's construction to Coltrane Changes.

## Bridge as stacked ii-V-Is

> Luckily, when we break this bridge section down into key centers, it
> begins to feel easier to understand. Instead of looking at it as a mish-
> mash of seemingly unrelated chords, you can just look at it as a bunch
> of ii-V-l’s in different key centers.

**Significance (p. 143).** Provides the practical navigational strategy for the bridge: reframe complex non-diatonic motion as a sequence of familiar ii-V-I units, each in its own momentary key.

## Whole-Half diminished scale formula

> It’s called the “Whole-Half” diminished because
> the intervallic formula for this scale is: W-H-W-H-W-H-W. The scale is
> based on alternating whole steps and half steps.

**Significance (p. 145).** Defines the Whole-Half diminished scale by its intervallic construction, giving students the theoretical basis for note choice over diminished 7 chords.

## Improv note choice over diminished

> Remember that it is being substituted for the VI7b9 chord, so any of
> those chord tones will serve you well.

**Significance (p. 145).** Gives a direct, actionable improv rule: treat the passing dim7 as a dominant b9 and target its chord tones, connecting harmony analysis to melodic practice.

## Connecting key centers by half/whole step

> It’s important to notice that | do my best to enter into each new key
> center by half steps or whole steps. I’m referring to the first and last
> notes of connecting bars. This isn’t a rule or mandatory by any means,
> but it does allow for a smooth flow in the lines.

**Significance (p. 147).** Teaches a concrete melodic technique for bridging rapid key changes—stepwise connection at bar boundaries—while correctly framing it as a preference rather than a rule.

## Disconnecting lines between key centers

> It's also perfectly fine to not connected the key centers together. |
> did this in bar 3 of this last lick. | ended on a half note and started a
> new line in the next bar. Nothing wrong with that. As long as your line
> doesn’t sound like it’s unnaturally starting and stopping, this can be a
> great solution.

**Significance (p. 148).** Validates the alternative approach of phrase separation at key-center changes, preventing students from over-applying the stepwise-connection technique.
