## six improvisation tools

> 1. Guide tones. | believe guide tones are helpful to connect chord
> changes together. That’s why I’ve mapped them out for you in
> every lesson.
>
> 2. Use the melody. Not sure which notes to choose? Start with the
> melody, and incorporate it into what you're playing.
>
> 3. Use the chord tones. The root and 5" are up for grabs too. Map
> out all of the chord tones. It can help you identify more note
> choices to work with.
>
> 4. Write a contrafact. Take a step back and slow the improvisa-
> tion process down. Compose your own simple melody so you can
> begin to discover how to turn note choices into musical phrases.
>
> 5. Use motifs. Sometimes creating rhythmic and melodic patterns
> over tricky passages can help you simplify things and develop
> themes.
>
> 6. Write your own licks, or transcribe them. Break the song down
> into smaller parts and compose licks over different chord pro-
> gressions. Even better: learn from the best and grab some licks
> from your favorite jazz musicians. Go straight to the records.

**Significance (p. 162).** Consolidates the book's full improvisation toolkit into one numbered list, making it the go-to reference for how to approach any difficult standard.
