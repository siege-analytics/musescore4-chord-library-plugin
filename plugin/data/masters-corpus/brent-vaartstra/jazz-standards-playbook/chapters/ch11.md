## backdoor dominant definition

> A backdoor dominant is a dominant 7" chord that substitutes the V7
> chord for a bVII7 chord approaching the | chord by a whole step. This
> works because the bVII7 has a lot of notes in common with an altered
> V7 chord.
>
> Example: Bb7-Cmaj/

**Significance (p. 177).** Defines the backdoor dominant concept with a concrete example, a harmonic device that recurs throughout the chapter's analysis of Stella.

## hybrid ii-V-I definition

> A hybrid ii-V-I is when a minor ii-V-i is mixed with a major ii-V-l. The ii
> and V are borrowed from the minor key, but they resolve to the major |.
>
> Example: Dmin7(b5)-G7(b9)-Cmaj7

**Significance (p. 179).** Defines the hybrid ii-V-I as a distinct harmonic construction, giving the reader a transferable concept applicable across many other jazz standards.

## hybrid ii-V-I expressive effect

> This compositional technique
> is used to add a sense of surprise. When you hear the minor ii-V being
> played, it gives you the sense that it will resolve to a minor chord, but
> then unexpectedly, it resolves to a major chord.

**Significance (p. 180).** Explains the perceptual/expressive function of the hybrid ii-V-I, giving the reader an aural target when listening or improvising.

## chord changes vs. original version

> The chord changes that jazz musicians play are different.
> They have the same essence, but because of the influence of mu-
> sicians like Miles Davis and the advent of the Real Book, the chord
> changes have evolved into more traditional jazz harmony. If you were
> to learn the chord changes from the original version, you wouldn't be
> playing the same stuff as other jazz musicians will on gigs and jam
> sessions.

**Significance (p. 168).** Establishes a practical performance rule: use the evolved Real Book changes, not the original film version, to be compatible with other jazz musicians.

## unresolved ii-V progressions

> The first thing to note is that we have a series of unresolved ii-V chord
> progressions within the first 8 bars. We have seen these before. This
> just means the progressions do not resolve to the | chord but move
> somewhere else. The key center is being eluded to, yet never comes
> home.

**Significance (p. 177).** Identifies the defining harmonic characteristic of Stella's opening — tonally evasive unresolved ii-Vs — which governs how the entire song should be understood and navigated.

## backdoor ii-V concept

> The ii chord is being added in front of the backdoor dominant. There-
> fore, the Bbmin7-Eb7 is really acting as a backdoor ii-V into the Fmaj7.

**Significance (p. 178).** Extends the backdoor dominant concept to the full backdoor ii-V, showing how a preceding ii chord can be incorporated into the substitution pattern.

## note map as improv tool

> When we come across songs like Stella, it can be beneficial to take a
> step back and map out the notes we have available to us. While scales
> are not musical by themselves, they can help us conceptualize which
> notes we can choose from. From there, we can start to build melodies
> and make sense of how to connect the harmony in our lines.

**Significance (p. 181).** Introduces the note-mapping approach as a conceptual scaffolding tool for improvisation over harmonically dense material.

## quarter note solo exercise

> One exercise | like to do to keep things simple and help me start to
> improvise is create quarter note solos. This means | stick to a quarter
> note rhythm in all of my note choices. | take out the rhythmic compo-
> nent from my improvisation and just focus on navigating the changes.

**Significance (p. 184).** Prescribes a specific practice technique that isolates harmonic navigation from rhythm, reducing cognitive load when learning to improvise over complex changes.

## cycle of 4ths minor ii-Vs

> Bar 25 marks the last section of the song. The first two bars are the
> same as the beginning of the tune, but this time we continue on with a
> cycle of 4ths. This cycle of 4ths is different than we have seen before
> in that they are a series of minor ii-V’s.

**Significance (p. 179).** Distinguishes the closing cycle-of-4ths progression as built from minor ii-Vs rather than dominant ii-Vs, a structural distinction that affects scale choice during improvisation.
