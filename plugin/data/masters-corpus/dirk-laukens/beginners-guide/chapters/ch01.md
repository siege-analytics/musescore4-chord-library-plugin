## Essential chord types

> The 17 chord shapes shown on the following pages are essential
> knowledge for every beginning jazz guitarist to get started. The chords
> you'll learn here are maj7, dominant 7, minor 7, half diminished 7 and
> diminished 7.

**Significance (p. 10).** This passage defines the complete set of chord qualities the chapter covers, establishing the harmonic vocabulary a beginning jazz guitarist must master.

## Root dot notation

> A red dot @ represents the root (aka 1 or bass note) of the chord. The root is
> the note that gives the chord the first part of its name (C or A for example). On
> this chart, all bass notes are C. By moving the chord shapes up or down the
> guitar neck you get other chords of the same type (you'll learn how a bit further,
> in the chapter about transposing chords).

**Significance (p. 10).** This defines the diagram notation system used throughout the entire book, making it foundational for reading every chord diagram that follows.

## Chord tone numbers

> The numbers (@@@_) inside the note markers are chord tones. These chord
> tones give the chord the second part of its name (maj7 or min7 for example).

**Significance (p. 10).** This explains how the numeric labels inside chord diagrams map to chord quality names, which is essential for understanding the notation throughout the method.

## String 6 root: right-hand technique

> The bass note is played with the
> thumb of the right hand, the other chord tones are simultaneously played with
> fingers 1, 2 and 3 (index, middle finger and ring finger).

**Significance (p. 10).** This prescribes the specific fingerpicking technique required for the root-on-string-6 chord voicings, establishing the right-hand approach used throughout this section.

## Transposing by moving root

> When doing so, you don't have to learn new chords, you
> just have to recognize where the root of the chord is and move it to the note you
> need.

**Significance (p. 13).** This states the core transposition rule of the method — that chord shapes are moveable and rooted, which unlocks all 12 keys from a single shape.

## Transposition rule: root = chord name

> Remember: the red note (in this case the lowest note), tells you what the
> name of the chord is when played on the guitar.

**Significance (p. 15).** This is the single governing rule for using moveable chord shapes across the neck, and the book explicitly flags it as a summary principle to memorize.

## ii V I vi progression

> you're ready to learn your first jazz chord
> progression: ii V | vi (if you’re not sure what these Roman numerals mean, click
> here to learn more about them in the appendix of this ebook).
>
> These chords, or variations of these chords, are found in countless jazz standards
> and are essential knowledge for anyone learning jazz guitar.
>
> The progression is taken from the major scale, where four of the seven chords
> are extracted and arranged to form this progression.

**Significance (p. 15).** This introduces the ii–V–I–vi as the foundational jazz progression, explaining its origin in the major scale and its ubiquity in jazz standards.

## Freddie Green rhythm

> The first rhythm (steady quarter notes
> aka four to the bar), is often called the
> Freddie Green rhythm, named after
> Count Basie’s guitarist. When playing this
> rhythm, accenting the 2nd and 4th notes
> of each bar gives you a swing feel that
> emulates Green's driving pulse.

**Significance (p. 19).** This names and defines the foundational jazz comping rhythm, and gives the specific instruction to accent beats 2 and 4 to achieve authentic swing feel.

## Beats 1 and 3: chord cutoff rule

> Go slow, count, and make sure you cut the chords off so they don't ring over on
> beats 2 and 4, which is important to maintaining the swing feel with this rhythm.

**Significance (p. 20).** This states an explicit rule about note duration — chords must be muted before beats 2 and 4 — which is a technique requirement, not just a stylistic preference.

## Beats 2 and 4 comping

> You now take the first and third beats out of each bar as you play chords on beats
> 2 and 4 in each measure.
>
> When doing so, you line up with the hi-hat on the drum kit, bringing a heavier
> swing to your comping in the process.

**Significance (p. 21).** This explains the rhythmic rationale for the 2-and-4 pattern by linking it to the drummer's hi-hat, teaching the guitarist how their comping fits the ensemble texture.

## Syncopation: rushing warning

> Though it sounds cool, it's hard to play syncopated rhythms and not rush the
> time. Because of this, work with a metronome and go slow until you're ready to
> speed this pattern.

**Significance (p. 22).** This gives an explicit practice rule — always use a metronome for syncopated figures — addressing the most common technical pitfall of this rhythm type.

## Anticipation defined

> The next rhythm places chords on the & of 2 and 4 in each bar. This technique is
> called “anticipation,” and is an essential jazz rhythmic concept.
>
> Anticipation is placing the chord from the next bar on the & of 4 in the bar
> you're on (such as the G7 on the & of 4 in bar 1).

**Significance (p. 22).** This defines anticipation — one of jazz's most characteristic comping devices — and provides a concrete example of how the next-bar chord is borrowed into the current bar.

## Charleston rhythm

> The final rhythm is called the Charleston, as it’s named after the famous dance
> from the early 1900s.
>
> In this rhythm, you play a chord on beat one of each bar, followed by a chord on
> the & of 2 in each bar.

**Significance (p. 23).** This names and defines the Charleston pattern — a cornerstone jazz comping figure — and specifies its two-attack placement (beat 1 and the & of 2).

## Autumn Leaves study: practice method

> Go slow, learn each four-bar phrase one at a time, and then put everything
> together from there as you learn the whole tune.
>
> After you can play these chords and rhythms, change the rhythms, as you expand
> this exercise in the woodshed.

**Significance (p. 24).** This prescribes a phrase-by-phrase learning sequence and then calls for rhythmic variation as an expansion exercise, modeling the method's broader approach to learning repertoire.
