## Learning jazz standards priority

> When learning these songs, you want to learn the melody, chords, and soloing, so
> you can function in a jam situation.
>
> Don't worry about learning all 10 at once, it’s better to have one that you can jam
> with other people, rather than have parts of several tunes under your fingers.

**Significance (p. 29).** This sets the method's core priority: depth on one tune over breadth across many, and functional jam-readiness as the measure of progress.

## Learning from recordings rule

> you can learn these songs from a Real Book, but make sure to listen to
> recorded versions by famous jazz musicians of these same songs.
>
> This introduces these songs and their interpretation to your ears, making it
> easier to learn when you take the chords, melody, and soloing to the fretboard.

**Significance (p. 29).** This establishes the method’s ear-first principle: recordings precede fretboard work so that interpretation is internalized before notes are placed.

## Minor blues turnaround variations

> As with any minor blues head, the turnaround for Birks’ Works has a few common
> variations that you will run into on a jam session. These variations include bVI-V-I,
> ii-V-I, or even biii-bVI/ii V/I.

**Significance (p. 30).** This defines the three standard turnaround substitution families a guitarist must recognize on sight in minor blues contexts.

## Summertime minor-key progressions

> It also acts as a nice introduction to common minor key progressions, such as ii V

**Significance (p. 33).** This identifies two transferable harmonic concepts — minor ii-V-I and tonic minor/major mixing — using Summertime as the teaching vehicle.

## Pentatonic as bridge to jazz soloing

> for beginner jazz guitarists, you can use the minor pentatonic or minor blues
> scale to solo over this entire progression. This makes it a great “bridge” tune for those
> coming from a rock and blues background and who are now studying jazz in their
> practice routine.

**Significance (p. 33).** This gives a concrete soloing rule — minor pentatonic/blues scale works over Summertime’s whole progression — explicitly bridging rock/blues vocabulary into a jazz context.

## Maiden Voyage modal harmony

> Either way, the chords are modal in nature, and move around to four different
> keys as you navigate this 32-bar form.
>
> A fun study in modal jazz, Dorian modes, rhythmic control, and key changes that
> challenges you in the woodshed and adds to your jam session Set list.

**Significance (p. 34).** This defines Maiden Voyage’s harmonic identity as a Dorian modal study with four key centers, framing it as the entry point for modal jazz vocabulary.

## Blue Bossa syncopation rule

> Because it’s a bossa groove, you need to spend time on syncopation in both your
> soloing and comping.
>
> Working on syncopation in your chords and lines not only prepares you to jam
> this tune, it prepares you for many other jazz jam situations as well.

**Significance (p. 38).** This gives a genre-specific rule — bossa requires syncopation in both comping and soloing — and generalizes it as a skill that transfers beyond this single tune.

## Autumn Leaves key duality

> It can be played in Em or Gm, and therefore is an introduction to tunes that are
> commonly played in more than one key, forcing you to learn it in both keys.
>
> There are also major ii V I’s and minor ii V I's to navigate in your comping and
> soloing, and the melody line is ripe for a chord melody arrangement.

**Significance (p. 39).** This defines Autumn Leaves as the canonical study for both key-transposition discipline and mixed major/minor ii-V-I navigation in a single form.

## Chord melody melody placement

> The melody has been moved to mostly the top 2 strings, compared to the whole
> fretboard in the single-note example. This is to accommodate the chords played
> below the melody line in this arrangement.

**Significance (p. 43).** This states the fundamental voicing principle of chord melody: melody is confined to the upper strings so chord harmony can be voiced beneath it.

## Chord melody definition

> Playing bass, chords and melody at the same time (aka chord melody) is not
> an easy thing to do.

**Significance (p. 47).** This provides the book’s explicit three-element definition of chord melody — bass, chords, and melody simultaneously — as a distinct technique category.
