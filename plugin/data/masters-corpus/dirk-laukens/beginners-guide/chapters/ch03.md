## Arpeggio definition

> An arpeggio is a broken chord, where the notes of the chord are played in
> succession instead of simultaneously.

**Significance (p. 48).** This is the foundational definition the entire chapter builds on, distinguishing arpeggios from strummed chords.

## Jazz vs. pop arpeggio technique

> In jazz (and blues, metal, etc), arpeggios are used for soloing instead of
> accompaniment. In contrast to arpeggios used in other genres of music, the
> notes of a jazz guitar arpeggio are usually played with a plectrum (unless you play
> fingerstyle) and muted after they are played, so they don't ring together. Another
> contrast is that these arpeggios are not based on a chord shape.

**Significance (p. 49).** This defines the jazz-specific execution technique—plectrum, note muting, and independence from chord shapes—that separates jazz arpeggio practice from classical or pop usage.

## Arpeggios in soloing: three uses

> ¢ Playing the chord tones in your guitar solo will outline the harmony of the tune.
> This gives your improvisation a sense of direction, making it more interesting to
> listen to.
>
> ¢ Arpeggios make it easier to improvise a nice voice leading, making your solos
> more melodic.
>
> * You can use arpeggios to add color and complexity to your solos by using
> substitutions.

**Significance (p. 50).** This enumerates the three functional reasons for using arpeggios in jazz improvisation, framing the chapter's practical goals.

## Scope of arpeggio knowledge required

> Every jazz guitarists needs to know how to play the arpeggios of all chord
> types in all positions of the guitar neck.

**Significance (p. 50).** This is the method's core prescriptive rule establishing the full scope of study expected from the student.

## 251 progression arpeggio types

**Significance (p. 51).** This anchors all subsequent arpeggio shapes to the ii-V-I context, explaining why minor, dominant, and major are the three essential types to learn first.

## Alternating arpeggios: voice leading rule

> The next step is alternating the arpeggios. We do this by playing the first arpeggio
> (Am7) for 1 bar and then switch to the nearest note of the second arpeggio (D7) in
> the second bar. The same happens when we switch to the third arpeggio (Gmaj7).

**Significance (p. 61).** This defines the voice-leading principle of connecting arpeggios by proximity rather than restarting each from the root, the key to musical-sounding sequences.

## Creative arpeggio improvisation rule

> Arpeggios can be started on any note and played in any order. You can
> mix notes, skip notes and use any rhythm you can think of. Be creative!

**Significance (p. 62).** This rule liberates the student from mechanical root-to-root patterns and is the conceptual bridge between exercise and improvisation.

## Approach notes technique

> The first arpeggio concept is called approach notes, where you approach
> any note in an arpeggio by one fret below. When doing so, you create a
> tension and release sound in your lines.
>
> The only rule is that you can’t resolve to the chromatic notes. So, if you play an
> approach note, you then have to play a chord tone afterwards.

**Significance (p. 74).** This defines the approach-note technique with both its execution instruction and its governing rule, making it the complete practical unit for applying chromatic color to arpeggios.

## Enclosures technique

> The next bebop technique uses two chromatic notes for each arpeggio note, as
> you encircle chord tones in your lines.
>
> When playing enclosures, you play one fret above, then one fret below,
> then the chord tone.

**Significance (p. 76).** This defines the enclosure technique—the most common bebop chromatic decoration—with a precise three-step execution sequence.
