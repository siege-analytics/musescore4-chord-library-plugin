## 3 to 9 arpeggio (dim7 from 3rd)

> eee ee
>
> eo
>
> This is called a 3 to 9 arpeggio, an essential learning for any bebop guitarist.

**Significance (p. 104).** Defines the "3 to 9 arpeggio" concept with its precise intervallic content, naming it as an essential bebop technique applicable to any 7th chord.

## Tritone substitution mechanics

> The basic application of a tritone sub is to take any 7th chord you see and
> play another 7th chord that occurs a tritone (#4 aka b5) away from that
> initial chord.
>
> For example: playing F#7 over C7. The reason that this sub works is that 7th
> chords with a bass note a tritone apart share the same 3rd and 7th:
>
> on | ee
>
> As you can see:
>
> ¢ The 3rd of C7 (E) is the same as the b7 of F#7 (E).
> ¢ The b7 of C7 (Bb) is the same as the 3rd of F#7 (A#=Bb).
>
> This is glue that holds the two tritone sub chords together.

**Significance (p. 105).** Provides a complete, interval-level explanation of why the tritone substitution works, making it a self-contained theory definition with a concrete example.

## Enclosure technique

> Here you see an enclosure over the 3rd of the underlying A7 chord, where D and
> C and being used to enclose the note C. This type of tension and release line (aka
> enclosure) is something that can be found in many of Charlie's and other solos
> from the Swing era.
>
> Enclosures have been used by just about every great jazz soloist over the
> past 80 or so years, and so it is an important concept to have under your fingers
> and in your ears as you advance your jazz guitar skills.

**Significance (p. 118).** Names and defines the enclosure technique, explains its structural logic (surrounding a target note), and establishes it as a universal jazz vocabulary item.

## Lower neighbor tone after leap

> By landing on a chromatic note after a leap, before resolving it to a chord tone on
> the next note, you can bring a strong focus to both the leap and the resolution
> point in your lines, something that Charlie and other swing musicians liked to do
> in their solos.

**Significance (p. 119).** Articulates the lower-neighbor-tone rule as a general melodic technique: use a chromatic passing note after a large interval to intensify both the leap and its resolution.

## b3 to 3 to 5 riff (blue note mixing)

> This Charlie Christian lick uses an idiomatic riff that runs from the b3 to the 3
> to the 5th of the underlying chord, in this case G7 and Cmaj7. Mixing the blue
> note (b3) with the diatonic 3rd and 5th of any chord is something that Charlie and
> many other Swing artists like to do in their soloing lines and phrases.

**Significance (p. 120).** Describes a reusable intervallic pattern (b3–3–5) as a generalizable idiomatic move applicable across chords, not just the one lick example.

> shape, alternating back and forth until you get to the chromatic approach notes

> notes, to bring an added Joe sound to the mix with this line.

**Significance (p. 128).** Describes Joe Pass's idiomatic chord-melody technique — splitting bass from treble and accenting only the chord portion — as a reproducible rhythmic device for solo guitar.
