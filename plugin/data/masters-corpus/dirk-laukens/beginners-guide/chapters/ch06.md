## Blues characteristics

> The tonic chord of a blues is a dominant 7 chord, a fact that doesn't fit very well
> in traditional music theory.
>
> ¢ The blues is not only about chord changes and scales, but is also about
> a certain sound, a feeling. Responsible for that sound are the blue
> notes: a lowered 3rd note and a lowered 5th note.
>
> * The 3 basic chords of a blues are all dominant 7 chords.

**Significance (p. 158).** Defines the core harmonic and tonal properties that distinguish blues from standard tonal music.

## Lead sheet chord embellishment rule

> those chords can be embellished with 9ths,
> 13ths, 6ths, and other color tones. So, if you hear or see a G13 chord and it's
> written as G7, that's a common approach to comping over lead sheet jazz-blues
> chord changes

**Significance (p. 159).** Practical comping rule teaching players how to read jazz lead sheets and expand basic chord symbols with color tones.

## I-IV-V blues form

> Notice
> how these changes use only three chords, the |7, V7 and V7 chords. Because of
> this, they are often referred to as I-IV-V blues chord changes.

**Significance (p. 160).** Defines the foundational terminology for the basic 12-bar blues form against which all later progressions are measured.

## Quick change blues progression

> Because there is a quick move to the IV7 chord and back to the tonic in the

## Count Basie blues innovations

## Kenny Burrell chord/single-note mixing

> one of the key elements to explore is mixing chords and single-
> lines during each phrase of an improvised chorus, which you can hear during the
> melody section of Chitlins especially.

**Significance (p. 185).** Defines the characteristic Burrell technique — interspersing three-note chord shapes with blues-scale single lines.

## Solo practice methods

> 1. Play the solo with a backing track over the first chorus. During the second
> chorus you improvise the single lines but keep the chords as is. Continue this
> alteration throughout the backing track.
>
> 2. Write out your own Kenny Burrell inspired solo over an A minor blues
> progression, using the blues scale for the single notes, and small, three-note
> chords for the comping sections of the solo.
>
> 3. Practice singing an improvised single-line where you see the single-notes in
> the solo above, then comp the chords on the guitar where they land. This is a
> great way to connect your ears and voice to your fretboard while working ona
> minor blues at the same time.

**Significance (p. 186).** Three structured practice protocols for internalizing chord-melody soloing, progressing from imitation to composition to ear-training.
