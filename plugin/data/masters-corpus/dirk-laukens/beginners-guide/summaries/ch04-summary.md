# Chapter 4 — Jazz Guitar Scales

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 teaches the deployment of four scales—major blues, natural minor (Aeolian), harmonic minor, and melodic minor—each assigned to specific chords within the minor ii–V–I progression and other jazz contexts, with the governing principle that scale choice follows harmonic change (Joe Pass's "When the chords change, you change"). The master's characteristic move is to justify each scale's use through a single differentiating feature: the major blues scale's b3 as the "blue note," harmonic minor's raised 7th that generates V7(b9,b13) tensions over the altered dominant, and melodic minor's raised 7th as an expressive tension device over m7 chords. The load-bearing terminology introduced includes the major blues scale's avoidance-of-clash rule (its lack of a 7th allows use over both dominant and major seventh chords), the Aeolian mode as the identity of natural minor, the V7alt (altered dominant) as the harmonic context requiring tonic harmonic minor rather than the chord's root, and the deliberate-choice model for expressive scale deployment (treating raised intervals as context-dependent decisions rather than automatic inclusions).
