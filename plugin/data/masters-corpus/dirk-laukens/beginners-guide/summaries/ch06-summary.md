# Chapter 6 — Jazz Blues

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch06.md`._

This chapter teaches the harmonic and melodic foundations of jazz blues by defining the characteristic chord qualities (dominant 7 tonic and basic I7-IV7-V7 changes), historically significant substitutions (Count Basie's #IVdim7 and VI7b9, bebop ii-V chains, tritone substitutions), and distinctive voice-leading techniques (contrary motion, sus chords for rhythmic tension). The load-bearing terminology includes quick change blues progression, Bird blues (defined by its Imaj7 tonic), tritone substitution, and the scale-mixing strategies that produce the jazz-blues sound: b3 and 3 mixing (minor blues + Mixolydian), Lydian Dominant over tritone substitutes, and harmonic minor over dominant 7b9 chords. The chapter highlights two master approaches—Kenny Burrell's chord/single-note mixing technique and Joe Pass's minor/major pentatonic mixing—while emphasizing that blues is defined not only by harmonic structure but by blue notes and sound/feeling, anchored in three structured solo practice methods that progress from imitation through composition to ear training.
