## root circle symbol

> A red circle represents the root (aka 1) of the chord. Most chords in this dictionary
> have a C root.

**Significance (p. 6).** This defines the visual encoding of the tonic/root in every diagram in the book, establishing the reference pitch all chord tones are measured against.

## chord tone numbers

> The black circles are the other notes of the chord. The numbers inside the black
> circles indicate the chord tone.

**Significance (p. 6).** This explains that fingering diagrams display harmonic function (scale-degree intervals) rather than finger numbers, which is essential for reading every chord entry correctly.

## fretting finger numbers

> The numbers (1, 2, 3, and 4) at the right side of each chord diagram indicate which
> fingers to use to fret the chord.

**Significance (p. 6).** This locates the left-hand fingering indicators and distinguishes them from the chord-tone numbers inside the circles, preventing the most common diagram-reading confusion.

## X symbol / muted strings

> The X symbol at the left side of the chord diagram indicates that those strings are not
> to be played. This means that most of the chords on this page cannot be strummed,
> but should be played fingerstyle, with your thumb and first 3 fingers of the right hand.

**Significance (p. 6).** This is a technique rule that governs right-hand approach for the majority of chords in the book — fingerstyle with thumb + 3 fingers rather than strumming.

## O symbol / open strings

> The O symbol at the left side of the chord diagram indicates that those strings are
> played open.

**Significance (p. 6).** This defines the open-string symbol, completing the set of string-status indicators needed to interpret any diagram in the dictionary.

## movable chord shapes

> Most of the chords on this page are of the movable type (not the open chords though).
> By moving the chord shapes up or down the guitar neck, you get other chords of the
> same chord type.

**Significance (p. 7).** This establishes the book's core transposition principle: every non-open shape is a template that can be slid to any root, multiplying the dictionary's reach across all twelve keys.

## movable shape transposition example

> For example: move the chord shape of a Cmaj7 voicing 2 frets higher and it becomes a
> Dmaj7 voicing.

**Significance (p. 7).** This concrete example demonstrates the two-fret = whole-step relationship that underpins all transposition of movable shapes in the book.
