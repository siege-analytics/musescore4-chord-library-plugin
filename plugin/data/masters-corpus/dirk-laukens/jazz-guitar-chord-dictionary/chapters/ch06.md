## Inversion definition: non-root bass

> use other notes in the bass than the root note.

**Significance (p. 45).** This is the core definition of a chord inversion as used throughout the chapter — placing a chord tone other than the root in the bass voice.

## Inversion order: root, 3rd, 5th, 7th

> Notice that for each group of chords, the root is in the bass for the first chord shape,
> then the 3rd, 5th, and the 7th after that.

**Significance (p. 45).** This establishes the systematic ordering of the four inversions presented in every chord-type group, giving the student the navigational logic for all the diagrams that follow.

## Applying inversions in comping

> Each of these chord inversions can be applied to that chord in your comping. For
> example, if you see a Cmaj7 chord symbol you can play any inversion of Cmaj7.

**Significance (p. 45).** This is the practical performance rule: any inversion is a legitimate substitute for a chord symbol, which is the foundational comping technique the chapter teaches.

## Voicing choice and sound quality

> Just know that each inversion will have a slightly different sound and some voicings will
> work better than others.

**Significance (p. 45).** This gives the student the evaluative rule — inversions are not interchangeable in all contexts, and ear-based selection is required.

## Bass note affects chord flavor

> Though they use the same notes, putting the root or the 3rd in the bass causes the
> chord to have a slightly different flavor.

**Significance (p. 45).** This explains the acoustic principle behind why inversions matter — same pitch content, different hierarchical emphasis produces a perceptible timbral/harmonic difference.
