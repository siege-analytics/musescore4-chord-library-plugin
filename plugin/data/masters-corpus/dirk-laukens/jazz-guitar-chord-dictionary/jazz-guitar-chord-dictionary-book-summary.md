# Book-level distillation — jazz-guitar-chord-dictionary

_Aggregated from per-chapter summaries under `summaries/`._

## Overview and Pedagogical Arc

This book is a systematic chord dictionary for jazz guitar, organized as a progressive hierarchy of voicing types. It begins with the mechanics of reading chord diagrams (Chapter 1), advances through foundational four-note drop 2 and drop 3 voicings (Chapter 2), and then expands outward through increasing harmonic complexity: shell chords (Chapter 3), extended chords (Chapter 4), altered dominant chords (Chapter 5), inversions (Chapter 6), open-string voicings (Chapter 7), and quartal harmony (Chapter 8). The structure is neither purely theoretical nor purely practical — each chapter pairs a voicing category with the contextual rules governing its deployment, consistently linking what to play with where it comes from (scale origin) and where it goes (harmonic function).

## The Movable Shape System

The book's master architecture rests on the movable chord shape: every non-open voicing is a transposable template that slides up or down the neck to any root, with a two-fret shift equaling a whole-step transposition. This principle, established in Chapter 1, multiplies the dictionary's utility across all twelve keys without requiring separate fingerings for each root. Chapter 2 extends this into a structured learning sequence — master one shape per chord type in isolation, then immediately apply it to a real jazz tune — a characteristic balance between systematic vocabulary building and ear-driven musicality.

## Voicing Economy and Ensemble Sensitivity

A recurring master move throughout the book is voicing economy: deliberate reduction of note count while preserving harmonic identity. Chapter 3's shell chords (guide tone chords) exemplify this most directly — three-note voicings built from root, 3rd, and 7th capture a chord's essential harmonic character while keeping the shape on the lower string sets. The chapter couples this economy with an explicit ensemble principle: shell chords occupy the bass register and therefore require soft tone control to avoid frequency clashing with the bass player. This reveals a characteristic rule — the choice of voicing is inseparable from the player's role within the rhythm section.

## Extended and Altered Harmony: Rules and Distinctions

Chapters 4 and 5 establish the book's most load-bearing terminological distinctions. Chapter 4 introduces a structural rule governing extended chords: major 6 and major 6(9) chords omit the seventh (the 6 replaces the 7), whereas all other extended chords such as maj9 retain the 7th. The presence or absence of the 7th fundamentally determines harmonic function and identity. A further distinction separates major 6 chords (root, 3rd, 5th, 6) from major 13 chords (which contain both the 7 and the 6/13). Because guitarists cannot voice all available intervals simultaneously, deliberate omission choices are required — an explicit constraint the book frames as a practical skill rather than a limitation.

Chapter 5 extends this logic into dominant alteration. The #11 and b5 are identified as enharmonically identical but contextually named. The C9#11 derives from the Lydian dominant scale and functions as bVII7 resolving to Imaj7; the C7b5 derives from the altered scale and functions as V7alt resolving to Im7 or Imaj7. The characteristic move here is the systematic triple pairing: voicing (what notes) + scale origin (where it comes from) + harmonic function (where it resolves), giving theoretical justification and practical deployment guidance together.

## Inversions, Open Chords, and Quartal Harmony

Chapter 6 establishes a comping freedom principle: any inversion of a chord symbol — root position, 3rd in bass, 5th in bass, 7th in bass — can legitimately substitute for that symbol in performance. Inversion selection is presented as an ear-based timbral decision rather than a fixed rule, with bass voice position creating different harmonic flavors from identical pitch content. Chapter 7 introduces open-string voicings as a distinct sonority available in guitar-friendly key centers, and establishes enharmonic notation as a necessary reading skill — for example, Bm11(b6) = Gmaj7(add 6)/B — enabling students to parse complex open voicings from multiple harmonic perspectives. Chapter 8 completes the harmonic vocabulary with quartal chords (4th chords), built by stacking 4th intervals above the lowest note rather than the thirds of tertian harmony. The book characterizes these as universally applicable over jazz standards — not limited to specific harmonic situations — and presents them in two fretboard positions: string sets 2-3-4-5 and 1-2-3-4 derived from the C Dorian scale.

## Methodology

The book's method is dictionary-plus-context: each voicing category is taught as a movable template, then immediately situated within harmonic function, scale origin, ensemble role, and performance guidelines. The pedagogical prescription is consistent — learn the shape, apply it to a real tune, train the ear alongside the hands. Ear-based selection is repeatedly invoked as the ultimate arbiter of which voicing, inversion, or color works in context, positioning the entire chord vocabulary as raw material for musical judgment rather than a set of fixed prescriptions.

Chapter 9 (More Jazz Guitar Chord Lessons, pp. 61-63) contains no load-bearing prose passages — it is composed entirely of notated chord-lesson references without extractable instructional text.
