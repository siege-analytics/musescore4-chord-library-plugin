# Chapter 5 — Dominant Chords with Altered Extensions

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches dominant chord alteration using extended and altered intervals—specifically the #11 and b5 extensions—to create harmonic tension over dominant 7th chords, with particular emphasis on their use in minor key progressions like the minor ii-V-I. The chapter clarifies the enharmonic distinction between #11 and b5 (both the same pitch but named contextually), then grounds each voicing in its parent scale: the C9#11 derives from the Lydian dominant scale and functions as a bVII7 resolving to Imaj7, while the C7b5 derives from the altered scale and functions as a V7alt resolving to Im7 or Imaj7. The load-bearing terminology introduced includes altered extensions, Lydian dominant, altered scale, bVII7, and V7alt—concepts that expand a player's harmonic vocabulary beyond basic dominant function. The chapter's characteristic move is the systematic pairing of voicing (what notes to use) with scale origin (where it comes from) and harmonic function (where it goes), giving readers both theoretical justification and practical deployment guidance.
