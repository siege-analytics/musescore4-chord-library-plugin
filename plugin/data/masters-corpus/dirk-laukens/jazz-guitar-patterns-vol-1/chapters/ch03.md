## Diatonic enclosure definition

> A diatonic enclosure is when you play one note above (the 9), then one note below (the 7), and
> finally your target note (the 1).

**Significance (p. 13).** This defines the core melodic device—diatonic enclosure—that Pattern 1 and much of the method's bebop vocabulary is built around.

## Pattern practice method

## Augmented arpeggio altered sound

> An augmented 7th arpeggio or triad is a convenient way to bring out an altered sound on dominant

## Half-diminished sub over dominant

> Playing a half-diminished arpeggio from the 3rd of a dominant chord brings out the 9 sound.
> For example: Bm7b5 over G7.

**Significance (p. 13).** This defines the Bm7b5-over-G7 substitution rule with its harmonic rationale, a reusable device that recurs in Patterns 5, 19, and 55.

## Harmonic minor over V7alt

> On a minor ii V I, you can play the tonic harmonic minor scale over the V7alt chord.
> For example: over Bm7b5-E7alt-Am7 (ii VI in A minor), you play A harmonic minor over the
>
> E7alt chord.
>
> When doing so, you create a 7b9,b13 sound over that chord.
>
> Another name for the harmonic minor scale played over a dominant chord is the Phrygian dominant
> scale.
>
> | A harmonic minor = E Phrygian dominant

**Significance (p. 13).** This passage defines the harmonic minor / Phrygian dominant equivalence and the specific alteration tones it produces, giving players a concrete scale-choice rule for minor ii-V-I contexts.

## Doubling notes technique

## Fmaj7 sub over half-diminished

> You can play a maj7 chord over a half-diminished chord, starting on the b5 of that half-
> diminished chord. For example: Fmaj7 over Bm7b5

**Significance (p. 13).** This states the generalized substitution rule for coloring half-diminished chords with a major-7 arpeggio, producing the beautiful #11 tension.
