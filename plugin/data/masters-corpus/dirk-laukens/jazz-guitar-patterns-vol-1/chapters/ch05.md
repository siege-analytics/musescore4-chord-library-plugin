## Harmonic minor over dominant (Phrygian dominant)

> You can play the tonic harmonic minor scale over any dominant chord.
> For example: C harmonic minor over G7
>
> When doing so, you create a 7b9, b13 sound over that chord.
>
> Another name for the harmonic minor scale played over a dominant chord is the Phrygian dominant
> scale.

**Significance (p. 150).** This is a foundational substitution rule defining how the harmonic minor scale generates a 7b9/b13 sound over dominants and introduces the Phrygian dominant concept.

## Diminished arpeggio over dominant (b9 sound)

**Significance (p. 150).** This defines the rule for applying diminished arpeggios over dominant chords and specifies exactly which starting chord tones to use.

## Symmetry of diminished chords

> The convenient thing about diminished chords and arpeggios is that their inversions are identical in
> shape and notes.
>
> This is because the interval structure of diminished chords is symmetrical.
>
> If you have a look at the intervals of a Cdim7 below, you will notice that the interval between each
> chord note is a minor third.

**Significance (p. 150).** This explains the theoretical reason (symmetrical minor-third stacking) that makes diminished inversions physically identical on the fretboard — a core practical insight for the guitarist.
