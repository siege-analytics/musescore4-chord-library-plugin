## Altered scale definition

> The altered scale is the 7th mode of the melodic minor scale.
>
> For example: G altered scale = Ab melodic minor scale starting from the note G.
>
> The altered scale is used to solo over dominant 7th chords, both in major and minor keys.
>
> The altered scale contains all four of the common altered notes (b9-#9-b5-b13), which are used to
> create tension over the underlying chord.

## Bebop scale definition

> The bebop scale is an 8-note scale.
>
> It is constructed by adding a chromatic passing note to 7-note scales such as the Mixolydian,
> Dorian, and Ionan modes.
>
> There are three kinds of bebop scales
>
> 1. The dominant bebop scale — Mixolydian + 7
> 2. The major bebop scale — Major scale + b6
> 3. The minor bebop scale — Dorian + 7

**Significance (p. 200).** This is the book's definitive taxonomy of the bebop scale family, specifying the construction rule and naming all three variants with their added chromatic tone.

## Bebop scale advantages

> The bebop scale gives you a framework for adding chromatic notes to your jazz solos.
>
> e The bebop scale adds a sense of tension and release to your soloing ideas.
>
> e When you start the bebop scale on a downbeat and on a chord tone, all the following chord tones
> of the scale will fall on downbeats and the other notes of the scale on upbeats.

**Significance (p. 200).** This passage states the three core practical benefits of the bebop scale, with the third being the metric alignment rule that is the scale's defining pedagogical payoff.
