# Chapter 3 — Study 3 - There Will Never Be Another You

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 teaches a comprehensive vocabulary of bebop patterns built on the foundational technique of diatonic enclosure—playing one note above (the 9), then below (the 7), before resolving to the target note—and prescribes the universal practice method of starting phrases with patterns over backing tracks while drilling positions through the circle of fifths. The chapter introduces defining bebop alterations including the natural 7 in minor contexts, hammer-on/pull-off triplet figures between altered tensions (b9 to #9), and the b6 interval from chord tones, while establishing reusable harmonic devices such as augmented arpeggios for altered dominants, half-diminished arpeggios (especially Bm7b5) over dominant chords, and the harmonic minor/Phrygian dominant equivalence for minor ii-V-I progressions. Characteristic moves borrowed from master improvisers—Pat Metheny's doubling-notes technique and Wes Montgomery's triadic substitutions (like Dm over G7)—demonstrate how fundamental arpeggio and scale devices can be colored through strategic chord substitutions like Fmaj7 over half-diminished chords and minor triads over dominants. Load-bearing terminology includes diatonic enclosure, altered sound, Phrygian dominant, doubling notes, and harmonic rationale, all framed as reusable, universally applicable techniques across scales, arpeggios, and patterns throughout the method.
