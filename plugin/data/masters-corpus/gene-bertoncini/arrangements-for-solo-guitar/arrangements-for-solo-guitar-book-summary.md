## Gene Bertoncini — *Arrangements for Solo Guitar* (Ambient Records, 2011)
*Compiled by Jason Ennis, Chris Ullrich, and Gene Bertoncini*

---

### What the Book Is

*Arrangements for Solo Guitar* is a notated performance folio, not a method book. It contains twenty-four solo-guitar arrangements drawn from two Bertoncini recordings — *Body and Soul* (chapters 1–13) and *Quiet Now* (chapters 14–24) — followed by a Glossary of Open-String Voicings (chapter 25). Every chapter from 1 through 24 is notation only: melody, inner voices, bass lines, fingerings, and the occasional parenthetical voicing note, with no explanatory prose. The book's entire written argument is concentrated in two short front-matter texts — the Preface and Jason Ennis's Notes about the Transcriptions and Notation — and it is those two passages that define how the music is meant to be read.

### Bertoncini's Conception: The Guitar as Little Orchestra

The Preface takes its keynote from Berlioz, who called the guitar a "little orchestra," and every arrangement in the book was conceived in that spirit. Bertoncini's premise is that any piece of composed music — a folksong, an operatic aria, a big-band shout chorus, an orchestral excerpt — can find its way to the guitar if the arranger thinks orchestrally rather than idiomatically. The journeys through these twenty-four pieces typically begin with melody carried in the soprano voice, though Bertoncini also places melody in the bass or nestles it inside interior voices of a chord. Surrounding that melody are the tools of a miniature ensemble: counterpoint between voices, cluster voicings that compress harmonic tension into a small physical space, and a highly particular attention to the timbral identity of each individual string. The guitar, in this conception, is not a chord-strumming or single-line instrument but a polyphonic one, capable of sustaining the kind of voice-leading and color differentiation normally associated with a chamber group.

### The Editorial Process: Between Transcription and Arrangement

Jason Ennis's Notes locate the book carefully between two categories. It is not a pure transcription, because the recordings themselves did not always capture every voicing or counterpoint note Bertoncini intended; and it is not a fresh arrangement, because the primary sources were Bertoncini's own handwritten manuscripts. The editors worked outward from those manuscripts, filling gaps by transcribing directly from the *Body and Soul* and *Quiet Now* recordings. Where a recorded performance omitted a voicing note or a counterpoint line that belonged to the conception, that note was restored and placed in parentheses in the printed score — a typographic signal distinguishing editorially supplied material from what Bertoncini wrote or played on the recording. The result is a score that aims to be more complete than either the manuscript or the recording alone.

### Fingerings and Open Strings

Particular care was taken to preserve Bertoncini's own fingerings, which appear to the lower right of the noteheads throughout the book. These are not generic guitar-method fingerings but the specific physical choices of one performer working out one interpretation, and their placement makes them navigational rather than pedagogical — they show where Bertoncini's hand went, not where a student's hand should go in the abstract. The most distinctive technical feature the editors document is Bertoncini's creative, unorthodox use of open strings within chord voicings: rather than stopping every note to control intonation and sustain uniformly, he incorporates ringing open strings as harmonic color, accepting their particular resonance as part of the voicing's identity. This technique recurs across both recording sessions and across both halves of the folio.

### Chapter 25: The Glossary of Open-String Voicings

Because the open-string approach is so central to Bertoncini's sound, the editors extracted a cross-referenced collection of these voicings and compiled them as chapter 25. The Glossary is organized by chord label and keyed to specific measures across the twenty-four arrangements — for example, the Em9 at measure 9 of *The Shadow of Your Smile*, the Db maj7(♯11) at measure 7 of *Body and Soul*, the A♭m9 at measure 3 of *'Round Midnight*. Read alongside the individual arrangements, the Glossary functions as a harmonic index to one of the book's defining characteristics: it makes visible, in condensed form, the harmonic vocabulary that is otherwise distributed across hundreds of pages of notation.

### What the Book Offers

*Arrangements for Solo Guitar* offers something specific and bounded: access to Gene Bertoncini's solo-guitar realizations of twenty-four jazz standards and ballads, presented as accurately as careful editorial work from manuscript and recording could achieve. It does not explain how to develop a solo-guitar style, how to build arrangements from scratch, or how to think about the guitar harmonically in general terms. Its "pedagogy," such as it is, is entirely implicit — located in the notation itself, in the fingerings, in the parenthetical restorations, and in the Glossary that isolates one recurring device for closer inspection. A guitarist who works through this book learns from Bertoncini's specific choices, not from his instructions.
