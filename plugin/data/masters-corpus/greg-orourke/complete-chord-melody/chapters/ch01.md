## Four-step chord melody method

> Pick a suitable tune
>    Change the key if necessary for better range and playability
>    Learn the melody on top 2 (or 3) strings
>    Choose chord voicings to add below melody notes

**Significance (p. 17).** This is O'Rourke's foundational four-step recipe that the entire course operationalizes.

## Tune selection rule

> For your first arrangement, I recommend you stick to ballads, rather than the faster bebop tunes. The
> slower tempo and longer-held melody notes in ballads will be much easier for your first project.

**Significance (p. 18).** Establishes the genre/tempo constraint that makes step 1 practical for beginners.

## Range rule of thumb

> Search for a key where you can play the melody on the top 3 strings, roughly anywhere from the
>  3rd to the 9th fret.

**Significance (p. 19).** Concrete, fretboard-region rule that drives the key-change decision in step 2.

## Range failure modes

> If the arrangement is too high, it will sound like it doesn’t have enough depth.
>    On the other hand, if your arrangement is too low in the register, it will sound too dark and
>    muddy.

**Significance (p. 19).** Names both failure modes that the range rule of thumb is designed to avoid.

## Horizontal melody fingering

> This will mean you will need to play more horizontally over the fretboard, rather than staying in a
> single position.

**Significance (p. 20).** Defines the fretting-hand approach that makes top-2-string melody fingering possible.

## Don't harmonize every note

> You don’t need a chord on every melody note.

**Significance (p. 23).** Headline pedagogical rule guarding against the most common chord-melody overplaying mistake.

## Place chords on long notes

> Notice on the above example, I mainly place chords on the long-held melody notes. It sounds great
> and is easy on the hands.

**Significance (p. 23).** Operationalizes the 'don't chord every note' rule by naming where chords should land.

## Sustain workaround

> The long melody notes ring out quickly
> on guitar, so rather than trying to hold them for their full length, I place a chord voicing to emphasize
> the change of chord instead.

**Significance (p. 23).** Identifies a guitar-specific physical constraint and the chord-substitution workaround.

## Plectrum bass-then-strum

> Quick tip: When using a plectrum, pluck the bass note first, followed by a quick strum of the rest
>  of the chord (you can see me doing this in the example below) – this can be a way to negotiate
>  chord voicings with tricky string skips.

**Significance (p. 28).** Concrete right-hand technique for plectrum players to handle non-adjacent string sets.

## Right-hand technique choice

> Because chord melody playing involves a lot of – you guessed it – chords, you need a technique that
> enables you to pluck chord shapes easily.

**Significance (p. 24).** Frames why right-hand technique selection is a load-bearing decision for the whole approach.
