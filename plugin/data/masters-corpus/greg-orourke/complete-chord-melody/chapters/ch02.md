## Three Harmonic Families

> A chord must belong to one of the following classifications:
>
>    Major – e.g. CMaj7, C6, CMaj9
>    Minor – e.g. Cm7, Cm9, Cm7b5
>    Dominant – e.g. C7, C9, C13, C7(b9)

**Significance (p. 33).** Foundational taxonomy O'Rourke uses to organize the whole chord dictionary.

## Minor and dominant sub-families

> Minor:
>
>    ‘m7’ sub-family – e.g. Cm7, Cm9, Cm13
>    ‘m7b5’ sub-family – e.g. Cm7b5, Cm11b5
>
> Dominant:
>
>    ‘7’ or ‘natural dominant’ sub-family – e.g. C7, C9, C13
>    ‘7alt’ or ‘altered dominant’ sub-family – e.g. C7(b9). C7(b13b9), C7(b5), C7alt

**Significance (p. 34).** Refines the 3-family taxonomy with the sub-classes that drive major-vs-minor ii-V-i voicing choice.

## Chord collection definition

> A chord collection is a group of chords that can be played over a particular chord quality or
> progression.
>
> They are useful because the sequence of chords in the collection includes every diatonic interval as
> the top note of the chords.
>
> Which means: no matter which note is in the melody line, you can harmonize it by referring to the
> chord collection.

**Significance (p. 35).** Defines O'Rourke's central organizing unit — every-interval-on-top is what makes the dictionary indexable by melody note.

## Why ii V I for collections

> The ii-V-I progression contains a chord from each harmonic family:
>
>    ii – minor
>    V – dominant
>    I – major

**Significance (p. 48).** Justifies organizing the chord vocabulary around ii-V-I as a single-progression cover of all three families.

## 2nd-string collection rationale

> As covered in module 1, when arranging chord melodies it’s helpful to have the melody mainly on the
> top 1st and 2nd strings.
>
> So far, the chord voicings we have learned in this lesson have the highest note on the 1st string.
>
> Now let’s add additional chord collections that have their highest note on the 2nd string.

**Significance (p. 57).** Names why the dictionary is duplicated into 1st-string and 2nd-string variants — to match the top-2-string melody constraint.

## Chord symbol definition

> A chord symbol is an attempt to describe the type of sound produced by a group of notes.

**Significance (p. 74).** O'Rourke's definitional framing for the symbol-as-formula approach.

## Stacking thirds

> Chords are constructed most often by stacking thirds.

**Significance (p. 75).** Core construction principle behind every voicing in the dictionary.

## Second-octave structures

> This is where chords like GMaj13 come from – from continuing to stack more and more thirds
> onto the structure, which gives us more and more colorful extensions

**Significance (p. 78).** Explains why extension numbers go past 7 and where 9/11/13 chord names originate.

## Theoretical vs playable

> On the guitar, there is a difference between the theoretically correct chord and the playable chord.
>
> Often notes can be omitted from the chord shape to get a similar enough sound, but with a much
> easier shape.

**Significance (p. 79).** Names the gap between theory and playable guitar voicings that the omittable-notes rule fills.

## Omittable notes list

> Intervals most commonly omitted include the 5th, the root (1st), the 11th, and the 9th. Note though
> that even the 3rd and the 7th can be omitted for more complex chords in certain situations as well.

**Significance (p. 79).** Specific rule for which notes can drop out of a voicing while preserving essential chord character.

## Extensions vs altered tensions

> An extension (sometimes called a ‘natural’ or ‘diatonic’ tension) is an interval of the chord which
> naturally occurs in the parent scale of the key of the progression. Extensions are included as part of
> the chord name. Examples are G9, G13, and GMaj9.
>
> Altered tensions are notes that are outside the parent scale of the key of the progression, and are
> (usually) added in parentheses after the chord name. Examples are GMaj7(#11), G7(b13#9).

**Significance (p. 80).** Defines the diatonic/altered distinction that drives chord-symbol notation throughout the book.

## Context defines chord name

> A chord’s sound is as much defined by the chords that are around it in the progression, just as much
> as the notes of the chord themselves.

**Significance (p. 84).** Conceptual rule that justifies why the same notes get relabeled and why context drives voicing choice.
