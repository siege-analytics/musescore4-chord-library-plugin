## Top-2-string melody constraint

> In order to fit our chord voicings underneath the melody notes, we need to change the fingering of
> the melody to sit on the top 2 (or 3) strings.
>
> Note: it’s ok to occasionally dip down the 3rd or even the 4th string if necessary – don’t get too rigid
> about only sticking to 2 strings!

**Significance (p. 98).** Reaffirms the top-2-string canvas rule with the explicit carve-out that prevents over-rigid fingering.

## Write down the arrangement

> Whatever method you choose, it’s important to write down your arrangement somehow. Writing gives
> you the ability to keep track of your ideas and thus refine them more easily (which can be difficult to
> do if you are arranging entirely by ear).

**Significance (p. 94).** Process rule: the arrangement must be notated (paper or software) for iterative refinement to work.

## Basic comping before voicing

> Something good to do before getting into the nitty-gritty of what voicings to use is to simply learn
> the basic chords of the song as if comping in a band.
>
> This is another way to train your ears to the chord progression and is a gentle way to dip your toes
> into applying chords to your chosen tune.

**Significance (p. 100).** Pre-step in the method: comp the song first so the ear knows the harmony before voicing selection.

## Harmonic analysis goals

> We are aiming to get 3 outcomes from this:
>
> 1. To work out the quality of each chord in the progression (major, minor, dominant).
> 2. To observe any key changes in the tune.
> 3. To see if we can spot common chord sequences in the progression, such as ii – V – Is etc.

**Significance (p. 105).** Defines the three specific outputs the analysis pass must produce before voicing assignment.

## Roman numeral conventions

> Harmonic analysis uses the following conventions:
>
>    Roman numbers are designated to indicate a chord’s function.
>    Upper case Roman numerals indicate a chord being major or dominant.
>    Lower case Roman numerals indicate a chord being minor.

**Significance (p. 105).** Notation rules that make the analysis output legible to other musicians and to the reader's own future self.

## Why interval analysis

> If we know what interval a melody note is in relation to a given chord, we will be able to find an
> appropriate voicing from our chord dictionary with that interval as the top note chord.

**Significance (p. 107).** States the bridge between melody analysis and dictionary lookup — the linchpin of the painting-by-numbers method.

## Interval relates to current chord

> The interval number we give a melody note must relate to the current chord in the
>  progression – NOT the overall key of the progression. This is a common mistake beginner
>  arrangers often make.

**Significance (p. 107).** Flags the most common student error and the correct mental model for interval labeling.

## Chord-tone vocabulary

> The interval numbers are the chord tone numbers, and could be one of the following:
>
>  1, b9, 9, b3, 3, 11, #11, 5,b13, 13, b7, 7

**Significance (p. 107).** Closed vocabulary of interval labels the dictionary is indexed on — essential for the lookup step.

## Passing-tone label

> Often in jazz melodies, the chromatic notes in a melody can be considered passing tones if they
>  are not on a strong downbeat and simply lead to a stronger chord tone, so you can use the label
>  PT for those.

**Significance (p. 107).** Defines the PT escape hatch for chromatic melody notes that don't get harmonized.

## Major scale reference rule

> To find the correct interval number, you always refer to the major scale of the current chord.

**Significance (p. 108).** Defines the reference scale used for labeling — not the chord's own scale, but always major-scale-of-the-root.

## Golden rule of interval analysis

> Always relate the interval to the chord at that point in the chord progression.

**Significance (p. 110).** O'Rourke's headline golden rule for the whole interval-analysis pass.

## Melody anticipates next chord

> Sometimes a melody note anticipates the next chord so is considered an interval of that
> chord (see the ends of bar 1, 3, 5, 7).

**Significance (p. 111).** Edge-case rule for how to handle melody notes that arrive before their harmonic context changes.
