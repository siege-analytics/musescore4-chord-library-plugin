## Where to place chords

> This final step is a straightforward process of applying a chord to the first melody note of each bar
> and/or chord change in the lead sheet.

**Significance (p. 113).** Concrete placement rule for the painting-by-numbers method — chord on bar 1 or chord-change beat.

## Painting by numbers rule

> Choose chord voicings that have the same interval of the melody note as the highest note in the
>  chord shape.

**Significance (p. 114).** The single load-bearing voicing-selection rule that the whole method depends on.

## Adapt voicing for melody

> a voicing that can be slightly adapted to have the melody note as the highest note and still sound
> good.

**Significance (p. 114).** Carve-out from the strict rule: voicings can be tweaked rather than always matched exactly.

## Three voicing-lookup inputs

> To know which voicing to pick we need to establish:
>
> 1. The string the melody note is on
> 2. The type of chord that is in the chord progression whilst that melody note is played
> 3. The interval of the melody note in relation to that chord.

**Significance (p. 114).** Defines the three index keys for the chord-dictionary lookup.

## Less is more

> In fact, less is more – placing chords at key places ‘here and there’ is easier on the hands and is
> usually more than enough to imply the harmony beneath the melody line. So don’t get carried away!

**Significance (p. 122).** Pedagogical rule against overharmonization, with the implied-harmony rationale.

## Playability over ambition

> “If it’s easy on the hands, the music will flow better – so most of the time making things as easy
>  as possible is musically the best option anyway”.

**Significance (p. 124).** Cited rule that justifies cutting voicings to improve playability — the key principle behind the refine step.
