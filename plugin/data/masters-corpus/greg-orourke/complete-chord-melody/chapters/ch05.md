## Illusion of solo guitar

> A good solo guitar arranger simply suggests these elements throughout the performance. Play a
> measure of walking bass, followed by a chord line, followed by unaccompanied single lines –
> alternating through these different devices gives the ‘illusion’ to the audience of a complete band.
>
> The listener’s mind will fill in the gaps.

**Significance (p. 138).** Defines the core aesthetic strategy for solo arranging — alternation, not simultaneity, sells the band illusion.

## Bass on 5th/6th strings

> The key difference between a ‘trio’ chord melody arrangement (to be played in a band) and solo
> guitar is the greater prominence of bass notes on the 5th and 6th strings.
>
> You no longer have a bass player to play with, so you need to fill that role ourselves – otherwise, the
> arrangement will sound thin and empty.

**Significance (p. 140).** Names the single load-bearing difference between trio and solo arranging in O'Rourke's framework.

## Open-string keys

> An excellent way around this is to choose a key for your arrangement that allows the use of open
> strings – especially for the bass notes.
>
> Choosing a key like A, E, or D gives you essentially another set of free fingers. Without needing to fret
> the occasional bass note, you can make things much easier technically.

**Significance (p. 142).** Specific key-selection rule for solo guitar — prefer A/E/D so open-string bass frees the fretting hand.

## Bass-upper proximity issue

> A common issue with many jazz guitar arrangements is that the bass is too close to the upper
> harmony, which results in a thin sound.

**Significance (p. 142).** Diagnostic for why solo arrangements sound thin and the spacing failure mode to avoid.

## Shell voicing definition

> These are called shell voicings – i.e. the ‘bare bones’ of what you need to define the sound of the
> chord: the root, 3rd, and 7th.

**Significance (p. 145).** Definition of the solo-guitar voicing primitive: root + 3rd + 7th, with the 5th removed.

## Drop the 5th

> You can remove the 5th from the chord and still retain the essential character of the sound.

**Significance (p. 145).** Specific rule for how shells are derived from bar chords — the 5th is the first cut.

## Avoid doubled notes

> Generally, try to avoid doubling notes in the chord – I’ve usually cut out notes from the voicing
> when doubling occurs. However, sometimes it still sounds ok so I’ve left doublings in when this is the
> case.

**Significance (p. 153).** Rule for refining solo voicings: cut doublings unless the doubling happens to sound good.

> beat 1, and the approach note of beat 4.

> These can be any of the following:
>

>    Chromatic notes, or
>    Arpeggios.
