## Substitution defined

> A substitution simply means replacing one chord with another.

**Significance (p. 213).** Provides the module's foundational definition that all later substitution techniques build on.

## Tritone substitution defined

> Tritone substitution is the process of substituting one chord for another a tritone away.

**Significance (p. 214).** Defines the most useful harmonic substitution in jazz per O'Rourke.

## Guide tones explain tritone sub

> C7 has E as the 3rd and Bb as the 7th.
>
> F#7 has E as the 7th and A# (which is the same as Bb) as the 3rd.
>
> The guide tones have essentially swapped places between these two chords.

**Significance (p. 216).** Gives the theoretical justification for tritone substitution via shared 3rd/7th guide tones.

## Common-tones enable substitution

> Because the two chords have those 3rd and 7th as common tones, they can be substituted for
>  one another.

**Significance (p. 216).** States the underlying principle (shared guide tones) that makes tritone substitution work.

## Tritone Substitution Rule

> Tritone Substitution Rule - You can replace any chord with another whose root is a tritone
>  away from the root of the original chord.

**Significance (p. 220).** Codifies the canonical tritone substitution rule in O'Rourke's own pedagogical formulation.

## Don't overuse substitutions

> Keeping things simple most of the time and just adding a substitution occasionally to brighten up the
> arrangement is the approach I find works best – a good arrangement is all about subtlety!

**Significance (p. 222).** Provides the taste/restraint rule that governs all of O'Rourke's substitution methods.

## Relative key definition

> G major and E minor are called relative key signatures to each other because they share the
>  same key signature.

**Significance (p. 223).** Defines the relative-key relationship that underpins the I-to-vi substitution rule.

## I to vi substitution rule

> Rule: you can replace the I chord of a key with the vi chord of the key.

**Significance (p. 224).** Codifies the relative-substitution rule for major-to-minor reharmonization.

## I to iii substitution rule

> Rule: you can substitute a major chord for chord iii of its key.

**Significance (p. 225).** Gives the second major-to-minor substitution rule, completing the major-to-minor pair.

## Chord addition defined

> This process is referred to as chord addition – adding extra chords to the harmony.

**Significance (p. 228).** Names the technique of inserting substitute chords as additions to the original harmony.

## Minor conversion concept

> Pat Martino was a great proponent of what’s known as the "minor conversion concept" - a theory
>  that any chord you see in a progression could be substituted for a minor chord instead.

**Significance (p. 229).** Cites the Pat Martino concept that licenses minor-for-anything substitution in this method.

## Minor for Dominant Substitution Rule

> You can use a minor 7 chord from the 5th of any dominant chord (which is like using the iim7 that
>  belongs to the dominant chord).
>
>  For example: an F7 chord’s 5th interval is C. So you could play Cm7 over F7 to get an interesting
>  sound.

**Significance (p. 230).** Codifies the minor-for-dominant substitution as a concrete rule with worked example.

## Back cycling root movement

> When one cycles anti-clockwise (backward) through the cycle of 5ths, we get a sequence of root
> tones that are separated by the interval of a 4th.
>
> This cycle of root notes a 4th apart is the most common root movement in Western music.

**Significance (p. 237).** Defines back cycling and grounds it in the most common root movement in the genre.

## Back cycling substitution rule

> Back cycling substitution rule - you can add chords to the chord progression of a song based
>  on the ‘back cycle’, i.e. the circle of fourths.

**Significance (p. 243).** Codifies the back-cycling reharmonization rule in canonical form.

## Melodic common tone substitution rule

> Melodic common tone substitution rule: substitute any chord for another chord, as long as
>  the new chord has a note in common with it that you use as the melody note on top.

**Significance (p. 247).** Codifies O'Rourke's fifth substitution approach, the one that produces modern Rosenwinkel/Kreisberg-style colors.

## Practice with metronome on 2 and 4

> Have the metronome click on every beat.
>    Then try the metronome clicking on beats 1 and 3 only.
>    Then have a go with the metronome on beats 2 and 4 only.
>
> The final version is most desirable for jazz as it helps give the music more of a swing.

**Significance (p. 252).** Gives the concrete metronome progression and identifies the 2-and-4 click as the jazz-preferred form.

## Practice 1-2 bars per session

> Instead, methodically work through just 1 or 2 bars per session. Spend time slowly practicing the
> fingering and carefully checking the notes are accurate and that everything is moving comfortably
> under your fingers.

**Significance (p. 253).** Specifies the per-session learning quantum O'Rourke prescribes for mastering an arrangement.
