## Fingerstyle plays multiple voices

> This technique allows you to easily play multiple voices at once on one guitar – enabling you to create
> complex chord melody arrangements and chord solos.

**Significance (p. 286).** Justifies fingerstyle as the enabling technique for the entire chord-melody method.

## PIMAC finger naming

> p = thumb (“pulgar” in Spanish)
>    i = index (“indice” in Spanish)
>    m = middle (“medio” in Spanish)
>    a = ring finger (“anular” in Spanish)
>    c = pinky finger (“chiquito” in Spanish)

**Significance (p. 287).** Defines the standard finger-naming convention used throughout the appendix and in all picking-pattern markings.

## Early hand position matters

> It’s very important for fingerstyle that, right from the start, you adopt a hand position that
> promotes agility and freedom of movement.

**Significance (p. 287).** States the foundational ergonomic rule that governs every fingerstyle exercise in the appendix.

## Tips for Correct Fingerstyle Hand Placement

> Keep the wrist naturally curved in your plucking hand – avoid flatting or overarching the wrist too
>    much.
>    Keep the wrist in alignment with the arm, avoid kinking the wrist at an angle (it’s ok to strike the
>    strings slightly diagonally).
>    Keep your hand relaxed as possible, take frequent breaks while you are still learning these
>    techniques.
>    Fingernails can be helpful for getting more depth and tone of the sound, but is entirely optional
>    (especially on electric guitar).

**Significance (p. 288).** Catalogues the four concrete hand-position rules that constitute O'Rourke's fingerstyle posture method.

## Rest stroke vs free stroke defined

> “Rest stroke” – when you play a string and then rest your finger on the string behind the one
>    you plucked at the end of the motion.
>
>    “Free stroke” – when you pluck the string and let your finger fly “freely” above the other strings
>    as you end the motion.

**Significance (p. 289).** Defines the two foundational plucking strokes that all subsequent exercises use.

## Prefer free stroke for jazz

> Although rest stroke is a good first stroke to learn to gain security with your plucking, at this point I
> would recommend focusing on free stroke as your main picking technique.
>
> In general, free stroke is a more much more useful for jazz guitar than rest stroke.

**Significance (p. 293).** Gives the explicit preference rule between the two strokes for the jazz-guitar use case.

## Thumb motion mechanics

> The motion should come from the lowest “3rd” joint of the thumb, with a small circular motion as you
> pluck – similar to the motion of a paddle on a rowing boat.

**Significance (p. 294).** Specifies the exact mechanical motion of the thumb, a load-bearing technical instruction.

## Thumb role in fingerstyle jazz

> For a typical fingerstyle jazz guitarist, the thumb is generally reserved for playing bass notes in
> chords or to play basslines alongside melodies on the higher strings.

**Significance (p. 294).** Defines the conventional functional role of the thumb in this method.

## Wrist angle for multi-string plucks

> Ensure that your wrist is rolled slightly more towards the headstock to allow more space between the
> index finger and thumb. If your hand position is incorrect, these two fingers will obstruct each other’s
> motion when those fingers pluck notes together.

**Significance (p. 298).** Gives the specific wrist-angle correction needed to make multi-string plucking ergonomically work.

## Two-finger pluck combinations

> The main ways to pluck adjacent strings is with the p and i fingers, or i and m fingers.

**Significance (p. 298).** Codifies the two canonical two-finger plucking pairs for adjacent-string work.

## Three-note pluck combinations

> For 3 notes at once, it’s preferable to use p i m, or i m a, so it’s worth practicing both these
>  combinations.

**Significance (p. 298).** Codifies the two canonical three-finger plucking groupings used in this method.

## Four-finger maximum, skip pinky

> I rarely use the little finger (c) on the right hand with fingerstyle technique – I tend to stick to 4-note
> voicings as a maximum which means you will only need to use your four strongest fingers – p i m a.

**Significance (p. 298).** States the explicit voicing-size cap (four notes) and the rule to omit the pinky from the picking hand.

## Sound multi-string notes simultaneously
