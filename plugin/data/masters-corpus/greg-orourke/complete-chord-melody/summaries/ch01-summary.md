# Chapter 1 — Module 1 - Chord Melody Quickstart

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

Chapter 1 lays out O'Rourke's foundational four-step chord-melody recipe that the entire course operationalizes: pick a suitable tune, transpose it if needed for range, learn the melody on the top 2 or 3 strings, then choose chord voicings below the melody notes (Four-step chord melody method). For step 1 he restricts beginners to ballads rather than bebop because slower tempos and longer-held melody notes are practically easier (Tune selection rule), and for step 2 he gives a concrete fretboard-region heuristic — find a key where the melody sits on the top 3 strings between roughly the 3rd and 9th frets (Range rule of thumb) — naming the two failure modes the rule is designed to avoid: too high sounds thin, too low sounds dark and muddy (Range failure modes). The top-string canvas forces a horizontal rather than positional fretting approach (Horizontal melody fingering). The chapter's headline pedagogical rule is that you don't need a chord on every melody note (Don't harmonize every note), operationalized by placing chords on long-held notes (Place chords on long notes) and exploiting the guitar's quick decay by using a chord change to imply sustain rather than holding the note out (Sustain workaround). Load-bearing terminology introduced includes the four-step method itself, the top-2-strings constraint, and right-hand technique choice (Right-hand technique choice) — with a concrete plectrum move of plucking the bass note first then quickly strumming the rest of the chord to negotiate tricky string skips (Plectrum bass-then-strum).
