# Chapter 9 — Appendix 2 - Fingerstyle Training

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 is a fingerstyle-technique appendix that justifies the right-hand method underwriting the entire book, opening with the claim that fingerstyle is what enables multiple simultaneous voices and therefore the whole chord-melody enterprise (Fingerstyle plays multiple voices). It establishes the PIMAC finger-naming convention (p/i/m/a/c) used throughout the picking-pattern markings (PIMAC finger naming) and devotes early space to ergonomics: the foundational rule that hand position must promote agility from day one (Early hand position matters) and a four-rule posture catalog covering wrist curve, wrist-arm alignment, relaxation, and optional fingernails (Tips for Correct Fingerstyle Hand Placement). It then defines the two canonical strokes—rest stroke (finger lands on the next string) and free stroke (finger flies free)—as the foundational pluck vocabulary (Rest stroke vs free stroke defined), with the explicit preference rule that free stroke is the main technique for jazz guitar (Prefer free stroke for jazz). O'Rourke's characteristic moves are tight mechanical specifications: thumb motion comes from the lowest joint with a small rowing-paddle circular motion (Thumb motion mechanics) reserved for bass notes and basslines (Thumb role in fingerstyle jazz), and multi-string plucking requires the wrist rolled toward the headstock to keep thumb and index from obstructing each other (Wrist angle for multi-string plucks). Load-bearing rules close the chapter: the canonical two-finger pairs are p+i or i+m (Two-finger pluck combinations), the canonical three-finger groupings are pim or ima (Three-note pluck combinations), voicings cap at four notes and the pinky is omitted from the picking hand (Four-finger maximum, skip pinky), and plucked notes must sound simultaneously to register as a chord rather than an arpeggiation (Sound multi-string notes simultaneously).
