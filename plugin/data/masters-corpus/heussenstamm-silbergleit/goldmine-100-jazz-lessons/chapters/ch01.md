## Comping defined

> Comping is the main form of chordal accompaniment in straight-ahead jazz and is usually quite variable in its rhythms and
> chord voicings. Like single-note improvisation, it involves constant choices on the part of the musician and requires a feel for
> the style.

**Significance (p. 4).** Foundational definition of comping distinguishing it from mechanical strumming.

## Upbeat emphasis in comping

> One important observation is that there's a strong emphasis on upbeats in this kind of playing (the “ands” of each beat we
> count in a measure). And along with that, many chords that we think of as coming in on beats 1 or 3 of a 4/4 measure can
> actually get anticipated, or played one short 8th note earlier.

**Significance (p. 4).** Identifies the two core rhythmic devices of jazz comping that subsequent exercises build on.

## Chord anticipation technique

> by hitting each chord on the “and” before its official time, we already take a step towards viable
> jazz comping. (Remember that if it’s part of an overall swung groove, the “and is later in the beat!)

**Significance (p. 4).** Concrete actionable anticipation technique with the swing-timing nuance flagged.

## Mix of downbeats, upbeats, sustained/short chords

> there is usually a mix of downbeats and upbeats, and also of sustained
> and short chords.

**Significance (p. 4).** Textural rule distinguishing jazz chording from uniform strumming.

## Repeating rhythmic figure exercise

> we try a different basic exercise towards good chordal rhythm in jazz, which is to choose a simple rhythmic figure that
> fits the feel of the music and repeat it through a progression.

**Significance (p. 4).** Method's pedagogical move: lock one figure across changes before adding variety.

## Turnaround defined

> A typical turnaround in a chord progression often consists of I-VI-ii-V-I harmonic movement, which, for example, could mean
> B»6-G7-Cm7-F7-B+6 in the key of B> (G7 being a common substitute for the Gm7 that occurs naturally in the B> major scale).
> We call it a turnaround because it so frequently functions to bring us to the top of the tune again, from the end of one chorus
> of a repeating song form.

**Significance (p. 5).** Formal definition of the turnaround with worked Bb-key example.

## Turnaround improv: major scale approach

> Let's first try improvising on this progression using only tones from the scale of the key: B> major.

**Significance (p. 5).** First of three graduated improvisational strategies.

## Scale implying chord motion

> even though we never leave the B> major scale, you can hear a sense of moving away from the home chord and
> back again in the line as the changes progress. That's because this idea was constructed in such a way as to reflect the sound
> of the basic Bb, Cm7, and F7 chords along the way, even if it kind of coasts through G7.

**Significance (p. 6).** Compositional principle: target chord tones within a single scale.

## Chord-tone-only improv approach

> we can be even more specific to the changes by using chord tones only for each, switching gears as necessary within
> the line. Before trying this, let’s take stock of what these tones are, looking at arpeggios through the 9th for the whole
> progression. That is, we'll call it Bsmaj9, G79, Cm9, and F7+9 for the sake of this exercise.

**Significance (p. 6).** Second strategy: arpeggio-based lines through the 9th, with explicit chord extensions.

## Altered scale application

> we'll now try playing through this progression with changing scales
> to reflect each new chord. Let's say for now that we'll keep the B+ major scale for both our Bsmaj7 and Cm7, and we'll use an
> altered (or super Locrian) scale for our altered F7 and G7 chords.

**Significance (p. 6).** Third strategy: per-chord scale switching, with specific rule for altered dominants.

## Mixing improv methods

> Once familiar with some of the ways we can get through these chords in our linear improvisation, it may be more natural
>
> to mix our methods a bit, playing now more simply, and now more specifically, with a bit of space along the way between
> phrases.

**Significance (p. 7).** Concluding rule: combine all three approaches fluidly.
