## ii-V-I chord quality definition

> The naturally occurring
> ii-V-I in a major key, using seventh chords, involves a minor seven for ii, a dominant seven for V, and a major seven for |.

**Significance (p. 9).** Foundational definitional rule for major ii-V-I chord qualities.

## Modal unity of ii-V-I scales

> If we were to approach improvisation on this chord movement by way of the most obvious scales, we might choose D Dorian
> for Dm7, G Mixolydian for G7, and C major for Cmaj7—only to realize that these are really all the same set of tones (modally
> related to each other)!

**Significance (p. 9).** Identifies the conceptual trap and motivates chord-tone-first approach.

## 7th-to-3rd voice leading rule

> Notice that there's a tendency for the 7th of a chord to lead to the 3rd of the next one, whether directly, or bouncing off of
> another note in between.

**Significance (p. 9).** Most important melodic voice-leading rule for ii-V-I improvisation.

## Scalar tones as chord extensions

> Most often, these additional scalar tones can be seen as color tones of the basic chords, anyway (A is the 9th for a G7
> or a 6th for a C major chord, while E can be a 9th on Dm/7, etc.).

**Significance (p. 9).** Reframes non-chord-tones as harmonic extensions.

## ii-V-I pacing variations

> these changes may occur in a quick
> two-bar framework (two beats apiece for the ii and the V), or they may be stretched out over a longer span with one measure
> apiece for those first two chords.

**Significance (p. 9).** Two standard rhythmic frameworks for ii-V-I.

## Tonicization within progressions

> When identifying the ii-V-| formation in songs, know that it may take place with something other than the real “I” chord of
> the key as its landing point. That is, if we're playing a progression that is basically in the key of C, but at some point we find
> the chords Gm7-C7-Fmaj7, we have just come across a ii-V-I in F within the tune,

**Significance (p. 9).** Recognizes transient ii-V-I cells tonicizing non-tonic chords.

## Minor ii-V-i chord qualities

> Notice that the ii chord is a minor seven with ab5th (sometimes referred to as “half-
> diminished"), the V chord is an altered dominant (at least with a flatted or raised 9th instead of a natural 9th), and the i chord
> is a minor seven:

**Significance (p. 10).** Defines three chord qualities distinguishing minor ii-V-i.

## Minor scale options for ii-V-i

> some others that are typically thought of
> for this purpose are the Locrian scale for the iim7+5 (amounts to the same notes as natural minor from the tonic), the altered
> (or super Locrian) for the V7, and the Dorian for the im7.

**Significance (p. 10).** Standard three-scale solution set for minor ii-V-i improv.

## Jazz blues vs. rock blues form

> When jazz musicians play a blues, a 12-bar form is still the norm,
> but the usual progression is not quite the same three-chord I-IV-V cycle commonly found in rock or the blues genre itself—
> related, but at least a little more involved.

**Significance (p. 13).** Frames the jazz/rock blues distinction.

## Jazz blues ii-V substitution

> Example 1 outlines the changes as they are typically thought of in jazz, with ii-V chord motion (Cm7 to F7) where we might
> have expected V-IV (F7 to B:7), and a VI chord thrown in to boot (G7).

**Significance (p. 13).** Identifies signature harmonic substitutions defining jazz blues.

## Chord progression as framework

> the official chord progression may be just a framework—a harmonic direction or set of possibilities—more than a
> super-specific list of chords to be played on each go-around.

**Significance (p. 13).** Foundational principle: written changes are a guide.

## Chord-tone-only improv exercise rationale

> In this
> lesson, we try, as an exercise, to solo on the progression with basic chord tones only and nothing else. In the process, we
> can begin to map out these chordal members on the fretboard (beyond where they sit in familiar chord shapes) and start to
> hear the chord changes in our single-note lines.

**Significance (p. 14).** Method and dual goal of chord-toning: fretboard mapping + harmonic ear.

**Significance (p. 14).** Concrete formula for chord-tone arpeggios from scales.

## Chord-tone exercise scope and limits

> Realize that this process can be expanded by mapping out the chord tones all over fretboard and by trying it in different
> keys and with other progressions altogether. It is not an improvisational end-goal in itself (even if it may yield some tasty
> licks), since in the long run we will surely allow ourselves to use other tones of a scale, more complex versions of the chords,
>
> and beyond, as well as musical ideas from entirely different sources. But it can open the door to true involvement with chord
> changes in our melodic playing.

**Significance (p. 15).** Contextualizes chord-toning as pedagogical step, not destination.
