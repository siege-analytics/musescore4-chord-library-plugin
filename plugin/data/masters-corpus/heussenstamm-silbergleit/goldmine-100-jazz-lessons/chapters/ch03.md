## Tritone Substitution Definition

> Tritone substitution is the replacement of a chord with another one of the same kind rooted a tritone away from the original
> (a distance of three whole steps). It most clearly applies to dominant seven chords and has the effect of turning a natural
> dominant seventh into a fully altered dominant.

**Significance (p. 17).** Foundational definition specifying interval and harmonic result.

## b5 Chord — V7 Meeting Point

> A simple 715 chord is the place where a dominant chord and its tritone substitute meet—where they are note-for-note the
> same, although in theory each tone is now a different member of the chord with some enharmonic spellings.

**Significance (p. 17).** Theoretical pivot making tritone substitution possible.

## Tritone Sub — Chromatic Root Motion

> The result is often that, where we had a V-I resolution in a progression
> (8-7 heading to Bmaj7, for example), we now have a chromatic root movement to get where we're going—actually a pretty
> common sound in many styles and tunes.

**Significance (p. 17).** Defining sonic consequence: chromatic bass instead of circle-of-fifths.

## Tritone Sub — Melodic 5-3-2-1 Descent

> In the tritone sub version, we'll descend through scalar tones 5-3-2-1 for
> each chord, resulting in an overall down-by-half-steps sound to the whole thing.

**Significance (p. 17).** Concrete single-note technique for tritone-sub melodic lines.

## Whole ii-V as Tritone Sub (Bebop)

> let’s look at a device common in bebop harmony—the use of a whole ii-V unit as a
> tritone substitution for the first two chords in a ji-V-I, sometimes with the original pair played first, and the subs worked in
> before it resolves.

**Significance (p. 18).** Extends tritone sub from single chord to entire ii-V pair.

## Bm7 — Maximum Distance from Bb7

> This chord, while related logically to the B+7 (by being the ii-V partner
> of E7, tritone sub of Bs), has an A natural as its 7th and gets us sonically further than we've been from any kind of 87 (which
> will always contain an A»).

**Significance (p. 18).** Pinpoints A natural vs A-flat as harmonic distance signal.

## Tritone Turnarounds — Parallel Chromatic Motion

> The D+13 used in place of a G7#9 results in virtually the same chord (with a different bass note, perhaps), but still the overall
> concept leads to a new sound for our turnaround, with chromatic, parallel movement of this dominant thirteen voicing. It may
> seem like random fret-by-fret sliding around with one chord shape, but it is not—it is logically linked to the more circle-of-
> fifths-based motion that was there in the first place, and it functions similarly in terms of harmonic direction—how it moves
> away from the | chord and brings us home again.

**Significance (p. 18).** Parallel chromatic motion retains original harmonic function.

## Minor Blues — i, iv, V Chord Types

> As we compare it to the main |, IV, and V chord structure of the standard blues, the i and iv typically become
> minor, while the V remains dominant and is often preceded by the VI chord, which is also a dominant seventh.

**Significance (p. 21).** Structural rule distinguishing minor blues from standard blues.

## Natural vs. Altered Dominant Rule

## Half-Step 3rd — Gm to G7 Signal

> A Gm chord of any kind moving to G7 (or, for that matter, to G major) entails the very clear change of the chordal 3rd (B+) going

**Significance (p. 22).** Half-step 3rd shift as the most audible minor-to-dominant change signal.
