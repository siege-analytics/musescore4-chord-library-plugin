## Constant Phrase Shape method

> we choose a short melodic rhythm to play repeatedly through a song form or
> chord progression. We come up with it without being specific about pitch and then fill in the blanks with notes as we go,
> appropriate to the harmonies at hand. The key element is that the rhythmic figure must stay the same, and the notes must fit
> into its framework.

**Significance (p. 24).** Core pedagogical instruction: fixed rhythm, variable pitch.

## Phrase shape sharpens note choice

> as much as this exercise would seem to mean a shift of focus from notes to rhythm, it really has everything to do with
> note choice after all. It forces us to listen for which tones of the present scale or chord sound good to us within that limited
> shape, if we pursue it with that concern in mind.

**Significance (p. 24).** Rhythmic constraint sharpens harmonic listening.

## Phrase shape as improvisation mindset

> Prioritizing the very shapes, rhythms, and spaces we'd like to hear in our solo and making our choice of notes fit into that
>
> famework. Ultimately, of course, we want the flexibility to play few or many notes, in long or simple phrase shapes, and a full
> musical idea is really a composite of tones and timing.

**Significance (p. 25).** Governing principle: an idea is a composite of tones and timing.

## Changing Rhythmic Shape method

> We begin by selecting a series of tones that can work over a given harmony, in the order we will play them for a single musical
> idea (not just a scale to be drawn from), We'll stick with a reasonable number of notes that could fit easily enough into one or
> two measures. Next, we see how many different ways we can come up with to rhythmically shape this row of tones, so as to
> make a nice statement within a particular groove and within the scope of one, two, or a few measures,

**Significance (p. 26).** Procedural definition of the rhythmic variation exercise.

## Rhythmic approaches enumerated

> Notice that there can be distinctly different approaches, such as grouping the tones in swung 8ths, stretching them out,
> adding some little slide-ins, or using triplets.

**Significance (p. 26).** Concrete rhythmic tools for reshaping a row.

## Call and response structural rule

> Structurally, this could happen in different ways. It doesn’t need to be in the context of a 12-bar blues, but some kind of
> symmetrical form is helpful, like so many tunes have, with four- or eight-bar sections that can easily be divided into one-, two-,
> or four-bar chunks for our call and response.

**Significance (p. 28).** Structural rule: symmetrical form enables call/response.

## Natural vs. altered dominant decision

> Nowhere is this truer than with dominant seven chords—the kind that may be named simply as “F7” or “B+7." With these, where
> there is no further detail given, the biggest decision for each is whether it will be a natural or altered dominant.

**Significance (p. 30).** Central interpretive rule for skeletal dominant chord symbols.

## Minor ii-V altered dominant rule

> for a minor ii-V-i, the tendency is strong for that V chord to be an altered dominant (this is the case generally for V-i
> motion from a dominant seven to a minor chord, although there are exceptions).

**Significance (p. 31).** Strongest prescriptive rule for altered dominant choice.

## Natural-to-altered dominant shift device

> A common harmonic move in jazz Progressions is for a dominant seven chord to change from natural to altered before it
> resolves in a V-I direction (like from D to G, or G to C, such that the root movement is down a perfect 5th). This gives it a sense
> of extra angle, urgency, or momentum to get to the next chord.

**Significance (p. 32).** Defines 'altered angle' device with functional purpose.

## Mixolydian to super Locrian scale switch

> for both D7 and G7, we first played from a corresponding
> Mixolydian scale and then switched to the super Locrian (or “altered”) scale. There are some tones in common between these.
> but right at those points of natural-to-altered change we hear a note that belongs only to the super Locrian (with at least
> one more such tone to follow, before the next change).

**Significance (p. 33).** Scalar mechanism for natural-to-altered melodic shift.

## Altered dominant scale categories overview

> There are certain popular scale choices for improvisation that correspond specifically to various kinds of altered dominants. In
> this lesson, we get an overview of them and then look at one case in which we may have to “improvise” our scalar information.

**Significance (p. 34).** Frames Lesson 16's taxonomy of altered scales by chord sub-type.

## Gap in standard altered scalar info

> Thinking of F harmonic minor (or its fifth mode, from C) does not give us the possibility of E,/Dé, and thinking of C half-whole
> diminished imposes an A-natural where we're using A; in these phrases. The C altered scale (mode of melodic minor) doesn’t
> allow for our natural Sth, the G that is certainly involved here, and whole tone is surely not it (doesn’t even give us a flatted
>
> or raised 9th)!

**Significance (p. 35).** Methodical elimination proving a real gap in scalar theory.

## Jazz blues stylistic boundary rule

> there are certain ways that jazz guitar players tend to delve into their bluesy side, usually without the more extreme
> bending, sliding, shaking, or distorted screaming that can be found in the rock or blues genres (although a touch of vibrato is
> nice). In playing jazz, we may want to play the blues, but without sounding as though we showed up at the wrong gig!

**Significance (p. 36).** Stylistic boundary rule for jazz blues with 'wrong gig' test.

## Reverse swing rhythmic figure

> at the beginning of the second full measure, there's a figure that | sometimes think of as “reverse swing"—an 8th
> and then a quarter note in a triplet, effectively giving us a short first half of the beat and a longer second half. Played with a
> certain attack at slower tempos, it can convey quite a blues attitude!

**Significance (p. 37).** Coins and defines 'reverse swing' rhythmic device.

## Blues devices hiding jazz vocabulary

> in the case of these jazz novices, | get the feeling that they are hiding behind these habits, relying on
> them for more obvious ways to sound wailin’ on the guitar, instead of dealing with the tonal and rhythmic language of a
>
> music that is new to them. So | encourage them to put those things aside for the moment, study the vocabulary of the style,
> and look for expressiveness in their choice of notes and rhythms.

**Significance (p. 37).** Prescriptive pedagogical rule: suppress blues gestures to acquire jazz vocabulary.
