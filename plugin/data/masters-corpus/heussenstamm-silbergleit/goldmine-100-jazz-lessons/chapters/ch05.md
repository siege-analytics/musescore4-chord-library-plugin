## 3/4 emphasis on 'and' of 2

> In jazz, there's frequently an emphasis on the “and” of beat 2 in a 3/4 measure. This is a point of time that, as the numbers go,
> would be exactly halfway through the bar. (Note, however, that if we're in swung time, it'll be a little later in that 2nd beat,
> giving the whole rhythm a certain lilt.)

**Significance (p. 38).** Structural rule for 3/4 emphasis with swung-time caveat.

## Finger roll — ascending mechanics

> What we need to do here, for distinct tones and a legato flow, is to roll with that first finger. In the first measure, place it on its
> tip as usual for the note C on the third string, and roll off the tip onto the flatter part of the finger to fret the second string, This
> takes some slow practice to get going smoothly, coordinating the release of the earlier note with the sounding of the next one.

**Significance (p. 42).** Core mechanics of ascending finger roll.

## Finger roll — descending pre-placement

> For the second measure phrase, the finger roll needs to happen in reverse. When descending with this technique (heading to
> a higher-numbered string and a lower pitch), we need to strategize a little more ahead of time: to play the note E here, place
> the index finger more flatly on its underside at the 5th fret of the B string, such that you can roll up onto its tip on the G string.
> Again, slow practice is the key, especially now for correct initial finger placement, as well as for the timing of things.

**Significance (p. 42).** Descending roll requires pre-positioning flat.

## Alternate picking by rhythm

> this approach involves a built-in groove in the picking hand, gained from allowing it to move
> continuously with the beat. It's kind of a micro-version of common strumming patterns from other styles, in which the right
> hand keeps moving down and up to the pulse of the music, catching all downbeats with a downstroke and all upbeats (the
> “and” counts) with an upstroke, regardless of where there is space in between.

**Significance (p. 44).** Defines rhythm-governed alternate picking.

## Straight-4 chord release timing

> Rhythmically, it is not just a continual blur of sustained quarter notes, each running right into the next. There needs to be just
> a little release between each chord (letting it be muted by lifting the pressure of your left hand). But it also should not sound
> like a march, with a short chopping sound on each beat due to too much release. You could think of the timing of this release
>
> as having to do with the swung-ness of the quarter-note rhythm (or better yet, just listen to some Freddie Green and try to get
> a feel for it).

**Significance (p. 46).** Precise release timing for straight-4 quarter notes.

## Two-note shell definition

> Basically, it’s the root, 3rd, and 7th of a chord that really define its main type, and we're talking about breaking various
> kinds of chords down to these essential tones (and then leaving off the root, to boot).
>
> What we have left are very compact, easy-to-play, and flexible little two-string voicings that can still serve to outline a chord
> progression, especially when put together with the walking line of a bassist.

**Significance (p. 48).** Defines shell voicings and rootless-with-bassist condition.

## Shell voicings — middle strings caveat

> for now we'll stick with these
> shell voicings that lie on the middle two strings only. This is both technically and sonically convenient, as it puts us in a mellow
> midrange that rings clearly enough while blending nicely with others in the band. Please be aware—we don't automatically
> get the right notes for these by taking any chord shape and cutting it down to the middle strings. In the examples above,
> that's where the needed tones happen to be.

**Significance (p. 48).** Warns against shortcut of mechanically trimming chords to middle strings.
