## Sequential ii-V practice strategy

> since we have the same kind of chords repeatedly, we can focus on
> some things to play just for those two chords and practice moving them around to different keys as needed. In this way,
> sequential progressions present a nice opportunity for clearly targeted practice.

**Significance (p. 70).** Core sequential practice strategy.

## ii-V pair melodic cells

> These quite traditional
> phrases use entirely C Dorian tones and basically outline the chords (compare with a Cm7 or F9 shape around the 8th fret), all
> with a sense of swing.

**Significance (p. 70).** Source scale logic for sequential melodic cells.

## Hemiola definition

> Hemiola refers to rhythmic
> figures that repeat as if to imply a certain meter, while being played in a different meter. For example, a three-beat long phrase
> that occurs repetitively, end-to-end, in 4/4 time would constitute a hemiola.

**Significance (p. 72).** Formal definition of hemiola.

## Blues scale hemiola groupings

> By
> experimenting with different starting or turning-around points, we can create various rhythmic groupings that either coincide
> or contrast with the meter of a tune. For example, if we start at the tonic, go up to the 5th tone and back, turn around on the
> bottom note again, and so on, the result is a unit of eight 8th notes, which will start at the same place in a 4/4 measure each
> time. If we leave off that top note in our up-and-down pattern, we get a six-note figure, again implying three over four.

**Significance (p. 72).** Mechanical rule for hemiola from blues scale.

## Tritone substitution three principles

> We'll use three principles or procedures: 1) turning dominant seven chords from natural to altered before resolving
> down a 5th, 2) the use of a tritone substitute, or dominant seven chord three steps removed from another one, to turn it into
> an altered dominant, and 3) the cyclical practicing of short melodic ideas to have them ready for use on different roots or in
> different keys.

**Significance (p. 74).** Three-step method for sneaky sub-chord cycle.

## Harmonizing one tone — definition

> attempting to harmonize one single note with all the chords in a progression—or at least a
> good part of it. This means choosing a note (probably in the key of the tune, but not always), looking for versions of the chords
> with that note at the top, and playing them in sequence.

**Significance (p. 76).** Defines the one-tone harmonization exercise.

## Double-stop intervals — physical rationale

> This can be done with different intervals (distances
> between the two notes that are played together), but it is physically easiest on the guitar with 3rds or 4ths, since they are
> relatively simple to play on neighboring strings in standard tuning.

**Significance (p. 78).** Why 3rds/4ths dominate double-stop technique.

## Perfect 4th harmonization — B/G string exception

> In the first version, we've harmonized the entire thing with the perfect 4th interval, which is particularly easy to find on the
> guitar, as most neighboring string pairs are tuned that distance apart (meaning that notes on the same fret of both strings
> will form a perfect 4th, save for the G and B strings together).

**Significance (p. 79).** 4th-interval rule with B-string exception.

## Transposition — fretboard signposts

> if you know the names of the open strings, you pretty easily know what notes you have at the 12th fret, the
> octave point. If you know how the guitar is tuned, you have some ready “signposts” at the Sth (or 4th) fret, where the note on
> one string is the same as the next string open.

**Significance (p. 81).** Fretboard navigation signposts for transposition.

## Slash chord definition

> the kind of chords whose symbols look like “D/G"
> or “Emaj7/F”—i.e., with a slash in the middle. These read as “D over G” or “E-flat major seven over F" and are used to designate
> chords for which the note under the slash is in the bass (or on the bottom of the voicing, anyway).

**Significance (p. 82).** Defines slash chord notation.

## Slash chord voicing caution

> When we see a slash chord on a chart, it can be a mistake to assume that we should go to the first place on the fretboard we
> usually think of for the main chord and then try to change the bass note.

**Significance (p. 82).** Avoidance rule against default-shape voicing patch.

## Baby strides — V-I focus method

> as an alternative to the tones-on-every-chord method, try focusing on the resolution
> (the “leading in’) to each new major key with simple, very short V-I lines that you may already play in other settings. We still
> wind up playing something on nearly every chord, if not all of them, but it’s a different way of getting our bearings.

**Significance (p. 84).** Target V-I resolutions instead of every chord.

## Half-step outside — brief excursion rule

> we can
> experiment with momentarily escaping the zone of the chord on which we are soloing. In this lesson, we'll try a couple of
> devices for moving a half step up from where we are expected to be and quickly back again.

**Significance (p. 86).** Half-step outside excursion rule.

## Tritone sub / Dbmaj7 outside rationale

> leading back into the chord from a half step above it can have some of the same
> mojo as V-I resolution—i.e,, like a G7 heading home to Cmaj7. G7 could pretty naturally be replaced by a D+ dominant seven
> chord, its tritone substitute, and having D+ major seven there instead gives the whole thing a new flavor.

**Significance (p. 86).** Justifies major-chord half-step outside via tritone-sub logic.
