## Scale sequencing method

> By using fingering positions on the fretboard, we can put sequences together if we familiarize ourselves
> with where these notes are. It takes a great deal of practice, but the reward is being able to construct lines quickly, which is
> essential in order to be a competent jazz musician. These sequences are built from two positions of the C major scale.

**Significance (p. 120).** Fingering positions as prerequisite for sequencing.

## Modes defined by background chord

> The C major scale has no sharps or flats, and so it is with the D Dorian minor scale. Since both of these sounds come from the
> same notes, what determines the difference? The answer is the chord or bass note being played in the background. What you
> have in the background affects the way the notes sound.

**Significance (p. 122).** Modes distinguished by background chord, not notes.

## E Phrygian scale degrees

> Using the notes from the C major scale again, we are creating a new mode called E Phrygian minor. This is a very popular
> Spanish sound, and you hear it in Latin jazz all the time. Remember, this is the C major scale over E minor. | don't want to
> confuse you with technical stuff, but the scale degrees based on E would be (1+2+3-4-5+6-47).

**Significance (p. 122).** E Phrygian interval structure with usage context.

## Grouping chords by key for soloing

> To simplify our approach in playing over so many chords, we should
> analyze any tune to see how chords can be grouped into keys. The purpose of this is to demonstrate that we only need to
> apply one scale to groups of chords that are related to one key.

**Significance (p. 124).** Reduce chord changes to single-scale regions.

## Non-diatonic chord handling

> Since those outside-the-key chords come and go quickly, you can
> choose to not pay attention to them. In that case, you can solo over the first section using just the F major scale. When these
> non-diatonic chords (Bbm7 and Gim7) pop up, you can emphasize their chord tones by adding them to your melodies.

**Significance (p. 125).** Two-part rule for handling non-diatonic chords.

## Pentatonics within diatonic major

> The C diatonic major scale (C-D-E-F-G-
> A-8) contains three minor pentatonic scales within it: A minor, D minor, and E minor. If you include the 11th or 45th note (Fé or G
> respectively), which is a common and useful sound in jazz, you can use the B minor pentatonic scale as well. This gives you four
> different pentatonic scales that are built from the C major scale.

**Significance (p. 126).** Four pentatonics from one major scale.

## Chord shape melody concept

> A simple but effective way to create melodies is to use chord shapes. The objective in creating melodies, and I've said this time
> and again, is to avoid sounding as though you're practicing a scale or even playing through a scale. | try to play in a way that
> hides the sound of the scale. In these examples, not every note is part of the chord shape, but the shape is the grid or model
> for the melodic idea.

**Significance (p. 128).** Chord shapes as melodic skeleton hide scale patterns.

## Riff shifting — chromatic

> Starting with a quick pentatonic riff in A minor, | climb chromatically in half steps until | get to C minor. | then finish the phrase
> in C minor. Any riff can be moved chromatically up or down; the way you finish is most important.

**Significance (p. 130).** Chromatic riff-shifting + phrase completion rule.

## Major-to-minor shift: minor 3rd rule

> One convenient
> habit many guitarists have is to move a major scale up three frets (or a minor 3rd), which turns the sound into the minor of
> that same key.

**Significance (p. 132).** Up-three-frets = minor of same root.

## Pentatonic vs. Mixolydian over dominant

> Through the study of the blues, | found this concept of switching from a minor pentatonic sound (1-+43-4-5-+7) to the
> Mixolydian mode, which is sometimes described as a diatonic major scale with ab7th (1-2-3-4-5-6-47). You can use these two
> sounds over one dominant chord—in this case, we'll use A7. | call the effect of the minor pentatonic bluesy and the Mixolydian
> jazzy. It's good to have options, and the goal is to be able to switch scales in the middle of a passage without a chord change.

**Significance (p. 134).** Bluesy/jazzy contrast with mid-passage switching goal.

## Playing changes: interval practice method

> Practice playing scales between these intervals. For example, play any type of scale, such as A major, and then go down a
> whole step to G and play G minor pentatonic. | mean any type of scale. Try moving the same scale up and down the fretboard
> the way you move barre chords. You have to get used to shifting the tonal center or root around.

**Significance (p. 137).** Treat scale shifts like barre chord shifts.

## Many scales over G7 dominant

> Without a doubt, the most significant musical application in jazz is the use of scales and modes over dominant seven type
> chords. The flexibility you have seems limitless. Once that b7th note is introduced in a chord, we have all kinds of options as to
> which type of scale or sound we wish to use. A lot of a person’s style and identification will be based on this fact.

**Significance (p. 142).** Dominant 7 as the central scale-choice battleground.

## Bebop dominant scale

> MIXOLYDIAN/IONIAN (BEBOP DOMINANT SCALE)
> This has the natural or major 7th as well as the +7th and was a favorite of Charlie Parker.

**Significance (p. 142).** Defines bebop dominant scale with Charlie Parker attribution.

## Diminished scale mastery imperative

> All jazz musicians need to get command of this sound. There could be a place for this in practically every jazz tune. Don't
> overuse this scale, but you should definitely master it.

**Significance (p. 143).** Mastery imperative paired with restraint caveat.
