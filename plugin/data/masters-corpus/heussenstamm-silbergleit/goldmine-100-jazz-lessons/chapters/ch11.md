## Chromatic ascending riffs technique

> You can take any riff and repeat it chromatically up the fretboard a half step (one fret) at a time. | got this idea by watching
> fusion players like John Abercrombie and John Scofield. The trick is to resolve the line by ending on notes that lie firmly in key.
> You can target notes up the neck and end up there.

**Significance (p. 148).** Half-step displacement + resolution to in-key targets.

## Whole-step chromatic variation

> You can take virtually any riff and apply this line of thinking. Try moving up whole steps (two frets at a time). You can create
> an interesting moment of tension in the middle of a solo.

**Significance (p. 149).** Whole-step variation creates mid-solo tension.

## Descending chromatic riff resolution rule

> In all your creative efforts to come up with melodies, it’s important to know you can try and reverse every idea to see what
> else you can think of. If you can play ascending chromatic riffs, for example, you can also play descending chromatic riffs.
> The important thing to be aware of is how you resolve and finish these lines.

**Significance (p. 150).** Symmetric ascending/descending; resolution is non-negotiable.

## Diminished scale construction and key equivalence

> A C diminished scale contains C-Db—E)-E-F#-G-A-B», or the 149-29 (or b3)-3-45
> (or #4)-5-6-47. We can construct a basic C7 chord using the 1, 3, 5, and+7 of this scale. If we stick to the theory that we can play
> the scale over any chord we created from it, then the C diminished scale will work over the C7 chord.

**Significance (p. 152).** Note content + numeric formula + dominant application rule.

## Diminished scale three-fret equivalence

> The same exact patterns and fingerings occur three frets above and below. So this scale will work for C, Dé (or E>), F# (or Gs), and
> A because these keys are three half steps apart.

**Significance (p. 152).** One fingering covers four keys.

## Diminished chord three-fret symmetry

> If we analyze the diminished chord, we find that it repeats itself every three half steps. In other words, B, D, F, and G# are
> all three half steps (or frets) apart, and the chords are built from the same four notes. In these examples, both the B and G#
> diminished chords could be thought of as the same chord (B° = B-D-F-G# and Ge = G¢-B-D-F). Both chords have the same
> notes; they're just in a different order.

**Significance (p. 154).** Only three distinct diminished-chord shapes exist.
