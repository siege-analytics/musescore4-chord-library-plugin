## Interval jumping with riffs

> By “interval jumping with riffs,” | simply mean taking a line and repeating it at another position. You could move it a half step
> (one fret), whole step (two frets), minor 3rd (three frets), major 3rd (four frets), etc.

**Significance (p. 170).** Definitional sentence with interval options.

## Playing down two whole steps: resolution logic

> Two common resolutions were used to create these lines. The V chord in the key of A is E. We hear this common sound and
> learn this theory from studying basic diatonic harmony, The E or E7 chord resolves smoothly into an A chord. A half step down
> resolution is nice too. Going from an F chord to an E chord would be going down a half step, and this too is a common sound
> in music.

**Significance (p. 172).** V-I + chromatic resolution justify key jump.

## Jazz: skipping chord tones in fast changes

> It's important to know in jazz that you don't have to play notes for every chord that comes up in a tune. Sometimes there are
> several changes at fast tempos, and it seems impossible to keep up. You can spot chords and target your areas so you don't
> have to worry about every chord,

**Significance (p. 172).** Liberates from chord-by-chord literalism.

## Playing off the five: one-note difference rule

> there's only one note difference between the key of A.
> E, and D; D has two sharps, A has three, and E has four. Remember that we're talking about jazz, and jazz is meant to represent
> free expression. So when you're jamming in A, you can improvise in D or E because these keys have only one note difference
> between them, and that one note won't sound bad, especially if you're flying over passages.

**Significance (p. 174).** Key-signature proximity rule.

## Whole tone scale: only two scales

> The first
> thing to understand is that there are really only two whole tone scales, and with that, you have the entire system covered, It’s
> been created by using notes that are two frets (or one whole step) apart. If you move it up one fret, you have the next set of
> notes for the other scale.

**Significance (p. 176).** Only two mutually exclusive whole tone scales exist.

## Whole tone scale: chord harmony breakdown

> If we analyze one of our two whole tone scales, we have A-B-C+-D#-F-G, If you relate all the notes to the A note and use it as
> the root, the relationships would be 1, 2, 3, 55, 25 (or $6), 67. Here are some of the pure chords you can create from this whole
> tone scale: A7(no 5th), A9(no Sth), A7»5, A7#5, A945, A9#5, etc.

**Significance (p. 177).** Maps whole tone to chord symbols.

## Enclosure: concept and targeting defined

> When we use the term “targeting” in music,
> we mean to say that we've picked out a note that we're aiming for. We basically arrange notes around that targeted note in a
> way that makes it sound as though we were heading in that direction.

**Significance (p. 178).** Defines targeting + enclosure logic.

## Enclosure: bebop sound technique

**Significance (p. 179).** Enclosure as essential bebop device.

## Free form targeting: method and rule

> Pick any note on the fretboard and devise a way to get to it. It’s good to use theory and scales you know, but it’s also possible
> to use shapes and forms that might not necessarily fall under any specific theoretical labeling.

**Significance (p. 180).** Only constraint is resolution to target note.
