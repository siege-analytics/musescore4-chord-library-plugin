## Two chord forms per root

> Notice that there are two chord shapes, or voicings, for each chord, One chord has its root or bass note on the sixth string and the
> other on the fifth string. The reason for this is so that you can always have the next chord coming up in a progression close by.
> You don't want to have to shift way up and down the fretboard all the time, especially when the song or tune has a fast tempo.

**Significance (p. 183).** Dual-root-string organizing rule.

## Movable chord shapes

> The next important point of all this is that ail
> chord shapes are movable when you're not using open-string voicings. You know that all these chords in this lesson are C chords
> right? If you move them up two frets, they become D chords of the same nature. The C7 chord moved up two frets becomes
> a D7 chord. The C7 chord moved up four frets becomes an E7 chord, etc.

**Significance (p. 183).** Multiplies vocabulary across 12 keys.

## Chord system via formula modification

> The world of chords can be a massive study depending on how deeply you want to get into it. The following systematic
> approach really helped me memorize enough fundamental chords so | could play blues and jazz tunes. If we study the A7
> chord and its formula, we can change it and create other chords. For example, A7 = A-C#-E-G; A6 = A-C#+-E-F#. The A6 chord
> has an F# instead of a G, as in the A7. There's only a half step or one fret difference.

**Significance (p. 184).** Formula-modification generates chord vocabulary.

## Three chord families

> For the thinking jazz musician, there are three types of chords, or three chord families: major, minor, and dominant. Each
> group has a characteristic sound that we have to learn to recognize by ear. When drilling students, it’s usually easy for them to
> identify the major and minor sounds. However, the dominant family somehow has to be easily recognized because it can be
> strange sounding sometimes. But, it’s not that difficult once you hear how it’s distinguished: it just doesn't sound like a major or
> minor chord!

**Significance (p. 186).** Three-family taxonomy for chord learning.

## Major, minor, dominant triad formulas

> The C major family is founded on the C, E, and G notes—these being the 1, 3, and 5, or major triad.
>
> The minor family is founded on C, B, and G notes—these being the 1, +3, and 5, or minor triad. Notice that, by only lowering
> or flatting the E note a half step, we've created another chord family. In other words, the only difference between a major triad
> and a minor triad is that the 3rd is a half step lower in a minor triad.
>
> The dominant (-7) family is founded with the same C, E, and G, or 1-3-5 major triad, but it also has the b7th, which is a Bs note
> in this case, added to the sound.

**Significance (p. 186).** Intervallic formulas for the three families.

## Circle of fourths chord movement

> The terms “circle of fourths” and/or “circle of fifths” are a common occurrence in music and, for the jazz musician, an important
> study that enables you to implement altered chords into your music. To move a chord up a 4th is simple to explain. Using the
> scale of C, let's count: C(1) D(2) E(3) F(4). From C to F is a 4th. So the chord change from C to F is up a 4th. It’s also five frets
> or five half steps higher.

**Significance (p. 189).** Circle of 4ths in fretboard terms.

## Chord melody rule: melody on top

> The main thing to remember in chord melody is to always have the melody of the tune or song
> on top of the chords as the highest sounding pitch. This is similar to a piano player playing the melody with the right hand
> and the chords with the left.

**Significance (p. 196).** Cardinal chord-melody rule.

## Melody within the chord technique

> | call this type of approach “melody within the chord.’ You hold the chord shape and pick notes in the middle of the chord
> without moving your left hand from the chord shape.

**Significance (p. 195).** Names 'melody within the chord' technique.

## Chord embellishment / Jazz 101

> Another thing to take into account is that every chord has been
> embellished. In other words, instead of playing a C major chord, we've made it a C6. Instead of playing an E minor chord,
> we've played an Em7, etc. This type of thinking is what | call “Jazz 101.” You can exchange, replace, or substitute any chord with
> another chord from the same family.

**Significance (p. 201).** Jazz 101 family-substitution rule.

## 4th and half-step resolution logic

> G7sus4: This chord is a 4th higher than the D13 chord. The beauty of the 4th principle in making a change with
> chords is that it allows you to pick any chord. Why? The answer is resolution. It’s a nice resolution to move chords by a
> 4th (D to G, G to C, C to F, F to Bs, etc.).

**Significance (p. 201).** Resolution justifies free chord choice via 4ths.

## Diatonic vs. non-diatonic changes

> For me, there are two main areas for chord-change consideration. One of those areas involves common root movements,
> such as up a 4th, down a step, etc. The other is what | call diatonic changes. Diatonic changes are the ones found within the
> key. In other words, the chords of C major are Cmaj7, Dm7, Em7, Fmaj7, G7, Am7, and Bm7+5.

**Significance (p. 201).** Two chord-movement categories: root-interval vs. diatonic.
