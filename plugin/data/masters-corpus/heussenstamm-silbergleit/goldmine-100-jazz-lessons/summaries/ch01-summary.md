# Chapter 1 — Comping Fundamentals and Turnarounds (Lessons 1-2)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

Chapter 1 introduces comping as the foundation of jazz accompaniment, defined as variable rhythmic and harmonic accompaniment distinct from mechanical strumming, with two signature devices: emphasis on upbeats (the "ands" in counted measures) and chord anticipation (playing chords one eighth-note early to create forward momentum while accounting for swing timing). The chapter teaches a pedagogical progression from locked rhythmic figures repeated through chord changes, to understanding the turnaround—a specific harmonic formula (typically I-VI-ii-V-I, exemplified as Bbmaj6-G7-Cm7-F7-Bbmaj6) that returns the form to its top—and then three graduated approaches to improvising over changes: scale-based lines using only the home key's tones while targeting chord-tone contours, chord-tone-only arpeggio lines through the ninth with explicit extensions, and per-chord scale selection (using altered or super Locrian for altered dominants). The master's characteristic move is the compositional principle that even single-scale lines can imply harmonic motion through strategic targeting of underlying chord tones. The core terminology load-bearing this chapter includes comping itself, upbeat emphasis, chord anticipation, turnaround, arpeggio-based improvisation, and altered scales, all organized around the key insight that jazz rhythm and harmony live in constant dialogue between fixed changes and flexible phrasing.
