# Chapter 2 — II-V-I Cells and Blues Comping (Lessons 3-6)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch02.md`._

Chapter 2 teaches the ii-V-I progression as a foundational harmonic cell, establishing the chord qualities (minor seven for ii, dominant seven for V, major seven for I) and voice-leading principle that the 7th of each chord resolves to the 3rd of the next. The chapter introduces both major and minor ii-V-i variants, with their associated scales (Dorian, Mixolydian, and major for major keys; Locrian, altered dominant, and Dorian for minor), and addresses how ii-V-I cells may tonicize chords other than the tonic. A central pedagogical move is the chord-tone-only improvisation exercise, which maps chord members across the fretboard while developing harmonic ear, framed as a stepping stone rather than an end goal. The chapter also presents jazz blues comping as a distinct form from rock blues, defined by ii-V substitutions and the principle that written chord progressions function as harmonic frameworks rather than rigid prescriptions. Key terminology introduced includes Super Locrian scale for altered dominants, half-diminished for the minor ii chord, and the concept of tonicization within progressions.
