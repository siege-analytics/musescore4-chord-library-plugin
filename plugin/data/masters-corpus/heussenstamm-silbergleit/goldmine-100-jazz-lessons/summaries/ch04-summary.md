# Chapter 4 — Phrase Shape, Call-Response, Altered Sounds (Lessons 11-17)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 introduces two core improvisational methods: Constant Phrase Shape, which fixes a rhythmic figure and varies pitch within it to sharpen harmonic listening, and Changing Rhythmic Shape, which takes a row of selected tones and reshapes them rhythmically using tools like swung eighths, slides, and triplets. The chapter establishes that a musical idea is fundamentally a composite of tones and timing, governed by phrase shape as an improvisation mindset. It presents structural rules for Call and Response (requiring symmetrical form), the "skating" technique of moving blues scale material through chord changes, and a critical taxonomy of altered dominant decisions: minor ii-V progressions strongly favor altered dominants, while a common jazz device shifts a dominant from natural to altered before resolution to create urgency. The scalar mechanism for this natural-to-altered shift involves switching from Mixolydian to super Locrian (altered) scales, though the chapter identifies gaps in standard scalar theory where no single scale fully supplies required tones. A stylistic boundary rule warns against extreme blues bending and distortion in jazz contexts, introducing devices like "reverse swing" rhythms, while a prescriptive pedagogical stance counsels suppressing blues gestures to focus on acquiring jazz vocabulary through tonal and rhythmic choice.
