# Chapter 6 — Extensions, Bossa, Octaves, Chromatics (Lessons 26-33)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch06.md`._

Chapter 6 teaches advanced jazz guitar voicing and soloing strategies by mapping scale degrees to chord extensions, starting with deriving minor seventh and dominant chords from their parent scales (Dorian and Lydian dominant respectively). The signature improvisational moves include soloing over Dm7 using Am9 arpeggio from the 5th up, employing B minor pentatonic on Cmaj7#11 to avoid dissonance with the tonic and 5th, and substituting Dm7 ideas over G7sus4 to access upper extensions. Key techniques introduced include octave fingerings with muted strings for ergonomic vertical neck movement, chromatic enclosure where strong beats land on basic chord tones, and the Bossa nova fingerstyle method using thumb for bass and fingers for muted chording. The chapter establishes critical equivalences—that the Lydian scale generates maj7#11 via stacked thirds, that 7sus4 equals a minor seventh chord built on its 5th, and that Bb melodic minor unifies multiple chord types—while introducing load-bearing terminology like altered (super Locrian) scale, Lydian dominant, and the jazz-specific unresolved 7sus4 voicing that does not revert to the major 3rd.
