# Chapter 7 — Sequences, Substitutions, Harmonization (Lessons 34-42)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch07.md`._

Chapter 7 teaches sequential practice strategies for ii-V progressions and introduces hemiola as a rhythmic device for creating metric contrast within a fixed time signature, with specific mechanical rules for applying hemiola patterns to blues scale figures. The chapter covers tritone substitution as a three-step method for altering dominant chords before resolution, and presents harmonization techniques including the one-tone harmonization exercise where a single note is harmonized across an entire chord progression, and double-stop intervals (particularly 3rds and 4ths) as the physically natural choice on guitar. Transposition is anchored through fretboard signposts—the 12th fret octave and the 5th/4th fret unison points—while slash chord notation (e.g., D/G) designates the bass note rather than implying a voicing patch of the default shape. The chapter also introduces the half-step outside device as a brief excursion away from the chord zone, justified through tritone-substitution logic where a major-seventh chord a half-step above can replicate the resolution effect of a V-I progression. Load-bearing terminology includes hemiola, tritone substitute, slash chord, and the one-tone harmonization method, which together form a toolkit for constructing melodic lines and navigating harmonic space.
