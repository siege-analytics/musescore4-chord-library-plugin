# Chapter 8 — Quartals, Inner-Voice Motion, Scales and Comping Texture (Lessons 43-50)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch08.md`._

Chapter 8 introduces quartal harmony, inner-voice motion, and comping texture as tools for advancing beyond scalar thinking in jazz guitar. The physics of quartal playing on guitar—built on the perfect 4ths between most string pairs—enables outside patterns and requires finger-rolling technique, while rootless voicings and sus4 devices create melodic motion within chord changes. The chapter warns explicitly that scales are starting points, not methods: they free the player initially but trap them in boxes if mistaken for complete note-choice systems, a distinction the author marks as central to improvisation beyond mechanical chord-tone selection. Comping density emerges as a primary dynamic lever—sparse guide-tone textures, simple pentatonic ideas layered over complex changes, and the choice to lay out entirely all shape intensity and musical conversation. Three named swing-feel techniques (playing behind the beat, playing straight 8ths, accenting upbeats) and the distinction between feel variation and rhythmic variation equip the player to inflect the same written figure in multiple ways. Ballad feel is defined by a specific mix of even 8ths and triplets, paired with arpeggiated (rather than struck) chord attacks—a textural shift that respects the slower harmonic rhythm of ballad forms.
