# Chapter 10 — Scales, Modes, Soloing Strategies (Lessons 59-70)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch10.md`._

Chapter 10 teaches scale sequencing through fretboard fingering positions as the foundation for constructing jazz lines quickly, then pivots to modes—where a single set of notes (like the C major scale) creates different sounds depending on the background chord or bass note, exemplified by the popular Spanish sound of E Phrygian. The chapter introduces a strategic reduction method: grouping chords by key to apply single scales across related chord progressions, with a two-part rule for non-diatonic chords that appear briefly (ignore them or emphasize their chord tones as needed). A characteristic teaching move is the revelation of multiple pentatonic scales hidden within one major scale, and the concept of chord shapes as melodic skeletons that disguise scale patterns. The author emphasizes practical scale-shifting techniques—chromatic riff-shifting, the minor-third rule to convert major to minor, and interval-practice methods that treat scale transposition like barre chord shifts. The dominant seventh chord emerges as the central battleground for scale choice in jazz, where options proliferate once the b7th is introduced, leading to specialized tools like the bebop dominant scale (favored by Charlie Parker) and the diminished scale, both of which demand mastery while resisting overuse.
