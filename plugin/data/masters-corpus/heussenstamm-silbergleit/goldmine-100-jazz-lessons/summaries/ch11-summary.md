# Chapter 11 — Dissonance, Chromatic Riffs, Diminished and Altered Minor (Lessons 71-79)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Chapter 11 teaches dissonance and chromatic techniques built on the tritone substitution principle—chords on the b5 share tones with the original—and introduces the four tension tones (#5, b5, b9, #9) that distinguish altered dominants from the native scale. The chapter systematizes three key scales: the diminished scale (half-step/whole-step formula) with its three-fret equivalence covering four keys, the harmonic minor scale overlaid on diminished chords, and the melodic minor scale (major with flat 3) with its seventh-chord harmonization. The master's signature move is chromatic displacement—ascending or descending riffs moved by half-steps or whole steps up the fretboard—requiring careful resolution back to in-key targets to create both tension and coherence. Super Locrian mode, the seventh degree of melodic minor, captures all altered tones and connects diminished harmony to dominant seventh voicings without the perfect fifth. Throughout, the chapter emphasizes scale-over-chord equivalences: diminished scales work over their generated seventh chords, harmonic minor over diminished triads found within it, and the enharmonic relationship between G super Locrian and Ab melodic minor.
