# Chapter 12 — Neighbors, Pentatonic Mixing, Geometric and Interval Lines (Lessons 80-89)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch12.md`._

Chapter 12 teaches foundational soloing techniques built on neighbor notes, pentatonic mixing, and intervallic design. Half-step neighbors—notes one fret above or below any scale tone—form the basis for jazz improvisation (essential) and blues playing (use sparingly), while pentatonic mixing exploits five overlapping pentatonic positions from different keys available simultaneously on the fretboard. Geometric patterns and sweep picking establish aural internalization of shapes before theoretical analysis, with clean articulation and finger release ensuring clarity in string-skipping lines built on 6th intervals. The chapter introduces interval jumping with riffs (repeating lines at half-step, whole-step, or 3rd intervals), whole-tone harmony (only two mutually exclusive scales covering the entire system), and targeting through enclosure—arranging notes around a chosen destination to create direction and the bebop sound characteristic of jazz. A recurring master move is selective attention: jazz musicians need not play every chord change but can target chord tones across fast tempos, and when jamming in one key, nearby keys with only one-note difference (such as A, D, and E) remain compatible for free expression.
