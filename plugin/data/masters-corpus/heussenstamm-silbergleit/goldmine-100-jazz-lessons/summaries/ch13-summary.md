# Chapter 13 — Chord Systems, Voicings, and Standards Interpretation (Lessons 90-100)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch13.md`._

Chapter 13 systematizes chord mastery through three organizing principles: dual voicings (root on sixth or fifth string to minimize fretboard movement), movable shapes (transposable across all twelve keys when not using open strings), and formula-based construction (modifying intervals within the three chord families—major, minor, and dominant—to generate vocabulary). The author teaches intervallic formulas (major triad = 1-3-5; minor = 1-b3-5; dominant = 1-3-5-b7) and introduces partial voicings and rootless chords to expand harmonic possibility. Three cardinal techniques emerge: chord melody (melody note always on top), melody within the chord (picking notes inside a held shape), and Jazz 101 substitution (exchanging chords within the same family). The circle of fourths and five standard chord-change intervals (half step, minor third up/down, fourth, fifth) provide navigation rules, while the distinction between diatonic changes (chords within the key) and root-interval movements (4ths, 5ths, stepwise) enables both structured and free chord choice.
