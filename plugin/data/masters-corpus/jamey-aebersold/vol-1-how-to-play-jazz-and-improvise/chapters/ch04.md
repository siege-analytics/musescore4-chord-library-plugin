## Seventh chord definition

## Ninth chord definition

> You can even extend the chord to include the 9th tone of the scale. This is called the ninth chord
> (9th chord). It uses the root, 3rd, Sth, 7th, and 9th tones of the scale. Remember, the 9th is also
> referred to as the 2nd... the tones are the same, just an octave apart.

**Significance (p. 15).** Defines the ninth chord and the 9th=2nd enharmonic equivalence.

## Scale tone characterization

> You may be thinking of roots as "home-base", 3rd's and Sth’s as "family" and 7th and 9th's as
> more exciting tones you might meet on a week-end.

**Significance (p. 15).** Intuitive tonal hierarchy for hearing and prioritizing scale degrees.

## Swing eighth-note rule

> In order to make eighth-notes “swing” or imply swing, they must be played like an eighth-note

**Significance (p. 15).** Core swing subdivision rule.

## Even eighths (bossa/rock)

> When playing a bossa nova or rock tune you will want to straighten out the eighth-notes and play
> them more evenly. This is called even eighths.

**Significance (p. 15).** Defines even eighths and the genre rule for abandoning swing.

## Saxophone/trumpet fingering tip

> Saxes and trumpets, keep your fingers close to the
> keys and play smoothly.

## Phrase awareness in jazz

> most Jazz music (Western Art Music) is built in 2, 4, and 8 measure phrases.
> Knowing this will give you an inner sense of form that you can use the rest of your life.

**Significance (p. 17).** Structural rule about jazz phrasing.

## Swing feel vs even eighths in performance

> Play with a swing feel. Don't play even
>
> cighth-notes. Listen to a recording by Duke Ellington, Count Basie, Woody Herman, or Thad Jones-
> Mel Lewis. The feeling should be loose without dragging.

**Significance (p. 17).** Performance directive — loose but not dragging — with model recordings.
