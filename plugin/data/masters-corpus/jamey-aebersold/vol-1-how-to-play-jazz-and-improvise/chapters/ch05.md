## Scale-to-chord improv progression

> You will notice we started with each note of the scale in whole notes and ended up playing the
> scale to the ninth, and back down the chord tones in various smaller note values. This gives you a
> degree of confidence which enables you to move on to the next step....which isactual improvisation.

**Significance (p. 20).** Explains pedagogical sequence from scale whole notes to ninth-chord arpeggios.

## First improvisation approach

> Put the recording on the first track and try playing any rhythm you choose and play just notes
> found in the scale. You may find yourself playing whole notes interspersed with eighth-notes or
> rests. Experiment at this point with anything your mind can come up with.

**Significance (p. 20).** First improvisation instruction — constrain pitch, free rhythm.

## Wrong note philosophy

> There is no such thing as a wrong note . . . just poor choices!
> Listen to the rhythm section while you play. Let them help you keep your place.

**Significance (p. 20).** Reframes wrong notes as contextual choices.

## Prearranged rhythm technique

> If you find yourself losing the form (getting lost and not changing to the next scale at the proper
> time), try improvising with a prearranged two measure rhythm. Use the notes of the scale but
> adhere to this rhythm.

**Significance (p. 20).** Prearranged-rhythm as remedial scaffolding.

## Abandoning prearranged rhythm

> You will find that using a prearranged rhythm quickly gets boring, but it will help you to keep your
> place while moving around inside the scales. You can abandon the prearranged rhythm after you are
> able to keep your place. For those who feel the need for the prearranged rhythm, try changing the
> rhythm with each new scale. You would need three rhythms for the first track (one chorus).

**Significance (p. 20).** Explicit exit criterion for prearranged-rhythm scaffolding.

## Extend instrument range

> Experiment with various rhythms of your own, and try to extend the range of your instrument to
> include all of its playable tones (within reason, of course).

**Significance (p. 20).** Range exploration as explicit improvisational goal.

## Melodic direction — ascending/descending

> Don't limit yourself by beginning every phrase in the low register and then proceed upward
> (ascending motion). Utilize descending motion and use melodic lines that combine ascending and
> descending motion.

**Significance (p. 21).** Targets beginner ascending habit, prescribes mixed motion.

## Register variety rule

> Avoid limiting your ideas to the middle or the most comfortable register of your instrument.
> Nothing is more monotonous than listening to players who confine their playing to their most
> comfortable register and refuse to utilize the high, low, or unfamiliar registers. Be prepared to take
> chances and experiment with less-used limits of your instrument.

**Significance (p. 21).** Names beginner register limitation and prescribes corrective.

## Memorize scales for melodic freedom

> In order to have as much freedom of concept as possible, memorize the scales to be used. If
> you have the scales memorized and mastered, your mind is freer to concentrate on melodic
> development. Your imagination works best when you feel secure.

**Significance (p. 21).** Cognitive rationale for scale memorization.

## Dynamic contrast rule

> Vary your dynamics! Lack of dynamic contrast has a dulling effect on the listener and the
> player. Listen to the phrasing and dynamics of the jazz greats.

**Significance (p. 21).** Names consequence of ignoring dynamics with listening model remedy.

## Articulation variety rule

> Don't tongue (staccatto) every note, and don't slur (legato) every note. Use a variety of
> articulations. Listen to recorded solos of people who play your instrument. Interesting players have
> an assortment of articulations at their disposal.

**Significance (p. 21).** Defines articulation poles and prescribes variety.

## Audiation before playing

> Concentrate on hearing, mentally, each tone before you play it. This requires constant
> anticipation and awareness. It will help prepare you for more advanced improvisation, as well as
> create in you an inner sense of pitch. A sense of pitch will greatly stabilize intonation and is
> extremely important when playing notes that are separated by a large interval.

**Significance (p. 21).** Defines audiation as deliberate technique linked to intonation.

## Direction, tension, and silence

> Always try to make the notes you play have a sense of direction. Be aware of tension and
> release. (See page 43 on Melodic Development.) Remember, every note you play is part of a larger
> musical idea. If you can't think of what should come next in a solo, try using silence. After all,
> music is nothing more than a combination of sounds and silences.

**Significance (p. 21).** Introduces melodic architecture and offers silence as tool.

## Repetition as listener direction

> Use repetition when soloing. Repetition is like watching road signs.
> It helps hold the listener's attention and directs them to the next musical phrase or event.

**Significance (p. 21).** Repetition as structural listener orientation.
