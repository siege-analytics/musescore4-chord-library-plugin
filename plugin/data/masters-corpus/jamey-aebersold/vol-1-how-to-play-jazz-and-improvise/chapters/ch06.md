## Chord tones on downbeats

> It is extremely important to place (play) chord tones on the downbeat, especially beats one
> and three! When you do this your melodies will sound more natural and tend to flow. Always
> know the chord tones 1, 3, 5 and 7 of each scale. Use them as anchors when building a solo.

**Significance (p. 22).** Central harmonic rule — chord-tone anchoring on strong beats.

## Pre-improvisation checklist

> 1. Listen to the recorded track first while watching the chord progression.
>
> 2. Review the needed scales and chords by practicing several of the first 20 exercises.
> (You may want to do this without the recording, then with it).
>
> 3. Begin memorizing the scales by the number of flats or sharps or by the fingerings.
>
> 4. Begin memorizing the order of the scales. This is called learning the form of the song.

**Significance (p. 22).** Four-step prescribed preparation sequence.

## Extending range beyond one octave

> You should expand the scale past one octave - as soon as possible. In the first 20 exercises I
> confined you to the range of one octave with an occasional 9th; but when improvising, we need all
> the range we can get to lend variety to our solos and offer full expression of our mind.

**Significance (p. 22).** Methodological progression beyond initial one-octave constraint.

## Two-octave scale practice

> Work each of the three scales over two
> octaves, if your ability will permit. If not, work within the range over which you have control.

**Significance (p. 22).** Concrete two-octave goal adapted to student level.

## Mind–voice–instrument hierarchy

> The voice (singing) usually can approximate the pitches, rhythms, and nuances of what the mind
> hears better than the actual instrument (sax, trumpet, piano, etc.). Since the instrument you have
> chosen is a learned device, it is the least able to reproduce the musical thoughts of your mind. Thus,
>
> persons who are better equipped technically will come closer to playing the thoughts of their mind
> on their instrument.

**Significance (p. 23).** Mind→voice→instrument model justifying technical development.

## Keeping the mind free while singing

> Be careful not to begin singing (mentally or with your voice) only musical ideas you know you
> can play on your instrument. Keep your mind free at all times. Let it roam, soar, take chances and
> generally be free from the regimentation of practicing.

**Significance (p. 23).** Rule against self-censorship at the creative stage.

## Purpose of practice exercises

> The only reason to practice exercises is to
> gain freedom on your instrument so you can improvise spontaneously.

**Significance (p. 23).** States the book's overarching pedagogical philosophy.
