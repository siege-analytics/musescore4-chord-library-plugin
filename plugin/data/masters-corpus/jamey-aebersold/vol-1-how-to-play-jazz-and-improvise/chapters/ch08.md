## Beats 1 and 3 target notes

> Beats 1 and 3 seem to want roots, 3rd's, Sth's, 7th's and 9th's (b9's on dom.7th chords that resolve
> up a perfect fourth). I'm speaking of 4/4 time. In so doing, the listener can plainly hear the intended
> harmony. They can also anticipate where your melodic line is going, the contour, the shape of it.

**Significance (p. 28).** Foundational melodic construction rule.

## Target notes / guide tones defined

> Sometimes we call these notes Target
> Notes or Guide-Tones or Goal-Notes. Since the 3rd and 7th of scales/chords are the most
> important tones in any scale, placing them on beats 1 and 3 is vital.

**Significance (p. 28).** Defines terminology and elevates 3rd/7th as primary structural tones.

## Chord tone awareness analogy

> Knowing where chord tones are on your instrument is like knowing where the kitchen,
> bathroom, front-door, and telephone are.

**Significance (p. 28).** Frames chord tone knowledge as spatial fluency.

## Bebop scale definition (dominant 7th)

> The bebop scale contains one added tone to each of the four most used scales.
> Dominant 7th, C7=C D E F G A Bb B C (The underlined tone is the added tone.)
>
> This scale is often played descending and would look like this:
> C7=CBBbAGFEDC

**Significance (p. 28).** Defines the bebop scale and characteristic descending form.

## Added tone must land on upbeat

> Don't allow the B natural (added tone) to fall on a downbeat. The added tone must always come
> on the upbeat in order to give it the jazz sound we are used to hearing. This is also called the 7th
> scale.

**Significance (p. 28).** Central rhythmic rule for bebop scale usage.

## Phrase starting tones and chromaticism

> Good notes to begin/start a phrase with are the chord tones:1,3,5,and b7. When you begin a phrase
> with the 2nd, 4th, or 6th notes of the scale on a downbeat, you must use additional chromaticism
> somewhere in the phrase in order to make the B natural fall on the upbeat. 3rd's and 7th's like to fall
> on beats 1 and 3. This makes your phrases sound more natural.

**Significance (p. 28).** Rule for correcting phrase-start alignment with chromaticism.

## Bebop scales for major, minor, half-diminished

> There are also BEBOP scales to be used over major, minor and half-diminished as well as the
> dominant 7th listed above.
>
> MAJOR=C DEF G GFABC
> MINOR= C D EbDE FGA BbC
> HALF-DIMINISHED = C Db Eb F Gb G Ab Bb C

**Significance (p. 28).** Extends bebop scale concept across chord qualities.

## 8-tone scale places chord tones on beat

> Using the simple half-step chromaticism (which we are referring to as the bebop scale) allows
> your lines to have shape and contour which more closely resembles those of the jazz masters. Since
> the scale has 8 tones, it helps to naturally place the chord tones ON the beat rather than have them
> scattered all around.

**Significance (p. 28).** Mechanical reason bebop scale works — 8 tones align to 4/4.

## Dom 7th bebop scale as ii-chord substitute

> The dom.7th bebop scale can act as a substitute for the minor ii chord. Example: C7 bebop scale
> (C D EF GA Bb B C) could also be played over the G- chord and vice versa. The chords are
> interchangable over the scale. Often, while G- to C7 is being sounded the soloist will use the single
> bebop scale: CD E F GA Bb B Cor you could think of it as a G- bebop scale: GA BbBCDEF
> G. They're the same.

**Significance (p. 28).** Key harmonic substitution: one bebop scale covers both ii–V chords.

## Bebop scale as jazz language glue

> Make friends with scales, especially the bebop scale.
> It's the "glue" of the jazz language.
> Don't leave home without it!

**Significance (p. 28).** Chapter thesis: bebop scale as connective tissue of jazz vocabulary.
