## Minor pentatonic over dominant 7th

> Blues heads (melodies) are often made up of a single pentatonic scale, usually a minor pentatonic
> scale superimposed over a dominant 7th chord/scale.

**Significance (p. 32).** Foundational rule for blues melody construction.

## Pentatonic scale selection for minor chords

> Use the pentatonic scale
> (minor pentatonic) that corresponds to the root of the minor scale/chord. The first choice pentatonic
> scale for eight measures of F minor would be the F minor pentatonic scale.

**Significance (p. 32).** Primary rule for matching pentatonic to minor chord.

## Multiple pentatonics within one scale

> Remember, there are
> several pentatonic scales within each minor, major, and dominant 7th scale. Experiment with the
> various pentatonic scales and get the sound of each in your head.

**Significance (p. 32).** Multiple pentatonic subsets within parent scale.

## Lydian scale definition

> The scale on the third line contains a raised 4th (#4 = F#). This scale is the
> first cousin to a major scale and is called lydian; CD E F# G A BC. It's a beautiful sound and much
>
> used.

**Significance (p. 32).** Defines Lydian mode by raised 4th.

## Chromaticism defined

> Chromaticism means using half-step intervals. When listening to jazz solos or analyzing
> transcribed solos you will undoubtedly come across notes that are not in the scale or chord being
> sounded.

**Significance (p. 33).** Core definitional statement for chromaticism.

## Approach chord tones from half step below

> A good beginning exercise is to approach each chord tone (root, 3rd, 5th, and 7th) from a half
> step below.

**Significance (p. 33).** Entry-level chromatic approach exercise.
