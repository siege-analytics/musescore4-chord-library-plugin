## Related scales and modes

> It may be helpful for beginners to relate the dorian minor scale to their related major key which,
> of course, lies one whole-step down from the minor. C major = D minor (dorian) = G7 (dominant
> 7th or mixolydian). These three scales share the same key signature...no sharps and no flats and have
> identical fingerings.

**Significance (p. 47).** Core scale-relationship rule: three scales share one key signature.

## 12-scale reduction rule

> When thinking of scales in this related manner there are really only twelve scales to learn or
>
> twelve key signatures to memorize. The 36 scales on page 60/61 can be reduced to 12 scales or
> twelve finger patterns on your instrument (left side of pages only!) Look on the scales page and
> see if you can find the ones that are similar. Example: C, D- & G7 are all alike. A, B- & E7 are all
> alike.

**Significance (p. 46).** Method's central reduction: 36 scales → 12 finger patterns.

## Improvising: goal-directed phrasing

> Ultimately, players should know in advance where they want their line to go and, with proper
> usage, the various elements will help them best achieve their goal. The individual is the manipulator
> of all the elements. Listening and emulating can be the finest teacher.

**Significance (p. 47).** Rule: plan line destination before playing; listening as primary teacher.

## Articulation priority in jazz

> After note choice, articulation is THE most important element in playing jazz. Dont' over-
> look it. Don't make the mistake of saying to yourself, "well, I play right notes and don't get
> lost. Why work on articulation? "| Without good articulation you have no jazz voice. Strong
>
> tatement but true. Just listen, and HEAR the difference.

**Significance (p. 48).** Highest-priority rule: articulation ranks second only to note choice.

## Avoiding excessive tonguing

> One of the major stumbling blocks that needs to be turned into stepping stones is the use of too
> much tonguing; "tat tat tat tat" or "tut tut tut tut". Note: For keyboard, bass, guitar, and others,
> tonguing in this article can mean Attack or Emphasize. When you play several tongued notes in a
> row (one right after the other) the effect is a choppy feel. The music of the past forty to fifty years
> has been leaning toward a more relaxed, legato, smooth sound and flow.

**Significance (p. 48).** Defines the over-tonguing error with stylistic rationale.

## Articulation accent control goal

> You want to get to the point where you can instantly accent (heavy, medium, light, staccato,
> legato, breath, throat, stab, jab, etc.) any note or group of notes anytime you choose without
> disrupting the FLOW and FEEL of your melodic lines.

**Significance (p. 49).** Mastery benchmark: full on-demand accent control.

## Articulation exercise practice rules

> Play all of these exercises with a metronome. Begin slowly and gradually increase the tempo, but
> don't increase the tempo too rapidly. Make sure you are listening to yourself as you play.

**Significance (p. 49).** Procedural rules for articulation exercises.

## Automaticity of articulation

> The articulation has to become automatic before it will begin to sound natural. Don't rush or
> force the exercises. Eventually, make up your own exercises and move the accented notes around in
> the scale. Gradually broaden the scale to include two octaves and then move on to include your
> instrument's entire range! Begin with a fairly heavy accent, then medium, then light.

**Significance (p. 49).** Internalization progression: heavy→medium→light, automaticity before naturalness.

## Practice across all keys rule

> MOST IMPORTANT: Don't get stuck practicing only one major scale or the chromatic scale.
> Use (practice) these articulation exercises over ALL scales and chords in ALL keys. Remember, we
> improvise in all keys, not just the easy ones. Don't we?

**Significance (p. 49).** Explicit directive against drilling only comfortable keys.

## Articulation exercise: up-beat tonguing

> Tongue every other note on the up-beats.

**Significance (p. 49).** Primary instruction for the up-beat tonguing drill.

## Staccato: advanced players only

> staccato is okay for advanced players, but beginners need to learn the more common jazz
> articulations first. Save the staccato for marching band.

**Significance (p. 48).** Sequencing rule restricting staccato to advanced players.
