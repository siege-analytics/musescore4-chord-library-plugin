# Vol. 1: How to Play and Improvise Jazz — Statement of Outputs

## Overview

*How to Play and Improvise Jazz* (Jamey Aebersold Play-A-Long, Vol. 1) is a foundational method for jazz improvisation structured around what the book calls the "right brain–left brain" approach: the left brain masters the finite, learnable content of scales, chords, arpeggios, and chord-scale equivalence; the right brain deploys that content spontaneously, listening intently and responding to the moment. Every chapter closes the gap between these two faculties — drilling patterns until they become automatic, then releasing control so that musical impulse, not calculation, drives phrase-making. The book's five prerequisites — desire, listening, a practice method, a rhythm section, and self-esteem/discipline — establish from the outset that technique is necessary but never sufficient; the ear and the creative will are co-equal requirements.

The book's entire technical architecture rests on chord-scale equivalence: every chord symbol simultaneously names a vertical sonority and a horizontal scale, and every scale choice is a choice about where on the tension-release spectrum to operate. The Scale Syllabus that results from this framework organizes scale choices within each chord category by dissonance level — from consonant options at the top to increasingly tense ones below — placing the control of tension and release directly in the improviser's hands. Dorian mode is designated the universal minor scale, the 4th scale degree is consistently demoted to a passing tone in major and dominant contexts, and the Guide-Tones (3rd and 7th) are identified as the structurally primary degrees that determine chord quality and must be targeted on strong beats.

Underpinning every technical prescription is the mind–voice–instrument hierarchy: musical intention originates in the mind (audiation), is tested through the voice (sing the scale before playing it), and only then executes through the instrument. Transposition through all 12 keys is treated not as an advanced skill but as a baseline daily discipline, with the Circle of Fourths and the II–V7 pattern as the primary vehicles. The book's final prescription — "play what your mind hears" — is not a coda but a distillation of everything that preceded it: technique is in service of imagination, and imagination is developed through lifelong listening, singing, memorization, and the permission to begin now rather than wait for perfection.

## Systems

### Chord-Scale Equivalence System

The central system of this volume treats every chord symbol as simultaneously naming a chord (vertical sonority) and a scale (horizontal melodic resource), creating a unified taxonomy of harmonic-melodic materials organized by quality. The improviser does not choose between "playing the chord" and "playing the scale" — the two are equivalent, and the choice of *which* scale within a quality category determines where on the tension-release spectrum the phrase operates. Within each chord-scale category, options are ranked from consonant to dissonant; scale selection is therefore aesthetic decision-making, not just harmonic compliance. Traversal rules govern how the improviser moves between chord-scale members across a progression — through guide-tone voice motion, common-tone continuity, the ii–V bebop substitution, and chromatic approach — while modification rules govern how a given chord-scale member may be altered or replaced within its category.

**Members:**
- **Major Scale / Chord (C△)** — Stable, consonant quality; 4th degree treated as passing tone.
- **Dominant 7th Scale / Chord (C7)** — Unstable quality resolving up a perfect 4th; 4th degree avoided; seven scale options available from DOM.7th through Spanish/Jewish.
- **Dorian Minor Scale / Chord (C-)** — Universal minor quality; preferred over pure minor throughout the method.
- **Half-Diminished Scale / Chord (C∅)** — Used as the ii chord in minor ii–V progressions; bebop scale covers it jointly with the resolving dominant.
- **Minor with Major 7th (C-△)** — Minor chord with raised 7th; listed in the reduced notation system.
- **Altered Dominant +9 (C7+9 / Diminished Whole Tone)** — Root, ♭9, +9, 3rd, +4, +5, ♭7; maximum-tension dominant variant.
- **Altered Dominant ♭9 (C7♭9 / Diminished)** — Root, ♭9, +9, 3rd, +4, 5th, 6th, ♭7; symmetrical 8-tone scale.

**Traversal Rules:**

1. **Guide-Tone Voice Motion at Chord Changes** — When moving from one chord-scale member to the next, prioritize the 3rd and 7th as melodic targets on beats 1 and 3, exploiting their tendency to resolve by half-step across chord boundaries. `VoiceMotion` (Chapter 8 summary: "the 3rd and 7th of scales/chords are the most important tones," p. 28; Chapter 11 summary: "Improvise on the 3rd or 7th of each chord," p. 38; Chapter 16 summary: "The two most important notes in any scale are the 3rd and 7th," p. 54.)

2. **Common-Tone Continuity Across Scale Changes** — When moving between chord-scale members, identify and lean on tones shared by both scales to create smooth, connected melodic transitions. `VoiceMotion` (Chapter 3 summary: "These are called common tones" and "moving from one chord symbol to another and playing logical musical phrases in a connected, smooth manner," p. 9.)

3. **Dominant 7th Resolves Up a Perfect Fourth** — When traversing from a dominant 7th chord-scale member, expect and target resolution to the chord-scale member a perfect 4th above, with altered tones moving by half-step to their targets. `VoiceMotion` (Chapter 16 summary: "Dominants typically want to resolve to a chord up a perfect 4th" and "Those tones usually resolve by half-step to a scale or chord tone," p. 54.)

4. **ii–V Bebop Scale Substitution** — The dominant 7th bebop scale covers both the minor ii chord and the dominant V chord in a ii–V progression, allowing a single scale to traverse both members. `SubstitutionExpand` (Chapter 8 summary: "The dom.7th bebop scale can act as a substitute for the minor ii chord," p. 28.)

5. **Chromatic Approach to Chord Tones Across Boundaries** — Approach each chord tone of the arriving chord-scale member from a half step below, from enclosure (scale tone above + chromatic tone below), or descending from a whole step above. `VoiceMotion` (Chapter 10 summary: "approach each chord tone from a half step below" and "scale tone above each chord tone, and using the half step leading tone from below," p. 33; Chapter 11 summary: "Lead into the 3rd or 7th by half step," p. 39.)

6. **Circle of Fourths Root Movement** — The harmonic foundation of jazz is root movement by perfect fourth; internalize and practice all chord-scale traversals through this cycle across all 12 keys. `_pending:root-cycle-traversal` (Chapter 19 summary: "the harmonic movement of a perfect fourth is found everywhere in jazz" and "practice their scales, chords, patterns and B♭ licks through the Circle of Fourths," pp. 64–65.)

**Modification Rules:**

1. **Dissonance Escalation Within Chord-Scale Category** — Within any chord-scale quality category, the improviser may substitute a more dissonant scale option from the Scale Syllabus list, moving downward from consonant to tense to increase harmonic tension. `SubstitutionExpand` (Chapter 15 summary: "scales near the top of each category will sound mild or consonant and scale choices further down the list will become increasingly tense or dissonant" and "Music is made of tension and release," p. 52.)

2. **Minor Scale Preference Order (Dorian First)** — For any minor chord-scale member, substitute scale choices in the order: Dorian, Bebop, Melodic, Blues, Pentatonic; Pure Minor is the last resort. `SubstitutionExpand` (Chapter 15 summary: "Dorian, Bebop, Melodic, Blues, Pentatonic," p. 53; Chapter 2 summary: "Every minor scale employed on the record and in the musical examples is in the Dorian mode," p. 7.)

3. **Dominant 7th Scale Palette Expansion** — Over any dominant 7th chord-scale member, the player may substitute from seven available scales: DOM.7th, Bebop, Lydian Dominant, Whole-tone, Diminished, Diminished Whole-tone, and Spanish/Jewish. `SubstitutionExpand` (Chapter 16 summary: "BEBOP = C7 = CDEFGABbBC," "LYDIAN DOM. = C7#4," and "Experiment with these scales over the Cycle of Dominant 7th Chords," p. 54.)

4. **Fourth Degree Omission / Passing-Tone Demotion** — In major and dominant 7th chord-scale members, the 4th scale degree must not be emphasized; it is demoted to a passing tone between 3rd and 5th. `OmissionAllow` (Chapter 2 summary: "The 4th is usually treated as a passing tone between the 3rd and 5th," p. 6; Chapter 19 summary: "Be careful when playing the 4th note of major or dominant scales. Treat it as a passing tone," p. 65.)

5. **Color Tone Addition — Major and Dominant** — Over major and dominant 7th chord-scale members, the 6th, 7th, 9th, and #4 are available as color tones that add expressive tension within the tension-release arc. `ColorToneRequire` (Chapter 7 summary: "The pretty notes for major and dominant 7th chords/scales are the 6, 7, 9, and #4," p. 25.)

6. **Color Tone Addition — Minor** — Over minor chord-scale members (Dorian), the 4th, 6th, 7th, and 9th are available as color tones that create tension to be resolved. `ColorToneRequire` (Chapter 7 summary: "The pretty notes for minor chords/scales are 4, 6, 7, and 9th," p. 25.)

7. **Pentatonic Subset Extraction** — Any chord-scale member may be modified by extracting one of its embedded pentatonic subsets for use as a lighter, sparser texture; the 4th degree is typically omitted from major-scale pentatonic subsets. `OmissionAllow` (Chapter 9 summary: "We usually avoid using the 4th note of the major scale as part of a pentatonic scale" and "sprinkle it in amongst other scale sounds," p. 30.)

8. **Blues Scale Overlay on Minor and Dominant Members** — Over static minor chords (2+ measures) or dominant 7th members, the blues scale may be overlaid as an intentionally clashing colorant; it must be deployed as extracted licks, not scalar runs. `NCTHarmonization` (Chapter 12 summary: "The blues scale can also be used over minor chords when the minor chord is sounded for 2, 4, 8, or 16 measures" and "Jazz players usually play bits and pieces of the scale or make up licks," p. 41; Chapter 11 summary: "the notes of the blues scale often clash with the given harmony, but that is what makes it sound like the blues," p. 39; Chapter 12 summary: "alternate between the dorian minor and the blues scale," p. 41.)

9. **Bebop Chromatic Tone Insertion** — Any diatonic scale member may be modified by inserting a single chromatic passing tone to create an 8-tone bebop scale; the added tone must always fall on an upbeat. `NCTHarmonization` (Chapter 8 summary: "The bebop scale contains one added tone to each of the four most used scales," "The added tone must always come on the upbeat," and "Since the scale has 8 tones, it helps to naturally place the chord tones ON the beat," p. 28.)

10. **Twelve-Key Symmetry Reduction (36 → 12 Patterns)** — The 36 apparent scales in the syllabus reduce to 12 finger patterns because major, Dorian minor, and dominant 7th scales built on related roots share identical key signatures and fingerings. `SymmetryMovement` (Chapter 14 summary: "The 36 scales on page 60/61 can be reduced to 12 scales or twelve finger patterns on your instrument" and "C major = D minor (dorian) = G7 (dominant 7th or mixolydian)," pp. 46–47.)

### Tension-Release Melodic Architecture System

The book's governing aesthetic system treats an improvised solo as a single dramatic arc built from parameterized tension and release events. The improviser does not simply navigate chord changes; they shape an emotional trajectory across those changes by consciously manipulating a set of expressive parameters. The four-stage solo form — statement, development, climax, release — provides the macro-level architecture, while moment-to-moment adjustments to melodic direction, rhythmic density, beat placement, register, dynamics, and silence provide the micro-level texture. This system encodes the book's deepest aesthetic claim: jazz is not a series of correct notes but a controlled, communicative arc from tension to release, and the improviser's authority depends on deliberate mastery of every variable in that arc.

**Members:**
- **Consonant Anchor Zone (Root, 3rd, 5th)** — Stable harmonic resting points that begin and end phrases; produce release.
- **Tension Zone (Non-Chord Tones, Color Tones, Passing Tones)** — Scale degrees and chromatic tones that produce instability requiring resolution.
- **Rhythmic Density Level** — Note-value stratum; faster values build tension, longer values release it.
- **Beat Placement (Ahead / On / Behind)** — Temporal relationship to the pulse; each placement carries a distinct expressive character.
- **Dynamic Level (Soft → Loud)** — Volume as a tension parameter.
- **Register Zone (Low / Middle / High / Extreme)** — Pitch region; extreme registers add tension.
- **Melodic Direction (Ascending / Descending / Mixed)** — Ascending builds tension; descending releases it.
- **Silence / Rest** — Structural space counted as part of the phrase.

**Traversal Rules:**

1. **Solo Arc: Statement → Development → Climax → Release** — The improviser traverses the solo arc in four stages: state a theme or motif, develop it, build to a climax, then release; each climax in a multi-chorus solo must exceed the previous one. `_pending:solo-arc-sequencing` (Chapter 13 summary: "Statement of theme (motif), development of theme, climax, and release," "direct the flow of your melodic line toward a natural climax and then immediately taper off," and "make each successive climax more dramatic than the previous one," pp. 44–45.)

2. **Beat Placement as Expressive Character Selector** — Moving from on-the-beat to ahead-of-the-beat increases forward energy; moving behind-the-beat creates a laid-back feel; beginners must establish on-beat placement first. `_pending:rhythmic-feel-placement` (Chapter 13 summary: "Ahead of the beat (on top), On the beat (right on), Behind the beat (laying back)," "playing ahead of the beat usually implies excitement and forward motion," and "Beginning improvisors should learn to play on the beat," pp. 43–44.)

3. **Phrase Resolution Targets Strong Beats** — Tension phrases resolve on beats 1 or 3; this rhythmic landing rule governs how the soloist exits a tension zone and arrives at a consonant anchor. `VoiceMotion` (Chapter 13 summary: "natural to end on beats one or three, in 4/4 time," p. 43; Chapter 6 summary: "place (play) chord tones on the downbeat, especially beats one and three," p. 22.)

**Modification Rules:**

1. **Tension Parameter Manipulation (Raise / Lower)** — At any moment in the solo, the improviser raises tension by applying elements from the tension list (ascending lines, wide intervals, dissonant harmony, dense rhythm, extreme register, loud dynamics, repetition) or lowers it by applying release elements (descending lines, chord tones, consonant harmony, silence, long note values). `_pending:tension-release-parameter-control` (Chapter 13 summary: "ELEMENTS WHICH PRODUCE TENSION ... ELEMENTS WHICH PRODUCE RELEASE" and "tension can be achieved through the manipulation of volume, direction of the melodic line, range, intervals," pp. 44–45.)

2. **Rhythmic Density Shift for Tension/Release** — The improviser modifies the current tension level by shifting note-value density: eighth and sixteenth notes build tension; whole and half notes with rests create relaxation. `_pending:rhythmic-density-control` (Chapter 7 summary: "8th and 16th notes build tension. Rests, whole and half-notes create relaxation," p. 25.)

3. **Silence as Structural Release Element** — Silence is not absence but a counted structural component of the phrase, deployable as a release tool and listener orientation device. `_pending:silence-as-release` (Chapter 5 summary: "If you can't think of what should come next in a solo, try using silence," p. 21; Chapter 19 summary: "rests count as part of your phrase," p. 65.)

4. **Tension Resolution Method Selection** — A tension zone may resolve melodically (to a consonant chord tone) or by artificial means: abrupt dynamic change, tempo change, key change, or use of rests. `_pending:tension-resolution-routing` (Chapter 13 summary: "resolve naturally - melodically (to one of the consonant tones), or by artificial means," p. 43.)

### Twelve-Key Memorization Practice System

This system governs how the student internalizes any scale, pattern, or progression across all 12 keys. It is not a performance system but a practice discipline whose endpoint is auditory anticipation: the state where the mind hears the progression before the fingers move. The members are the ordered rungs of a 12-step memorization ladder, progressing from a single root note through increasingly complex intervallic and harmonic patterns to broken thirds. Traversal rules specify how the student advances through the rungs within a single key before transposing to the next, and which root sequences to use for comprehensive 12-key coverage. Modification rules govern ear-first internalization (sing before playing) and the adjustment of entry note value based on experience level. The seven-step scale practice algorithm from Chapter 2 functions as a compressed version of this ladder for initial scale introduction.

**Members (in sequence):**
- **Step 1 — Root / Tonic Note**
- **Step 2 — First Two Notes**
- **Step 3 — First Three Notes**
- **Step 4 — First Five Notes**
- **Step 5 — Triad (1, 3, 5)**
- **Step 6 — Seventh Chord (1, 3, 5, 7)**
- **Step 7 — Ninth Chord (1, 3, 5, 7, 9)**
- **Step 8 — Full Scale Up and Down**
- **Step 9 — Sixth Chord (1, 3, 5, 6)**
- **Step 10 — Scale Up to 9th, Back Down Chord Tones**
- **Step 11 — 9th Chord Up, Scale Down**
- **Step 12 — Broken Thirds**

**Traversal Rules:**

1. **Sequential 12-Step Rung Progression** — The student traverses the memorization ladder one rung at a time within each key, completing all 12 steps before declaring mastery; by step 12 the mind should be hearing the progression in advance. `_pending:sequential-rung-traversal` (Chapter 7 summary: the complete 12-step procedure culminating in "Play scale in broken thirds up and down" and "By the twelfth chorus your mind will be HEARING the chord/scale progression in advance," p. 26.)

2. **Transposition Through All 12 Keys via Root Sequences** — After mastering a pattern in one key, transpose it through all 12 keys using one of six prescribed root sequences (chromatic, Circle of Fourths, and others) to guarantee complete coverage. `SymmetryMovement` (Chapter 18 summary: "practice the above exercises, using as a guide any of the following root sequences," p. 62; Chapter 3 summary: "make sure you have mastered the pattern or lick in one key before moving on to the next key," p. 9; Chapter 20 summary: "memorize one example and then play it through all 12 keys," p. 68.)

3. **Chromatic (Half-Step) Transposition Drill** — Patterns may also be traversed chromatically — key by key in half-steps — as a combined ear training and finger coordination exercise. `SymmetryMovement` (Chapter 3 summary: "move (practice) patterns up and down my instrument chromatically (in half-steps)," p. 9.)

**Modification Rules:**

1. **Ear-First Internalization (Sing Before Play)** — At any rung of the memorization ladder, the student must first internalize the material aurally — singing the scale or pattern — before executing it on the instrument. `_pending:ear-first-internalization` (Chapter 3 summary: "memorize the SOUND of the scales and chords," p. 12; Chapter 15 summary: "try singing the scale with your voice. Improvise with your voice over the scale you are learning and then play on your instrument what your voice sang," p. 52; Chapter 18 summary: "Use it to sing with then transfer the music to your instrument," p. 60.)

2. **Note-Value Entry Point Adjustment by Experience Level** — The student modifies the rhythmic density of practice at each rung based on their experience: beginners start with whole notes, advancing players with quarter or eighth notes. `DensityFloor` (Chapter 2 summary: "A beginner may want to start with whole notes," p. 8.)

3. **Seven-Step Scale Practice Algorithm** — Within a single key, the student applies the seven-step practice sequence as a compressed entry into the full 12-step procedure for initial scale introduction. `_pending:sequential-rung-traversal` (Chapter 2 summary: the seven-step method progressing from "Play the scale from root to the 9th and back down slowly, slurred, several times," p. 7.)
