# Chapter 8 — The Bebop Scale & Ear Training

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch08.md`._

Beats 1 and 3 should land on chord tones—roots, thirds, fifths, sevenths, and ninths—so listeners hear the intended harmony and anticipate the melodic direction, a foundational rule formalized as targeting "Guide-Tones" or "Goal-Notes" (with thirds and sevenths being structurally primary). The bebop scale adds a single chromatic tone to the four most-used scales, creating an 8-tone pattern that naturally aligns chord tones to strong beats in 4/4 time; the added tone must always fall on the upbeat to preserve jazz language, and this principle extends across dominant 7th, major, minor, and half-diminished chord qualities. A key substitution technique treats the dominant 7th bebop scale as interchangeable with the minor ii chord it resolves to, allowing a single scale to cover both chords in a ii–V progression. The bebop scale serves as the connective tissue of jazz vocabulary, making it essential for developing the spatial fluency of chord-tone awareness across the fingerboard.
