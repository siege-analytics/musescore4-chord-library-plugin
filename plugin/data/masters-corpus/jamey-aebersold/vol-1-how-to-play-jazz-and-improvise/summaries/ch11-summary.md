# Chapter 11 — Playing The Blues

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Chapter 11 teaches the foundational techniques of blues improvisation, beginning with singing as a direct pathway to authentic musical voice, then progressing through structured methods like practicing on 2–3 notes to internalize rhythmic feel, and mastering the 12-bar blues harmonic structure (basic three-chord progressions and Charlie Parker's descending "Bird Blues" variation). The chapter emphasizes guide tones—particularly the 3rd and 7th of each chord, approached by half-step—as the primary tool for tracking harmonic movement, while the blues scale itself is presented as intentionally clashing with the underlying harmony to create the characteristic blues sound. Across these techniques, the master's characteristic moves include the insistence on ear training before technical completeness (learning roots, then partial scales, then full scales in stages), the practice of mental rehearsal away from the instrument, and the transcription-and-imitation method rooted in jazz tradition, all converging on a single load-bearing principle: leading tones and guide-tone improvisation rule blues playing.
