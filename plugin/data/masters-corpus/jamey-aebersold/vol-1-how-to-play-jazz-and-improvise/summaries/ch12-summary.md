# Chapter 12 — Seventh Chords & Time And Feeling

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch12.md`._

The blues scale (root, ♭3, 4, ♯4, 5, ♭7) conveys a funky, down-home, bluesy sound and works over static minor chords lasting 2+ measures, often alternated with dorian minor for variety—though overuse should be avoided and jazz players typically extract licks rather than play it as scalar runs. Seventh chords layer a third atop triads, creating four diatonic types in minor (minor 7th, major 7th, dominant 7th, half-diminished 7th) with a fixed sequence when built on each degree of the dorian scale. The chapter's load-bearing terminology includes the blues scale formula, the four seventh-chord qualities, and positional voice names (root, third, fifth, seventh), with the core principle that all diatonic sevenths share tones with the parent minor scale and can function as both horizontal (melodic) and vertical (harmonic) material during improvisation over the tonic minor.
