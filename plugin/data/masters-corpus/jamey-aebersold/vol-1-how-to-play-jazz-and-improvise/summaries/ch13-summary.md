# Chapter 13 — Tension And Release; Related Scales And Modes

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch13.md`._

Chapter 13 teaches the foundational principles of melodic tension and release in jazz improvisation, establishing that the root, third, and fifth are the consonant anchor tones while other scale tones create tension that must resolve either naturally (to consonant tones) or through expressive means like dynamic or tempo changes. The master's characteristic approach is systematic and architecturally comprehensive: seventh chords are practiced as scale-derived sets, phrases resolve on strong beats, and note placement relative to the pulse (ahead, on, or behind) creates distinct expressive characters—with beginners anchored to the beat before developing personal timing feel. The chapter introduces load-bearing terminology including the three-placement system (ahead/on/behind the beat), the dragging/rushing distinction, and the four-stage compositional sequence (statement, development, climax, release), while providing paired reference lists of tension-producing elements (ascending lines, wide intervals, dissonant harmony, repetition) and release elements (descending lines, chord tones, consonant harmony, silence) that codify how to shape solo architecture as a single dramatic arc building to climax then tapering off.
