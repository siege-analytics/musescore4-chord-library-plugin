# Chapter 14 — Points To Keep In Mind & Articulations

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch14.md`._

This chapter establishes articulation as the second most critical element in jazz playing after note choice, and provides a systematic approach to developing it. The master's characteristic move is the reduction principle—just as 36 scales compress to 12 finger patterns, articulation mastery follows a progression from heavy to medium to light accents, practiced with metronome discipline across all keys rather than comfortable ones alone. The chapter introduces the core terminology of articulation types (heavy, medium, light, staccato, legato, breath, throat, stab, jab) and establishes that the foundational error is over-tonguing—the "tat tat tat" choppy feel that contradicts modern jazz's legato aesthetic. Key load-bearing concepts include the priority rule (articulation second only to note choice), the automaticity progression (exercises must become automatic before sounding natural), and the deliberate restriction of staccato to advanced players. The chapter's directive is unambiguous: develop on-demand accent control across your instrument's entire range and all keys, not just the easy ones.
