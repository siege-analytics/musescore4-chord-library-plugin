## Diatonic 7th chord substitution

> Take, for example, an Fmaj7 chord. When improvising over this chord you
> can use an Fmaj7 arpeggio. But you can also look for arpeggios that have
> notes in common with Fmaj7. The most common candidate is the one found
> on the 3rd (A) — Am7. Fmaj7 and Am7 have three notes in common. The
> Am7 arpeggio contains the same notes as Fmaj7 apart from the root, F.
> Mixing these arpeggios when improvising gives you more options for
> creating musical colours.

**Significance (p. 14).** Establishes the book's central substitution principle — choosing arpeggios by shared tones rather than by the root chord — which underpins every lick example that follows.

## Upper-structure arpeggios over G7

> Jazz musicians will often play a new arpeggio from any note in the
> underlying chord. For example, if the underlying chord is G7 (which contains
> the notes G, B, D and F) we will play arpeggios from the third (B) fifth (D) or
> even the seventh (F).

**Significance (p. 14).** The key rule governing all upper-structure arpeggio choices throughout the chapter, defining when and why a musician selects a substitute arpeggio.

## Arpeggio-on-the-3rd rule

> As you can see, playing a m7b5 chord on the 3rd of a dominant 7 chord
> causes us to avoid playing the root (G) and adds the rich-sounding 9th (A).
> Playing an arpeggio on the 3rd of the chord is one of the most common uses
> of arpeggios in jazz. It sounds so good that many musicians always use it
> instead of the original arpeggio.

**Significance (p. 15).** Codifies the single most important substitution rule in the chapter — m7b5 on the 3rd of a dominant — and explicitly elevates it above other options.

## Higher-chord-tone arpeggio caution

> As you move up the chord and play arpeggios from the higher notes (5th and
> 7th), you include fewer notes from the original chord, so you need to be a bit
> more careful.

**Significance (p. 15).** A direct practice rule warning that 5th- and 7th-rooted substitutions carry more harmonic risk, calibrating how boldly the technique should be applied.

## Coltrane 1-2-3-5 pattern

> Coltrane mastered the approach of using four-note groupings to spell out
> chords as a way of navigating bars that contain two chords, played at a fast
> tempo. He used a 1 2 3 5 pattern (the 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 5th degrees of the
> scale) and varied the order of the sequence to create different melodies. Here
> I’m playing it in reverse over the G7 chord: 5 (D), 3 (B), 2 (A) and 1 (G).
> Coltrane patterns are a useful way to break up scalic lines and create interest
> while playing diatonically.

**Significance (p. 17).** Defines the Coltrane pattern concept with its exact interval formula and demonstrates both forward and reverse application, making it actionable.

## Shell voicing definition

> A shell voicing is a chord voicing that use only the root, 3rd and 7th intervals.
> Jazz musicians often use just two notes to define the sound of a chord, such
> as root and 3rd, root and 7th, or 3rd and 7th without the root. Playing lines
> like this with no exotic ingredients forces us to dig deeper to create a strong
> melody.

**Significance (p. 19).** Defines shell voicings precisely and gives the pedagogical reason for using them in lines.

## Quartal harmony definition

> Traditionally, chords are formed by stacking intervals of a 3rd. For example,
> the notes C, E, G and B form Cmaj7. But it is also possible to stack chords in
> Ath intervals e.g. C, F, B and E. Stacking notes in 4ths is known
> as quartal harmony and was popularised in jazz by Miles Davis during his
> modal period.

**Significance (p. 19).** The chapter's definition of quartal harmony, establishing the concept before it appears as both a melodic and voicing device.
