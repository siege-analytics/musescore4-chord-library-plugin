## bebop melodic counterpoint rule

> One of the melodic counterpoint rules commonly used in bebop is that a large
> interval in one direction is best resolved with a stepwise motion in the
> opposite direction.

**Significance (p. 46).** Named compositional rule that recurs across multiple example lines.

## #9 as chromatic passing note over V7

> The dominant 7th line in this example uses a common “trick” — which is to
> add in the #9 as well as the b9. In this instance the #9 is an Eb, which sounds
> great over the C7 chord. It functions a bit like a chromatic passing note, as it
> leads nicely to E — the 3rd of C7.

**Significance (p. 47).** Defines a reusable dominant-line device — combining b9 and #9 — and explains voice-leading logic.

## motif-based vs target-note approach

> Notice that the transition from Gm7 to C7 is accomplished with a motif. The
> descending Bbmaj7 arpeggio motif is repeated using the descending E
> diminished arpeggio. This is a different approach to moving from one chord
> to the next by targeting chord tones. It’s good to have both motif-based and
> target note ideas in your arsenal of lines.

**Significance (p. 48).** Two distinct compositional strategies for navigating chord changes.

## 5th of II chord targets 3rd of V

> Playing an arpeggio from the 5th of the II chord is a fantastic way to target
> the third of the V chord that follows it. To the ears, it sounds like a suspended
> chord, but without moving very far from the key.

**Significance (p. 49).** Transferable rule for II–V voice-leading.

## triads in 1-3-5 sequence for variation

> note, using the same pattern. The effect is to create a repeating motif.

**Significance (p. 49).** Reordering triad tones and quartal arpeggios as a motivic technique.
