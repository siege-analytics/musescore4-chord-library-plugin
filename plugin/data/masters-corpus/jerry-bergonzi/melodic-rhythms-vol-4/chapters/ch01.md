## rhythmic variety vs. density

> Needless to say, using different rhythms in every bar is more sophisticated but it is not necessarily more effective. This exercise improves your awareness of which rhythms you are using as well as your awareness of where you are in the bar. More notes per bar is not better than a few, just different.

**Significance (p. 7).** This is the method's foundational rule about note density: sophistication and effectiveness are not the same, and the goal is awareness rather than complexity.

## disconnected note groupings

> Another idea to consider is that your note groupings don’t necessarily have to be connected. In this
> following example I am thinking of three note groupings although the listener wouldn’t be able to
> tell that because the groupings are overlapped. The point being made here is that practicing in this
> way can lead you to new or different rhythmic ideas. How I’m thinking about these groupings of
> notes will effect the articulation of the lines.

**Significance (p. 10).** This rule that internal mental grouping affects articulation even when invisible to the listener is a core pedagogical principle linking thought process to physical execution.
