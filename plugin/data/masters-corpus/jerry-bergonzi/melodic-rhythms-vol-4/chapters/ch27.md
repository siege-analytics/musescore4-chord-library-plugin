## Less Notes / Breathing Room

> Very often, less is more. Playing less notes at times allows the music to breath more. It also gives
> the rhythm section space for a little interaction and dialogue. Try playing solos on any of the tunes
> using only half notes, quarter notes, and dotted quarters. How rigidly you interpret this is entirely
>
> up to you. Sometimes a little limitation can be a great teacher.

**Significance (p. 110).** Rhythmic restraint via limited note values.

## Accents as Framework

> Try taking any tune and playing just accents as if you were the saxophone or trumpet section in a
> big band. After playing through several choruses, slowly fill in around the accents.

**Significance (p. 110).** Two-stage exercise from accents to fills.

## Feeling Time in 1, 2, or 4

> To enhance your flexibility with the time it is great practice to play tunes with different time feel-
> ings in mind. Play a tune first thinking and feeling the tune in four, feel and think four beats to the
> measure. Next play that tune in two, feeling and thinking of the tune in half time (feeling beats
> one and three}. Notice that your ideas seem to take a different shape and that your feeling of the
> tune can be more relaxed.

**Significance (p. 111).** Shifting metric subdivision internally while tempo stays constant.

## Fast Tempo in Two or One

> Fast tempo tunes are easier to play when you are feeling the tune in two. Sometimes to get a more j
> lyrical feel on a tune, or even a ballad feel you can think in one, one beat per measure. If the tune is
>
> around 400, of course thinking in one is easier.

**Significance (p. 111).** Collapse felt pulse to two or one at fast tempos.

## Escaping Eighth-Note Trap

> But if you want to get out of that zone for contrast try feeling the music in two
> or one. Try even perceiving a fast tune as a ballad.

**Significance (p. 111).** Cognitive remedy for eighth-note trap.

## Metronome on Off-Beats

> Try playing in back of the time or on top of the time. Try using the metronume on 2 and 4, or land
> 3, or just 3, or just 4, or on the and of 2 and 4:

**Significance (p. 111).** Specific metronome placement variations.

## Internalizing the Rhythm Section

> Also, practice without using a metronome. Internalize the rhythm section. Try to meditate on a
> tempo. Pick a tempo and try to just hear quarter notes, then add triplets or whatever the appropri-
> ate rhythms are that you want to hear. Notice that when you become tranquil inside you then have
> a better perception of the time. Relax, above all, relax!

**Significance (p. 112).** Mental/meditative practice routine; relaxation improves time perception.
