# Chapter 3 — 7-3 RESOLUTION

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 introduces **digital patterns** (David Baker's term) — 4-8 note cells structured by scale-degree number (e.g. 1-2-3-5 over C major gives C-D-E-G) — and **scalar patterns** (longer, scale-based, often "non-terminal" or "perpetual motion" runs). A master inventory of common 4- and 8-note cells is given, with generative rules for retrograde, fifth-rooting, and chord-quality alteration (1-3-5-3 to 1-b3-5-b3 for minor). The chapter establishes the **rhythmic-level rule**: a cell occupies one steady rhythmic level (most often eighth notes), giving a direct duration mapping — a 4-note cell covers two beats, an 8-note cell covers four. Coker draws the diagnostic distinction that digital patterns favor fundamental chord tones while scalar patterns treat all scale degrees as equal. The chapter's quantitative spine is a Coltrane "Giant Steps" analysis showing digital patterns at roughly 35–40% density across the solo, with the remaining 60–65% largely change-running or pattern-adjacent material — load-bearing empirical evidence that pattern study is non-optional, and that even Coltrane practiced these patterns deliberately before applying them to tunes.
