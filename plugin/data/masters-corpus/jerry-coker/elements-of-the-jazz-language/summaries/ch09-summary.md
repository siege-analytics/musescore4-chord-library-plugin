# Chapter 9 — SEQUENCE

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 introduces **harmonic generalization** — using a single scale to accommodate two or more chords of a progression — and ranks the strategies from safest to most advanced. The simplest case is the tonic major scale over II-V-I in major (the scales of D dorian and G mixolydian are both C major reindexed), producing no "wrong" notes but possibly weak chord-tone emphasis. Pentatonic generalization is even safer: a single C pentatonic covers ten common chords (C major, F major, Bb maj+4, A-7, D-7, G-7, C7, G7sus, D7sus, Gb7 alt) with no collision tones at all. The blues scale generalizes across blues progressions and, after Parker's example, across the last four measures of "Rhythm Changes" A sections — even with 4-8 underlying chords. The chapter then handles collision-tone cases: harmonic minor over a minor II-V-I works because such progressions are brief, the aggregate effect is minor, and players instinctively avoid the collision tones; an analogous diminished-scale generalization covers just the minor II and V. Coker reframes the device as a deliberate compositional option (not a cop-out), warns that play-alongs play against the grain (a live rhythm section would adapt), and anchors the rules to concrete tunes (Corea's "500 Miles High," Shorter's "Speak No Evil").
