# Chapter 10 — CESH

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch10.md`._

Chapter 10 introduces **enclosure** — a melodic device that approaches an object note from both the upper leading tone (a half-step above) and the lower leading tone (a half-step below), formalized as the three-note ordering UPPER-LOWER-OBJECT. Coker carefully distinguishes canonical enclosures from near-enclosure approximations that analysts will frequently encounter in transcribed solos, and warns that real enclosures are routinely decorated or embellished, extending past three notes. The chapter places enclosure into the book's broader claim about element combination: jazz-language devices are quickly successive, overlapping, and interlocking, illustrated by an example combining six elements in a two-measure phrase. Quantitative usage data (Rollins 5x in "Airegin," Clifford Brown 35 in four choruses of "Confirmation," Clark Terry 17 in seven blues choruses) sets the normative density target, and Baker's HOW TO PLAY BEBOP is again named as the authoritative supplementary source. The chapter pivots into **sequence** — a melodic fragment immediately followed by one or more variations — and elevates sequence above all other elements as the syntactic layer of jazz, defining it as the level at which the language becomes communicative "sentences" rather than "letters and words." Sequences may be melodic, rhythmic, harmonic, or interval-based, and may be transparent or heavily disguised while still functioning communicatively.
