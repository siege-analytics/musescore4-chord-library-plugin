# Chapter 11 — QUOTES

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Chapter 11 completes **sequence** practice and introduces **CESH** (Contrapuntal Elaboration of Static Harmony). For sequences, Coker states the non-negotiable prerequisite — the ability to play any motive in any key with any chord-type — and prescribes a methodical regimen: play tune melodies in all 12 keys, learn to slightly alter motives to fit different chord-types, and practice a single motive throughout complete progressions while varying rhythms and adding or deleting notes. A taxonomy of sequence transformations names three modes (transposition, common-tone stasis under different chords, rhythmic alteration), with Parker exemplifying anchored-pitch sequences with metric displacement and J.J. Johnson showing that even a two-note cell can sequence. The CESH section defines the device etymologically (static harmony = one chord remains; contrapuntal elaboration = one chord member is in motion), enumerates its four varieties (minor and major, each with either root or fifth in motion), and identifies minor-with-root-in-motion as the most common and the focus for study. Coker warns students that CESH appears in incomplete or heavily embellished forms in real solos and pivots the pedagogy by anchoring CESH to familiar standard-repertoire melodies. The chapter closes with a directed listening canon for sequence-rich players (J.J. Johnson, Joe Henderson, Kenny Dorham, Horace Silver, Randy Brecker, Wes Montgomery, Bill Evans).
