# Chapter 13 — "GONE BUT NOT FORGOTTEN" LICK

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch13.md`._

Chapter 13 closes CESH and introduces **quotes**. Coker frames CESH as a harmonic element with no canonical melodic realization — students learn shared melodic tendencies from solo examples but must also develop their own from the included starter list of CESH patterns to practice in all keys. Quotes are defined as phrases taken from a known tune, deployed when the harmonic setting (chord or progression cell) of the improvised tune matches that of the source. Coker distinguishes two cognitive types: the deliberate humorous interjection (usually appreciated because the listener knows the tune) and the accidental/emergent quote that arises when an improviser stumbles onto a familiar opening interval and decides to complete the allusion. Horace Silver exemplifies the extreme case — Silver works quotes not only into his solos but into his compositions — and Coker warns analysts that partial or implied quotes carry irreducible interpretive ambiguity (even interviewing the player rarely recovers intention). The chapter completes its taxonomy by naming two additional quote varieties: quotes from the melody of the tune being played at the moment, and quotes from well-known recorded solos by influential jazz artists.
