# Chapter 14 — OTHER CONSIDERATIONS

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch14.md`._

Chapter 14 makes the chapter's central pedagogical claim about **quotes**: of all the elements taken up in the book, quotes are among the hardest to learn — not because finding harmonic settings or transposing the phrase is difficult, but because the prerequisite is broad tune knowledge. Coker turns this into a professional consequence: contractors hiring young players complain about a deplorable lack of tune repertoire, and such players get hired once but never again. He operationalizes "broad repertoire" by cataloguing the cross-genre reach of bebop masters — Gillespie and Hubbard quoting Bizet, Silver quoting a Chopin Prelude, Parker quoting the standard "Honey," Bill Evans quoting "March of the Grenadiers" — alongside extensive quotation of jazz originals and revered recorded solos. The chapter thus shifts the burden of quote-study from technique to repertoire-acquisition, framing tune knowledge as both an aesthetic and career requirement.
