# Chapter 16 — APPENDIX A - CHORD PROGRESSIONS FOR ALL PLAY-ALONG TRACKS

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch16.md`._

Chapter 16 covers **other considerations** — bar-line shifts, side-slipping, and errors — plus the framing for solo analysis. **Bar-line shifts** are reframed as legitimate phenomena (not novice errors); they arise from two accidental causes (harmonic generalization across a II-V or a momentary pause leading to a "better late than never" entry) or as deliberate tension devices, exemplified by Cannonball Adderley's intentional half-step displacement of the bridge of "So What." The analyst's procedural rule is to inspect chords both before and after a suspected error before judging. **Side-slipping** and **outside playing** are defined as the same deliberate out-of-key tension device, and Coker provides the twelve-minus-seven arithmetic: with seven scale tones accommodating most chords, five "wrong" notes remain, to be used in convincing groups or sparingly with smooth delivery. **Errors** are defined residually — what cannot be reconciled to any named chromatic device — with two observable cues: inexplicable space after a wrong note (the player's natural balk) and incorrect harmonic placement of a known phrase like the Cry Me A River lick. The chapter ends with the book's framing claim that the catalogued elements are connective tissue — what remains in a master's solo after tagging all elements is often the most original material and must be studied with equal care. A well-constructed solo should exhibit a significant subset of catalogued elements, and individual style emerges from how each player emphasizes, omits, or transforms them.
