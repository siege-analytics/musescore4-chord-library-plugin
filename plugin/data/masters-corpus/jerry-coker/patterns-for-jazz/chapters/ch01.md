## Pattern practice rationale

> should first absorb the foundations for patterns, such as scales, modes, simple chords, and four basic
> kinds of chord movement: (1) cycle of fifths; (2) chromatic; (3) stepwise; and (4) in minor thirds.

**Significance (p. 3).** This passage defines the four foundational chord movement types that organize the entire book's progression of exercises.

## Chord symbol context rule

> The authors feel that the practice of patterns has little value unless the student understands
> what musical situations befit the pattern. Used in the wrong place or the wrong key, the best patterns
> will fail, even in free-form jazz. We have therefore placed accompanying chord or scale symbols
> above each pattern. The observation of that symbol while practicing, then, becomes crucial to an
> understanding of how the pattern is used.

**Significance (p. 3).** This establishes the core rule for how every pattern in the book must be practiced — always with awareness of the governing chord or scale symbol.

## Eighth-note rhythmic default

> Most of the patterns contained herein are presented in eighth notes (the rhythmic level of most
> jazz improvisation), in a continuing fashion, without rhythmic variation, and without rhythmic
> phrase-endings.

**Significance (p. 3).** This defines the rhythmic baseline for all written patterns and explains why the notation looks metrically uniform throughout the book.

## Applying patterns to improvisation

> When the practiced patterns are applied to an impro-
> visation, it is expected that the rhythms would be loosened, so that the idea takes on a more lyrical.
> natural, and less mechanical feeling.

**Significance (p. 3).** This gives the key performance rule: learned patterns must be rhythmically freed when used in actual improvisation to avoid sounding mechanical.

## Full instrument range extension

> It would be advisable to extend all patterns to the full range of your instrument,
> rather than to stop with perhaps only one octave, as it may appear in the written example.

**Significance (p. 4).** This is an explicit practice instruction directing students to go beyond the notated range, maximizing the technical benefit of each exercise.

## Minimum tempo requirement

> The metronome markings can be applied as desired. The minimum tempo given should be
> achieved before going on to the next pattern, since the patterns increase progressively in difficulty.

**Significance (p. 4).** This sets the advancement criterion — minimum tempo mastery gates progress to each successive pattern.

## Slow practice for internalization

> In many. instances, it would be helpful to play the patterns very slowly at first, to aid in bearing the
> pattern, and then work up to at least the minimum tempo.

**Significance (p. 4).** This gives the recommended two-phase practice method: slow for aural absorption, then up to tempo.

## Transposition requirement

> The instructions for transposition, sometimes even the completion of the pattern in the given
> key, must be followed to achieve adequate results. A pattern can be used in any key with any kind
> ™@ chord, if you can transpose and sometimes adjust that pattern.

**Significance (p. 4).** This makes transposition a mandatory practice step, not optional, establishing that patterns are only useful when mastered across all keys.

## Articulation default rule

> The articulation throughout the book is slurred except where marked, but the articulation
> could be changed like the rhythms, if by practicing the pattern different ways it is learned more
> completely.

**Significance (p. 5).** This states the book-wide articulation default (slurred) and gives explicit permission to vary it as a deeper learning strategy.

## Major Triad definition

> By extracting tones No. 1-3-5 from the C Major scale, we arrive at the notes forming the C Major
> Triad.

**Significance (p. 1).** This defines the Major Triad by its scale-degree formula (1-3-5), which is the foundational chord construction method used throughout the book.

## Major Sixth Chord definition

> Tones No. 1-3-5-6 of the C Major scale form the C Major Sixth Chord.

**Significance (p. 1).** This defines the Major Sixth Chord by its scale-degree formula and introduces the numerical chord-building system applied to all subsequent chord types.

## Major Seventh Chord definition

> Tones #1-3-5-7 form the C Major Seventh Chord.

**Significance (p. 2).** This concisely defines the Major Seventh Chord by scale degrees, continuing the additive chord-building system from triad through extended chords.

## Major Ninth Chord definition

> Tones #1-3-5-7-9 form the C Major Ninth Chord.

**Significance (p. 2).** This completes the four foundational Major chord types by adding the ninth, establishing the full chord vocabulary that anchors the book's first section.

## Chord Tone Chart usage

> This chord tone chart organizes the tones of al! Major scales in the order needed for the construction
> of Major chords. Reading up from the chord root C (also indicated as tone #1), we see the tones:
> C-E-G-B-D-F-A, which are all the notes in the C Major scale. Note that the ninth, eleventh and
>
> thirteenth are also the second, fourth and sixth tones respectively, as they are the same letter names.

**Significance (p. 3).** This explains how to read the Chord Tone Chart and clarifies the enharmonic equivalence of extensions (9=2, 11=4, 13=6) — a concept applied in all subsequent chord analysis.

## Pre-exercise chord fluency requirement

> In preparation for the following exercises, be able to recite, write and play the following chords
> in every key: Major Triads, Major Sixth Chords, Major Seventh Chords and Major Ninth Chords.

**Significance (p. 3).** This sets a prerequisite standard — full-key fluency in all four Major chord types — before the pattern exercises begin.
