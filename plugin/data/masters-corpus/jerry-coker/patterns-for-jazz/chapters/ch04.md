## Major scale unifies chord types

> the major triad, M6, M7, and M9 chords all use the same basic scale: the major scale
>
> of the chord root. For example, a C, CM6, CM7, and CM9 will all share the C major scale.

**Significance (p. 16).** This is the book's foundational conceptual claim — that four chord qualities (triad, M6, M7, M9) share one parent scale — which justifies the entire organizational structure of the method.

## Triad symbol as convenience notation

> The use
> of the triad symbol in the following patterns is merely for convenience, since it would be impossible
> to determine exactly which chord is being used when the scale is common to all the chords mentioned.

**Significance (p. 16).** This rule explains a notational convention the student will encounter throughout the rest of the book, preventing misreading of chord symbols as prescriptive chord quality.

## Scope shift to scale patterns

> Up to this point, our main concern has been the exercising of the four related rypes of major chords,
> formed by extracting their respective tones from the major scale of the chord root. Now we will
> concern ourselves with various patterns on the scale (major) itself.

**Significance (p. 16).** This passage marks the method's explicit pivot from chord-tone exercises to scale-pattern exercises, defining the structural boundary between two stages of the curriculum.
