## seventh chord scale-degree functions

> In the key of B-Flat Major. seventh chords formed on tones I and IV are Major Seventh chords. In
> the same key, seventh chords formed on tones II, II] and VI are Minor Seventh chords. Seventh chords
> formed on tones V and VII are Dominant and Half-Diminished, respectively.

**Significance (p. 81).** This passage establishes the foundational rule for which chord quality belongs to each scale degree in any major key — the bedrock of Roman-numeral harmonic analysis used throughout the chapter.

## most common m7 and M7 functions

> We can state in general terms that, in most instances, the M7 chord is likely to function as IM7
> rather than FVM7. Also the m7 chord functions more commonly as a IIm7 than as a HIm7 or Vim?.

**Significance (p. 82).** This practical heuristic tells the improviser which function to assume by default when a chord's key is ambiguous, reducing the decision burden in real-time playing.

## IIm7–V7 as key-establishing progression

> The Hm7-V7-IM7 progression is important, therefore, because it establishes a specific key. Also, the
> scales of these three chords will share exactly the same key signature. In fig. 25, the Gm7 uses a
> Dorian Mode on the note G (which has the parent key of F Major); and the FM6 uses the F Major
> scale.

**Significance (p. 83).** This explicitly states the theoretical reason the IIm7–V7–IM7 matters for improvisation: shared key signature means a single parent scale covers all three chords, and it names the Dorian mode as the correct choice for the IIm7.

## chord-group reading for improvisers

> A musician who can read accurately in gronps of notes, rather than just a few at a time, will develop
> good reading habits and greatly improve sight reading. In the same manner, an improviser who can
> read groups of chords that are related to each other, rather than improvising on one chord at a time,
> will also be developing good reading habits which consequently allow for more spontaneity in
> playing.

**Significance (p. 84).** This passage articulates the pedagogical principle behind the entire chapter's pattern approach: thinking in harmonic units (IIm7–V7) rather than individual chords is the skill that enables idiomatic jazz improvisation.
