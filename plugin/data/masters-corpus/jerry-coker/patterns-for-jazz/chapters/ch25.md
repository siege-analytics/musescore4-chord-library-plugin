## Turnaround harmonic purpose

> Because there is so often a tonic chord in the last two
> measures, and because the beginning of new sections (or repeats of previous sections) often begins
> with a tonic chord, a device is needed which would remove the excessive use of the tonic and at the
> same time give the phrase-endings a sense of direction, namely to return gracefully to the beginning
> of a repeated section.

**Significance (p. 118).** This explains the functional necessity of the turnaround, grounding the technique in harmonic logic rather than mere convention.

## Polychord / bitonal triad definition

> Polychords® or bitonal triads*® (alternate term) exist when two chords are used simultaneously. In
> the jazz idiom" the second chord, normally a triad, is added above the first chord, usually the given
> chord and nearly always some kind of seventh chord, providing a very colorful effect; yet, the added
> triad is chosen so as not to change the function of the bottom (given) chord.

**Significance (p. 121).** This is the foundational definition of the chapter's central concept, establishing both what a polychord is and the governing constraint that the added triad must preserve the bottom chord's function.

## Two methods for choosing added triad

> proper choice of
> the added triad is achieved in one of two ways: either by using both the given chord and one of its
> substitutions; or by locating a second chord whose tones are made up of ninths, elevenths, or thir-
> teenths of the given chord plus, perhaps, a tone or two from the given chord itself,

**Significance (p. 121).** This passage gives players two concrete, actionable methods for constructing polychords, making the concept practically applicable rather than merely theoretical.

## Available alterations on dominant seventh

> It has already
> been stated that ninths may be major, minor, or augmented when applied to the dominant seventh.
> It is also true that the fifth of a dominant seventh may be unaltered (perfect), lowered (diminished),
> or raised (augmented), without changing the chord’s function. The eleventh can be perfect or aug-
> mented, and the thirteenth is usually major, though in rare cases it can be lowered (minor thirteenth).

**Significance (p. 121).** This catalogs the full set of available tensions and alterations on a dominant seventh, which is the raw material from which polychord upper structures are drawn.
