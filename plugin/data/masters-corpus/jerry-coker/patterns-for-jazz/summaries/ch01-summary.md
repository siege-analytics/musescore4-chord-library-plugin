# Chapter 1 — Major Triads

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

Chapter 1 introduces major triads and extended major chords (sixth, seventh, ninth) constructed by extracting scale degrees 1-3-5 and beyond from the C Major scale, establishing a foundational chord-building vocabulary organized by the Chord Tone Chart. The characteristic pedagogical moves include: enforcing chord-symbol awareness during all pattern practice (never practicing patterns divorced from harmonic context), setting mastery criteria via minimum-tempo requirements before advancing, requiring full-instrument transposition across all keys rather than single-octave work, and mandating slow internalization followed by tempo-driven practice. Load-bearing terminology includes the four foundational chord movement types (cycle of fifths, chromatic, stepwise, minor thirds), the scale-degree formula notation (1-3-5 for triads, 1-3-5-7-9 for ninths), and the enharmonic equivalence principle where extensions map to scale tones (9=2, 11=4, 13=6). The chapter establishes core practice rules: slurred articulation as default (with permission to vary for deeper learning), eighth-note rhythmic notation as the baseline that must be rhythmically freed in actual improvisation to avoid mechanical execution, and transposition as a mandatory rather than optional step for achieving adequate results across any key and any chord type.
