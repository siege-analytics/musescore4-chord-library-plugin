# Chapter 3 — Major Seventh and Ninth Chords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 introduces Major Seventh and Ninth Chords through systematic practice patterns organized around directional alternation—a core pedagogical principle where students practice chords in both ascending and descending forms, alternating the direction on successive repetitions across patterns 2, 3, and 4 (pages 12–15). The chapter establishes two complementary rules: the ascending/descending alternation principle (start ascending, alternate directions) and the descending/ascending alternation principle (start descending, alternate directions), ensuring comprehensive bidirectional facility with seventh and ninth chord voicings. This dual-entry-point methodology prevents students from defaulting to a single directional habit and builds symmetrical technical control. The master's characteristic move here is pedagogical rather than harmonic: the insistence on systematic alternation as a training device transforms rote pattern practice into deliberate, bidirectional muscle memory. No new harmonic concepts beyond the chord types themselves are introduced; the load-bearing terminology consists of the chord names (Major Seventh, Ninth) and the directional-alternation principles that structure their practice.
