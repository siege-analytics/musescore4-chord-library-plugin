# Chapter 9 — Dominant Seventh and Ninth Chords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 introduces scale tone seventh chords—four-note chords built on each degree of a major scale, defined by their interval distance of seven tones rather than harmonic type—and establishes the foundational terminology of mode as a scale with a specific parent-key relationship, emphasizing the book's pedagogical choice to use key signatures rather than interval measurement. The chapter introduces the two-dimensional interval nomenclature of distance (letter-name count) and quality (whole and half steps), which together provide the precise naming system for all chord formulas. The core technique is extracting scale degrees 1-3-5-7 from the Mixolydian mode to form the dominant seventh chord, with the critical principle that both dominant seventh and ninth chords share the same Mixolydian scale for improvisation. A mandatory mastery checkpoint requires students to write out the family of modes for every major key and internalize all mode names with their parent keys and note sequences before proceeding further, establishing this material as a structural gate in the method's sequence.
