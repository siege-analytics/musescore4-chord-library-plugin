# Chapter 25 — Polychords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch25.md`._

Chapter 25 introduces polychords (or bitonal triads), the simultaneous sounding of two chords—typically a seventh chord with an added triad above it—that create colorful harmonic effects while preserving the bottom chord's function. The chapter establishes two methods for choosing the added triad: using the given chord and one of its substitutions, or constructing a chord from the ninth, eleventh, or thirteenth extensions of the given chord. A foundational concept introduced is the turnaround device, which replaces the final two measures of static tonic with a brief tonic chord followed by either a V7 or flat-II7/flat-IIM7 ending, solving the harmonic problem of excessive tonic repetition while directing motion gracefully back to the section's beginning. The chapter's characteristic move is systematic expansion of dominant seventh alterations—establishing that the ninth may be major, minor, or augmented, the fifth perfect, diminished, or augmented, and the eleventh and thirteenth similarly variable—while maintaining dominant function as long as the third remains major and the seventh minor. For non-keyboard players, the chapter presents alternation techniques and patterns (starting with Pattern 180) that allow two chords to sound together through rhythmic articulation rather than simultaneous voice-leading.
