# Chapter 27 — More on the Diminished Scale

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch27.md`._

Chapter 27 extends the diminished scale's theoretical foundation and practical application by establishing that one diminished scale serves four dominant-seventh chords simultaneously—a core efficiency principle for jazz improvisation. The chapter introduces the diminished-fifth substitution rule, which links chords whose roots fall within the same diminished-seventh chord (tritone and minor-third intervals apart), and the half-step rule, stating that a diminished scale pitched one half-step above a dominant seventh chord supplies all four essential color tones (flat-9, +9, +11, 13). Built on the diminished scale structure of eight alternating whole and half steps, the chapter leverages the scale's symmetry to teach that only three distinct diminished scales exist chromatically, covering all twelve roots—a principle unique among scales. The chapter concludes by introducing chromatic non-harmonic tones, where passing tones fill whole-step intervals to create a chromatic scale while preserving the diminished character through careful placement, extending the scale into free improvisation.
