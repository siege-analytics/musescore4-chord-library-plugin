## Down-up stroke definition

> The down-up stroke
>          This is the most common stroke on the guitar.
>          Use this exercise to control your accent on the downbeats.
>          Apply to all strings.

**Significance (p. 7).** Establishes the default picking stroke and its purpose: accenting downbeats across all strings.

## Wrist and elbow mechanics

> Practice keeping your wrist straight but not stiff. Remember: the movement is from the elbow.

**Significance (p. 7).** Specifies the load-bearing mechanical rule for right-hand motion that governs every picking exercise in the book.

## Up-down stroke definition

> The up-down stroke
>
>      Use this exercise to control your accent on the downbeats but with an up-stroke.
>      Apply to all strings.

**Significance (p. 8).** Introduces the inverted-accent variant, training the upstroke to carry the downbeat accent.

## Right-hand finger contact rule

> Do not touch the guitar with the fingers of the right hand.

**Significance (p. 8).** States the canonical right-hand-isolation rule that prevents anchoring and constrains picking technique throughout the method.

## Mixed strokes definition

> Mixed strokes
>       This exercise is designed to alternate the accents with a down-stroke then an up-stroke.
>       Apply to all strings.

**Significance (p. 9).** Defines accent alternation between down and up strokes, the synthesis of exercises 1 and 2.

## Pick guard contact rule

> Do not touch the pick guard or strings with the fingers of the right hand.

**Significance (p. 9).** Extends the no-anchor rule to the pick guard, reinforcing free-floating right-hand posture.

## In-between stroke definition

> In between stroke
>
>            This is the hardest stroke to master. It involves going between two adjacent strings.
>            This is a critical part of your right hand technique. Take your time with this one.
>            Apply to any two adjacent strings using the double-stop of your choice. Do not let notes ring!

**Significance (p. 10).** Names the hardest stroke and prescribes its application: adjacent-string double-stops with no sustain, central to right-hand mastery.
