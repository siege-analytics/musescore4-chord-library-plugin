## Purpose of arpeggio chapter

> Although this is not a book about arpeggios, I feel it is important to include a few
>      basic arpeggios so that you can see the picking method applied to arpeggios.

**Significance (p. 15).** Frames the chapter as a demonstration that the alternate-picking system extends to arpeggios, not a comprehensive arpeggio study.

## Articulation over speed

> The difficulty with any arpeggio exercise , is keeping the notes from ringing. Articulatioin is more important
>     han speed.

**Significance (p. 15).** Establishes the central technical challenge of arpeggio picking: muting/separating notes, not playing fast.

## Major triad warm-up value

> Major triad exercises: This makes a great warm-up exercise. It is very difficult to
>      play with clean articulation. The result is well worth the time. This exercie alone will
>     facilitate the separtion of notes with any phrase that contains an arpeggio or arpeggio
>      fragment.

**Significance (p. 19).** Designates the major triad exercise as a foundational warm-up that develops note separation for all arpeggio playing.

## Fingering and picking consistency

> Fingering and picking is the same throughout.

**Significance (p. 19).** Signals that one fingering/picking pattern transposes across the arpeggio exercises, reinforcing the system's universality.

## Sweep picking rejection

> Minor 9 arpeggios: I have seen this referred to as sweep picking
>       I see no reason to make any distinction. Everything follows the rule:
>       Higher string = "down stroke" Lower string = "up stroke"

**Significance (p. 21).** Bruno's core polemic: sweep picking is not a separate technique, just the directional rule applied to arpeggios.

## Minor 11 sextuplet fingering

> Minor 11 arpeggios: Because of the fingering it is possible to play 6 notes in one beat.(sextuplet)
>       The fingering sets up the consecutive down and up strokes to go across four strings.

**Significance (p. 22).** Shows how fingering choice creates the consecutive same-direction strokes that enable sextuplet arpeggios across four strings.

## Saxophone/piano phrasing reference

> Saxophone players
>       and pianists use phrases like this.

**Significance (p. 22).** Justifies arpeggio vocabulary by reference to non-guitar instruments, signaling these are general musical phrases, not guitar tricks.

## Fingering enables speed

> Minor 9th arpeggios: Below are several ways to pick this. Again, the fingering makes
>       the speed possible.

**Significance (p. 24).** Restates the chapter thesis: fingering choice, not picking aggression, is what unlocks fast arpeggio playing.

## Rule exception at rests

> I have found when there is a rest between phrases,
>         it is possible to break the rule and use whatever stroke you
>         please to start the phrase.

**Significance (p. 24).** Gives the one sanctioned exception to the higher-string/lower-string rule: phrase starts after rests can use either stroke.

## Practice both starting strokes

> Try starting any phrase with both strokes, down and up. There should be only a slieght difference in sound.

**Significance (p. 24).** Prescribes a practice routine that verifies the picking method produces consistent tone regardless of starting stroke.

## Adjacent-string arpeggio design

> Minor 9th arpeggios:By using fingerings that place notes on adjacent strings, either higher or lower,
>        these large two octave arpeggios become possible.

**Significance (p. 25).** Articulates the design principle for two-octave arpeggios: arrange notes on adjacent strings so the directional rule generates fluid picking.

## Picking unified across textures

> I often run across guitarists
>      who become misled into thinking that picking for single lines is different than picking for arpeggios.

**Significance (p. 25).** Names the misconception the chapter exists to dispel: single-line and arpeggio picking are governed by one system.

## Music drives picking, not fretboard

> You have to be careful
>      to NOT let the fingerboard make the music. The music comes from inside; the picking mechanism described in this book
>      is designed to make it easier for the music to be realized.

**Significance (p. 25).** Closes the chapter with Bruno's aesthetic axiom: the picking system serves musical intent, never dictates it.
