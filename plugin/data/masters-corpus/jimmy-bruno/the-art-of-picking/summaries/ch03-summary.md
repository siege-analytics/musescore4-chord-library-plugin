# Chapter 3 — Consecutive Strokes

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 develops consecutive same-direction strokes as essential jazz vocabulary, drilling consecutive down-to-up and up-to-down strokes on adjacent-string double-stops with a strict no-ringing constraint that makes muting a defining requirement of the technique. Bruno states the stage's core practice rule (Precision over speed): play evenly, because rhythmic precision of the 8th notes matters more than tempo. The chapter then introduces Bruno's signature exception to his own picking rule (Triplet exception to alternate picking): for repeating triplet patterns like triad arpeggios, alternation is suspended in favor of three consecutive down-strokes followed by three consecutive up-strokes across three adjacent strings (Three-down then three-up). Crucially, he distinguishes drill technique from playing technique (Rule applies in real phrases) - the exception lives in the exercise; in actual melodic lines strict alternation returns. The chapter also introduces sixteenth-note reversal on the same string as a distinct sub-skill tied to repeated-note phrasing on chord voicings, and emphasizes accent placement as the load-bearing musical feature when the broken-rule pattern is recast into sixteenths.
