# Chapter 5 — Arpeggios

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 demonstrates that the directional picking system extends to arpeggios, framed not as a comprehensive arpeggio study but as proof of the method's universality (Purpose of arpeggio chapter, Picking unified across textures). The central technical challenge Bruno identifies is articulation, not speed - keeping notes from ringing into one another - and he designates the major triad exercise as a foundational warm-up that develops note separation for all arpeggio playing (Articulation over speed, Major triad warm-up value). The chapter's signature polemic (Sweep picking rejection) dismisses sweep picking as a separate technique: it is simply the higher-string/lower-string rule applied to arpeggios. Bruno repeatedly stresses that fingering choice, not picking aggression, is what unlocks speed - fingerings that place notes on adjacent strings enable consecutive same-direction strokes, sextuplet minor 11 runs across four strings, and large two-octave minor 9th arpeggios (Minor 11 sextuplet fingering, Fingering enables speed, Adjacent-string arpeggio design). The chapter introduces one sanctioned exception to the rule - phrases starting after a rest may begin with either stroke (Rule exception at rests) - and reinforces the both-strokes practice routine that verifies tonal consistency. It closes with Bruno's aesthetic axiom (Music drives picking, not fretboard): the picking mechanism serves musical intent and must never let the fingerboard dictate the music, and arpeggio vocabulary is justified by reference to saxophone and piano phrasing rather than guitar idiom.
