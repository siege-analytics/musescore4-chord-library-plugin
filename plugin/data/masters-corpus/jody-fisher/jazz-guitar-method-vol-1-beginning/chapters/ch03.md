## constructing larger chords from triads

> Most chords are built on top of triads, so constructing larger chords is simply a matter of
> adding additional scale tones to the original triad.

**Significance (p. 30).** This establishes the foundational principle of chord construction used throughout the chapter — all extended chord formulas derive from this additive method.

## Maj7 chord formula

> The formula for Maj7 chords is: Root-3-5-7. This means that we combine the root, 3rd,
> 5th and 7th degrees of the major scale to build the chord. Notice that what we have
> actually done is add the 7th degree to a major triad.

**Significance (p. 30).** Defines the Maj7 formula explicitly and ties it back to the triad-plus-extension principle, giving the reader a reusable construction rule.

## extensions beyond the octave

> Many chords use notes that actually lie beyond a one octave major scale. Since an octave
> contains only eight notes, these would include chords that have 9ths, | ths and |3ths.
> These are called extensions. Where do these notes come from? We get the extended
> notes (beyond the octave) by continuing the major scale up through a second octave.

**Significance (p. 30).** Defines the term 'extensions' and explains the numbering system (9, 11, 13) that underpins all subsequent extended-chord formulas in the chapter.

## chord naming by highest extension

> Chords are usually named for the highest extension present. So, even
> t though the '7 and 9 are part of the chord, it is still called min! 1.

**Significance (p. 31).** States the naming convention rule that governs how extended chords are labeled.

## right-hand execution of chords

> Another consideration is how to execute these larger chords with the right hand. Most

**Significance (p. 31).** Gives a practical rule about pick vs. fingerpicking for larger chords.

## major 6th chord formula

> The formula for Maj chords is as follows: Root-3-5-6. In the key of F this would
> be spelled FA, C and D. In the key of C it would be C,E, G andA.

**Significance (p. 33).** Defines the Maj6 formula with concrete key examples.

## connecting major scale fingerings across neck

> Getting around the guitar fingerboard smoothly is an art. So far, you have learned six
> different fingerings for the major scale, with each one staying in one area of the finger-
> board. Here are some ways to connect these major scale fingerings that will help you
> move fluently around the neck. Practice these and try to make up your own connections.
> Practice very slowly until these fingerings are seamless.

**Significance (p. 35).** Introduces the connecting-fingerings concept and gives the key practice directive.

## three-octave scales and horizontal playing

> The six major scale fingerings you have learned span two octaves. They are useful for
> playing in one position at a time. In the next two lessons, you will be shown four three-
> octave major scale fingerings. These will eventually help you play horizontally, (on the
> fingerboard, that is—not while laying down), which is a very natural approach for all stringed
> instruments. When you start improvising with the major scale (in just a few lessons) you
> will find that your ideas are very different when you approach the scales in this manner.

**Significance (p. 40).** Defines horizontal playing as a distinct approach.

## which chord tones not to drop

> Roots, 5ths and 9ths can be dropped in various combinations. Why can we do this?
> The combination of remaining voices can actually imply the missing sounds. As with all
> rules, there are exceptions, but it is generally not a good idea to drop 3rds and 7ths
> because these are the tones that define major, minor and dominant chords.

**Significance (p. 46).** Gives the essential voicing rule — never drop 3rds and 7ths — explaining why those tones are structurally load-bearing.
