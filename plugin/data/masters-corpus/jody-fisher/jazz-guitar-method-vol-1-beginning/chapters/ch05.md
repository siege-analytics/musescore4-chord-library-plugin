## Roman numerals: communication

> Communication. If someone is trying to explain a chord progression to you, it is
> easier to see the nature of the progression if they say,“ii-V-I in C, then iii-vi-ii-V-I in
> F then ii-V-I in B”” than if they rattled off a whole list of chord names. As you will
> see, many types of songs follow rather predictable patterns, and these patterns are
> usually described in Roman numerals.

**Significance (p. 67).** Establishes the first core reason for Roman-numeral notation.

## Roman numerals: transposition

> Transposition. Transpose means to change the key. If you know your scales and the
> diatonic chords for those scales, transposing is no big deal. Learn all of your songs in
> Roman numerals. Then you will have learned the material in a “universal key.” You can
> then simply “plug into” the actual key you want. Working on this now will save you
> much work (and possible embarrassment) later.

**Significance (p. 67).** Defines transposition and gives the book's central pedagogical rule.

## Generating modes from major scale

> Creating modes from scales is a way of generating new scales for improvising over various
> chords. To create a set of modes, you simply play the scale from a note other than the
> root and continue ascending for an octave.

**Significance (p. 69).** Defines the foundational technique for mode generation.

## Mode-to-chord correspondence

> Each mode of the major scale corresponds to the diatonic chord that shares the same
> root. The C lonian mode is used to improvise over CMaj7, the I chord. The D Dorian
> mode would be used over Dmin7, the ii chord.

**Significance (p. 69).** Establishes the core rule linking each mode to its corresponding diatonic chord.

## Non-diatonic chords in Roman numeral notation

> The chords that are natural to the key are indicated with Roman numerals, as usual. If the
> root of the chord belongs in the key, but the chord type is different than usual (for in-
> stance, it should be min7, but now it’s 7), it is necessary to indicate the new chord type. It
> may also be necessary to change from lower case to upper case Roman numerals, or vice
> versa. For instance, in C: Emin7=iii, E7=I17. Or, Dmin7=ii, D7=117). You may use sharp
> # and flat > signs to show a non-
>
> diatonic root.

**Significance (p. 71).** Gives the rule for notating chords that deviate from diatonic norms.

## Parent key thinking

> The term
> parent key or key center refers to the major scale from which the chords were derived.
> Therefore, in the first two measures we are thinking of our parent key as C Major, so the
> progression is I-ii-V7-I. In measures three and four we would think of our parent key as
> F Major, so the progression is ii-V7-I.

**Significance (p. 71).** Defines the concept of parent key/key center.

## Parallel vs. derivative mode approach

> Many
> players like to think of them as separate scales apart from the major scale itself. The
> essence of this approach is to learn separate fingerings for each mode, rather than relating
> each mode back to its parent major scale. This is known as the “parallel-approach,” and is
> a quite effective way of thinking. This book will also cover the “derivative approach,” but
> you should realize that there are actually five separate approaches one could use.

**Significance (p. 72).** Introduces and contrasts the two main pedagogical approaches to modes.

## Scale choices per chord type

> Since harmonizing any major scale always produces two Maj7 chords (I and IV), three
>
> min7 chords (ii, iii and vi), one dominant 7th chord (V7) and one min7’5 chord (vii), it is
> safe to say:
>
> |. Every Maj7 chord has two major scale choices to use for improvising.
> 2. Every min7 chord has three major scale choices to use for improvising.
> 3. Every dominant 7th chord has one major scale choice.
>
> 4. Every min7’5 chord has one major scale choice.

**Significance (p. 77).** Encodes the definitive rules for how many parent-scale improvisation choices are available per chord quality.

## Vertical chord scales: concept

> In all of the previous chord scales you have learned, the chords progressed up the finger-
> board on a single string set, from lower to higher positions. This was valuable because it
> allowed you to see how each voice in each chord ascended scale-wise. It is also a good
> idea to learn chord scales in a vertical direction, across the string sets. This way you will
> learn all the diatonic chords in closer proximity.

**Significance (p. 76).** Defines vertical chord scales and pedagogical rationale.

## Modal tunes: improvisation approach

> Modal tunes are usually based around very few chord
> changes, so improvisation is based on just a few
> scales. The melody is usually written in a single mode.

**Significance (p. 80).** Defines the structural characteristics of modal tunes.

## Mixed modal/diatonic improvisation rule

> The tunes in this lesson contain situations where both diatonic thinking and modal
> thinking are necessary. When you have a tune that consists mainly of diatonic chords, use
> the major scale of the I chord. Then, when you get to a chord that is not natural to the
> key, switch to the modal approach.

**Significance (p. 83).** Gives the practical decision rule for blending diatonic and modal thinking in real tunes.

## Transposition practice directive

> A little hands-on experience will show you how automatic this kind of thinking can
> become. Don’t put this off—you need to learn how to do this. You'll pick this up very
> rapidly if over the next few days you transpose four or five tunes into six different keys.

**Significance (p. 71).** Delivers an explicit practice prescription for transposition.
