## motif definition

> A motif is a short musical phrase. For instance, it can be a lick,a spontaneous idea, or a
> melodic pattern. For our purposes, a motif will mean a short phrase that becomes a theme
>
> in your solo, This theme becomes the basis for repetition or variation.

**Significance (p. 94).** Defines the foundational concept of motif as the structural unit of a solo.

## variation on a theme

> If you have established a
> theme in your solo, examples of variation would include |) maintaining the rhythmic figure
> but changing the pitches of the notes, or 2) maintaining the pitches of the notes and
>
> changing the rhythmic structure.

**Significance (p. 94).** Gives two concrete techniques for developing variations.

## repetition and tension/release

> Repetition also
> causes tension and keeps the listener's attention. Breaking the repetition eases the ten-
> sion and resolution is achieved. This tension/release concept is central in almost all forms
> of improvisation and composition. A good solo is often the result of a tasteful treatment
>
> of tension and release.

**Significance (p. 94).** Articulates the tension/release principle as the central organizing rule of improvisation.

## repetition builds intensity

> Repetition tends to build intensity. This is why some players save this technique for the
> conclusion of their solo. There are many ways of achieving this effect. Sometimes you can
> fit repetitive phrase through many chord changes. Sometimes it works better over static
> chord vamps (repetitions).

**Significance (p. 94).** Gives a practical rule about placement of repetition within a solo's arc.

## mastery checklist before advancing

> Before moving on, you should be able to play all the tunes in this book. This means being
> comfortable playing the melodies in two or three octaves; playing chord changes in at least
> two areas of the fingerboard; improvising solos that reflect your understanding of the
> major scale and its modes; and melodic patterns with openings, bodies and conclusions.

**Significance (p. 95).** The book's explicit exit-criteria checklist.

## tone, technique, time

> Strive for good tone and technique. Keep good time. These are some of the areas of
> study that all jazz musicians work on.

**Significance (p. 95).** Condenses the book's overarching practice priorities into a direct rule.

## playing with others

> Try to play with as many other musicians as you can. This is of great value and cannot be
> overstressed.

**Significance (p. 95).** States unambiguously that ensemble experience is the highest-value activity beyond solo practice.
