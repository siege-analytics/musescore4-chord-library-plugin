# Chapter 4 — The Harmonized Major Scale / Major Chord Scales

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 teaches the harmonized major scale—constructing triads and 7th chords by stacking 3rds on each scale degree—and introduces the concept of the chord scale, defined as "the chords in a key played scale-wise (I-ii-iii-IV-V-vi-vii°)," which provides the foundation for diatonic thinking in jazz improvisation. The chapter defines improvisation as "the art of composing a new melody spontaneously," emphasizing that "your improvised melody must fit the chord changes" and introducing progressional improvising, where "a single scale will work well over an entire chord progression." Through the lens of diatonic thinking, students learn that they "may use the major scale over any of the chords that are constructed from it," including handling key changes by switching scales mid-progression. The chapter prescribes a concrete three-step procedure for learning a new tune: master the chord progression in two fingerboard areas, learn the melody (ideally an octave higher), then practice improvising using note values from whole notes to free playing, listening carefully to develop familiarity with the sounds before worrying about solo content. By constructing and practicing chord scales across the fingerboard, students systematically "expand your chord vocabulary" and internalize the voice-leading principle that "each voice moves in a scale-wise motion" from chord to chord.
