# Chapter 5 — Roman Numerals, Transposition, and Modes

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches Roman numerals as a universal language for chord progressions—enabling musicians to communicate patterns like "ii-V-I" across any key—and establishes transposition as the central pedagogical principle: by learning songs in Roman numerals rather than specific chord names, you master material in a "universal key" and can "plug into" any actual key needed. The chapter introduces modes as improvisation tools generated by playing the major scale from notes other than the root, with each mode corresponding directly to its diatonic chord (C Ionian over CMaj7, D Dorian over Dmin7), and explains the parent-key concept—the major scale from which chords derive—to anchor harmonic thinking. Two pedagogical approaches are presented: the parallel approach (learning separate fingerings for each mode) and the derivative approach (relating modes back to the parent major scale). The chapter codifies definitive rules for improvisation choices: every Maj7 and min7♭5 chord has one parent scale option, every min7 has three, and every dominant 7th has one, alongside the practical directive that modal tunes rely on few chord changes and single-mode melodies, while mixed tunes require switching between diatonic thinking (for chords natural to the key) and modal thinking (for non-diatonic chords). Mastering transposition through hands-on practice—transposing four or five tunes into six different keys over a few days—makes this thinking automatic.
