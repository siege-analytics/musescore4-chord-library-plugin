# Chapter 6 — How Jazz Works (Coda)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch06.md`._

Chapter 6 synthesizes the book's jazz fundamentals by defining the three-part jazz performance structure—head, solos, head—and establishing that "a good jazz guitarist must be able to: play a song's melody in two or three octaves, play the chord changes in several areas of the fingerboard, and improvise freely through the chord changes." The chapter consolidates essential technique rules: left-hand positioning with the thumb in "hitch-hiking" position, alternate picking with equal up- and down-stroke volume, and the principle that "playing fingerstyle is the only way to sound the voices simultaneously," distinguishing harmonic voicing from pick-based strumming. Core concepts are anchored in strategic practice methods—learning scales two strings at a time, understanding "locked position" (scales with no open strings that allow transposition), and "limiting rhythmic options" to isolate melodic hearing from automatic finger patterns. The chapter concludes by framing solo construction as a three-part architecture: an opening that "start[s] with something that is easy for the listener to follow, or something startling," a body that "gets a little busier to develop themes," and a conclusion that "lets your listeners know the solo is ending." Throughout, the chapter emphasizes economy of motion, consistent positioning whether sitting or standing, and the interplay between locked-position (vertical) thinking and open-position (horizontal) thinking to avoid limiting improvisational ideas.
