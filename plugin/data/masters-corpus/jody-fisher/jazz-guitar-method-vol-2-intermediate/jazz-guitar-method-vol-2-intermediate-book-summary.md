# Book-level distillation — jazz-guitar-method-vol-2-intermediate

## Overall Arc and Pedagogical Structure

This book is a structured jazz guitar method organized around a single load-bearing harmonic unit—the ii-V7-I progression—that is introduced in Chapter 1 and never fully leaves the stage. The method moves from harmonic foundation (Chapters 1–2) through comping and rhythm technique (Chapter 3), then through chord-specific lick vocabulary (Chapter 4), blues (Chapter 5), rhythm changes (Chapter 6), and turnarounds (Chapters 7–8), before closing with a capstone chapter (Chapter 9) on tone, practice strategy, and ear training. The arc is deliberately cumulative: each chapter either introduces a new harmonic context (blues, rhythm changes, turnarounds) or a new layer of vocabulary (licks, voicing substitutions, altered extensions) to be applied over frameworks already in place. Chapter 6 explicitly practices deferred complexity, asking students to master the sound of rhythm changes with familiar scales before tackling the advanced alterations that arrive later—a pattern visible throughout the method.

## Characteristic Systems and Terminology

The book's foundational unit of harmonic analysis is the **ii-V7-I progression**, established in Chapter 1 as the structure that establishes key and governs most improvisation decisions. Chapters 2, 3, 4, 7, and 8 all orbit around it. The master's naming system for fingerings—identifying positions by the string and finger holding the lowest root (e.g., "6/2" for sixth string, second finger)—is introduced in Chapter 1 and used throughout. Chapter 2 introduces the critical distinction between **essential chord tones** (3rd and 7th, which must be present) and **expendable tones** (roots and 5ths, which may be omitted), establishing **rootless voicings** as a practical consequence. Chapter 2 also introduces **swing eighths** as the rhythmic foundation of jazz phrasing, and **target-note technique** (also called *spelling out the changes*)—the practice of landing improvised lines on chord tones to articulate the underlying harmony. Chapter 3 extends this with three harmonic vocabulary devices for the V7 chord: voicing upper extensions via a Major 7th arpeggio rooted on the flat-7, substituting diminished triads from the 3rd, 5th, or 7th, and ascending scalar lines from chord tones. Chapter 3 also introduces the **three-string-set framework** for organizing voicings across the fingerboard.

## Rules and Prescriptions

Several explicit rules govern the method. Chapter 1 states the primary improvisation rule: **use the major scale of the I chord over diatonic ii-V7-I progressions**, with the hard exception that this rule is suspended if any chord contains altered tones (raised or lowered 5ths, 9ths, or 11ths) outside that major scale. Chapter 1 also defines arpeggios—via George Van Eps's phrase—as "melted chords," single notes of a chord sounded sequentially. Chapter 2 establishes that chord substitution must be judged by **context and ear, not theory alone**, and notes the practical symmetry of rootless 7b9 voicings, which can move at minor-third intervals without changing chord quality. Chapter 3 establishes the rhythmic rules for comping: **down strokes on downbeats, up strokes on upbeats**, with string damping to control sustain, and the aesthetic principle that swing rhythm is *implied* rather than mechanically predictable. Chapter 5 introduces three scalar tools for blues soloing—the **minor pentatonic scale**, **blues scale**, and **Mixolydian mode**—and Chapter 7 states the scaling rule for turnarounds: **four-measure turnarounds double the chord durations of their two-measure equivalents**.

## Methods and Lick-Based Vocabulary Building

The book's explicit pedagogical method is a cycle of **learn, transpose, apply**. Chapter 4 makes this most direct: learn sample licks for each chord type (major, minor, dominant, half-diminished), transpose to all twelve keys, and immediately insert licks into actual solos—never storing them in isolated study. Chapters 5 and 8 reinforce this by directing students to memorize licks from provided solo examples to build personal improvisational vocabulary. Chapter 3 introduces the foundational philosophy underpinning all of this: **improvisation is creative reorganization of learned vocabulary**, with licks functioning as word-like building blocks (analogous to scales as alphabet). Chapter 7 extends the method to turnarounds, directing players to memorize and transpose turnaround examples while actively listening to how other musicians deploy them, making **ear-training and transposition** the twin engines of mastery.

## Ear-First Principle and Advanced Practice

Chapter 9 crystallizes the method's deepest prescription: **hear first**. Tone is controlled by hands and technique, not equipment. Practice rules include focusing deeply on a small number of difficult areas rather than spreading effort thinly, rotating practice focus every few weeks, and prioritizing repertoire for live contexts. The chapter prescribes specific exercises to escape positional thinking: practicing scales, modes, and arpeggios on **single strings**, and improvising over recorded progressions on one string to force fresh ideas untethered from learned patterns. The culminating prescription for advanced students is to study one artist **deeply and comprehensively**—biography, compositions, transcribed solos, repeated listening—before moving on, making depth of immersion the mark of mature development. The single-string method and the hear-first principle together form the book's answer to the gap between knowing patterns and actually improvising: the brain must initiate the musical idea before the fingers execute it.
