# Chapter 1 — Chapter 1

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

This chapter introduces the ii-V7-I progression as the foundational harmonic concept in jazz, explaining that it's essential because it establishes the key of the I chord. The chapter teaches that any ii-V7-I can have altered or extended chords while retaining its identity, provided the roots maintain perfect fourth spacing and the chord qualities remain minor, dominant, and major respectively. The primary improvisation rule is to use the major scale of the I chord when playing over diatonic ii-V7-I progressions, with the critical exception that this rule doesn't apply if any chord contains altered tones (raised or lowered 5ths, 9ths, or 11ths) that fall outside the major scale. The chapter also introduces arpeggios—defined through George Van Eps's memorable characterization as "melted chords" or single notes of a chord sounded sequentially—and establishes the scale and arpeggio fingering naming system used throughout the series, where fingerings are identified by the position of the lowest root (e.g., 6/2 for sixth string, second finger).
