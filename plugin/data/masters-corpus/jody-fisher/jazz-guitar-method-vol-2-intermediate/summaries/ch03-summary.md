# Chapter 3 — Chapter 3

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 teaches swing jazz comping—the accompanying technique guitarists use behind soloists—through both rhythmic and harmonic frameworks. Rhythmically, it covers strum direction (down on downbeats, up on upbeats), string damping to control sustain, and the aesthetic principle that swing rhythm is implied rather than predictable, leaving the guitarist to accent various parts with improvisation and surprise. Harmonically, it introduces three key vocabulary devices for the ii-V7-I progression: (1) playing a Major 7th arpeggio rooted on the flat-7 degree of the V7 chord to voice its upper extensions; (2) substituting diminished triads from the 3rd, 5th, or 7th of the V7 to create altered extensions; and (3) ascending scalar lines starting from chord tones, optionally alternated with descending patterns for contour interest. The chapter also introduces a three-string-set framework for organizing chord voicings across the fingerboard and explains rootless voicings—omitting roots to allow more extensions and rely on the bass player or ear-implied root. Finally, it establishes the foundational philosophy that improvisation is creative reorganization of learned vocabulary, introducing licks (pre-planned phrases for specific chords) as the word-like building blocks of solo vocabulary, analogous to scales as the alphabet.
