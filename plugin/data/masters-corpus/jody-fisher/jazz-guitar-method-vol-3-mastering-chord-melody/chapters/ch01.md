## Fingerstyle finger assignment

> Generally, p controls the sixth and fifth strings, while ij is on the fourth, m is on the third,
> ais on the second and ¢ is on the first.

**Significance (p. 7).** This establishes the foundational fingerstyle finger-to-string mapping that governs right-hand technique throughout the method.

## Fingerstyle advantages over pick

> One real advantage of playing chords in this manner is that you don't have to worry about
> muting strings or barring over a note that is unwanted in the chord. You pluck only the
> tones you want to hear.

**Significance (p. 7).** This explains the core benefit of fingerstyle—selective control—which justifies why the technique deserves dedicated practice.

## Fingerstyle simultaneous voice sounding

> With fingerstyle techniques you sound harmonies like a key-
> board instrument—all the notes of a chord at the same moment.

**Significance (p. 7).** This distinguishes fingerstyle from pick playing by describing its unique sonic capability to voice chords truly simultaneously.

## Single-note fingerstyle technique options

> To play single-note lines fingerstyle, most jazz players either alternate their first two fin-
>
> gers (i,m) or alternate their thumb and second fingers (p,m).

**Significance (p. 7).** This provides the two primary alternating-finger patterns for melodic fingerstyle playing, grounding students in jazz technique.

## Pick with fingers hybrid technique

> Usually the pick is held between the Pp and i fingers and takes care of the sixth, fifth and
> fourth strings while m covers the third, a covers the second and c handles the first.

**Significance (p. 7).** This describes the hybrid approach that allows transitioning between fingerstyle and pick without repositioning the hand.

## Hybrid technique limitation

> The major disadvantage is that you are
> limited to playing four-note chords, since two of your fingers are occupied with holding
> the pick.

**Significance (p. 7).** This establishes a concrete trade-off in the hybrid method—voicing range is sacrificed for ease of switching between techniques.

## Learning multiple right-hand techniques

> Like almost everything else with guitar it is best to learn every technique instead of relying
> on just one or two. This way you will be prepared for any musical situation that comes
> your way

**Significance (p. 8).** This rule establishes flexibility and preparation as the guiding principle for mastering right-hand options.
