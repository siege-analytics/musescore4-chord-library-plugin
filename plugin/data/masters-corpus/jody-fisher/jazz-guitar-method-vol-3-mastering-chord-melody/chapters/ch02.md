## Finding chord voicings with melody on top

> The next step is to find chord voicings that have the melody notes
> on top.

**Significance (p. 9).** This states the fundamental goal of the harmonization process—melody-driven voicing selection is the core principle for combining melody and harmony on guitar.

## Octave adjustment for harmony

> It is often necessary to raise the melody one octave in order to use larger chord voicings.

**Significance (p. 9).** This teaches a practical technique for expanding harmonic possibilities when a melody note would otherwise be too low for effective chord voicing.

## Lead-sheet arrangement guideline

> When using a lead-sheet (a sketch of a song that shows words, melody and chord
> symbols only) as a source for your basic melody and chord changes, it is generally
> helpful to raise the melody an octave. Check the highest and lowest notes in the
> song to see if an octave change would make the tune easier to harmonize.

**Significance (p. 11).** This rule establishes a standard practice for adapting lead-sheet melodies to guitar, providing a decision framework for octave placement.

## Key selection strategy

> Consider the possibility that the song might lie on the fingerboard better in a
>
> different key. Changing the key can often change the basic character of the tune
> itself. For instance, you may want to try keys that allow the use of open strings.
> The extra investigation is usually well worth the effort.

**Significance (p. 11).** This rule teaches that key choice impacts both playability and musical character, directing arrangers to explore multiple keys beyond the original.

## Melody study in single notes

> Learn to play the melody in single notes first. Explore it in every octave to make
>
> sure you understand what the melody is about.

**Significance (p. 11).** This foundational step establishes that deep melody understanding must precede harmonic arrangement and shapes the entire approach to chord melody.

## Memorizing chord voicings

> Memorize chord changes and be able to play them in various areas of the finger-
>
> board.

**Significance (p. 11).** This rule emphasizes the importance of fingerboard knowledge and flexibility in executing harmony options across multiple positions.

## Basic arrangement before enhancement

> Arrange your song with the basic chords first. When you have a handle on the basic
>
> arrangement, then dress up the harmonies with passing chords, substitutions
> and harmonizations.

**Significance (p. 11).** This procedural rule establishes the correct sequencing: build structural foundation before adding decorative harmonic techniques.

## Arrangements as skeletal frameworks

> Don't carve your arrangements or chord changes in stone. Basically, you want
>
> skeletal arrangements from which you can improvise melodically, harmonically
> and rhythmically. Once you gain confidence with this approach, it is a lot more
> fun, which, never forget, is the whole idea.

**Significance (p. 11).** This rule reframes arrangement as a flexible, improvisational tool rather than a fixed composition, emphasizing musical creativity and enjoyment.

## Chord enhancement definition

> To
> enhance means to simply add extensions or alterations to the basic chord. To most jazz
> musicians, all the chords within a given quality, or family, (major, minor, dominant, etc.) are
> interchangeable. In other words, a Major 6th chord can be thought of in the same light as a
> Major 9th chord or a Major 7th chord. Functionally, they are all the same.

**Significance (p. 12).** This defines the conceptual framework for understanding chord enhancement—chords within the same family are functionally equivalent, enabling flexible substitution.

## Enhancement vs. substitution

> It should be noted that chord enhancement is not the same as chord substitution. Chord
> substitution implies replacing a chord with another chord that has a different root.

**Significance (p. 12).** This distinction clarifies two different harmonic techniques and prevents confusion between adding extensions and changing harmonic function.

## Context and taste in enhancement

> Context is everything. Taste should be
> your primary concern. Not everything needs to be enriched.

**Significance (p. 12).** This rule provides a quality standard that tempers the previous explanation of enhancement: musical judgment should guide when and how much to enhance.
