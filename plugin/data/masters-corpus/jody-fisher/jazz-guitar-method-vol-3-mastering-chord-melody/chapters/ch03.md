## Diads definition and use

> Diads, also known as double-stops, are two-note harmonizations. They work very well for chord tones and non-chord tones. Any interval is possible, but 3rds and 6ths are the most common.

**Significance (p. 18).** Establishes the foundational technique of two-note harmonization and specifies the preferred intervals for practical application.

## Adding non-chord tones to chords

> Very often we add a non-chord tone that is in the melody to the chord. This will change the name of the chord, as you will be adding higher extensions and altered tones. This is a common way to come up with enhancements.

**Significance (p. 19).** Teaches the core technique of chord extension through melody integration and explains how this creates chord alterations.

## Voice leading definition

> Voice leading is the term we use to describe the movement of individual voices from one
> chord to another. This is very important when choosing voicings for a chord melody
> arrangement.

**Significance (p. 21).** Defines the essential concept of voice leading and establishes its critical role in chord-melody arranging.

## Scalewise voice movement

> Good voice-leading often means
> arranging your chords so that the voices actually travel in whole and half steps.

**Significance (p. 21).** Provides the key principle for creating smooth, idiomatic voice leading through stepwise motion.

## 3rds and 7ths in chord definition

> Usually, it
> is the 3rd and the 7th degrees of the chords. This is because chords are defined by their
> 3rds and 7ths which differentiate major, minor and dominant chords.

**Significance (p. 22).** Explains why the 3rd and 7th are the most important chord tones for voice leading, as they define chord quality.

## Passing chords definition

> A passing chord is a chord added between two others to create a smoother transition.
> Very often you can harmonize both chord and non-chord tones as passing chords.

**Significance (p. 23).** Introduces the technique of using intermediate harmonies to smooth chord progressions.

## Half-step dominant passing chords

> One common application of passing chords is the use of a dominant chord (altered or
> unaltered) with a root that is a half step higher, or lower, than the chord being approached.
> These chords can end up being non-diatonic, or from outside the key.

**Significance (p. 23).** Explains a primary technique for applying dominant passing chords and permits non-diatonic harmony for smoothness.

## Diminished chord repetition property

> diminished chords repeat themselves every three frets, so you can choose from
> among many possible locations on the fingerboard.

**Significance (p. 28).** Provides a practical technical insight for locating diminished chord voicings on the guitar.

## Diminished as V7 substitute

> Approaching the | chord with a diminished chord from one half step below the root is a
> common substitute for the V7. The reason this works is that the two chords have three
> notes in common and both resolve nicely to I.

**Significance (p. 30).** Establishes the harmonic equivalence between diminished and dominant seventh chords, enabling practical substitution.

## Quartal harmony definition

> In conventional harmony, we use chords that are constructed primarily from stacking 3rds.
> In quartal harmony, chords are constructed by stacking 4ths.

**Significance (p. 33).** Defines the alternative harmony system based on fourths rather than thirds.

## Single-note melody approach

> Some players prefer a chord/melody style with fewer chords. They will play mostly single
> notes and add chords only when the chord changes. Sometimes this is the best way to deal
> with chord tones and non-chord tones alike.

**Significance (p. 36).** Presents an alternative minimalist approach to harmonizing non-chord tones.

## Octave doubling for emphasis

> Doubling melody notes in octaves will create a stronger effect than single notes, and some-
> times you might find this kind of emphasis desirable. Listen to Wes Montgomery and
> George Benson to hear great examples of this kind of playing.

**Significance (p. 36).** Teaches the technique of octave doubling and provides authoritative models for the style.
