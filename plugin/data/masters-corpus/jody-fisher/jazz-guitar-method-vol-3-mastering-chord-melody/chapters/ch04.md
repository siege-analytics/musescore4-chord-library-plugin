## Like qualities as passing chords

> like qualities” is similar to the material in Lesson 4 of Chapter 2,
> but instead of using only dominant chords as passing chords, use the same quality as the
> chord you are approaching from either a half step above or below.

**Significance (p. 38).** Defines a core harmonic technique: substituting chords of the same quality a half-step away is a basic method for chord embellishment.

## IV chord approach to major I

> When approaching a major I chord you may precede it with a IV chord (a major chord
> whose root is a perfect 4th higher). You can use this technique to handle non-chord tones
> or as harmonization of a chord tone.

**Significance (p. 39).** Teaches a specific harmonic function: using the subdominant as a passing chord creates smooth voice-leading and handles both melodic and harmonic material.

## Secondary dominants concept

> Many jazz chord progressions utilize root movements (from one chord to another) of
> 4ths. As you already know, V7-I is the most common chord progression. In this tech-
> nique, you may consider any chord a I chord and precede it by its own V7 chord, which
> can be enhanced or altered.

**Significance (p. 39).** Defines secondary dominants as a generalization of the V-I principle that can be applied to any chord, expanding the harmonic vocabulary.

## ii-V7-I defines a key

> ii-V7-I progression defines or establishes a key because of the way the roots, 3rds and
> 7ths of these chords move

**Significance (p. 40).** Explains the harmonic power of the ii-V7-I progression: its voice-leading structure creates a strong tonal statement.

## Backcycling definition and method

> Backcycling is a way to extend the ii-V7-I approach covered in Lesson 4.

**Significance (p. 44).** Defines backcycling as a systematic extension of ii-V7-I by repeating the 4th-movement pattern.

## Tritone substitution principle

> When a progression travels from V7 to I, we often replace the V7 chord with a dominant
> chord whose root is a diminished Sth (or augmented 4th) away. We call this interval a
> tritone because it is equal to the distance of three whole steps. The technique of substitut-
> ing a chord a tritone away also works with secondary dominant chords.

**Significance (p. 45).** Teaches tritone substitution as a rule applicable to any dominant chord, enabling reharmonization while maintaining harmonic function.
