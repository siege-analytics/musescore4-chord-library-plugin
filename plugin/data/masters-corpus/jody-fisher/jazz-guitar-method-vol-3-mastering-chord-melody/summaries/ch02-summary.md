# Chapter 2 — Combining Melody and Harmony

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch02.md`._

Chapter 2 teaches the systematic process of arranging melodies with harmonic backing on guitar, starting from understanding melody in single notes across all octaves and proceeding through deliberate key selection to find optimal fingerboard positions. The core techniques include finding chord voicings that place melody notes on top, using octave adjustments to accommodate larger voicing shapes, and building arrangements in layers—basic chords first, then enhanced with passing chords, substitutions, and extensions. The chapter introduces the concept of chord families (major, minor, dominant, etc.) where chords within the same quality are functionally interchangeable, distinguishing chord enhancement (adding extensions to the same chord quality) from chord substitution (changing the root). A characteristic move throughout is the emphasis on flexibility and playability: exploring multiple keys, learning voicings in various fingerboard positions, and treating arrangements as skeletal frameworks rather than fixed compositions. The load-bearing terminology includes lead-sheet adaptation, chord enhancement, chord substitution, and the foundational principle that melody drives voicing selection while taste and context determine how much harmonic enrichment serves the music.
