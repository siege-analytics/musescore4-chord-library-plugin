# Joe Pass Guitar Method (1977) — Statement of Outputs

## Overview

*Joe Pass Guitar Method* (1977) is a twelve-chapter instructional work organized as a staged ascent from foundational fingerboard orientation to sophisticated jazz harmony and autonomous improvisation. The book's master navigational concept — introduced in Chapter 1 and sustained through every subsequent chapter — is the Chord Form: the A, E, C, D, G, and Intermediate Forms serve as vertical anchors on the fingerboard, freeing the improviser from note-by-note searching by providing position-regions within which scalar and melodic material becomes automatic. All melodic and harmonic instruction in the method radiates outward from this anchor. The book's four compositions — Joe's Blues (Ch. 8), Blues for Nina (Ch. 9), Alison (Ch. 11), and Grete (Ch. 12) — function as applied laboratories rather than mere repertoire, embodying in notation the harmonic and voice-leading principles articulated in prose elsewhere.

The book's most characteristic pedagogical move, visible from Chapter 1 through Chapter 11, is the insistence that all learned material must pass through the ear before being applied. Pass states this as an explicit rule in Chapter 1 ("the student is cautioned to use his ears") and restates it behaviourally in Chapter 2, where students are directed to sing or hum their musical ideas while playing them. This ear-first doctrine governs every subsequent chapter: jazz licks in Chapter 7 must be memorized and internalized until the student can hear them; Joe's Blues in Chapter 8 requires students to verify each improvised phrase against the chord they have chosen; and NOBS in Chapter 10 grounds voice leading in a principle of acoustic logic — stepwise motion or common-tone connection — rather than mere theoretical correctness.

The method's harmonic vocabulary is built in carefully sequenced layers. Chapter 5 establishes foundational scale-to-chord pairing rules; Chapter 7 installs the Dominant 7th as the gateway to jazz phrasing; Chapter 10 generalizes this into a principle of dominant flexibility while imposing an economy rule on voicing density; and Chapter 11 applies all of these rules to a real song. Across all twelve chapters, the book teaches a single unified method: anchor to Chord Forms, derive melody from chord tones, reduce voicings to their essential notes, connect voicings by stepwise motion or common tones, and — above all — let the ear govern every decision.

## Systems

### System 1: Chord Forms Navigation System

The Chord Forms Navigation System is the central structural architecture of the Joe Pass Guitar Method. Six Chord Forms — A, E, C, D, G, and Intermediate — define position-regions across the fingerboard, providing vertical frameworks within which scalar and melodic material becomes automatic rather than searched-for. The system specifies how the player moves between Forms via pivot-finger shifts, how practice should be ordered (chromatic key order within a Form, or Form order across a key), and — critically — that every scale-line exercise must begin and end with the parent chord voicing so that melody is never severed from its harmonic root. Mastery of the A–E–C/D Forms is prioritized as the most efficient path to full fingerboard coverage. The ear-verification rule — that learned patterns cannot be applied mechanically without first passing through the ear — is the non-negotiable governor of the entire system. (Chapter 1 summary; Chapter 2 summary; Chapter 4 summary)

**Members:** A Chord Form, E Chord Form, C Chord Form, D Chord Form, G Chord Form, Intermediate Chord Form.

**Traversal Rules:**
- **Pivot-Finger Position Shift** (`PositionContinuity`) — Use the 1st or 4th finger to play two notes when shifting from one Chord Form position to the next. *(Chapter 4 summary, p. 18 — "shift to any string by using either the first finger or fourth finger to play two notes.")*
- **Form-Order and Chromatic-Order Practice Traversal** (`PositionContinuity`) — Traverse all Forms either in chromatic key order within a single Form, or in Form order (C–A–G–E–Intermediate–D) for a given key, covering two octaves per scale. *(Chapter 1 summary, p. 8 — "practice in all Forms either chromatically or in Form order.")*
- **Chord-Frame Scale-Line Traversal** (`PositionContinuity`) — Every scale-line exercise must begin and end with the parent chord voicing. *(Chapter 4 summary, p. 18 — "chords on which these scale lines are based should be played at the beginning and ending of each exercise.")*
- **Chromatic Half-Step Neck Extension** (`PositionContinuity`) — Extend chromatic patterns up the neck by half-steps, moving through successive Form positions. *(Chapter 4 summary, p. 18 — "starting with open E, then moving up by half-steps to extend the exercise.")*

**Modification Rules:**
- **Scale Extrapolation Across All Forms** (`SymmetryMovement`) — Any scale given in abbreviated form must be repeated by the student in all six Forms with the same fingering. *(Chapter 1 summary, p. 5 — "These scales should be repeated in all Forms; the fingering is the same.")*
- **A–E–C/D Form Priority Rule** (`FamilyCoherence`) — Prioritize mastery of A, E, and C/D Forms first; together they cover the fingerboard thoroughly. *(Chapter 1 summary, p. 5.)*
- **Ear-Verification Rule** (`_pending:ear-gate`) — All learned material must pass through the ear before being applied; mechanical application without listening is prohibited. *(Chapter 1 summary, p. 8; Chapter 2 summary, p. 11.)*
- **Dominant 7th Flat-Seventh Form Rule** (`ColorToneRequire`) — When improvising on a Dominant 7th, anchor to the Basic Chord Form and simply flat the seventh tone to reduce mistakes. *(Chapter 7 summary, p. 23.)*

### System 2: Dominant Flexibility and Substitution System

This system governs how the player selects, alters, and substitutes dominant chords across the blues and jazz repertoire taught in the method. Its members are the dominant chord quality families — plain dominant 7th, extended dominants, altered dominants, augmented dominant (Dom7+5), tritone substitute family, and compound altered dominants — each representing a distinct harmonic color available to the improviser. Traversal rules specify which scales pair with which dominant qualities (diminished scales exclusively for plain Dom7; whole-tone scales exclusively for Dom7+5) and establish that melodic lines grow chord-first rather than scale-first. Modification rules define the full menu of interchangeability: extended and altered extensions may substitute freely for plain dominants; E7 and the Bb9 family are tritone-equivalent and interchangeable; compound altered dominants resolve to extended tonics for chromatic voice movement. Free, ear-driven choice among available substitutions — verified phrase by phrase against the chosen chord — is the governing principle. (Chapter 5 summary; Chapter 7 summary; Chapter 8 summary; Chapter 10 summary; Chapter 11 summary)

**Members:** Plain Dominant 7th, Extended Dominants, Altered Dominants, Augmented Dominant (Dom7+5), Tritone Substitute Family, Compound Altered Dominant.

**Traversal Rules:**
- **Diminished Scale Exclusive Dom7 Pairing** (`FamilyCoherence`) — The three diminished scales are played exclusively against Dominant 7th chords. *(Chapter 5 summary, p. 20.)*
- **Whole-Tone Scale Dom7+5 Pairing** (`FamilyCoherence`) — The two whole-tone scales fit exclusively with Dominant 7+5 chords. *(Chapter 5 summary, p. 20.)*
- **Chord-Derived Melodic Line Generation** (`FamilyCoherence`) — Melodic patterns grow out of the chord at the start of each line; lines are chord-first, not scale-first. *(Chapter 5 summary, p. 21.)*

**Modification Rules:**
- **Extended/Altered Dominant Free Substitution** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — Any of +9, b9, 13, 9(6), 11, +5, b5 extensions may be freely substituted for a plain dominant chord. *(Chapter 10 summary, p. 30.)*
- **Tritone Substitution Rule** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — E7 and the Bb9 family are interchangeable; tritone-equivalent dominants substitute freely. *(Chapter 8 summary, p. 24.)*
- **Tonic Dominant Color Variant Palette** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — For the tonic dominant (G7), any of G13, G9, G9(6), G7+9, G7(b9) may be used as color substitutions. *(Chapter 8 summary, p. 24.)*
- **Compound Altered Dominant Chromatic Resolution** (`NCTHarmonization`) — Compound altered dominants (G7+5/b9) resolve to extended tonics (C9(6)) for chromatic voice movement. *(Chapter 11 summary, p. 31.)*
- **Turnaround Bar Pivot Substitution** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — In bar 8, Bm7 may be replaced with F-family dominants or converted to B7/B9/B13 in the second half of the bar. *(Chapter 8 summary, p. 24.)*
- **Ear-Driven Harmonic Choice Verification** (`_pending:ear-gate`) — Students freely choose from available alternatives and verify each improvised phrase against the chord they have chosen. *(Chapter 8 summary, p. 24.)*

### System 3: Voicing Economy and Voice-Leading System

This system governs how chords are voiced and how those voicings connect across a progression. Its members are the voicing-density categories — three-note voicings (preferred), four-note voicings (maximum permitted), and thick/dense voicings (explicitly rejected) — and the harmonic-function chord types that populate actual progressions: tonic (I), subdominant (IV), supertonic (ii), dominant (V), and substituted harmony. Two complementary rules govern movement between chords: voicings must connect through stepwise voice motion or share a common tone, and all chord changes must be reduced to three or four notes, rejecting unnecessary doubling or thickness. These principles, established in NOBS (Chapter 10), are applied in practice in Alison (Chapter 11), where F13, Bbm7, and Ebmaj7 substitute over a C7–F7–Bbmaj7–Eb backbone, and where the ii–V–I with altered dominant extensions (Dm7–G7(13)/G7(+5)–Cmaj7) serves as the core voice-leading template. (Chapter 10 summary; Chapter 11 summary)

**Members:** Three-Note Voicing, Four-Note Voicing, Thick/Dense Voicing (Rejected), Tonic Chord (I), Subdominant Chord (IV), Supertonic Chord (ii), Dominant Chord (V), Substituted Harmony.

**Traversal Rules:**
- **Stepwise Motion or Common-Tone Connection** (`VoiceMotion`) — Voicings must either lead into one another through stepwise motion or share a common tone. *(Chapter 10 summary, p. 30.)*
- **ii–V–I Altered Dominant Voice-Leading Template** (`VoiceMotion`) — The ii–V–I with altered dominant extensions is the core voice-leading template. *(Chapter 11 summary, p. 31.)*
- **Chord-Voicing Substitution Over Backbone** (`SubstitutionExpand`) — Substituted voicings move over the underlying functional backbone (C7–F7–Bbmaj7–Eb). *(Chapter 11 summary, p. 31.)*

**Modification Rules:**
- **Four-Note Density Ceiling** (`DensityCeiling`) — All chord changes must be reduced to three or four notes; no voicing may exceed this density. *(Chapter 10 summary, p. 30.)*
- **Altered Dominant Color-Tone Requirement for Voice Leading** (`ColorToneRequire`) — In the ii–V–I template, the dominant must carry at least one altered extension to generate chromatic voice motion. *(Chapter 11 summary, p. 31.)*
