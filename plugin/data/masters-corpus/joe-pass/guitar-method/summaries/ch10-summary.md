# Chapter 10 — NOBS

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch10.md`._

Chapter 10 introduces dominant chord substitution, teaching that extended and altered dominants (+9/b9/13/9(6)/11/-+5/b5) can be freely substituted for simple dominant chords to add harmonic color and sophistication. The chapter establishes a critical voicing principle—all chord changes should be reduced to three- or four-note chords for economy and clarity—rejecting unnecessary doubling or thickness. A master principle of voice leading governs smooth progressions: voicings must either lead into one another through stepwise motion or share a common tone that connects them, prioritizing legibility and elegance over dense sonorities. These three interconnected rules (dominant flexibility, voicing reduction, and common-tone continuity) form the foundation of modern jazz harmony, replacing rigid voice-leading strictures with a principle of economical yet flexible harmonic movement.
