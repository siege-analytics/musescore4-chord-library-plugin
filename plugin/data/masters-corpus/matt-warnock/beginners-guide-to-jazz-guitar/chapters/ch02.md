## ii V I progression importance

> The ii V lis the most important progression in jazz, and therefore it’s the best
> place to start when learning jazz guitar.

**Significance (p. 4).** This establishes the foundational pedagogical premise of the entire method — that the ii V I is the organizing principle from which all other learning flows.

## ii V I chord shapes

> To begin, here are four chords that fit over the ii V | progression, with the Cmaj7
> and Cmaj9 being two colors of the Cmaj7 sound.
>
> Start by playing these chords on your own, memorize them, and then add in a
> metronome when ready.
>
> From there, get these chords onto the backing track when you can, play it as
> written, then start to play with the rhythms and picking from there.

**Significance (p. 4).** This passage introduces the core chord vocabulary (Dm7, G7, Cmaj7, Cmaj9) and gives the explicit learning sequence: solo practice → metronome → backing track → rhythmic variation.

## Freddie Green rhythm definition

> The first rhythm is called the Freddie Green rhythm, as it was made famous by
> Count Basie guitarist Freddie Green.
>
> The rhythm is all quarter notes, meaning you play one chord per beat.

**Significance (p. 5).** This defines the Freddie Green comping rhythm by name and its structural rule — one chord per beat in quarter notes — giving the student a labeled, reproducible technique.

## Freddie Green accent rule

> To give it a more authentic Freddie sound, accent the 24 and 4" chords/beats in
> each bar.
>
> This imitates the hi-hat and helps you bring a higher level of swing to the rhythm.

**Significance (p. 5).** This is a specific performance rule — accent beats 2 and 4 — that distinguishes authentic swing feel from mere chord-on-every-beat strumming.

## Charleston rhythm definition

> Charleston rhythm, as it’s based on the dance
> from the 1920s.
>
> This rhythm places the first chord on beat 1 of each bar, then the second chord is
> on the & of 2, between beats 2 and 3 in each bar.

**Significance (p. 6).** This defines the Charleston rhythm by name and gives its precise placement rule (beat 1 and the & of 2), making it a teachable, repeatable rhythmic pattern.

## mixing comping rhythms

> From there, mix the Freddie Green and Charleston rhythms together to expand
> on this idea in your comping.

**Significance (p. 6).** This gives the student a compositional rule for developing their own comping — combining the two named rhythms rather than playing either one verbatim — marking the transition from imitation to improvised application.
