# Chapter 4 — Chapter 3 — Approach Chords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 teaches approach chords, a chromatic comping technique where you play the same chord voicing one fret above or below your target chord, then resolve into it to create tension and release. The foundational rule is simple: approach chords are executed by moving the target shape chromatically up or down one fret, establishing the precise mechanical action on the fretboard. The pedagogical sequence—approach from above, then from below, then mixing both directions—governs how students internalize the technique through deliberate practice stages. A characteristic move in the chapter is the emphasis on coloring simplified lead sheet symbols (reading Cmaj7 but playing Cmaj9, for instance), reflecting the real-life jazz convention where performers add harmonic color to basic chord symbols written on lead sheets. Once the shapes are secure, the chapter explicitly instructs students to vary rhythm and picking patterns to expand the exercise beyond the basic mechanics, teaching self-extension as part of mastery. The load-bearing terminology includes approach chords, resolve, comping, and the directional distinction of from above versus from below.
