## Replacing strummed chords

> The old style of strumming chords
> for guitar will never do in modern playing, so we will have to work out a complete system. With this style of
> chords you will learn to play chord solos, make all kinds of introductions, play background for horn players, and all
> kinds of tricky fill-ins.

**Significance (p. 3).** States the book's central premise: rhythm strumming is obsolete and must be replaced by a systematic vocabulary that powers solos, intros, comping, and fills.

## The 26 essential chords

> Below you have 26 chords which you must learn to use right away. There are more chords to learn, but these are
> the most important. We have to limit ourselves to the most important chords, because, as you know, there are so
> many meaningless chords for guitar that this has to be done.

**Significance (p. 3).** Defines the curated 26-chord vocabulary that the rest of the method numerically references, and rationalises the radical pruning by attacking 'meaningless' chord shapes.

## Two starting keys

> If you will notice, some of these chords are designed for the key of G major and some for C major. These are the
> two keys that we are going to work with first. From these keys we will transpose to all of the other keys.

**Significance (p. 3).** Anchors the method's pedagogy in G and C as the seed keys from which every other key is derived by transposition.

## Transposition is mandatory

> It is very important that everything in the book be transposed to all of the other keys. You should be able to play
> any song in any key by the time you finish this book, and learning to transpose is the only thing that can help
> you to do this.

**Significance (p. 3).** Elevates transposition from a study aid to the single non-negotiable practice that defines mastery of the method.

## Keep a music writing book

> Get a music writing book, and write everything in it for retaining as you go on.

**Significance (p. 3).** Prescribes the physical study artefact (hand-written transposition notebook) that the later lessons assume the student is producing.

## Chromatic chord exercise

> Take your G Chords which are the first three chords in Lesson One, and practice them chromatically always up the neck of your guitar as shown below.

**Significance (p. 3).** Establishes Baker's signature drill: moving each chord shape chromatically up the neck to internalise voicings as portable hand-shapes.

## Six core rhythm chords

> By the way, you are only using Chords No. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 for these Exercises. Just transpose them to the proper keys.

**Significance (p. 4).** Restricts the rhythm-chord palette to a tight numbered set, reinforcing the catalog-numbering system that underpins every subsequent comping example.

## Standard vs. new chords

> First, I'm going to write out the standard changes as they are in some songs, and below them I will write the new ones, the way they should be played against the old ones.

**Significance (p. 4).** Introduces the two-line standard/new substitution format that becomes the dominant pedagogical device of the book.

## Don't skip lessons

> Before I take you into Lesson 4, I want to remind you that every lesson in this book is important, (so you are not to skip anything!!). Give every Exercise all the study you can.

**Significance (p. 5).** Codifies the sequential, no-skipping study discipline that the method's cumulative chord-numbering scheme depends on.

## Most important rhythm chords

> In Lesson 3 you had Chord Exercises in the key of F, G, A♭ and B♭. The six chords that we used for those four keys are the most important Rhythm Chords in these keys.

**Significance (p. 5).** Cements the doctrine that six numbered shapes cover the four foundational keys, framing how comping vocabulary scales by transposition.
