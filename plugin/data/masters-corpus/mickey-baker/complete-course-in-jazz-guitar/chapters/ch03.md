## Review before new keys

> Now we come to Chords in the key of C, Dé and E+ Major. I do hope that you have followed my instructions to
> the letter up until now, because if you don’t understand your lessons from one to seven, you surely won't under-

**Significance (p. 9).** Marks the shift to a second three-key cluster (C, D♭, E♭) while reinforcing that the new material is unintelligible without mastery of the prior key-set.

## Chord exercises up the neck

> First, we will work out Chord Exercises as we did in Lesson No. 2. These exercises are to be worked always up and
> down the finger board, just as you have done with all of the other chords.

**Significance (p. 9).** Reapplies the chromatic up-and-down-the-neck drill as the default mechanism for absorbing any new chord shape.

## Major triad substitution rule

> Now then, any chord on a major mode can be used in place of the major triad.

**Significance (p. 10).** States the substitution axiom that licenses replacing every plain major chord with Cma7/Cma6/Cma9 voicings throughout the method.

## Avoid strumming for color

> We don't want to strum a C major chord for the whole two bars, because it doesn’t have enough color, and be-
> sides it sounds corny, so we add color by using progressions that are adaptable as follows:—

**Significance (p. 10).** Articulates Baker's aesthetic motivation: static strumming is 'corny', motion through adaptable voicings is jazz.

## Minor and dominant substitution

> The same thing is true with the minor and dominant chords.
> Such as, if you have a Dmi chord (Any minor chord can be used on the minor mode) Dmi6, Dmi7 and Dmi9
> are adaptable, because they are all on the same mode. With dominant chords there is no limit to what can be done.
> For instance if you have a G7 Chord — you could use G13, G9, G7#5+9, G13#549, G75, G11, G1349 and so on.

**Significance (p. 10).** Extends the substitution rule from major to minor and dominant chords and enumerates the dominant-extension menu that drives all later progressions.

## Substitutions need progression

> It all depends on how the chords are progressing. You just can’t throw these chords in any place, they have to be
> part of some kind of progression.

**Significance (p. 10).** Constrains the substitution licence: voicing choices are governed by voice-leading and progression, not free swap.

## Years to internalize

> This can’t be done overnight. It took me years to figure them out, so it should take you at least two or three months
> to learn them in the key of C major alone.

**Significance (p. 10).** Sets the timescale expectation - months per key - that frames the whole book as long-term study rather than quick reference.
