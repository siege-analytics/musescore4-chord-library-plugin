## Two position sets per key

> With our new chords we only have two sets for each key. Like in the key of C major, one set of chords starts
> at the third position and the other set at the 8th position. This helps you in many ways. One is that we eliminate
> all of those unnecessary chords that there are for guitar. Another great help is that once you master both sets for
> each key, you will be able to transpose from one key to another with great ease.

**Significance (p. 12).** States the two-positions-per-key doctrine that is Baker's central architectural claim about jazz-guitar voicings.

## Tritone-sub via relative minor

> Getting back to the chords, you may notice that most of the time when we have a seventh chord, we substitute
>
> - minor chords in their place. If the chord is G7, I may use Dmi7 to Dmi6, or Dmi7 to G13, or something like
>
> that. The fifth of any dominant chord can be substituted in place of the Dominant itself. Let’s say that you have
> two bars of D7. All right, the 5th of D is A. All you have to do is count up with your fingers D, E, F, G, then A.
> A-minor is a very close relative to D7. Now you know that if you have a dominant 7 chord you can substitute its
> telative minor which is the 5th of the chord.

**Significance (p. 12).** Defines Baker's signature ii-for-V substitution rule - replace any V7 with the minor a fifth above - which is the engine of almost every reharmonisation in the book.

## Build a dominant/minor chart

> In order to really get an understanding of this, you must write out all dominant 7 chords from C7 up to B7 and
> beside them put the relative minor like this: — the Sth of C7 is G minor, the 5th of D¢7 is A’ minor, and go
> always up to B7 and Ff minor.

**Significance (p. 12).** Prescribes the explicit twelve-row dominant/relative-minor chart that operationalises the substitution rule for every key.

## Vamps for jump/novelty tunes

> Now we come to vamps. Vamps are very important in jump tunes and novelty tunes.
> There are many ways to play these vamps, but I’m only writing out the most important progressions for now.

**Significance (p. 12).** Defines vamps as a distinct, genre-anchored category (jump and novelty) and signals that only canonical vamp progressions will be taught.

## Apply bass/arpeggio to vamps

> Try playing these vamps string bass style (see Lesson No. 8) and arpeggio style. This will make the vamp sound
> fuller and better.

**Significance (p. 12).** Reuses the two characteristic textures (string-bass, arpeggio) explicitly on vamps, treating them as universal sonority tools.

## Transpose vamps to seven keys

> After you have worked these vamps out in this key which is C major, transpose them to the keys of: Dé, Eé, F,
> G, A, and Bé, then make up charts similar to this one in each key. Then practice them in every key.

**Significance (p. 12).** Specifies the exact seven-key transposition target list that the vamp study must cover.
