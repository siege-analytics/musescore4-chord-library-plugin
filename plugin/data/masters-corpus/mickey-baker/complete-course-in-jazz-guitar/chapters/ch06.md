## Dominant as leading chord

> This Lesson is the key to all of the other lessons. Here I have written out the best possible way to connect one chord to another using the dominant as leading chord for each example.

**Significance (p. 17).** Declares the chapter's organizing principle: every chord-connection scheme in the book is built around the dominant functioning as the lead-in chord, which governs all subsequent voice-leading choices.

## Three connection categories

> First we have dominant to dominant connections, second, we have dominant to minor connections and third, dominant to major connections. With these few chord connections you can build progressions around any song.

**Significance (p. 17).** Defines the three-category taxonomy Baker uses to reduce all comping situations to a small set of substitution moves, the conceptual scaffolding the rest of the book relies on.

## Transpose to all keys

> After you have worked each of these examples out, make up charts similar to this one in the keys of Db, Eb, F, G, Ab and Bb (See lesson No. 12)

**Significance (p. 17).** Establishes the all-keys transposition discipline Baker enforces on every chord pattern in the book, making position-shift fluency a baseline expectation rather than an advanced exercise.

## Applying chords to songs

> Get four or five popular tunes ( sheet music ) or any kind of song that you like. Study the chord connections, then
> take your new chords and apply them to these songs.

**Significance (p. 17).** Sets the practical application loop: take learned voicings out of the chart immediately and substitute them into real repertoire, which is how Baker's method exits the exercise stage.

## Intros and endings approach

> In your next few lessons I have written introductions, endings, and a lot of chord tricks that modern guitarists use.
> After you learn all of the material, you should put it to work right away. This material can be used with most any
>
> melody, so what you should do first, is to listen to the melody of a song, then try different introductions on them,
> do the same with endings.

**Significance (p. 17).** Frames intros/endings as transposable harmonic templates layered onto any melody, which is the characteristic working-guitarist move Baker is transmitting.

## Ear development by progression-building

> With these chords there are a world of things that can be done. A lot can be done toward developing your ear by
> constantly listening to songs and working out progressions for them.

**Significance (p. 17).** States the ear-training rationale behind the exercise drills: harmonic vocabulary plus repeated listening-and-fitting is the loop that turns the chord catalog into musical intuition.

## Standard chord books resource

> Another thing is to get
> standard chord books, which carry the chords to all of the old tunes. They will be very helpful to you in working
> out chord progressions.

**Significance (p. 17).** Identifies the external source material a student is expected to mine for chord changes, anchoring the method in real-tune practice rather than isolated exercises.

## Transpose intros and endings

> You are also to transpose each introduction to as many keys as possible, and the same with the endings, and the
> other material.

**Significance (p. 17).** Extends the all-keys mandate explicitly to intro and ending phrases, which is what turns a memorized lick into a usable harmonic device on the bandstand.
