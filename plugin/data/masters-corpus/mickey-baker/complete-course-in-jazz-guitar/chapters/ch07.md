## Chords with two names

> When we are in one key a chord may be Gma? while still in another key — the same chord has a different name,
> | Emi7. Below I have analyzed the harmonic structure of these chords. You are to learn both names of these chords
> in all keys.

**Significance (p. 19).** Introduces the relative-substitution principle (Gma6 = Emi7) that underlies most of Baker's later solo-over-changes thinking, requiring the student to internalize each shape under two functional identities.

## Workbook chromatic drill

> Its best to write them out in your work book like this, Gma6 is also Emi7; A’ma6 is also Fmi7. Do this chromat-
> ically always up the finger board with each chord, because in the next few lessons I'm using these chords, and
> both names in some progressions.

**Significance (p. 19).** Defines the chromatic notebook drill Baker uses to fix dual-naming in muscle memory, and signals that subsequent lessons will assume the student can recognize either name on sight.

## Diagram drop-off

> A lot of these progressions will not be diagrammed as they were before. I'll
> simply write the chord and put its number over it, so it will be up to you to find them.

**Significance (p. 19).** Marks the pedagogical pivot from spoon-fed diagrams to numbered-position recall, forcing the student to operate from the chord-number system Baker established earlier.

## Eleven sequences as standards

> In this, our last lesson in chord study, I have written out some of the most used chord progressions in Jazz today. These are standard changes, and if you analyze them and work with them long enough, you will find that they appear in many different songs in various places.

**Significance (p. 23).** Frames the eleven cycles as the canonical harmonic skeletons of the standard repertoire, the substitution-targets every later solo lesson is meant to be played over.

## Sequence transposition assignment

> There are eleven different examples and below each example I have the new changes that sound best in their places. Work them out thoroughly in the keys that they are in, then make up charts of each in all of the other keys: F, G, Ab, Bb, C, Db, and Eb. Then work them out in these new keys.

**Significance (p. 23).** Specifies the exact seven-key transposition regime for the sequences, which converts pattern memorization into the cross-key fluency required for real-time substitution.

## Sequences as transposing aid

> These charts will be very helpful to you in transposing from one key to another. They will also play a very important part in Lesson No. 35 and 46 (See Lesson No. 45 and 46).

**Significance (p. 23).** Flags that the eleven sequences are not isolated exercises but the substrate for the later run-connection and solo lessons, making this chapter the harmonic spine of the book.
