## Ear development practice

> Practice playing your guitar with
> records, listen to solos by horn players, learn to steal solos from records.
> Anything that you hear another musician play, try to play it yourself. Strum
> the chords to any song that you like and hum ideas, — then apply the ideas
> to the guitar.

**Significance (p. 26).** Codifies Baker's prescribed ear-training routine — records, horn solos, transcription by imitation, and hum-then-play — as the non-negotiable companion practice to the written exercises.

## Jam sessions for timing

> Another very important thing for you to do is to hang around jam sessions.
> Do as much sitting with other musicians as possible. Doing this will help to
> develop an ear for music and it also helps with your timing.

**Significance (p. 26).** States that timing is developed socially in live ensemble play, not in solitary practice, framing the bandstand as essential pedagogy rather than optional.

## Two hours per lesson

> Also, give each Lesson at least two hours study a day!

**Significance (p. 26).** Sets the concrete daily-practice quantum Baker assumes for Part Two, calibrating reader expectations about the pace of progress through the solo material.

## 28 exercises as key to solo work

> First, we have exercises from Lesson 24 to 30. Each one is as important to you as eating and sleeping, because
> within these 28 exercises lies the key to solo work. So you are to live with them for the rest of your life.

**Significance (p. 27).** Identifies the specific span of exercises (Lessons 24-30, 28 exercises) as the lifelong technical foundation for everything that follows, marking them as the book's mechanical core.

## Slow practice rule

> Take each exercise and practice it as slowly
> as possible. Only increase the tempo when you can play it relaxed at a slow tempo. Remember, if you cannot piay
> an exercise slowly, you surely can’t play it fast.

**Significance (p. 27).** Establishes the relaxed-slow-before-fast rule that governs how every exercise in Part Two is to be approached, a load-bearing technique-acquisition principle.

## Up-and-down stroke mandate

> Every exercise and solo in this book is to be played with an up and down stroke. In order to play with an up
>
> and down stroke you must start right now. Anything that you play from now on, you are to use up and down
> strokes.

**Significance (p. 27).** Imposes alternate picking as the universal right-hand requirement for the rest of the book, the picking-discipline rule all subsequent runs presume.

## Memorize exercise names

> You are to learn the name of these exercises by heart from one end of the guitar to the other. This will not only
> make you more familiar with your instrument, but each of these runs will play a very important part in your solo
> work,

**Significance (p. 27).** Defines a naming/labeling discipline so the exercises function as a callable vocabulary later — you must be able to retrieve any run by name across the whole neck.

## Notation key for tablature

> The sign "X" is for open string.
> The sign "S" is for the string.
>
> The sign "F" is for the fingering.

**Significance (p. 27).** Defines the symbol legend Baker uses across every exercise diagram in Part Two; without it the S./F. annotations under each exercise are unreadable.

## Picking sequence drill

> *Practice all exercises with Down and Up Strokes first, AVAVAYV.
> Then with up and down. VAVAVAY.

**Significance (p. 28).** Specifies the two-pass picking drill (down-up first, then up-down) that operationalizes the alternate-stroke rule and ensures the wrist is trained in both stroke orientations.
