## Relative run substitution

> In Blues No. I for the first four bars I’m using an Emi7 run. You know that the chord for these four bars is G
> major for three and G7 for one. But Emi7 and Gma6 are the same (relatives), so it can be used as a major run
> (See Lesson 19). The same rule that applies to chords applies to runs.

**Significance (p. 34).** Extends the chords-with-two-names principle from Lesson 19 into the solo domain: a minor-7 run substitutes freely for its relative major chord, the central improvisational license of Baker's method.

## ii-V over bars 9-10

> In Blues progression some-
> times at the ninth and tenth bar we use the V7 for one bar (which is D7 in the key of G major), and the IV7
> for one bar (which is C7 in the key of G major), this allows you to use an Ami7 run for one bar and a Gmi7 run
> for one bar.

**Significance (p. 34).** Defines the V7/IV7 substitution Baker plants at bars 9-10 of the blues and explains how the corresponding ii-min7 runs (Ami7 for D7, Gmi7 for C7) sit on those bars — the workhorse blues-reharm move.

## Altered dominant principle

> Notice
> that when connecting a dominant chord to a major the dominant in most of the time altered, (G7#529 to Cma
> ot G135¢9 to Cma). By altering chords like this they blend together perfectly. This same thing applies to solo
> work.

**Significance (p. 34).** States the always-alter-the-V-into-I rule, a load-bearing voice-leading principle that bridges Baker's chord substitutions to his run choices.

## Substituting minor-7 runs over V

> Whenever you have one or two bars of D7 going into G major, you may use an Ami7 run to a Cmi7 run
> which will blend right into a G major run.

**Significance (p. 34).** Gives the concrete Ami7 → Cmi7 → G major run substitution recipe over V → I, one of the most characteristic Baker improvisational moves and a template for transposing the trick to any key.

## Close relatives in solos

> In the last two bars we have a Bmi7 run which is a relative to Gma7.
> Bmi7 and Gma7 are such close relatives that one can be used for the other at any time.

**Significance (p. 34).** Adds the iii-min7 / I-maj7 relative pair to the substitution toolkit, broadening the relative-substitution rule beyond vi-min7 / I-maj6.

## Universal application

> Now this is something that can be done in any song
> blues, ballad, or bounce tune.

**Significance (p. 34).** Generalizes the altered-V and relative-substitution moves beyond the blues form to ballads and bounce tunes, marking these as cross-genre vocabulary.

## Riff runs deferred

> in the last two bars we have what I call a riff run. Riffs can be used any place, but we're not ready to
> o into riffs yet, so you just accept it for what it is now.

**Significance (p. 34).** Names the riff-run category as a distinct device that lives outside the chord-derived run system, foreshadowing later material while flagging it as exception territory.

## Transpose blues solos

> All of this material is in G
> major and it should be transposed to the keys of: A’, Bé, C, Dé, E4 and F major. The best thing to do is to set
> aside a whole day for each key.

**Significance (p. 34).** Specifies the one-day-per-key practice schedule for transposing Lesson 31 across six keys, an unusually concrete prescription for how to convert single-key knowledge into universal recall.

## Make up new solos drill

> I want you to take the same runs that we have
> in Blues No. 1, 2 and 3. Then change them around in as many different ways as you can possibly think of, just as
>
> I have done.
>
> You should be able to make up at least three more solos for each one of these blue examples.

**Significance (p. 35).** Sets a quantified composition exercise (three new solos per blues example) that operationalizes the transition from imitation to original idea generation.

## Hum runs against chords

> After you've learned the connections by heart, practice strumming the chords and humming the runs to yourself, By
> doing this, you can hear just how these runs sound against the chord progressions. Then take the same runs and
> try to make new ideas.

**Significance (p. 37).** Prescribes the strum-and-hum drill for internalizing run-against-chord sound, the connective tissue between the harmonic substitutions and the melodic vocabulary.

## Tempo and timing variation

> All you have to do is to use different tempos, and timings, and you will get new effects.

**Significance (p. 37).** Names tempo and timing variation as the cheapest, most reliable ways to generate fresh material from existing runs — a phrasing-development rule of thumb.

## Blues solo position

> This solo is in the key of G major, and should be played between the 7th and 12th frets on your guitar. With
> this information you should very easily find the proper fingering.
>
> After you work this solo out in G major, transpose it to the keys of D?, E¢, F, Aé and Be,
> In each of these keys you are to use the same fingering. You only change the position.

**Significance (p. 38).** Pins the Lesson 36 solo to a specific neck region (7th-12th fret) and reasserts that transposition is achieved purely by position-shift with identical fingering, the bedrock mechanical rule of the method.

## Fill-in solo method

> If you will notice I've written two or three bars, and in some places the bars
> are blank. This of course is where you fill in. Over these blank bars I have
> written the type of run I'd like you to use, so all you have to do is fill
> them in.

**Significance (p. 40).** Describes the scaffolded-improvisation exercise format Baker uses in Lesson 37: notated bars interleaved with labeled blank bars, training the student to generate phrases that match a specified run-type.

## Blend over throw-together

> Above all, don’t just throw the runs together. Make sure
> that they blend together as well as those that I have written.

**Significance (p. 40).** States the over-arching phrasing aesthetic Baker insists on: connection and blending of runs, not concatenation — the criterion separating mechanical execution from musical solo.
