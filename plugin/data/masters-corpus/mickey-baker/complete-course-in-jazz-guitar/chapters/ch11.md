## Definition of riff runs

> Riff Runs are only ideas
> put together and forced against the chord. They don’t have to be true to the chord.

**Significance (p. 42).** Defines the central concept of the chapter and licenses the non-diatonic note choices that follow.

## Riffs over G major chords

> Take the first three bars of Lesson No. 37. You know that the chords there are all G major but I’m using a riff
> here which has tones in it that are not Major at all, yet it fits against the chords.

**Significance (p. 42).** Concretely illustrates the forcing-against-the-chord rule with a worked example the student can verify.

## Why riffs replace chord runs in vamps

> The chord
> changes in Vamps move a little too fast for us to use chord runs, so we have to run ideas, which is where riffs
> come in,

**Significance (p. 44).** Explains the structural reason riffs are the right vocabulary for vamp tempos, governing material selection.

## Internalize riffs by singing

> Learn them all by heart, keep practicing them until
> you can sing them as you play them. Once you can sing them to yourself, try playing the chord progressions and
> singing the riffs together.

**Significance (p. 44).** Prescribes Baker's signature internalization method: ear-driven memorization through sung-against-chord practice.

## Substituting minor for dominant

> You know that the Sth of a
> dominant chord can be substituted in the dominant place, because they are
> closely related,

**Significance (p. 49).** States the substitution rule that explains why minor runs appear over dominant chords in the bridge solos.

## Defining position by first finger

> The first six are in what is known as the 3rd position, because your first finger
> falls behind the 3rd fret on your Guitar. The 2nd six are in the 7th position, because your first finger falls behind
> the 7th fret on your Guitar. In other words, the fret that your first finger falls behind is the position that you
> are in.

**Significance (p. 48).** Defines Baker's position-numbering convention, a load-bearing terminology used throughout subsequent lessons.

## Two key positions per key

> In the key of G major, the 3rd and 7th positions are the most important in that key. In the key of Ab major
> it would be the 4th and 8th positions, in B¢ major the 6th and 10th positions and so on.

**Significance (p. 48).** Establishes the two-position framework that organizes solo work across every key.

## C major position exception

> Now, when we get to the
> key of C major, we can use the 8th position, but we can’t use the 12th position, because it is too far up on the
> fingerboard. So, this means that in the key of C major we only have one position for riff runs which is the 8th
> position.

**Significance (p. 48).** Carves out the practical limit of the two-position rule and tells the student which position to use in C.

## Set positions for solo work

> Just as it was with chords it is also with solo work. Having set positions to take solos from makes things so much
> easier. You always know where to start and end your solos.

**Significance (p. 48).** States the underlying pedagogical principle that fixed positions anchor improvisation, paralleling Baker's chord-system approach.

## Up-and-down stroke mandate

> all of this material is to be played with an up and down stroke,
> right from Lesson No. 24 up to this point.

**Significance (p. 49).** Restates the picking-hand rule that governs every run in the book, a load-bearing technical constraint.

## Simplify runs that are too dense

> If some of these runs have too many notes in
> them for you, break them down, use less notes, and play them a little slower.

**Significance (p. 49).** Gives a practical adaptation rule for executing dense runs at tempo, important for student progression.
