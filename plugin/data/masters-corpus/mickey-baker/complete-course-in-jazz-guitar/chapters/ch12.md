## Writing out builds reading

> By continually writing out this material like this, you
> gain a wider musical knowledge, and it helps your reading ability tremendously.

**Significance (p. 52).** States Baker's transposition-by-writing rule and the rationale linking written practice to reading skill.

## Analyze run connections

> Work out each solo to itself. Then analyze it, take close notice as to how I connect my runs.

**Significance (p. 52).** Directs the student to study run-to-run transitions, the load-bearing skill behind building longer solos.

## Forcing riffs against foreign changes

> 2 lot of places in those solos I am using riffs, while the chord changes for these measures are
> completely foreign to the riff itself (See Lesson No. 38). But I have forced the riffs against the progressions.

**Significance (p. 56).** Reinforces the chapter 11 “force against the chord” rule and tells the student where to spot it in the new material.

## Sing riffs over progressions

> study the riffs in each solo, then the chord progressions for those
> measures. Then sing the riffs while you play the chord progressions. This way you can hear just how the riffs
> sound against the chords.

**Significance (p. 56).** Specifies the ear-training drill Baker treats as the verification step for any forced-riff usage.

## New 3rd position for solos

> I am introducing a new position for solo work. This position is to be used for the keys of A4, Bé, C, Dé, and Ee
> major ( it can be used for F, and G major but it is a little out of range).

**Significance (p. 56).** Introduces an additional position with explicit key-applicability constraints, extending the position framework.

## Range of the new position

> These riffs are in the key of Bé major and this position extends from the 3rd to the 6th frets on your Guitar.

**Significance (p. 56).** Pins the fret-range of the newly introduced position, essential for locating the material on the fingerboard.

## Groove riff definition

> When you go through this solo that I have written out, you will find that the first and the last courses are the
> same. This is known as a groove riff. Groove riffs are repeated consecutively.

**Significance (p. 57).** Defines the chapter's new concept — the groove riff — and its structural placement at solo boundaries.

## Where to use groove riffs

> You usually start and end a song with groove riffs. These kind of riffs are only used against Blues and vamp
> changes (progression).

**Significance (p. 57).** Gives the placement-and-applicability rule for groove riffs, scoping the technique to specific song forms.

## Repetition over 12 bars

> in the first and last 12 bars I am using the same run over and over again, That’s the way this
> type of riff is used.

**Significance (p. 57).** Quantifies the repetition pattern that operationalizes the groove riff in a blues form.

## Solo entirely in 3rd position

**Significance (p. 57).** Constrains the execution position for the groove-riff solo, reinforcing the position-discipline rule.
