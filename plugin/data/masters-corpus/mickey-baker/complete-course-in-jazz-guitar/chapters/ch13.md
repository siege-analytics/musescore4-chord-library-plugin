## Finding groove riffs by singing

> If you have a hard time trying to make up groove tiffs, take your Guitar and play the chord progressions to the
> Blues, and start singing any of your riffs, and keep singing them until you can get one that fits in the 12 bar sequence
> three times, as well as the one in Lesson No. 48.

**Significance (p. 59).** Gives a concrete procedure for inventing groove riffs that fit a 12-bar form, an actionable creative rule.

## Implied-melody principle

> I have the
> sample melody written out for the first course, and in the remaining courses I'm building around the melody. Play
> this solo slowly and make it as effective as possible. Go over it consecutively until you know it by heart. Notice
> how you can always hear the melody though it’s not really there.

**Significance (p. 59).** Names the load-bearing aesthetic goal of melody-building solos: the listener should still hear the tune.

## Build by running chords around melody

> I want you to make up a solo with this song, and build around the melody as I have done. All you have to do
> is run chords around it. Keep playing the melody and trying to fit in chord runs until you get some ideas,

**Significance (p. 59).** States Baker's method for constructing melody-based solos — chord runs woven around the tune.

## Position locked at 7th fret

> Every note in this solo is between the 7th and 11th fret on your Guitar (the 7th position).
>
> You are not to leave
> this position to play any part of this solo,

**Significance (p. 61).** Imposes a strict position constraint that disciplines the student's fingerboard control during melody work.

## Solo pattern definition

> Here in each course of these solos
> I have set a different solo pattern, and I keep that pattern going throughout
> the solo.

**Significance (p. 63).** Defines “solo pattern” as a per-course rhythmic frame held constant — the chapter's central new concept.

## Same timing, different notes

> Take Example No. 1; in
> the first bar of the solo we have one quarter note, four sixteenth notes and
> three quarter triplets (lazy triplets). In the second bar you have the same
> identical timing, only the run is different.

**Significance (p. 63).** Operationalizes the solo-pattern rule with an exact rhythmic template the student can transfer to other bars.

## First measure sets the pattern

> Notice how the first measure sets the pattern for. all of the measures
> to follow. This is what we call solo patterns.

**Significance (p. 63).** Distills the solo-pattern rule into a one-line generative principle for building any pattern-based solo.

## Patterns library for future reference
