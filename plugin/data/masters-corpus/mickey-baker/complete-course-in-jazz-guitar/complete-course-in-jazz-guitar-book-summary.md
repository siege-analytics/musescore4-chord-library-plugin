# Book-level distillation — complete-course-in-jazz-guitar

## Central Thesis

Mickey Baker's *Complete Course in Jazz Guitar* Vol. 1 (1955) is built on a single architectural polemic: rhythm strumming is obsolete and must be replaced by a curated, numerically referenced chord vocabulary that powers comping, intros, fills, and ultimately solo lines. Chapter 1 declares this directly and prunes the guitar's chord universe down to 26 essential chords (later extended to 30), dismissing the rest as 'meaningless,' and establishes the load-bearing mandate that defines mastery in the rest of the book - every voicing, intro, vamp, run, and solo must be transposed to every key. The method is unapologetically practical over theoretical: chords are catalogued by number rather than by Roman-numeral function, substitutions are taught as recipes attached to progressions, and ear training is operationalized through jam sessions, record-stealing, and singing-against-the-chord rather than through abstract analysis. The book's spine is the two-line standard-vs-new substitution format introduced in Chapter 1 - the original changes printed above, Baker's reharmonization printed below - and that format carries the entire pedagogy from rhythm comping in Part One through single-note improvisation in Part Two.

## Pedagogical Arc

The book moves in a deliberate two-part sequence from chord vocabulary to single-note improvisation, with each part gated on mastery of the previous. Chapter 1 (Lessons 1-5) installs the 26-chord catalog, the chromatic up-the-neck drill, the manuscript-notebook discipline, and the six-chord rhythm palette (Chords No. 1-6) restricted to the four foundational keys F, G, A flat, and B flat. Chapter 2 (Lessons 6-8) pivots into melody-chord playing, intros, and the two characteristic accompaniment textures Baker elevates above strumming - arpeggio and walking string-bass - while operationalizing the transposition mandate as a literal hand-rewriting of Lesson 4. Chapter 3 (Lessons 9-12) opens the second three-key cluster C/Db/Eb and installs the substitution axiom: any plain major triad can be replaced by Cma7/6/9, any minor by Dmi6/7/9, and any dominant by an extended menu including 13, 9, 7#5+9, 13#5+9, 7b5, 11, and 13b9 voicings - with the load-bearing caveat that substitutions must serve voice-leading, not be 'thrown in.' Chapter 4 (Lessons 13-14) introduces vamps, fill-ins, and Baker's central claim that each key has only two position sets (in C, third and eighth positions), and codifies the dominant-to-relative-minor substitution as a written twelve-row chart. Chapter 5 (Lessons 15-16) extends the catalog to chords 27-30 (open-string-root horn-backing voicings) and culminates in Lesson 16 - flagged by Baker as 'the key to all of the other lessons' - the dominant-as-leading-chord principle and its three-category taxonomy (dominant-to-dominant, dominant-to-minor, dominant-to-major). Chapter 6 (Lessons 17-18) applies this to real tunes via standard chord books. Chapter 7 (Lessons 19-23) is the harmonic spine: the two-names principle, root-omitted tritone-equivalent voicings (G7#5 = Db9b5), the bop blues template, and eleven canonical chord-progression sequences as the standard harmonic skeletons of the repertoire. Part Two opens at Chapter 8 (Lessons 24-26) with the pivot to single-note technique, alternate picking, and the 28-exercise mechanical foundation. Chapters 9-10 (Lessons 27-37) drill scale/minor/dominant/diminished runs, narrow the blues to five horn-friendly keys F/G/Ab/Bb/C, and extend the relative-substitution principle from chords into runs (Ami7 run over D7, Gmi7 run over C7). Chapter 11 (Lessons 38-43) introduces the riff as 'ideas put together and forced against the chord,' justifies it structurally for fast-moving vamp changes, installs the two-position-per-key vocabulary (C major as the lone exception), and defines rhythm changes. Chapter 12 (Lessons 44-48) introduces the additional 3rd position (3rd-6th frets) and the groove riff - same riff repeated, first and last courses matching, executed without leaving 3rd position. Chapter 13 (Lessons 49-52) closes with two solo-construction methods: building around the implied melody under a 7th-11th-fret position lock, and the solo pattern as a per-course rhythmic frame in which the first measure sets the timing for everything that follows.

## Characteristic Baker Moves

A tight set of named operational moves recurs across the book and gives it its distinctive working-guitarist signature. The numbered chord catalog: chords are referred to by number (No. 1 through No. 30), drilled chromatically up the neck, and cross-referenced via circled-number annotations over every progression diagram (Chapters 1, 3, 5). The chromatic up-and-down-the-neck drill: every new chord shape - whether rhythm voicing, intro shape, two-names voicing, or run fingering - is internalized by sliding the same shape chromatically along the neck (Chapters 1, 2, 3, 7, 9). The two-line standard-vs-new format: the original changes on top, Baker's substitutions on bottom, used from Lesson 1 through the bridges and bop blues lessons. The dominant-to-relative-minor substitution: any dominant-7 can be replaced by the minor a fifth above it (G7 by Dmi7, D7 by Ami), drilled as a twelve-row written chart from C7 through B7 (Chapter 4). The dominant-as-leading-chord principle: every chord-to-chord connection in jazz can be built by inserting the appropriate dominant between them, organized into the three categories dominant-to-dominant, dominant-to-minor, dominant-to-major (Chapters 5, 6). The chords-with-two-names principle: one shape carries two functional identities (Gma6 = Emi7, Ama6 = Fmi7), internalized under both names in all keys (Chapter 7). Root-omitted tritone-equivalent voicings: G7#5 and Db9b5 are the same shape with different roots, the structural mechanism behind Baker's b5 substitutions (Chapter 7). The Cycle No. 1 / ii-V replacement recipe: replace each standard change with a two-voicing ii-V or relative-minor pair - Gmi7-Gmi6 for C7, Dmi7-G13b9 for G7 (Chapter 7). The relative-run substitution: a minor-7 run substitutes freely for its relative major chord because the same relative rule that applies to chords applies to runs - the central improvisational license of the method (Chapter 10). The always-alter-the-V principle: the V chord on the way to I is always altered, with the Ami7-to-Cmi7-to-G-major recipe over V-to-I as the workhorse template (Chapter 10). The forced riff against foreign changes: riffs are 'ideas put together and forced against the chord' and need not be diatonic - because vamp changes move too fast for chord-tone runs, riffs are the primary melodic unit there (Chapters 11, 12). The position-by-first-finger system: position is named by the fret behind which the first finger falls, each key has exactly two governing positions (C major excepted), and fixed positions anchor solo work the same way they anchor chords (Chapter 11). The groove riff: a riff whose first and last courses match, repeated consecutively across the first and last 12 bars of a blues, executed without leaving a single position - typically the 3rd (Chapter 12). The implied-melody principle: build solos by running chords around the melody under a strict position lock so the listener still hears the tune even when it is not literally present (Chapter 13). The solo pattern: a per-course rhythmic frame held constant throughout the solo, where the first measure sets the timing for everything that follows (Chapter 13). The fill-in solo method: blank bars carry a labeled run-type so the student scaffolds improvisation by selecting from the named run vocabulary (Chapter 10). And the sing-against-the-chord internalization rule: riffs and runs must be sung against the changes until they are heard before they are played (Chapters 10, 11, 12).

## Sanctioned Departures and Bounded Exceptions

The book is otherwise rigorously universal - transpose everything, drill everything, alternate-pick everything - but it carries a small set of explicitly sanctioned departures that the student must learn to recognize. Open-string-root vamp shapes (Chords 27-30) are key-specific and cannot be uniformly transposed, which is why the new vamp study abandons C major for G; Baker flags this directly as a critical caveat on the otherwise-universal transposition mandate (Chapter 5). The transposition target is narrowed from all keys to seven (Db, Eb, F, G, A, Bb plus the home key) for vamps (Chapter 4), to six (Db, Eb, F, G, Ab, Bb) for intros and endings (Chapter 6) and for blues solos (Chapter 10), and to the five horn-friendly keys F/G/Ab/Bb/C for the blues runs themselves (Chapter 9) - the principle is universal coverage, but the practical target list contracts to working-guitarist reality. The riff run is explicitly flagged as a deferred category outside the chord-derived run system - it does not have to be diatonic and is forced against the chord rather than derived from it (Chapters 10, 11). The minor-for-dominant substitution licenses minor material over dominants in riff work (Chapter 11). The two-position-per-key rule has C major as its lone single-position exception (Chapter 11). The additional 3rd position (3rd-6th frets) introduced in Chapter 12 is explicitly key-scoped: usable in Ab/Bb/C/Db/Eb major, only marginally usable in F and G. Dense runs are sanctioned to be thinned and slowed rather than abandoned - a pragmatic adaptation rule (Chapter 11). And the diagram drop-off: from Chapter 7 onward Baker pivots from spoon-fed diagrams to numbered-position recall, treating diagram-dependence as a training-wheels phase the student must outgrow.

## Aesthetic Axioms

A compact set of aesthetic claims underwrites every prescription in the book and accounts for its reputation as the 1955 practical-over-theoretical text. Static two-bar strumming is 'corny' and lacks color - motion through adaptable voicings is what makes the music jazz, and the entire substitution apparatus exists to serve this aesthetic (Chapter 3). Substitutions are not free - they must serve an actual progression and its voice-leading, never be 'thrown in' for color alone (Chapter 3). Combinatorial creativity outranks vocabulary size: a small fixed chord set generates unlimited intros if the student varies rhythm (root-then-chord, arpeggiated, recombined) - the constraint is the engine of invention, not its enemy (Chapter 2). The exercise vocabulary IS the improvisational material - blues solos are built from the exact runs of Lessons 27-30 with no modification, so practice and performance share a single substrate (Chapter 9). Blending outranks throwing-together: runs are joined into connected lines, not stacked atop each other - the aesthetic that distinguishes a musical solo from a lick parade (Chapter 10). Ideas are heard before they are played: riffs and runs are sung against the chords until internalized, then executed - imagination precedes fingers (Chapters 10, 11). Timing is developed socially at jam sessions, not alone with a metronome (Chapter 8). Ear development happens by playing along with records, stealing horn solos, and humming ideas against the chord - the working musician's autodidactic loop (Chapter 8). The listener should still hear the tune even when it is not literally present - the implied-melody criterion that anchors all solo construction (Chapter 13). Position discipline is itself an aesthetic: groove-riff solos must be executed without leaving a single position, and melody-built solos lock between 7th and 11th frets (Chapters 12, 13). And the meta-axiom that closes the book: repeated review of earlier lessons is itself a learning mechanism, since each pass reveals something new (Chapter 13).

## The Method as a Unit

Taken together, Vol. 1 records an integrated working-guitarist method with four interlocking commitments. First, vocabulary is numbered, finite, and load-bearing: 30 chord shapes, a small two-position-per-key system, 28 run exercises, eleven canonical progression sequences, and a fixed-symbol notation key (X open string, S string, F fingering). Second, transposition is the mechanical definition of mastery - mandatory in principle, contracted to practical key-lists in practice, and operationalized as physical re-writing into a manuscript notebook and a scrap book the student must build alongside the work. Third, substitution is the harmonic engine, organized into a small number of named principles (relative-minor for dominant, two-names, root-omitted tritone-equivalents, dominant-as-leading-chord with its three connection categories, always-alter-the-V) that drive every reharmonization in the book and license the relative-run substitutions that unify chord study with solo study in Part Two. Fourth, single-note improvisation is built directly out of the exercise vocabulary: chord runs come from the chord shapes, blues solos come from the run exercises, riffs are licensed to be forced against foreign changes, and solos are organized by position lock, implied melody, and per-course rhythmic patterns. The whole sits inside an explicit practical-over-theoretical stance: chords are numbered rather than function-labeled, ear is developed at jam sessions and against records rather than through analysis, and the load-bearing artifacts are the 26-to-30 catalog, the chromatic up-the-neck drill, the standard-vs-new two-line format, the manuscript notebook, and the student's own hand re-writing every lesson into every key.
