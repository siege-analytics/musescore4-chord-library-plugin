# Chapter 3 — Chords in C/Db/Eb, Harmonic Structure and Chord Progressing (Lessons 9-12)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 shifts the workspace to a second three-key cluster - C, D flat, and E flat major - and reapplies the chromatic up-and-down-the-neck drill as the default mechanism for absorbing each new chord shape (Review before new keys; Chord exercises up the neck). Its central theoretical contribution is the substitution axiom: any chord on the major mode (Cma7, Cma6, Cma9) can replace a plain major triad, and the same licence extends to minor (Dmi6/7/9) and to a near-unlimited dominant menu including 13, 9, 7#5+9, 13#5+9, 7b5, 11, and 13b9 voicings (Major triad substitution rule; Minor and dominant substitution). Baker frames this aesthetically: static two-bar strumming is 'corny' and lacks color, while motion through adaptable voicings is what makes the music jazz (Avoid strumming for color). The substitution licence is then bounded by a load-bearing constraint - voicings cannot be thrown in anywhere, they must serve an actual progression and its voice-leading (Substitutions need progression) - and the circled-number annotation system over each chord ties every progression diagram back to the Lesson 1 catalog (Chord-number annotation). The chapter also sets explicit study-timescale and drill-load expectations: months per key, twelve repetitions of each example daily (Years to internalize; Daily repetition mandate).
