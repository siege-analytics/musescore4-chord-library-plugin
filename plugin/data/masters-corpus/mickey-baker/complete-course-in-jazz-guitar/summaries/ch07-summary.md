# Chapter 7 — Chords with Two Names, Intros, Bop Blues, Endings and Sequences (Lessons 19-23)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch07.md`._

This chapter (Lessons 19-23) teaches the relative-substitution principle — that one chord shape carries two functional names (Gma6 = Emi7, Ama6 = Fmi7) and must be internalized under both identities in all keys (Chords with two names), drilled chromatically up the fingerboard in a workbook (Workbook chromatic drill). Baker pivots the pedagogy here from spoon-fed diagrams to numbered-position recall (Diagram drop-off) and documents the load-bearing tritone-substitution insight that G7#5 and Db9b5 are equivalent root-omitted voicings (Root-omitted voicings), the mechanism behind his b5 substitutions. The chapter then introduces the bop blues reharmonization template in G major (Bop Blues in G major) and culminates in eleven canonical chord-progression sequences framed as the standard harmonic skeletons of the repertoire (Eleven sequences as standards), to be transposed through F, G, Ab, Bb, C, Db, and Eb (Sequence transposition assignment). The characteristic Baker move on display is replacing each standard change with a two-voicing ii-V or relative-minor pair (e.g. Gmi7 Gmi6 for C7, Dmi7 G13b9 for G7 — Cycle No. 1 over standard changes), and the chapter explicitly flags these sequences as the substrate for the later run-connection and solo lessons (Sequences as transposing aid), making it the harmonic spine of the book.
