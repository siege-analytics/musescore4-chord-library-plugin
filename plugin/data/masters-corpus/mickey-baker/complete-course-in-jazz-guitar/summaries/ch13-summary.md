# Chapter 13 — Building Around the Melody and Solo Patterns (Lessons 49-52)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch13.md`._

Chapter 13 teaches two complementary approaches to constructing solos: building around a stated melody, and building from a fixed rhythmic template called a solo pattern. The melody-building method is governed by an aesthetic rule — the listener should still hear the tune even when it is not literally present (Implied-melody principle) — and operationalized by running chords around the melody until ideas emerge (Build by running chords around melody), all executed under a strict position lock between the 7th and 11th frets (Position locked at 7th fret). The chapter also offers a concrete creative procedure for inventing groove riffs by singing candidate riffs against a 12-bar blues until one fits three times through (Finding groove riffs by singing), tying back to Chapter 12's groove-riff concept. The load-bearing new term is the solo pattern, defined as a per-course rhythmic frame held constant throughout the solo (Solo pattern definition), demonstrated by identical timing across bars with different pitches (Same timing, different notes) and distilled to the generative principle that the first measure sets the pattern for everything that follows (First measure sets the pattern). Baker's characteristic moves are visible in his framing of the chapter's worked examples as a permanent pattern library keyed to progression type (Patterns library for future reference) and in the meta-rule that closes the chapter — repeated review of earlier lessons is itself a learning mechanism, since each pass reveals something new (Review to keep learning).
