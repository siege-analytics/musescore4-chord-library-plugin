## Relative chords — same tones

> the C6 chord and the
> Am7 chord have the same tones. They are called relatives for this reason

**Significance (p. 12).** Defines the concept of relative chords by identity of tones, establishing the theoretical basis for chord substitution used throughout the chapter.

## Relative chords — upper voices

> The G9 chord and the Dm chord are relative because the three tones that form the Dm
> chord are the upper three tones of the G9 chord

**Significance (p. 12).** Extends the relative-chord definition to a subset relationship, showing how a triad can be extracted from an extended dominant chord.

## II chord substitution for V

> When there is achord progression that moves only from dominant to tonic, it is possible
> to use the II chord instead of the V and resolve into the V from the II

**Significance (p. 12).** States the core substitution rule of the chapter — inserting the II chord before the V — which underpins all four progression variants that follow.

## Expanding I-V-I with II

> if you are playing in the key of C Major and you have one bar of "C", two bars
> of "G7", and one bar of "C", (Example 22) it is possible to use the progressions in Ex-
> ample 22 Parts A B C and D. Notice how in each of these examples we add more chords
> to the cycle yet, basically, the chords move from I to V and from V back to I.

**Significance (p. 12).** Grounds the abstract substitution rule in a concrete four-bar skeleton and directs the student's attention to the invariant I-V-I motion beneath all elaborated versions.

## Rule: eventual dominant resolution

> no matter how many chords you add to the cycle, they should
> eventually reach the dominant and from there resolve into the tonic.

**Significance (p. 13).** Gives the governing rule for all chord-cycle elaboration: any number of added chords is permissible as long as the dominant-to-tonic resolution is preserved.
