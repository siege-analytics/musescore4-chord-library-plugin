## Forced Harmony defined

> "Forced Harmony” (adding
> chords via progressions that move away from, then lead back into the tonic.) It keeps the
> music active rather than letting it die on a "C" or "G" chord as it was originally written.

**Significance (p. 14).** This is the book's explicit definition of Forced Harmony, a core compositional concept the chapter is built around.

## Upper Partials of dominant chord

> The dominant chord canbe the dominant 7th, 9th, 11th,
> aug. lith or 13th. The Sth or the 9th can be Flatted, nevertheless, these are still the
> “Upper Partials" of the dominant chord (Example 23.) They can be used to resolve into
> the tonic.

**Significance (p. 14).** This passage defines the full range of altered dominant extensions (Upper Partials) and states their functional role: resolving to the tonic.

## II chord Upper Partials and resolution chain

> With the II chordof the scale the same principal applies, you have the 6th, 7th,
> 9th, Lith, and 13th. Any of these forms of the II chord can be used to resolve into the V
> which in turn resolves into the tonic (Lesson 7.)

**Significance (p. 14).** This establishes the II-V-I resolution chain using Upper Partials of the II chord, the foundational jazz harmonic movement the method teaches.

## II chord substitution for V

> Now that you know that the II chord can be readily substitutedfor the V, let us discuss the
> "Upper Partials" of these chords.

**Significance (p. 14).** This sentence asserts the II-for-V substitution rule as established knowledge and pivots the lesson toward extended/altered harmony.

## Chord melody arrangement philosophy

> When writing an arrangement for a song, which is
> exactly what you are doing when you make up a chord melody solo, you use the ideas and
> musical sounds that sound best to you rather than the ideas and chord cycles that were
> originally written for the song.

**Significance (p. 14).** This is the method's core rule for chord-melody arranging: player judgment over original changes, which frames why altered chords and substitutions are valid.

## Original progressions as starting point

> The original chord progressions of the song are routine
> and much is left to your imagination. For example, when listening to a Jazz arrangement
> of a standard song, the chord progressions used in most cases are foreign to the original
> progressions written for that song

**Significance (p. 14).** This gives the student explicit license — and the jazz rationale — for departing from written chord sheets when building a solo arrangement.
