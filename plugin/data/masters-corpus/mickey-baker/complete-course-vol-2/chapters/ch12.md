## Authentic Cadence definition

> At the close
> of these sequences there is a "Cadence" which ends a musical thought. This is done by
> progressing into the dominant chord of the tonality and from there into the tonic. We call
> this the “Authentic Cadence".

**Significance (p. 20).** Core definition of the chapter's central concept, establishing both the term and its harmonic mechanism (V to I motion).

## Eight/sixteen bar sequences

> Most popular music is written ineither eight or sixteen bar sequences.

**Significance (p. 20).** Establishes the structural context in which cadences operate, anchoring the concept in common popular-music form.

## Introduction vs. ending distinction

> The
> difference between an introduction and an ending is that the introduction stops on the V
> (Example 29). From this point you approach the first chord of the song. An ending
> approaches the V and moves on to the I, which is the final statement.

**Significance (p. 20).** Gives a precise, actionable rule for distinguishing introductions from endings based on whether the cadential motion stops on V or resolves to I.

## Cadence in short sequences

> The sequence does not have tobe eight or sixteen bars. There are introductions and end-
> ings in which the "Authentic Cadence" appears that only consist of one or two bars.

**Significance (p. 20).** Expands the application of the Authentic Cadence beyond standard phrase lengths, teaching that the concept applies at any structural scale.
