## chords with multiple names

> Many of the chords that were used in the examples of lesson 19 have multiple names.
> This fact is evident in many cases of chord and melody playing. The most important of
> these chords are analyzed in exercise 50. Study the analysis and review lesson 19. Pay
> minute attention to how chords appear time and time again bearing one name at one point
> and another the next.

**Significance (p. 31).** Establishes chord enharmonic equivalence and multiple naming conventions.

## arpeggiated bass melodic line

> In many cases of chord and melody playing the original chord structure is dropped for
> certain melodic passages. For example, in exercise 49 all the chords are played arpeg-
> gio but the first note of each chord is the most important. It forms a melodic line at the
> base of the chord and gives the whole example originality. If each of those notes were
> written out in half tones, you would have a counter melody to the original.

**Significance (p. 31).** Teaches a foundational chord-melody technique: prioritizing the bass voice of arpeggiated chords to create an independent counter-melody.

## creating non-standard chords for melody

> It is not always possible to harmonize the melody with chords that are familiar to you. At
> this point it becomes necessary to create chords that will fit the melody and also blend
> well musically with the cnord cycles you are creating. For instance, in the Sth bar of ex-
> ercise 49, there is a D Diminished chord which is not a standard fingering. However, it
> does fit well into the pattern used. As you go along you will find it necessary tocreate
> chords for effect that may never be useful to you again.

**Significance (p. 31).** Direct rule and rationale for inventing non-standard chord voicings as needed.
