# Chapter 7 — Chords and Song Forms

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch07.md`._

Chapter 7 teaches the application of four specific chords (1, 4, 15, and 17) to well-known standard tunes, establishing a methodical approach to harmonic practice within real song repertoire. The master's characteristic move is the voice-leading progression from major or minor 9th chords to their 6th chord resolutions, a structural rule that governs both exercises and defines the harmonic motion being drilled. Through this practice, students develop toward counterpoint—the musical technique of two melodic lines playing against one another—which emerges as the conceptual destination these chord exercises build toward. The chapter introduces a universal fingering rule for chord diagrams: the first finger always falls behind the fret that represents the position the chord occupies, as exemplified by the Dm69 chord in fifth position. The load-bearing terminology includes chord position, the 9th and 6th chord qualities, and counterpoint itself—all foundational concepts that integrate harmonic knowledge with ensemble thinking.
