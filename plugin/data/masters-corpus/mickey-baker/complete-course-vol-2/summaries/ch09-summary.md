# Chapter 9 — Related Chords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 teaches the concept of relative chords—chords that share identical tones or where one chord's tones form a subset of another's (such as C6 and Am7, or Dm as the upper three tones of G9)—and applies this principle to chord substitution within progressions. The core technique demonstrates how to insert the II chord before the V chord in a I-V-I progression, transforming a simple four-bar skeleton (one bar I, two bars V, one bar I) into four elaborated variants that add intermediate chords while preserving the underlying harmonic motion. The master's characteristic move is methodical expansion: each of the four examples in Example 22 adds more chords to the cycle, yet all maintain the fundamental I-V-I shape, showing how substitution creates complexity without obscuring underlying structure. The load-bearing rule states that no matter how many chords are added to elaborate a cycle, they must eventually reach the dominant and resolve into the tonic, establishing the constraint that governs all permissible elaborations. This chapter introduces the concept of relative chords and the II-V substitution as foundational tools for reharmonizing simple progressions.
