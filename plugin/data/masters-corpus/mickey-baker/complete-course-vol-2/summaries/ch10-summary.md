# Chapter 10 — Altered Chords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch10.md`._

Chapter 10 introduces Forced Harmony—a compositional technique that adds chords via progressions moving away from and back to the tonic, keeping music active rather than static—and establishes the Upper Partials as the load-bearing concept for chord alteration: the dominant can be extended to 7th, 9th, 11th, augmented 11th, or 13th, with the 5th or 9th potentially flatted, all resolving to the tonic; the II chord follows the same principle with its own Upper Partials (6th, 7th, 9th, 11th, 13th) resolving through the V to the tonic, forming the jazz II-V-I resolution chain. The master's characteristic move is privileging player judgment and arranging creativity over literal adherence to original chord progressions, positioning the student's ear and "musical sounds that sound best to you" as the authority for substitutions and alterations. The chapter asserts that II can substitute for V and that jazz arrangements characteristically diverge from standard written progressions, granting the soloist explicit license to rebuild harmony through extended chords and harmonic movement rather than playing changes as written.
