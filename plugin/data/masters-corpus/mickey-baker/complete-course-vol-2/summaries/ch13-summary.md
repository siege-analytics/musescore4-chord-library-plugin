# Chapter 13 — Symetric Harmony

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch13.md`._

Symmetric Harmony is a key-signature-free system in which chords progress freely without the hierarchical tonal center of diatonic harmony, introduced at cadences and structural breaks where the tonic chord anchors endings. The chapter's core principle states that in Symmetric Harmony every chord functions as a tonic, with practitioners selecting any number of tonics; the practical framework divides the 12-tone scale into four equal parts using a T1-through-T4 notation system where each chord is labeled with a "T" and number to indicate which tonic it represents. The master technique centers on the diminished 7th chord, which moves freely up and down the fingerboard in minor thirds, maintaining the same tones across positions but in different arrangements. Because a diminished chord is constructed from successive minor thirds, each tone within it serves as a potential root or tonic, making the chord enharmonically equivalent regardless of inversion or position—a property demonstrated in Exercise 31b. This system exemplifies the characteristic move of treating harmonic movement as a non-linear, symmetrically distributed space rather than a tonally centered journey.
