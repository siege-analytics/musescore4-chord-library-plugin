# Chapter 15 — Symetric Introductions and Endings — Part 1

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch15.md`._

Chapter 15 teaches how to apply the Symmetric Cycle—a harmonic device that transcends diatonic harmony—as an ending device to create dynamic, unexpected closures in guitar compositions. The master's characteristic move is the directional glissando in minor thirds: if a phrase ends high on the fingerboard, move the final chord down in minor thirds; if it ends low, move it up. The chapter introduces a concrete application method: take existing introductions and endings from earlier lessons and replace their final chords with Symmetric Cycle endings, as demonstrated in Exercises 36 and 37, which mirror the opening bars of Exercises 29C and 30A before concluding with this technique. A key expansion is that Symmetric Cycle need not use the entire chord—partial chord fragments (as shown in Lesson 11, Exercises 25A and 25B) can execute the same glissando movement, creating smoother voice-leading while maintaining the harmonic surprise. The load-bearing terminology centers on the Symmetric Cycle itself, minor thirds as the intervallic vehicle for the motion, and the concept of chord fragments as viable alternatives to complete voicings.
