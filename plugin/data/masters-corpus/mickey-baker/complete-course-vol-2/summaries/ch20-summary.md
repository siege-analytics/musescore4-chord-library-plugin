# Chapter 20 — Chord Analysis

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch20.md`._

Chapter 20 teaches chord analysis through the critical insight that chords possess multiple names and enharmonic equivalences, requiring players to recognize the same chord structure bearing different names across different contexts. The master's characteristic move is prioritizing the bass voice in arpeggiated chord passages—allowing the lowest note of each chord to form an independent melodic line or counter-melody, which he demonstrates in Exercise 49 by dropping the original chord structure in favor of arpeggio patterns where the bass note drives originality. A foundational technique introduced here is chord-melody playing, where the arpeggiated bass creates harmonic foundation while carrying melodic content. The chapter establishes the load-bearing principle that chords need not follow "standard" fingerings when serving the melody: non-standard voicings are legitimate tools for musical blending and effect, requiring players to invent chords that fit both the melodic line and the harmonic cycle being created. This adaptive approach—creating chords for specific musical context rather than relying only on familiar forms—becomes essential as harmonic complexity increases.
