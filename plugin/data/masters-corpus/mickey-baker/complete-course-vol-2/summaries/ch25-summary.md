# Chapter 25 — Counterpoint

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch25.md`._

Counterpoint is the movement of two or more independent melodies simultaneously rather than the use of chord harmony, representing an essential technique in modern music that derives from classical tradition and particularly benefits small ensemble playing. The fundamental rule is that when two lines move against one another, one melody can sustain itself for several beats while the other line moves in faster note values, creating rhythmic contrast and textural variety. The technical terminology labels these voices as the Cantus Firmus (the original melody) and the Counter melody (the added melody), though simpler names suffice in practice. The master's characteristic approach prioritizes harmonic integrity above all—the examples avoid bad harmonic movement and move chordally from one direction to another without lingering excessively on single notes, ensuring that melodic independence never compromises the underlying harmonic foundation. A key technique is the sustaining counter melody, which extracts only the first note from each chord to create a strong, straight-line bass quality while the primary melody dances freely above it, as demonstrated in the progression from Exercise 49 to Exercise 65.
