## choro origins and instrumentation

> The term “choro” was generically used to describe a small group (flute, cavaquinho
> and seven stringed guitar), often with one group member as a soloist (improvisor).
> Actually it is also played with other instrumentation (clarinet, trombone and a
> few percussion instruments — particularly “pandeiro” and “surdo”). ,

**Significance (p. 86).** Defines the canonical choro ensemble and names the specific instruments a guitarist must understand when situating their role in the style.

## choro melodic characteristics

> Most choro melodies are based on 16th notes with frequent use of melody
> embellishments. It’s usually written in 2/4, but you will also find it in 3/4 (choro
> waltz).

**Significance (p. 86).** Gives the two defining rhythmic and metric rules a player must know to recognize and perform authentic choro phrasing.

## choro harmony rules

> In the harmony the use of triads, seventh and diminished chords are common,
> usually in an inverted position. The bass line is also very important.

**Significance (p. 86).** States the harmonic vocabulary and the structural priority of inverted voicings and bass movement that distinguish choro accompaniment from other styles.

## choro form structure

> The choro form is frequently divided in 3 parts — A, B and C — with a modulation

## pandeiro pattern on guitar

> Like other Brazilian styles, the patterns that we use on the guitar for the choro are
> simulations of the rhythm section. This basic pattern is a typical “pandeiro”
> pattern applied to the guitar.

**Significance (p. 88).** Establishes that choro guitar rhythm is a percussive simulation of the pandeiro, which governs how all subsequent patterns are understood.

## muting practice technique

> You can practice these patterns and variations muting the strings with your left
> hand. It’s also a good idea to practice away from the guitar, clapping hands in the
> high voices and tapping the low voices with your foot.

**Significance (p. 88).** Provides a concrete two-part practice method—left-hand muting and body-percussion subdivision—for internalizing the rhythmic independence between high and low voices.

## switching between variations

> Each variation can be played through the entire song or you can switch between
> the variations to create a new pattern. It’s also common to use a different pattern
> or add bass line movements at the end of a section or a “cadenza.”

**Significance (p. 89).** Gives the practical rule for deploying multiple rhythmic variations within a single performance, including the convention of changing patterns at section endings.
