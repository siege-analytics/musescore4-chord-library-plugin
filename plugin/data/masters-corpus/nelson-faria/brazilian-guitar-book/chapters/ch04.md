## Frevo rhythmic character

> Primarily dance oriented, the frevo is basically a march, with syncopated figures in
> the melody and in the rhythmic patterns, usually up tempo.

**Significance (p. 102).** Defines the foundational rhythmic identity of frevo: syncopation and tempo context that governs all the guitar patterns.

## Frevo time signature

> Frevo is usually written in 2/4 although you can find the use of 3/4 or 4/4. The
> examples in this book are written in 4/4. The melodic accent comes on the up
> beat and the melodies are pretty much syncopated.

**Significance (p. 102).** Gives essential metric and accentuation rules for reading and performing frevo examples.

## Frevo bass and harmony style

> The
> bass part is much like a jazz “walking bass”, playing even quarter notes, and the
> harmonies are quite simple, usually turnarounds.

**Significance (p. 102).** Cross-references frevo bass to jazz walking bass and notes turnaround-based harmony, shaping how to comp.

## Right-hand pattern: syncopation placement

> Keep in your mind that the syncopations are played on
> the top voices of the chord, while the bass note comes on the beat.

**Significance (p. 104).** Core rule for basic frevo guitar pattern: prescribes which voices carry the syncopation and which carry the downbeat pulse.

## Bass note: root vs. fifth

> As in other Brazilian styles, the bass line keeps switching between the root and
> the fifth of the chord, and it's a better choice to play the fifth below the root.
> If the bass note is already placed on the 6th string, you may keep the same note
> for the whole measure.

**Significance (p. 104).** Governs left-hand voicing choices for every frevo chord, dictating the idiomatic bass motion.

## Using and combining variations

> at the end of a section or a "cadenza".
