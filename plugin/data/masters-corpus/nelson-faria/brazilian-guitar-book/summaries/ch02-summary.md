# Chapter 2 — PART II - Bossa Nova

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch02.md`._

Chapter 2 teaches the bossa nova rhythm by grounding all right-hand patterns in the "clave," a foundational rhythmic cell that must remain directionally consistent throughout a piece, with the core technique split between fingers handling syncopated upper voices and thumb anchoring bass notes on root-fifth alternation. The chapter introduces four variations of increasing complexity—Variation #1 adds 16th-note syncopation equivalent to samba patterns, Variation #2 extends to two-measure units, Variation #3 employs staccato articulation with registral reduction, and Variation #4 serves specific performance contexts like singer's pauses with optional 16th-note displacement—each with precise fingering and harmonic-rhythmic alignment rules. Characteristic of the method's pedagogical approach, Faria prescribes off-guitar practice (clapping the bass-upper voice split) to internalize the rhythmic independence before executing it on the instrument, and provides explicit five-note chord fingering instruction for dense voicings. The load-bearing terminology introduced is "clave" (the rhythmic cell), "syncopation" (as applied to upper-voice displacement), and the concept of "extended patterns" spanning multiple measures.
