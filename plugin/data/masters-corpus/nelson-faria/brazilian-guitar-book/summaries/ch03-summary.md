# Chapter 3 — PART III - Choro

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 teaches the essential characteristics and performance practices of choro, a Brazilian style defined by 16th-note melodies in 2/4 (or 3/4 waltz) time, played by a small ensemble of flute, cavaquinho, and seven-stringed guitar with frequent soloist improvisation. The harmonic vocabulary centers on triads, seventh chords, and diminished chords typically in inverted positions, with a strong emphasis on bass-line movement, while the form follows a standard three-part structure (A, B, C) that modulates to the V or IV chord key center in part C. Guitar patterns in choro simulate pandeiro (a percussion instrument) rhythms, requiring the guitarist to coordinate high and low voices as independent rhythmic streams—a skill developed through left-hand muting practice and body-percussion exercises (hand-clapping and foot-tapping). The chapter introduces the practical flexibility of switching between multiple rhythmic variations throughout a song and the idiomatic convention of changing patterns at section endings or cadenzas, establishing variation deployment as a core performance technique in the style.
