# Chapter 4 — PART IV - Frevo

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 introduces frevo, a syncopated march style that is dance-oriented, typically up tempo, and most commonly written in 4/4 time with melodic accents on the upbeat. The guitar's foundational pattern places syncopations on the top voices of the chord while the bass note (which alternates between the chord's root and fifth, preferably with the fifth below the root) lands on the beat, mirroring the "walking bass" approach of jazz. The chapter establishes that frevo bass lines keep the sixth string rooted where possible and that harmonies are simple, usually turnarounds. Faria teaches five variations that can be deployed individually throughout a song or mixed to create new patterns, with distinct rhythmic characters: Variation #1 introduces new syncopation on the third beat, Variation #4 demonstrates an up-beat-only texture (optionally executed with left-hand muting of downbeats), and Variation #5 illustrates marcha rancho in 3/4 meter. The load-bearing terminology introduced includes the frevo's syncopated rhythmic character, the bass-root-fifth alternation pattern, the walking-bass analogy, and the concept of structural variation deployment across sections and cadenzas.
