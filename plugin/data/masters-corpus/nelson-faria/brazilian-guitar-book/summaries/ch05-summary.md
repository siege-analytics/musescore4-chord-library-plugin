# Chapter 5 — PART V - Baião

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches the foundational patterns and variations of baião, a Brazilian rhythmic style originating from the northeast (Ceará, Maranhão, and Bahia) that emerged in the 1940s from the bumba-meu-boi folk dance. The core technique centers on a two-layer texture: upper voices that simulate percussion instruments (cowbell, berimbau) against syncopated pedal-tone bass lines, all grounded in the mixolydian and lydian b7 modes with simple harmonic movement. Faria introduces five key variations—cowbell/syncopated bass, berimbau open-string technique, broken chords style, and salsa-inflected bass syncopation—each deployable either continuously through a song or mixed to create new patterns, with the characteristic load-bearing terminology being the pedal-tone rule (syncopated bass against on-beat upper voices) and the cross-training practice method of clapping high voices while tapping low voices with the foot to internalize the bipartite rhythm.
