## Activities defined

> The next area of study to be discussed is of a melodic nature. As the term “linear improvisation” suggests,
> melodic line forms must be improvised over various harmonic structures, creating a solo. These line forms
> (hereafter to be referred to as activities) can be related to both a chord and scale form.

**Significance (p. 7).** This passage establishes the book's core terminology — "activities" as the label for melodic line forms — which is the foundational concept the entire method builds upon.

## G minor / Bb major relationship

> The key we will be dealing with at the present time is G minor. Since G minor is derived from a Bb major
> scale (Gm being the relative minor of Bb major), it is important to know where on the guitar neck the Bb
> major (Gm) scales are located. For it is from these scales, along with the previously mentioned chord forms,
> that five forms of activities can be realized.

**Significance (p. 7).** This defines the theoretical foundation — relative minor relationship — that explains why Bb major scale patterns are used to generate G minor activities throughout the chapter.

## Horizontal scale coverage

> Notice that like the four
> chord inversions, the five scale patterns move horizontally up the fingerboard, until all twelve frets (one oc-
> tave) are covered.

**Significance (p. 8).** This is the defining structural rule of Phase I: five scale forms covering the neck horizontally in one key, which is the method's characteristic organizational principle.

## Scale forms not root-based

> NOTE: The scale forms illustrated herein do not necessarily start and end on the root of the scale. They in-
> stead display every accessible scale tone in position.

**Significance (p. 8).** This rule is critical because it reframes how the student reads the diagrams — positional coverage rather than root-to-root scalar thinking — which governs how all five forms are interpreted.

## Chord form dictates activity

> When learning these activities it’s a good idea to play their respective chord forms immediately before play-
> ing each activity. Each chord form will dictate the activity to be played (except in the case of the first chord
> form which names both the first and fifth activities).

**Significance (p. 10).** This is the method's central performance rule: the chord inversion heard or played determines which activity (line form) is selected, making chord-activity pairing the operative decision system.

## Chord-to-activity mapping table

> For: CHORD FORM Uses ACTIVITY
> Form1 Forms 1&5
> Form 2 Form 2
> Form3 Form 3
> Form4 Form4

**Significance (p. 10).** This table is the method's definitive lookup rule showing exactly which activity corresponds to each chord inversion, including the special shared-inversion exception for forms 1 and 5.

## Activity #1 position and key

> The first activity (in the key of G minor) starts in the third position, and spans from the second to sixth fret on
> the fingerboard. It clearly outlines a G minor scale which should make it relatively easy to memorize and as-
> sociate with its naming key. At this time it should be made clear that the form of activity does not make refer-
> ence to the key it is in, Bb.
>
> EXAMPLE: The first activity, starting in the third position is the key of G minor.
> The first activity starting in the fifth position is the key of A minor, etc.

**Significance (p. 11).** This passage teaches the transposition principle — the activity form is position-independent and key is determined solely by where on the neck the form is played — which is essential for applying activities across all twelve keys.

## Activity #2 arpeggio basis

> in the key of G minor, lies totally in the fifth position, spanning the fifth to eighth fret. It
> outlines a Bbmaj7 arpeggio, starting from the maj7 degree of the Bbmaj7 (an “A” note).

**Significance (p. 12).** This defines Activity #2's harmonic content as a Bbmaj7 arpeggio starting from the major 7th degree, giving students both a positional anchor and an intervallic framework for the line.

## Activity #3 position exception

> The third activity, with the exception of the Bb note on the second string, lies in the seventh position. The G
> minor scale and arpeggio are visible throughout this activity.

**Significance (p. 13).** The explicit flagging of the out-of-position Bb note teaches that activities are primarily positional but allow single-note extensions, and that both scale and arpeggio outlines coexist within one activity.

## Activity #5 octave relationship

> The fifth and final form of activity in the key of G minor completes the total coverage of the fingerboard in one
>
> key (G minor), over a distance of twelve frets (one octave). Notice that both the fifth and first activities start
> with the same note on the sixth string (a “G” note).
>
> ERGO: The next logical form to follow, would be the first form of activity one octave higher.
>
> Remember: The first and fifth activities share the same chord inversion.

**Significance (p. 15).** This passage establishes the cyclical structure of the system — activities 1 and 5 bookend one octave and share a chord inversion, making the system infinitely repeatable up the neck.

## Transposing to all 12 keys

> Before moving on to PHASE II, the student should learn to play the remaiming eleven keys, horizontally up
> the fingerboard. To achieve this, simply transpose the four chord forms and five forms of activities either up
>
> or down the other eleven keys.
>
> EXAMPLE: Horizontal movement in the key of C minor (Eb major).
>
> 1) Locate the four chord inversions and scales.
> 2) Play the appropriate activity (line form), according to the chord inversion (form 1, 2, 3, etc.).

**Significance (p. 16).** This is the completion rule for Phase I — the exact two-step procedure (locate inversions/scales, then play the matching activity) that must be mastered in all 12 keys before advancing.

## Phase I completion requirement

> Upon completion of this phase of study you should be able to play all twelve keys horizontally
> up the fingerboard. Then and only then should you move onto PHASE II.

**Significance (p. 16).** This is a sequencing rule that enforces mastery of horizontal movement across all keys as a prerequisite, establishing the pedagogical gating structure of the entire method.
