## Chord substitution: same name/quality

> L. Substitute chords of the same name and quality. _ II. Converting to minor through substitution.
>
> Cmaj6 = Am7 (Relative minor)
>
> Cmaj = Cmaj7 = Cmaj9 etc. Cmaj7 = Am9 (Relative minor)
> Cm6 = Cm7 = Cm9 etc. Cmaj9 = Em7 (Secondary relative minor)
> C7 = C9 = C13 — ete. C7 = Gm
>
> Cm7b5 = Ebm6

**Significance (p. 50).** This table is the conceptual core of the entire phase: it gives the specific equivalences that allow complex compositional chords to be recast as minor forms, enabling the method's approach to linear improvisation.

## Chord substitution reduction procedure

> PROCEDURE
>
> 1) Study the song in its original form (page 52).
> a) Play the melody.
> b) Study the chord changes.
> 2) On pages 53 and 54 you will find the chord diagrams and the simplified breakdown of the harmonic struc-
> ture (lower stave).
> a) Learn the chord voicings (provided in both musical and graphic forms).
> b) Study the substitutions found immediately below the compositional harmony.
> 3) Study the analysis on pages 55 through 57.
> a) Relate each substitution to the compositional harmony, with the aid of the written analysis.
> b) Memorize the substitutions.
> 4) Examine the sample solo on pages 58 through 62.
> a) Play through the sample solo until the study can be performed nonstop.
> b) Record the changes on tape and play the solo over them.
>
> 5) Now that you’ve learned the tune thoroughly, compose your own solo over the changes, utilizing the sub-
> stitutions given or any of your own substitute forms.

**Significance (p. 50).** This five-step procedure is the method's complete workflow for applying substitution to a real composition, sequencing the student from analysis through memorization to original improvisation.

## Chord substitution concept intro

> Application through substitution and reduction. As was previously mentioned, complex chord changes can
