# Book-level distillation — linear-expressions

_Aggregated from per-chapter summaries under `summaries/`._

## Overall Arc and Pedagogical Structure

The book teaches a complete, phase-structured method for jazz guitar improvisation built on a single organizing principle: harmonic structure dictates melodic choice. The four phases move from the local to the global — Phase I establishes horizontal coverage of the neck within one key; Phase II extends the same system vertically across all twelve keys; Phase III integrates both axes through line studies anchored to real chord progressions; Phase IV completes the system by teaching harmonic substitution and reduction, enabling the player to handle any chord complexity encountered in actual repertoire. Each phase presupposes the preceding one, and Chapter 1 explicitly requires mastery in all twelve keys before Phase II is attempted, making the progression non-negotiable.

## The Core Unit: Activities

The master's fundamental term is **"activities"** — melodic line forms improvised over harmonic structures. Activities are not free melodic invention; they are positionally and harmonically determined. Chapter 1 establishes that five scale forms in G minor (derived from the Bb major scale through the relative minor relationship) cover the neck horizontally, with each form matched to a specific chord inversion. The system's foundational rule is: **the chord form dictates which activity is played**. Activities 1 and 5 share the same chord inversion and bookend an octave, making the system cyclically repeatable. Activities are position-independent — their interval shapes are fixed — and transposing them to all twelve keys is the explicit prerequisite for advancing.

## Chord-to-Activity Mapping and Positional Systems

The operative decision system throughout all four phases is chord-to-activity pairing. When a player encounters a chord, they identify its inversion, and that inversion maps to the corresponding numbered activity form. Chapter 2 applies this rule without modification across all twelve minor keys (Abm7 through Gm7) in a single fingerboard sector, delivering the transposition content entirely through fingerboard diagrams and notation rather than prose — confirming that no new conceptual machinery is introduced; only coverage is extended. The term **"position"** carries a specific load: it refers to where on the fingerboard a form is played, and position determines key. Changing position while keeping the same form shape changes the key; changing the form while staying in position changes the activity.

## Line Studies: Structural Method and Composition

Chapter 3 introduces **line studies** as the integration vehicle. Studies ascend the fingerboard in successive pairs, each pair anchored to numbered forms of activity in G minor, while maintaining fixed chord progressions shared by letter designation. The pedagogical design is explicitly cyclical — studies carry continuation arrows — and the chapter includes student-completed studies (5A/5B) that convert passive drilling into active composition. The five-step workflow the chapter prescribes is: (1) study the original form, (2) learn voicings and substitutions, (3) memorize through analysis, (4) play sample solos, (5) compose original solos. Chapter 3's load-bearing innovation is a **chord substitution equivalences table** showing how complex chord types (maj6, maj7, maj9, dominant 7, dominant 13) reduce to minor forms — either relative minor or secondary relative minor — which is what makes the activities system applicable to real jazz harmony rather than only to pure minor contexts. The chapter explicitly directs students toward canonical jazz standards ("Stella by Starlight," "All the Things You Are") as the destination for these techniques.

## Substitution and Reduction: The Harmonic Reduction Rules

Chapter 4 completes the system by providing explicit interval-formula rules for handling the chord types not covered by straightforward relative minor substitution. The load-bearing rules are:

- **m7b5 → minor via minor third**: an m7b5 chord is treated as an incomplete dominant ninth; the substitution minor root lies a minor third above the m7b5 root.
- **Dominant ninth → minor via perfect fifth**: the minor substitute for a dominant ninth is rooted a perfect fifth above (or below) the dominant root.
- **Altered dominants → step-up minor**: altered dominant chords are handled by moving to the minor form one half-step above the dominant root.

The chapter introduces the **augmented triad** as an incomplete minor-major-7th chord with three enharmonic root names, expanding the player's substitution vocabulary. For slash chords, the relative minor and secondary relative minor substitution methods from Phase III are applied. On scale choice, the chapter makes an explicit prescription: prefer **melodic minor** over harmonic minor when voicing altered dominants, because melodic minor provides the b7 the harmonic minor lacks.

## The Master's Characteristic Move

Throughout all four phases, the master's characteristic analytical move is **reframing unfamiliar chords as inversions or incomplete versions of familiar diatonic structures**. Complex or altered chords are not treated as isolated entities requiring separate memorization; they are revealed as partial constructions of — or interval-transpositions from — chords the player already knows. This reductionist logic converts apparent harmonic complexity into simple interval formulas executable in real time, and it is the conceptual engine that makes the entire activities system extensible from the simple G minor forms of Chapter 1 to the fully altered and substituted harmony of jazz standards in Chapter 4.
