## Tune as poem

> He conveyed to me that each tune is like a poem, teaching
> us the syntax of language and how to assemble words and set up rhymes, and every song has
> something to teach about this music.

**Significance (p. 5).** Frames each standard as a syntactic lesson, not just material to solo over, which shapes the way tunes are studied in later chapters.

## Melody as the story

> He drew an analogy to songs being akin to plays or movies,
> where the melody represents the story itself—the core element. The customs, lights, and props
> serve as complementary elements that need to be properly oriented towards the story.

**Significance (p. 5).** Establishes the hierarchy that the rest of the method depends on: melody is primary; harmony and ornamentation are supporting elements.

## Comedian-and-jokes analogy

> To further illustrate this concept of narrative, he offered an analogy, comparing learning to tell
> stories from a tune to a comedian crafting jokes. Comedians learn numerous common jokes,
> allowing them to understand setups, narratives, and punchlines that resonate with the audience.

**Significance (p. 5).** Operationalizes narrative practice: improvisers learn shared forms (setup/punchline) by studying canonical examples, mirroring how comedians learn standards.

## Tunes over solos

> he emphasized
> that learning tunes is more significant than learning solos or phrases because the ideas found in
> jazz compositions are the best ideas the composers could come up with, which is why they
> decided to create a tune around them.

**Significance (p. 5).** Inverts the common transcription-of-solos curriculum: the composer's ideas in the tune itself are the most concentrated source of musical vocabulary.

## Skips transcription, learns phrases

> I recall asking him once if he transcribed
> solos, and he mentioned that he did it when he was in music school upon request, but he stopped
> doing it afterward. He prefers to learn small phrases.

**Significance (p. 5).** States Bernstein's actual practice unit (the small phrase) and explicitly de-prioritizes full-solo transcription as a learning method.

## Core elements of a tune

> Bernstein possesses a masterful understanding of the fundamental
> elements that contribute to the greatness of a tune, and he skillfully expands upon these elements
> to construct captivating narratives. These can range from simple rhythmic figures to more
> intricate aspects like the core notes of a melody.

**Significance (p. 5).** Names the raw material a player should be mining from each tune: rhythmic figures and core melody notes that become seeds for improvisation.

## Oral-tradition lesson method

> Bernstein enjoyed teaching me tunes in a primarily oral traditional manner. He
> would play the sections one by one and elucidate his thought process when learning the tune. We
> didn't rely on lead sheets or any type of charts during these sessions.

**Significance (p. 5).** Describes the concrete classroom practice that enacts the language analogy: tunes are transmitted by ear and demonstration, not by reading.
