## Start with root, third, seventh

> However, he stopped me and advised me to start with basic
> chords first—just the root, third, and seventh of each chord. He encouraged me to focus on
> listening to the melody and understanding the relationship between the basic chords and the
> melody before making more informed decisions about incorporating chord extensions.

**Significance (p. 9).** Concrete practice rule for comping: limit voicings to R/3/7 until the melody-chord relationship is internalized, then add extensions.

## Foundation before embellishment

> Bernstein ensures that
> he can create something tasteful using the fundamental elements before exploring new ideas. It's
> akin to working with layers, where he prioritizes building a solid foundation with the basic layers
> before progressing to new techniques or embellishments.

**Significance (p. 9).** Generalizes the previous example into a layered approach for all comping work: tasteful basics first, embellishment second.

## Dressing the notes

> He shared with me the concept of ensuring that the chords played always
> enhance the melody, likening it to "dressing the notes." According to Bernstein, you can make
> each note feel good by giving it the right "outfit" and making it feel important.

**Significance (p. 9).** Names a signature Bernstein concept: comping's job is to costume each melody note so it sounds important, a load-bearing metaphor for voicing choice.

## B7#11 outfit example

> Bernstein explained that if the bass were to play the
> same melody note, it could make the melody feel weak. Instead, he prefers to play a B7#11
> chord, where the F becomes the #11, allowing the melody note to have a different "outfit" that
> enhances its impact and makes it feel good.

**Significance (p. 9).** Worked example of dressing-the-notes: substituting a tritone-related chord reframes the same melody note as a tension and prevents the bass-doubling weakness.

## Build own guide tones

> he proceeded to explain how one can create their own guide tones for each chord, aiming
> to establish a strong and captivating melodic line. Afterwards, you have the freedom to
> determine how to fill in the remaining notes of the chord.

**Significance (p. 10).** Defines a comping-construction procedure: design a guide-tone line first as a secondary melody, then voice-fill around it.

## Fill in around guide tones

> Furthermore, he elaborated that once you have established a solid structure with strong guide
> notes and bass notes, you have the flexibility to incorporate any notes in between. The idea is to
> consider notes that would "dress better" the guide tones, enhancing their impact and making
> them feel pleasing.

**Significance (p. 10).** Specifies the second phase of the comping construction: inner voices are chosen to dress the guide tones, recursively applying the dressing-the-notes principle.

## Reharm to anticipate melody

> Another aspect that Bernstein enjoys doing is reharmonizing sections of the melody when he
> anticipates the notes I'm going to play in the song's heads. This creates intriguing harmonic
> movements, "dressing the notes," and enhancing their sound while still respecting the essential
> harmonic narrative of the song.

**Significance (p. 10).** Extends dressing-the-notes from accompaniment to real-time reharmonization driven by anticipating the soloist's melodic choices.

## Record and play over comping

> Bernstein shared with me his practice of recording himself while comping
> for extended periods of time. He would then listen back to the recordings and play over them.
> According to him, this practice was highly valuable for two reasons. Firstly, it allowed him to
> dedicate a significant amount of time to practicing comping. Secondly, it provided an
> opportunity to experience how it feels to play over his own comping, enabling him to assess the
> interest and effectiveness of his comping ideas based on his own playing experience.

**Significance (p. 11).** Specifies a concrete practice routine: record comping, then solo over it, using the soloist-perspective as feedback on the comping's effectiveness.

## Rhythm equal to chords

> Bernstein emphasized the importance of rhythm in his comping, considering it to be equally vital
> as the chords he played. He believed that finding captivating rhythms that propelled the music
> forward was a key aspect.

**Significance (p. 11).** Elevates rhythmic figures to the same status as voicing choice within comping, reinforcing the chapter-4 claim that rhythm carries meaning.

## Rhythmic motif from Star Eyes

> In one lesson, he showcased this idea by incorporating the iconic
> intro's rhythm from "Star Eyes" by Charlie Parker into his comping over the soloist. This
> rhythmic figure not only added a pleasing feel to the music but also served as a motif for
> variation and development.

**Significance (p. 11).** Illustrates how a borrowed rhythmic cell becomes a developable motif in comping, exemplifying the language/phrase approach applied to accompaniment.

## Flexible groove with soloist

> Even when I made occasional mistakes or
> unintentionally affected the timing, he would gracefully adjust and slow down or speed up with
> me, ensuring that the music continued to feel good. His deep sense of groove allowed him to
> flexibly adapt and mold the groove to suit the nuances of my playing

**Significance (p. 11).** Describes a responsive-comping ideal: the comper bends groove to support the soloist's reality, not the other way around.
