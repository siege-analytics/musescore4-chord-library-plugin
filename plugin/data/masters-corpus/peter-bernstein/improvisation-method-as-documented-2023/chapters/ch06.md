## Record comping, then solo over it

> Bernstein shared with me his practice of recording himself while comping
> for extended periods of time. He would then listen back to the recordings and play over them.
> According to him, this practice was highly valuable for two reasons. Firstly, it allowed him to
> dedicate a significant amount of time to practicing comping. Secondly, it provided an
> opportunity to experience how it feels to play over his own comping, enabling him to assess the
> interest and effectiveness of his comping ideas based on his own playing experience.

**Significance (p. 11).** Defines a concrete practice routine for developing comping by becoming one's own soloist.

## Rhythm equals chords in comping

> Bernstein emphasized the importance of rhythm in his comping, considering it to be equally vital
> as the chords he played. He believed that finding captivating rhythms that propelled the music
> forward was a key aspect.

**Significance (p. 11).** States the pedagogical maxim that rhythmic content of comping is as load-bearing as voicing choice.

## Borrow rhythmic motifs for comping

> he showcased this idea by incorporating the iconic
> intro's rhythm from "Star Eyes" by Charlie Parker into his comping over the soloist. This
> rhythmic figure not only added a pleasing feel to the music but also served as a motif for
> variation and development.

**Significance (p. 11).** Concrete example of importing an existing rhythmic motif into comping as developmental material.

## Jazz as tension and resolution

> According to Bernstein, jazz music revolves around the interplay of tension and resolution, or
> V's and I's with various flavors. The crucial skill to develop is the ability to transition creatively
> between these chord progressions.

**Significance (p. 12).** Frames Bernstein's entire harmonic philosophy around tension/resolution rather than chord-by-chord verticality.

## Chords as tools, not static entities

> He explained to me that harmony, in his view, involves understanding how to navigate from one
> chord to another. Chords are not static entities, as they may sometimes appear on a lead sheet,
> but rather tools for manipulating tension and resolution. To move from one sound to another, he
> employs a wide range of passing chords and interweaves little melodies in between.

**Significance (p. 12).** Core conceptual reframing: chords are transitional devices, not destinations, justifying passing chords and inner melodies.

## Internal motion within a single chord

> Bernstein emphasized the importance of playing chords within a single chord.
> When a tune calls for a chord to be sustained over 4 or 2 measures, it becomes essential to
> explore the play between harmonic tension and resolution within that chord. Also utilizing space
> and rhythm effectively is key while doing this.

**Significance (p. 12).** Rule for sustained-chord situations: generate internal tension/resolution rather than holding static harmony.

## Tension is where creativity lives

> According to Bernstein, the tension is where the fun and creativity in music truly come alive. It
> is in these moments that you can explore various ways to navigate and shape that tension.

**Significance (p. 12).** Pedagogical maxim locating creative work in tension zones, not resolutions.

## Avoid the root of the I chord

> while playing "Ornithology" by Charlie Parker, he demonstrated his
> preference for not playing the roots of the I (one) chords. Instead, he would opt for an I69 chord
> derived from the V of the respective I (one) chord. This choice adds a unique tension and flavor
> to the I (one) chord, not resolving the tension fully.

**Significance (p. 12).** Specific characteristic move: substitute a V-derived I69 to keep tension alive over the tonic.
