## Don't overemphasize the I chord

> According to Bernstein, avoiding an excessive emphasis on the I (one) chord is a wonderful way
> to maintain a sense of forward momentum in the music. He believes that when the I chord is
> overemphasized, it can give the impression that the song is being dragged or held back.

**Significance (p. 13).** Rule for forward motion: under-resolve the I to keep the tune moving.

## Tritone ii-V substitution

> he
> sometimes likes to play the ii V a tritone up or dawn the original during the dominant chords that
> resolve. This is like playing the related ii chord of the tritone substitution.

**Significance (p. 13).** Defines a specific substitution device: insert a tritone-distance ii-V over a resolving dominant.

## Rhythmic placement of substitutions

> rhythm plays a vital role, particularly in the case of the ii V
> with a tritone up, as playing it in the incorrect position within the bar can create a sense of
> awkwardness.

**Significance (p. 13).** Warns that substitutions depend on rhythmic placement, not just harmonic logic.

## Voice leading as choir writing

> Bernstein likened creating good voice leading to composing for a choir, drawing parallels
> between the two. Jim Hall had a unique way of envisioning each string on the guitar as an
> individual, aiming to bring joy to all by providing them with beautiful melodies to sing.

**Significance (p. 13).** Conceptual frame for guitar voice leading via Jim Hall: treat each string as a singer.

## Creativity as variation generation

> For Bernstein, creativity lies in taking good musical ideas and creating variations that convey the
> same meaning but with different flavors. It involves honing the craft of generating multiple ideas
> from a single starting point, developing the ability to produce ten new ideas from one and then
> ten more from those ten.

**Significance (p. 13).** Defines creativity operationally as recursive variation from a seed idea.

## Open approach to variation

> This method involves taking the overall structure of the melodic line as a starting point and
> freely experimenting without many constraints. The primary guide in this process is the ear,
> which helps in discovering variations that retain the essence of the original while introducing
> distinct flavors.

**Significance (p. 14).** First named variation technique: ear-led free variation preserving line structure.

## Target notes around a chord tone

> This alternative method involves crafting phrases that center around a particular note of the
> chord. This selected note can be any fundamental note or extension of the chord, and it serves as
> the central element of the phrase.

**Significance (p. 15).** Second variation method: build phrases around a chosen target note from the chord.

## A note's flavor depends on harmony

> He chose the note D for both examples to also explain how a note can have a different flavor
> depending on the harmonic context.

**Significance (p. 17).** Pedagogical point that the same pitch carries different meanings under different chords.

## Two-note target practice

> The next step in this process is to expand upon the previous method by selecting two notes and
> creating phrases that revolve around them. This approach adds an extra layer of complexity and
> introduces a different quality to the lines.

**Significance (p. 19).** Expansion of single-target practice to dual-target phrase construction.

## Chromatic notes around target

> when
> you focus on a specific degree of the scale, you have the freedom to use the entire chromatic
> scale to target that note. Every note can relate to any other note within the context of any chord.

**Significance (p. 19).** Liberates note choice: any chromatic note may approach a chosen target.

## Party-for-the-target analogy

> Bernstein employs an analogy to describe this approach as a vibrant party dedicated to the target
> note. It's a celebration where not only can you invite your family members (the corresponding
> scale notes), but also your friends and neighbors (the outside notes).

**Significance (p. 19).** Memorable pedagogical analogy distinguishing diatonic vs. chromatic relationships to a target.

## Phrasing vs pressing buttons

> He stressed that this
> aspect distinguishes a genuine musical phrase from merely "pressing the buttons" and allowing
> the fingers to take control.

**Significance (p. 21).** Articulates the difference between intentional melodic phrasing and finger-pattern playing.
