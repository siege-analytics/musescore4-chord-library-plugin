## Contrast as narrative tool

> He shared the concept of utilizing
> contrasting elements during solos, such as combining soft and loud notes, short and long notes,
> and conveying serious and lighthearted moments. He emphasized how this contrast can engage
> listeners and serve as a powerful tool for developing an interesting narrative.

**Significance (p. 26).** Defines contrast (dynamics, length, mood) as the engine of narrative engagement in a solo.

## Strategic use of subdivisions

> Rather than
> playing every possible subdivision in the first chorus, he suggested creating anticipation by
> introducing faster subdivisions later in the solo and then returning to slower ones. This approach
> builds curiosity in the listener, wondering what will happen next.

**Significance (p. 26).** Rule for solo pacing: withhold density early, deploy and release it later for tension.

## Silence as punctuation

> He frequently
> stressed the significance of silence in solos, comparing it to punctuation in language. He believed
> it was crucial to clearly articulate the beginning and end of an idea.

**Significance (p. 26).** Maxim that silence functions linguistically to delimit ideas in improvisation.

## Rhymes between ideas

> we also explored the idea of rhymes in soloing—connecting melodic ideas
> through call and response or developing and resolving tension in similar ways. He likened this to
> poetry, where simple and predictable rhymes coexist with more complex rhymes that occur in
> unexpected places.

**Significance (p. 26).** Introduces 'rhymes' as a vocabulary for connecting solo ideas across time.

## Practice away from the instrument

> He introduced the concept of practicing away from the instrument—singing ideas over
> the song's form. According to him, this fosters a "proper chain of command" where imagination
> leads the delivery of the story, rather than the instrument dictating what to play.

**Significance (p. 26).** Specific practice technique to invert the dependency between imagination and fingers.

## Cadenza as parking a car

> He often likens it
> to parking a car, where you are the driver and have the control to decide when the ride ends. No
> one can exit the car until you stop.

**Significance (p. 27).** Memorable analogy locating performer agency at the end of a cadenza.

## Cadenzas need clear endings

> He draws a parallel between ending a conversation and concluding a
> cadenza. Just as a conversation requires a clear ending to avoid discomfort, a cadenza
> necessitates a definitive conclusion.

**Significance (p. 27).** Rule that cadenzas must resolve unambiguously, like the end of a conversation.

## Technique redefined as expressive range

> For him, technique is more about the ability to explore the full
> expressive range that the instrument has to offer.

**Significance (p. 27).** Redefines 'technique' away from speed and toward expressive control.

## Fingering serves articulation

> the guitar's nature allows us to play the same melody using various fingerings, and
> Bernstein's choice of fingerings is closely related to the desired articulation suggested by the
> song.

**Significance (p. 28).** Rule: fingering selection is driven by desired articulation, not by hand convenience.

## Strong fingers on target notes

> This melody contains prominent target notes, and he demonstrated how he deliberately plays
> these notes with the strongest fingers available, often opting for the middle or ring finger.

**Significance (p. 28).** Specific technique: assign middle/ring finger to melodic target notes for tonal weight.

## Single-string intervals and slides

> He also
> shared his preference for feeling intervals in certain parts of the melody, choosing to play them
> on a single string to better sense the interval's distance and sometimes adding a subtle slide when
> transitioning to the second note to emphasize the interval further. Additionally, playing on a
> single string provides a more consistent sound, offering a phrasing advantage.

**Significance (p. 28).** Concrete characteristic move: play intervals on one string with a slide to emulate horn phrasing.

## Imitate horn-player phrasing

> Bernstein sees
> technique as the ability to adapt to the phrasing and articulation of other musicians, particularly
> horn players.

**Significance (p. 27).** Reframes technical goals around adapting guitar phrasing to horn-player models.
