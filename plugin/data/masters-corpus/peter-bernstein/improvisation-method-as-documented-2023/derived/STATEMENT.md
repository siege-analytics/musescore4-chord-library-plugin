# An Approach to Jazz Improvisation: A Method by Peter Bernstein — Statement of Outputs

## Overview

Moreno's NYU document records Peter Bernstein's teaching as the deliberate inversion of academic jazz pedagogy: improvisation is language use, not scale deployment, and every concept is anchored to a specific tune rather than to an abstract harmonic exercise (Chapter 1; Chapter 2). The chapter on jazz-as-language names the pedagogical failure the method is designed to correct — the chord-scale drill that hands students scales over chord progressions without narrative or meaning — and replaces it with a jazz-as-conversation analogy in which the player needs vocabulary and syntax to assemble musical sentences that tell a story (Chapter 2). Vocabulary is acquired the way children learn to speak (by listening and imitation), ideas/phrases (not scales) are the unit of practice, and improvisation is the recombination of known phrases under a syntax rather than real-time token generation. This linguistic frame is the spine on which every later chapter hangs.

The pedagogical arc moves from epistemology to delivery. Chapter 1 establishes source and method (oral tradition, living lineage, no formal system). Chapter 2 installs the language analogy as conceptual spine. Chapter 3 operationalizes the analogy into a curriculum for learning a tune (standard-as-poem, melody-as-story, the comedian-and-jokes analogy, tunes outranking solos, the small phrase as practice unit, lessons taught orally without lead sheets). Chapter 4 codifies melody primacy as the load-bearing rule: the first practice task on any tune is to play the melody as a monophonic instrument, and a player must not rush to 'step 12' (harmonic embellishment) before completing 'step 1' (melody mastery). Chapter 5 applies that hierarchy to comping via the dressing-the-notes metaphor, prescribing a layered build (root-3-7 first, extensions only after the relationship to the melody is heard) and a two-phase comping-construction procedure (design your own guide-tone line, then fill inner voices to dress those guide tones). Chapter 6 reframes harmony itself as the interplay of tension and resolution, with chords as tools rather than destinations. Chapter 7 builds the vocabulary-development apparatus — tritone ii-V substitution, three named variation methods, target-note practice, and a permissive chromatic stance. Chapter 8 names the central aesthetic — connected narrative outranks correctness-of-notes — and unifies soloing and comping under a single target-note system. Chapter 9 organizes the narrative and technical apparatus for solos and cadenzas (contrast, pacing, silence, rhymes, away-from-instrument practice, definitive endings). Chapter 10 closes with the aesthetic stance: technical prowess serves beauty and storytelling, and personal style emerges from the inherent beauty within one's limitations.

Taken together, the document records an integrated method with four interlocking commitments. First, terminology is fixed and load-bearing: jazz-as-language, tune-as-poem, melody-as-story, target notes, dressing the notes, guide-tone line, internal motion, party for the target, rhymes, contrast, one-shape moving-hand, chain of command. Second, every concept is contextualized in a specific tune rather than presented as an abstract system — the methodological rule installed in Chapter 1 and re-asserted in Chapter 2. Third, melody is the organizing axis for everything: it dictates the first practice task (monophonic delivery), the basic chord relationship, the comping aesthetic (dressing the notes around guide tones), the solo content (lines leading to the tune's own target notes), and the unified voicing-and-soloing system (top of comping voicings = solo landing points). Fourth, narrative outranks correctness — connected ideas with purpose, contrast, silence, and rhymes are the evaluative criteria, and the player is trained to internalize phrases by listening and imitation, to practice away from the instrument so imagination leads, and to read the tradition impressionistically rather than literally.

## Systems

### Melodic Narrative System (Jazz as Language)

Bernstein's load-bearing conceptual system: improvisation is language use, not scale deployment. Every concept is anchored to a specific tune rather than to an abstract harmonic exercise; the standard is a poem and the melody is its story. The system's central aesthetic — connected narrative outranks correctness-of-notes — unifies vocabulary acquisition (by listening and imitation), the practice unit (small phrases, not transcribed choruses), and the evaluative criterion for any played line (does it tell a story?). Contrast, silence, and rhymes are the narrative grammar; target notes are the structural landing points around which lines are built.

### Melody-Primary Improvisation System

Codifies the method's load-bearing pedagogical rule: melody is primary and harmony exists to serve it. The order-of-operations is explicit — the first practice task on any tune is to play the melody as a monophonic instrument, with attention to lyric syllables as a guide to articulation, before any harmonic embellishment. Target notes are the operational link between the melody and the harmony: when the melody lands on a specific chord tone, the player notes it and lets it dictate voicing choice, illustrated by the Dm6-vs-Dm7 worked example where the melody's b7 overrides the default minor-tonic Dm6. The 'juggling on a unicycle' maxim warns against texture-overload at the cost of melodic feel.

Members: Monophonic Melody Delivery; Conservative Shell Voicing; Extension and Signature Run Layer; Melody Target Note.

### Comping Aesthetic System (Dressing the Notes)

Applies the melody-first hierarchy to accompaniment via the signature 'dressing the notes' metaphor: comping's job is to give each melody note the right outfit so it feels important. The concrete practice rule is to begin with root-3-7 voicings only, internalize their relationship to the melody, and add extensions only after that relationship is heard. The chapter prescribes a two-phase comping-construction procedure — design a guide-tone line per chord as a secondary melody, then fill the inner voices to dress those guide tones. Tension and resolution are generated even inside a single sustained chord, and rhythm in comping is elevated to equal status with voicing choice. The system also picks up the two contrarian harmonic moves — under-resolving the I chord and the tritone-distance ii-V over a resolving dominant — both treated as forward-motion devices that keep narrative tension alive.

Members: Root-3-7 Shell Voicing; Guide-Tone Line; Inner-Voice Dressing; Extension-as-Outfit (e.g., B7#11); Rhythmic Motif.

### Vocabulary Development System (Recursive Variation and Target-Note Practice)

The apparatus by which a player expands the internalized library of phrases that improvisation recombines. Vocabulary is acquired the way children learn to speak — by listening and imitation — and creativity is defined operationally as recursive variation: ten ideas from one, then ten from each of those. Chapter 7 enumerates three named variation methods (ear-led free variation that preserves overall structure, phrases built around a single chosen target note, phrases built around two simultaneous target notes) and pairs them with a permissive chromatic stance under the 'party for the target' analogy: diatonic notes are family and chromatic notes are friends and neighbors, so any note can approach any target if it serves the line. The system closes with practice meta-rules — practice away from the instrument so imagination leads the fingers, learn classic lines first and then progressively abstract them, and let fingering serve articulation rather than convenience (with the strongest fingers assigned to melodic target notes for tonal weight) — plus the guitar-specific 'one shape, moving hand' move.

Members: Ear-Led Free Variation; Single Target-Note Variation; Two Simultaneous Target Notes; Diatonic 'Family' Notes; Chromatic 'Friends and Neighbors'; Classic Line.
