# Chapter 2 — Jazz as a Language and the Importance of Narrative

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch02.md`._

This chapter establishes the conceptual spine of Bernstein's method: improvisation is language use, not scale deployment. It reiterates the methodological rule that all ideas are presented inside specific tunes (Concepts live in tunes) and names the pedagogical failure it is designed to correct — the chord-scale drill that hands students scales over chord progressions without narrative or meaning (Critique of scale-chord pedagogy). Bernstein's central analogy is jazz-as-conversation: a player needs vocabulary and syntax to assemble musical sentences and paragraphs that tell a story (Jazz as conversation), and that vocabulary is acquired the way children learn to speak — by listening and imitation, not from books (How children learn language). The load-bearing operational moves are treating ideas/phrases (not scales) as the unit of practice (Ideas as words, scales as not-words), recombining known phrases under a syntax rather than generating tokens in real time (Generate vs. recombine), and, especially early on, learning situational selection — which phrase fits which situation — over exhaustive vocabulary recitation (Practical vocabulary use).
