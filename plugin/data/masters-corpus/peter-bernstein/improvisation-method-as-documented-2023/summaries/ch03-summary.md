# Chapter 3 — How to Learn a Tune

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

This chapter teaches how Bernstein wants a student to approach learning a tune, extending the language analogy from chapter 2 into a concrete curriculum. Each standard is treated as a poem whose syntax, word-assembly, and rhymes the player is studying (Tune as poem), and within that poem the melody is the story itself while harmony and ornamentation are supporting customs, lights, and props (Melody as the story) — the hierarchy that the rest of the method depends on. Bernstein operationalizes narrative practice through a comedian-and-jokes analogy (setups, narratives, punchlines learned by studying canonical examples) (Comedian-and-jokes analogy) and inverts the standard transcription-of-solos curriculum: the composer's own ideas in the tune are the most concentrated vocabulary source, so tunes outrank solos (Tunes over solos) and the practice unit is the small phrase, not the full transcribed chorus (Skips transcription, learns phrases). The raw material to mine is named explicitly — rhythmic figures and core melody notes that become seeds for improvisation (Core elements of a tune) — and the chapter closes with the concrete classroom practice that enacts all of this: tunes are taught orally, section by section, with no lead sheets or charts (Oral-tradition lesson method).
