# Chapter 5 — Comping and Supporting the Melody

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

This chapter applies the melody-first hierarchy to comping and introduces Bernstein's signature 'dressing the notes' concept. The concrete practice rule is to begin with root-3-7 voicings only, internalize the relationship between those basic chords and the melody, and add extensions only after that relationship is heard (Start with root, third, seventh) — a layered foundation-before-embellishment discipline (Foundation before embellishment). The load-bearing metaphor is dressing the notes: comping's job is to give each melody note the right outfit so it feels important (Dressing the notes), demonstrated by substituting a B7#11 voicing so the melody note becomes a tension rather than being weakly doubled by the bass (B7#11 outfit example). The chapter then specifies a two-phase comping-construction procedure: design your own guide-tone line per chord as a secondary melody (Build own guide tones), then fill the inner voices to dress those guide tones, recursively applying the same dressing-the-notes principle (Fill in around guide tones). Bernstein extends this from accompaniment into real-time reharmonization that anticipates the soloist's notes while preserving the song's harmonic narrative (Reharm to anticipate melody), and he prescribes a concrete practice routine — record yourself comping, then solo over the recording to feel whether the comping is interesting from the soloist's seat (Record and play over comping). Rhythm is elevated to equal status with voicing choice (Rhythm equal to chords), with a borrowed rhythmic cell from 'Star Eyes' shown becoming a developable motif (Rhythmic motif from Star Eyes), and the chapter closes on a responsive-comping ideal: the comper bends groove to the soloist's reality rather than the reverse (Flexible groove with soloist).
