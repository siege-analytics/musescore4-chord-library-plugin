# Chapter 6 — Harmonic Movement, Tension and Substitutions

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch06.md`._

Chapter 6 lays out Bernstein's harmonic philosophy and the practice routines that operationalize it. The chapter's load-bearing claim is that jazz is fundamentally the interplay of tension and resolution—V's and I's with various flavors—and that chords are not destinations but tools for manipulating that tension (Jazz as tension and resolution, Chords as tools, not static entities). From this reframe flow several rules: generate internal tension/resolution even when a single chord is sustained over multiple measures (Internal motion within a single chord), and locate creative work in tension zones rather than at resolutions (Tension is where creativity lives). The chapter also gives a concrete practice routine—record yourself comping for extended periods, then solo over the playback to evaluate the comping from a soloist's seat (Record comping, then solo over it)—and insists that rhythm in comping is as load-bearing as voicing, including the practice of borrowing rhythmic motifs such as the intro of Parker's 'Star Eyes' (Rhythm equals chords in comping, Borrow rhythmic motifs for comping). Two characteristic moves surface explicitly: weaving passing chords and inner melodies between target sonorities, and avoiding the root of the I chord by substituting a V-derived I69 to keep tension unresolved over the tonic (Avoid the root of the I chord).
