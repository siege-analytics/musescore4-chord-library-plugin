# Chapter 9 — Cadenza, Technique and Flexibility

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 organizes Bernstein's narrative and technical apparatus for solos and cadenzas. Contrast—soft against loud, short against long, serious against lighthearted—is named as the engine of narrative engagement (Contrast as narrative tool), supported by a pacing rule that withholds dense subdivisions early and deploys them later to build anticipation (Strategic use of subdivisions), the linguistic role of silence as punctuation between ideas (Silence as punctuation), and 'rhymes' as a vocabulary for connecting motifs across time through call-and-response or parallel tension-resolution (Rhymes between ideas). To invert the dependency between imagination and fingers, Bernstein prescribes practicing away from the instrument by singing ideas over the song's form, establishing a 'proper chain of command' where imagination leads (Practice away from the instrument). Cadenzas are framed by two analogies—parking a car and ending a conversation—both insisting on definitive, unambiguous endings under the performer's control (Cadenza as parking a car, Cadenzas need clear endings). The chapter then redefines technique away from speed and toward expressive range and the ability to adapt to other musicians' phrasing, particularly horn players' (Technique redefined as expressive range, Imitate horn-player phrasing), with concrete characteristic moves: fingering is chosen to serve articulation rather than convenience, the strongest fingers (middle or ring) are assigned to melodic target notes for tonal weight, and intervals are felt by playing them on a single string with a subtle slide to emulate horn phrasing (Fingering serves articulation, Strong fingers on target notes, Single-string intervals and slides).
