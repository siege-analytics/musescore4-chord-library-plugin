## Three harmonic family system

> In order to use simple substitution as a reharmonization technique, you must understand
> the division of the seven diatonic chords into three groups or families. Each of these
> chord families has a function. A chord’s function is its tendency fo move or remain
> stable within a musical phrase.

**Significance (p. 6).** This defines the foundational analytical framework—chord families and function—on which the entire chapter's substitution method depends.

## Tonic family definition

> The tonic family of chords has a resting function. Chords in this group tend to sound
> stable. They have little sense of forward motion and are almost always found at the
> phrase endings of popular and standard tunes. Diatonic chords built on the first, third,
> and sixth degrees of a scale are the members of this group.

**Significance (p. 6).** This defines the tonic family (IMaj7, III-7, VI-7) and explains why these chords are interchangeable as simple substitutions.

## Fourth degree as tendency tone

> Tonic chords share several common tones. The chords are considered restful because
> they do not contain.the fourth degree. of the scale, which-is-F-in.the.key_of.C.

**Significance (p. 6).** This gives the acoustic reason why tonic chords are stable, anchoring the harmonic family logic to a concrete scale-degree rule.

## Subdominant family definition

> Chords in the subdominant family have a moderate tendency to move ahead within
> the musical phrase. All chords in this family contain the restless fourth degree of the
> scale. Chords built on the second and fourth scale degrees make up this group. The
> V7sus4 is also included in this family, because it contains the fourth scale degree instead
> of the third. (Using a suspended fourth instead of a third eliminates the tritone that gives
> a dominant family chord its characteristic sound. The tritone function is described below.}

**Significance (p. 7).** This defines the subdominant family and explains the borderline case of V7sus4, which is essential for correct substitution decisions.

## Avoiding unwanted b9 intervals

> Avoid unwanted }9 (or 52) melody/harmony intervals when using simple
> substitution. The )2 interval is a half step. It is also known as a 59, which is an octave
> plus a minor second. This melody/harmony interval creates a dissonance strong enough =
> to destroy the basic function of the chord. In general, avoid choosing a substitute chord
> that creates a b9 interval with any one of the melody notes.

**Significance (p. 10).** This is the primary voice-leading rule constraining simple substitution—substitutions that create b9 clashes must be rejected regardless of correct family membership.

## V7(b9) exception to b9 rule

> The V7(b9] is the only common exception to this rule. The V7(b9) has become an
> acceptable sound in many pop and jazz songs. For example, a C7b9 moving to FMaj7
> in the key of F major works because the b9 is combined with a tritone interval, Both the
> b9 and tritone intervals follow established melodic tendencies when they resolve to the
> Fmaj7. Many listeners perceive 69 combinations that do not follow such well-established
> paths of resolution as errors or wrong notes.

**Significance (p. 10).** This carves out the single permitted b9 exception and explains its acoustic justification, preventing misapplication of the general rule.

## Avoiding tritone on minor seventh chords

> Avoid unwanted tritone (#4/!11) melody/harmony intervals on minor
> seventh chords.

**Significance (p. 11).** This is the second core constraint rule: a tritone melody/harmony clash on a minor seventh chord destroys its subdominant or tonic function by making it sound like a dominant structure.

## #4 on IVMaj7 vs. II-7 contrast

> It is interesting that the IVMaj7 chord (FMaj7) can be used with #4 in the melody, while
> its simple substitution, II-7(D~7), does not work as effectively with the same melody/
> harmony combination—even though both chords share subdominant function.

**Significance (p. 11).** This is a nuanced rule showing that family membership does not guarantee interchangeability when a specific melody note is present—IVMaj7 and II-7 diverge on #4 tolerance.

## Avoid b13/13 in lead of II-7

> The use of 13 in the lead of minor seventh chords, which produces a tritone with the
> third, is even more awkward when the minor seventh is a Il-7 followed by V7. The
> musical flow of the cadence seems more satisfying when the tritone interval and its
> greater sense of motion are reserved for the G7. Avoid using a 13 or b13 in the lead
> of II-7 chords.

**Significance (p. 11).** This extends the tritone avoidance rule specifically to the II-V context, giving a voice-leading reason to keep harmonic tension reserved for the dominant chord.

## Harmonic rhythm definition and effect

> Once you have chosen your primary chord substitutions, you can add additional chords
> to help smooth out the progression. Adding chords increases the number of chords used
> in each bar. This is referred to as increasing the harmonic rhythm.
>
> In general, more active harmonic rhythms produce a more energetic musical phrase,
> while slower harmonic rhythms are more languid.

**Significance (p. 12).** This defines harmonic rhythm and establishes the expressive rule linking chord density to energy level, a key tool for stylistic shaping of a reharmonization.

## Keep it simple rule

> Another general rule fo follow when reharmonizing popular tunes: keep it simple.
>
> Once you have chosen your primary chord substitutions, you can add additional chords
> to help smooth out the progression.

**Significance (p. 12).** This is an explicit procedural rule prioritizing restraint as the default approach before adding density, framing harmonic rhythm increase as a secondary step.

## Note: not all chords need changing

> Note: It is not necessary to change all of the original chords to get an interesting
> reharmonization.

**Significance (p. 13).** This practical rule corrects a common over-correction impulse, instructing students that partial substitution is valid and often preferable.
