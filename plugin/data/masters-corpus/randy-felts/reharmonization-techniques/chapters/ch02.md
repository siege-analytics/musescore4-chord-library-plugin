## Target chord definition

> A harmonic destination is referred to
> as a target chord. Ii is a critical structural element in the musical phrase. While any
> chord in a progression may be thought of as a potential target chord, successful target
> chords are often the last or nextto-last chords of a phrase.

**Significance (p. 17).** Defines the foundational concept of 'target chord' that the entire diatonic approach technique depends on.

## Strong cadence rule

> Strong cadences are created whenever the approach chord (the chord immediately
> preceding the target] and the target chord share no more than two pitches. The general
> rule is: strong cadences = few common tones between the cadential chord and its target.

**Significance (p. 19).** Gives the defining rule for identifying and constructing strong cadences, the primary vehicle for diatonic approach technique.

## Common tones and root motion

> Diatonie chords whose roots are a third or a sixth apart have many common
> tones. Diatonic chords whose roots are not a third or a sixth apart have fewer common
> fones.

**Significance (p. 19).** States the root-motion principle that determines common-tone count and therefore cadence strength.

## Diatonic approach chord tone rule

> In order to retain a diatonic sound, the melodic lead of the approach chord must be a
> chord tone—1, 3, 5, or 7.

**Significance (p. 19).** This is the central constraint rule of diatonic approach technique governing which melody/harmony relationships are permissible.

## Weak cadence definition

> Weak cadences, also referred to as gentle cadences, are produced when the
> approach chord and the target chord have three or more notes in common.

**Significance (p. 20).** Defines weak cadences with both the common-tone criterion and the root-motion criterion, distinguishing them from strong cadences.

## Deceptive cadence definition

> Deceptive cadences are produced when a dominant seventh chord moves
> unexpectedly. For example, a V7 chord is expected to resolve by perfect fifth to |
> major. Motion from the V7 to any chord other than IMaj7 is “deceptive.”

**Significance (p. 20).** Defines the deceptive cadence type for reference in subsequent chapters, completing the three-cadence taxonomy.

## Working backwards method

> Working backwards from the cadential approach chord, | support each of
> the remaining melody notes with a chord, making sure each of the chords is diatonic
> to the key and that each chord has a root in the lead.

**Significance (p. 21).** Describes the characteristic compositional procedure of diatonic approach: harmonizing a melody by working backwards from the target chord.

## Avoiding hyperactive harmonic rhythm

> This example exposes a poiential weakness of this technique: an extremely active
> harmonic rhythm. Active harmonic rhythm can easily sound overdone or too busy. You
> can avoid a hyperactive harmonic rhythm by limiting yourself to short “runs” of notes
> approaching a target chord.

**Significance (p. 21).** States an important practical rule against overuse of the technique, directing students to limit harmonic rhythm density.

## bVIIMaj7 as nondiatonic substitute

> There is one nondiatonic chord that may be used with. diatonic approach technique, the
> LVIIMaj7. In the key of C, the bVIIMaj7 is BbMaj7. It produces a rich sound without over-
> powering other diatonic chords.
>
> Though the BbMaj7 chord is not diatonic to C major, it blends smoothly with the other
> diatonic choices. That is because the BbMaj7 is diatonic to the C Dorian scale, a key that
> is parallel to C major.

**Significance (p. 22).** Introduces the one permitted nondiatonic chord and provides its theoretical justification via the parallel minor/Dorian relationship.

## Diatonic texture definition

> Diatonic texture is derived from a single scale or key. By limiting the lead of a diatonic
> approach chord to 1, 3, 5, or 7, one can be sure that the texture of the phrase will
> remain diatonic. The sound is homogenous and smooth.

**Significance (p. 22).** Defines diatonic texture and restates the 1/3/5/7 lead rule as the mechanism for maintaining it.

## 9, 11, 13 in lead — risk rule

> The use of 9, 11, or 13 in the lead greatly increases the possibility that the progression
> will quickly move out of the original key. it also means that you will more frequently
> encounter unwanted melody/harmony relationships, such as dissonant b9 or #11
> intervals. Though the result may sound interesting, its chromatic sound may be too
> dissonant for use in a mainstream pop style. To maintain a truly diatonic texture, restrict
> your choice of melody/harmony combinations fo 1, 3, 5, or 7 in the lead of the
> diatonic chords.

**Significance (p. 23).** Gives an explicit rule against extended-interval leads (9/11/13) for maintaining diatonic texture in mainstream pop style.

## Avoid b9 and tritone combinations

> No matter which technique you use, always avoid poor
> melody/harmony combinations—unwanted b9s and tritones.

**Significance (p. 23).** States the universal melody/harmony rule that applies across all reharmonization techniques in the book.

## Harmonizing downbeats only

> To reharmonize, diatonic chords (and a bVIIMaj7) with 3 in the lead support melody
> notes that fall on rhythmic downbeats. | do not harmonize melodic notes that fall on
> weaker rhythmic beats.

**Significance (p. 24).** Provides a practical technique for applying diatonic approach to non-quarter-note melodies without creating overly dense harmonic rhythms.
