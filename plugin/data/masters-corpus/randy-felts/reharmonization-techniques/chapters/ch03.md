## Tritone substitution procedure

> To use tritone substitution, start with a target chord. Then, work backward from the target
> chord, harmonizing each melody note with a series of dominant seventh chords a perfect
> fifth apart.

**Significance (p. 28).** States the core procedural rule for tritone substitution — direction (backward), interval (perfect fifth), and starting point (target chord).

## Altered tension resolution tendency

> Chord tensions are called altered when they are a half step above or below a 5,
> major 9, or a major 13. In the performance practice of pop and jazz standard tunes,
> altered tensions such as the 69, #9, b5, b13 melody notes have most often been resolved
> in a downward direction.

**Significance (p. 29).** Defines 'altered tension' and establishes the core performance-practice rule that these tones resolve downward — underpins Errors 2, 4, and 5 in the error table.

## Exception rule for upward movement of altered tensions

> Almost any motion up or down from b9, #9, b5, or b13 is possible.

**Significance (p. 30).** Qualifies the downward-resolution rule: upward motion is allowed but must be contextually justified, not reflexive — a key nuance for application.

## Extended dominants: purpose and overuse warning

> The strong sense of forward motion and resolution created by reharmonizing with chains
> of extended dominants helps drive short runs of notes toward a target chord. As with
> diatonic approach technique, be careful not to overuse this method, since it can produce
> a very busy harmonic rhythm that overpowers the melody.

**Significance (p. 30).** Names the harmonic function of extended dominant chains (forward motion toward target) and provides a practical constraint against overuse.

## Definition of interpolated chord

> Any chord placed between two dominant chords may be described as interpolated.
> This new chord briefly delays resolution. Commonly, the interpolated chord is a minor
> seventh chord placed between two dominants in a pattern.

**Significance (p. 31).** Defines 'interpolated chord' — a structural concept used throughout subsequent examples and reharmonization decisions.

## Skipping interpolated chords in extended dominant chains

> Sometimes, you may choose to ignore a single interpolated or interrupting chord when
> reharmonizing with extended dominant chords.

**Significance (p. 31).** Permits a specific reharmonization option (skipping interpolated chords), expanding the set of valid moves when applying the extended dominant technique.

## Reducing chord density at fast tempos

> Chords appear only on strong beats, 1 and 3, of the second measure
> in order fo maintain the extended dominant sound while reducing the number of chords
> per bar. Reducing the number of chords in each bar will help to produce a smoother
> sound, and is particularly desirable if the performance tempo is medium to fast.

**Significance (p. 32).** Teaches a practical density-management rule: limiting chords to strong beats preserves the extended-dominant effect while avoiding rhythmic clutter at faster tempos.

## Converting dominant chords to minor sevenths for smoother texture

> More variations of these patterns may be found by converting dominant chords that fall
> on strong beats to minor seventh chords. This yields a slightly smoother texture.

**Significance (p. 33).** States a transformation rule (dominant → minor seventh on strong beats) that produces a distinct timbral outcome — foundational to extended II-V7 patterns.

## Rhythmic suitability for extended dominant / II-V7 technique

> Notice that these melodies consist mainly of quarter notes, dotted quarter notes, or half
> notes. The extended dominant or extended IL-V7 technique works best with melodic
> rhythms of this sort. With only one or two melody notes per chord, you can quickly solve
> melody/harmony clashes with tritone substitutions.

**Significance (p. 35).** Specifies the rhythmic conditions under which the entire chapter's technique is most effective — a scope limitation the student must understand before applying the method.

## Definition and effect of displacement

> Displacement changes the shape of a harmonic progression by moving a target chord
> to a point later in the progression. This chord movement maintains emphasis on the target
> chord, but stretches the overall shape of the progression by shifting the locations of har-
> monic cadences.
>
> Displacing a target chord creates an open space within the phrase. That open space can
> then be filled with new chord changes. If a target chord is displaced by only a beat or
> two, the reharmonization will be similar to the original phrase. Displacement of more
> than a measure will create a more dramatic change.

**Significance (p. 36).** Defines displacement as a reharmonization technique, explains its mechanical effect (open space), and calibrates the relationship between displacement amount and degree of harmonic change.
