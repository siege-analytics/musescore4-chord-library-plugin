## Bass line direction and cadential strength

> Bass lines usually move toward target chords with strong, stepwise root motion, Chords
> built upon these notes will have a strong cadential flow, and will sound most effective at
> phrase endings.

**Significance (p. 53).** Foundational thesis: bass-line root motion drives cadence.

## Consistency of bass-line direction

> Be sure to move your bass lines consistently up or down into the target chord. Failure to
> maintain a consistent direction will weaken the cadential possibilities of the chord
> progressions derived from the bass line.

**Significance (p. 54).** Prescriptive rule with explicit consequence.

## Dissonance limit between melody and bass

> Also, consider the interval combinations between the melody and the bass line. If you
> desire a smooth reharmonization, avoid more than three consecutive dissonant intervals
> between the bass line and the melody. (In this context, dissonant intervals are seconds,
> fourths, fifths, and sevenths. Minor sevenths, major seconds, and augmented
> fourths/diminished fifths are the most dissonant combinations.)

**Significance (p. 54).** Specific quantified constraint on dissonance.

## Treating bass notes as chord tones 1 3 5 7

> Think of the bass notes as chord tones 1, 3, 5, or 7 of potential reharmonized chord
> changes. Thinking of these notes as 9, 11, or 13 will produce hybrid chord voicings
> (see chapter 14) or may produce chord symbols that are overly complex (see chapter 7).

**Significance (p. 54).** Core practical rule for deriving chords from bass notes.

## Third-inversion chords: prerequisite root-position context

> Be cautious about using chord tone 7 in the bass (third inversion), unless a root position
> form of the same chord is used directly before the inversion.

**Significance (p. 55).** Voice-leading prerequisite for third-inversion chords.

## Hybrid chords: ambiguity

> if the chords are not in this sequence, the chord structure with the 7 in the bass will often
> sound like an incomplete voicing of the bass note. For example: C7 with a Bb in the bass
> (symbol: C/B) will be heard as a type of Bb chord with tensions 9, #11, and 13, unless
> it has a C7 before it to establish context.

**Significance (p. 56).** Explains the perceptual mechanism of hybrid chord ambiguity.

## Hybrid chords: definition and warning

> These incomplete, ambiguous voicings are called hybrid chords. They have a
> distinctive sound and texture, and should be used carefully. If used indiscriminately,
> hybrids can break up the sonic texture and mood of a phrase.

**Significance (p. 56).** Defines hybrid chords and articulates the aesthetic risk.

## Harmonic rhythm: keep it simple

> Remember the rule: Keep it simple. When working with busy harmonic rhythms in which
> there are two, three, or four chords per measure, relatively simple tonal progressions
> work best.

**Significance (p. 56).** Named rule constraining chord choice under dense harmonic rhythm.

## Melodic variation as a reharmonization tool

> A few subtle changes to the melody line may be used to solve melody/harmony conflicts
> and to provide color and variety to the reharmonization. This approach works best with
> melodies that are extremely familiar. It should not be overused.
