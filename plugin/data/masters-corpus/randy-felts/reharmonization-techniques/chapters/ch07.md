## Diatonic tensions implied in jazz style

> While diatonic tensions are implied in standard jazz style, all nondiatonic tensions
> should be included in the chord symbols.

**Significance (p. 67).** Core rule: asymmetry between diatonic (omit) and nondiatonic (always include) tensions.

## Triadic chord symbols inappropriate in jazz standards

> In Fig. 7.3, the use of triads is inappropriate and stylistically incorrect. The standard tune
> style is not presenied clearly by triadic chord symbols. Standard jazz chord symbols
> usually include additional pitches—such as sevenths.

**Significance (p. 65).** Why seventh chords are baseline vocabulary.

## Effect of seventh chords on harmonic analysis clarity

> Changing the chord symbols to seventh chords brings their representation into sync with
> the normal chord vocabulary of jazz standards. The use of major seventh chord symbols
> on Eb and Ab makes the chords easier to analyze as IMaj7 and IVMaj7.

**Significance (p. 66).** Functional payoff: harmonic function becomes legible.

## Diatonic tensions are overkill to write explicitly

> Tensions 9 and 13 are diatonic to the key and are
> automatically implied in a standard tune style. Because diatonic tensions are part of jazz
> style, it would be considered overkill to write them out explicitly. Often, when a writer
> includes diatonic tensions in the chord symbol, he or she really is suggesting a specific
> chord voicing without writing it out in notation.

**Significance (p. 67).** Reasoning behind the omit-diatonic-tensions rule.

## Avoid mixing sharp- and flat-key chord symbols

> Another common chord symbol notation error is to mix chord symbols derived from sharp
> keys and flat keys within the same short phrase. It is visually confusing, and will make it
> much harder for the musicians to read and perform the music correctly.

**Significance (p. 68).** Names error and practical consequence for performers.

## Avoid the word 'add' in chord symbols

> Avoid the word “add” in chord symbols. Tension 11 is mislabeled this way most offen.

**Significance (p. 68).** Direct prescriptive rule with most frequent offender identified.

## Diminished seventh chords: context determines correct symbol

> Any diminished seventh can be
> expressed as a series of four enharmonically equal chord symbols. The chords below
> share the same chord tones. However, context will determine which chord symbol best
> represents the sound and harmonic function of the chord.

**Significance (p. 68).** Enharmonic equivalence means symbol choice is a functional decision.

## C#°7 preferred over E°7 to show dominant function

> C#°7, on the other hand, contains all the tones of A7, the dominant of D minor, without
> the root. Using C#°7 to represent these pitches makes the dominant resolution between
> these two chords much easier to grasp.

**Significance (p. 69).** Concrete demonstration of enharmonic respelling clarifying function.

## Two clues a line cliché is hidden in a progression

> Two clues that a line
> cliché is hidden in the progression are:
>
> 1. Presence of a secondary dominant chord with an augmented quality
>
> 2. Presence of a diminished seventh that does not resolve by step

**Significance (p. 69).** Diagnostic criteria for hidden line clichés.

## Line cliché notation principle

> A line cliché is most clearly represented as a single chord with a line moving against it.
> Diminished seventh chords normally resolve by half-step root motion.

**Significance (p. 69).** Notational prescription for line clichés.

## Overly complex symbols hide tonic function

> This chord progression is overly complex and hides the tonic (T) function of the C triad.
> Also, G°7 has a dominant sound approaching F6, but the dominant relationship is not
> clearly expressed by the chord symbol.

**Significance (p. 70).** Dual problem: obscured tonic, unexpressed dominant relationship.

## Slash chords vs. functional symbols

> Complex chord symbols like E-7/C or D~7/G may sometimes be clear and
> functionally accurate if they show true hybrid chord function or if they are part of a
> progression over a sustained note—a pedal tone.

**Significance (p. 70).** Slash notation is correct when reflecting hybrid or pedal function.
