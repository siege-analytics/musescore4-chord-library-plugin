## Definition and placement of turnarounds

> Turnarounds are reharmonized within the existing song form. The turnaround is often
>
> specifically designed to replace the long IMaj7 chord found in measures 31 and 32 of
> many jazz standards.

**Significance (p. 73).** Foundational definition.

## First chord of a turnaround

> The first chord of a turnaround (measure 31) can be any chord that supports the melodic
> line, but is often a chord with tonic function.

**Significance (p. 74).** Governing rule for first chord position.

## Second chord of a turnaround — the link

> The second chord of a typical turnaround
> (measure 31, beat 3) is a chord that connects forward to one of the chords in measure
> 32. Think of the chords in measure 32 as target chords. The second chord of the
> turnaround resolves by strong root motion into one of the targets. It forms a link between
> the first and third or fourth chords in the turnaround.

**Significance (p. 74).** Defines the link function and introduces target-chord concept.

## Third chord of a turnaround — subdominant

> The third chord of a typical turnaround (measure 32, beat 1} is a chord that has a
> subdominant function (SD) and leads by strong root motion into the last chord of the
> turnaround.

**Significance (p. 74).** Assigns subdominant function to third chord position.

## Fourth chord of a turnaround — dominant

> The fourth and usually last chord of a typical turnaround (measure 32, beat 3) is a chord
> that has dominant function and leads by strong root motion into the first chord of the new
>
> phrase.

**Significance (p. 74).** Completes the four-chord template specifying dominant function.

## Personal reharmonization of turnarounds

> An arranger or composer may rewrite an existing turnaround to give the tune a personal
> flavor.

**Significance (p. 73).** Frames reharmonization as creative tool.

## Common first-chord options

> most of the turnarounds here begin with a chord in the
>
> tonic family or a chromatic variation of a tonic family chord.

**Significance (p. 76).** Distills common-practice norm.

## Catalog of viable first-chord types

> ® tonic substitution (variations on the III-7 and VI-7)
>
> ® modal interchange chord with a root based on Ill or VI
> © IV chord or modal interchange variation of IV {IV-, IV-6, IV-7}
> © #V-7(b5) chord (a chord that is considered a chromatic variation of IV}
>
> © bIIMaj7 (a modal interchange chord that shares many common tones with IV-]
> e bVIIMaj7 or bVII7 (modal interchange chords that share two common tones
>
> with IV or IV-}

**Significance (p. 77).** Comprehensive enumeration of first-chord options.

> You may think
> of the “A” root of the chord as VI in the key. In this case, it functions as a kind of
> substitution for IMaj7, since | and VI both have tonic function within any key and may
>
> substitute for each other.

## Shortening the melody note for harmonic freedom

> As a general rule, you can drop
> the melody note after it has been established for at least two beats. This new open space
> in the melody allows you to insert other chord choices that would have clashed with the
> long Eb in the lead.

**Significance (p. 83).** Melody duration governs available substitutions.
