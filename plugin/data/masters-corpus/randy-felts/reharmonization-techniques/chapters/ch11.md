## Definition and purpose of line cliché

> The line cliché allows the writer to sustain a particular chord while adding momentum to
> the progression.

**Significance (p. 109).** Core functional definition.

## Warning about overuse on non-tonic chords

> However, repeated use of a line cliché on a chord other than the “I”
> may cause the listener to hear the chord being embellished by the line cliché as a new |
> chord. Overuse of this technique may weaken the sense of key within the progression.

**Significance (p. 109).** Tonal-center risk constraint.

## Primary location of line clichés

> Although line clichés are found on the | chord more offen than on other diatonic chords,
> a short line cliché (one measure or less) may be applied to any diatonic chord.

**Significance (p. 109).** Normative home of the technique with broader licensing.

## Half-step vs whole-step motion

> Although most line clichés move in half-step increments, whole step motion between the
>
> fifth and the root is also possible.

**Significance (p. 106).** Defines intervallic constraint.

## Whole-step motion to avoid clashes

> The moving line illustrates that
> the descending {or ascending) line does not have to move exclusively by half steps.
> Whole-step motion also works, and is sometimes needed to prevent melody/harmony
> clashes.

**Significance (p. 107).** Functional reason for whole-step choice.

## Harmonic rhythm matched to tempo

> The harmonic rhythm should fit with the performance tempo.

**Significance (p. 108).** Governing principle for harmonic rhythm selection.

## Very fast tempo requires sparser rhythm

> If performance tempo is very fast, an even more sparse and extended harmonic rhythm
> would be a good choice.

**Significance (p. 108).** Extends tempo-rhythm relationship.

## Range of the chromatic line

> The line cliché movement is most often in half steps between the fifth and root of
>
> the chord being embellished.

**Significance (p. 106).** Structural pitch range definition.

## Line cliché in major keys

> Line clichés may also be used to reharmonize melodies in major keys.

**Significance (p. 109).** Extends technique beyond minor.

## Passing sevenths as related technique

> Short passing notes against a single chord may be used in the same spirit as a full line
> cliché. The reharmonized example below uses stepwise, passing sevenths in the bass fo
> increase the sense of movement within the chord progression.

**Significance (p. 110).** Introduces related passing-sevenths technique.

## Root position precedes seventh-over-bass voicing

> Notice that a
