# Chapter 1 — Simple Substitution

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

This chapter teaches the foundational harmonic framework for simple substitution reharmonization, organizing the seven diatonic chords into three functional families: the tonic family (I, III, VI) which sounds stable and rests at phrase endings, the subdominant family (II, IV, and V7sus4) which contains the restless fourth degree and moves forward moderately, and the dominant family (V7, VII-7(b5), and contextually V7sus4) which contains the tritone interval and drives strongly toward resolution. The master's characteristic move is anchoring all substitution rules to acoustic principles—the absence of the fourth degree explains tonic stability, the tritone explains dominant motion, and the b9 interval's dissonance explains why certain substitutions fail despite correct family membership. The chapter introduces the load-bearing terms chord function (a chord's tendency to move or remain stable), tritone (the augmented fourth between scale degrees 4 and 7 that creates forward motion), harmonic rhythm (the density of chords per bar, controlling phrase energy), and establishes two primary voice-leading constraints: avoid b9 melody/harmony intervals except in the V7(b9) resolution context, and avoid tritone clashes on minor seventh chords, with a specific II-7 exception in the II-V cadence. The procedural ethic—keep it simple, not all chords need changing—frames substitution as selective rather than wholesale.
