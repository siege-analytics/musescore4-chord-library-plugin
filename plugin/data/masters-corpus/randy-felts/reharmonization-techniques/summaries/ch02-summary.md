# Chapter 2 — Diatonic Approach

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch02.md`._

Chapter 2 introduces the diatonic approach technique, which constructs chord progressions by working backwards from a target chord—a harmonic destination that typically falls on the last or penultimate chord of a phrase. The foundational principle is that strong cadences occur when the approach chord and target chord share few common tones (typically two or fewer), achieved by choosing root movements that avoid thirds and sixths. To maintain a diatonic texture, the melody lead of approach chords must be restricted to chord tones (1, 3, 5, or 7), ensuring a homogenous, smooth sound without chromatic intrusions; extensions like 9, 11, or 13 risk unwanted dissonances like b9 or tritone intervals. The technique distinguishes three cadence types: strong (few common tones), weak or gentle (three or more common tones), and deceptive (V7 resolving to anything other than I). A practical safeguard against hyperactive harmonic rhythm is to limit the technique to short runs and to harmonize only melodic notes on rhythmic downbeats, while the one permitted nondiatonic chord—bVIIMaj7—adds richness by borrowing from the parallel Dorian mode.
