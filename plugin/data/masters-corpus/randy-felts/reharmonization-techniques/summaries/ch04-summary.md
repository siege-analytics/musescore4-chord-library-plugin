# Chapter 4 — Displacement

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 introduces displacement as a reharmonization technique that relocates dominant chords to create harmonic variation while maintaining melodic integrity. The core methods include pairing displaced dominants with II-V patterns (especially half-step higher), using tritone substitution to resolve melody-harmony clashes, and applying sus4 chord qualities when the melody contains tension notes that would clash with the new harmony. Two special cases establish transformational rules: when a b3 in the melody is displaced by a half-step II-V7 pattern, it becomes tension 9 on the II-7, and when b5 appears in the lead of a minor 7(b5) chord, displacement converts it to tension 11 on the II-7. The technique works most effectively with cadential dominants as targets, since these chords anchor the phrase structure and their displacement creates a noticeable shift in harmonic shape. Tritone substitution serves as a primary tool for fixing melody-harmony problems that arise from displacement, allowing the arranger to recontextualize tension notes as chord extensions rather than clashing elements.
