# Chapter 7 — Correction of Faulty Chord Symbols

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch07.md`._

Chapter 7 teaches the correction of faulty chord symbols in jazz standards through principles balancing precision and readability. The core asymmetry is that diatonic tensions (9ths and 13ths) should be omitted because they are stylistically implied, while nondiatonic tensions must always be included explicitly. Seventh chords form the baseline vocabulary of jazz chord notation and should replace bare triads to clarify harmonic function; for instance, IMaj7 and IVMaj7 are more analytically transparent than I and IV. When enharmonic equivalence allows multiple spellings of the same pitch collection—particularly with diminished sevenths—context and harmonic function determine the correct symbol, chosen to make voice-leading and resolution patterns legible. Practical errors to avoid include mixing sharp- and flat-key symbols within a phrase, using add instead of tension notation, and writing overly complex slash chords that obscure functional relationships; instead, simplify to symbols like Cmaj7 or G7sus4 that reveal harmonic intent. Line clichés are best notated as a single chord with a moving line rather than as a series of chromatic diminished sevenths.
