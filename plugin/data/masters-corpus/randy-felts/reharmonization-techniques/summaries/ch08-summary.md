# Chapter 8 — Turnarounds

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch08.md`._

Turnarounds are reharmonized chord progressions that typically replace the long tonic chord in measures 31-32 of jazz standards, functioning as bridges between phrases or into new keys. The standard four-chord template positions a tonic-function chord first, followed by a link chord that resolves to target chords, then a subdominant chord, and finally a dominant chord that resolves into the next phrase. Common first-chord options include tonic substitutions (III and VI roots), modal interchange chords, IV variations, chromatic alterations like #V-7(b5), and bII or bVII modal interchange chords, all rooted in tonic or near-tonic function. Arrangers and composers personalize turnarounds by reharmonizing existing progressions, and the template can be expanded with extra measures for longer song forms. A key compositional technique involves shortening melody notes after two beats to create harmonic freedom for chord substitutions that would otherwise clash with sustained melodic pitches. Similar turnaround construction methods can also be employed to modulate into new keys, extending the device flexibility beyond simple phrase turnarounds.
