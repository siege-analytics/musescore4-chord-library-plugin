# Chapter 9 — Extended Endings and Modulatory Interludes

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch09.md`._

Chapter 9 covers extended endings and modulatory interludes. Extended endings provide cadential surprise by resolving to a final major-seventh chord in a new key, often introduced by a deceptive cadence after the original V7 and using motivic melodic material from the song. Modulatory interludes are transitions between choruses or songs that include a key change, typically 2-8 measures long, where the new-key downbeat is treated as a target chord approached via strong root motion and pickup-aware deceptive-cadence chords like VI-7(b5) or bIIIMaj7. Common pop modulation distances are up a minor second, major second, or minor third, with key choice constrained by instrumental and vocal range. The chapter prescribes notational conventions (double bar lines, new key signature) and reinforces the III/VI tonic-substitution principle from Chapter 1 as a tool for extended-ending construction.
