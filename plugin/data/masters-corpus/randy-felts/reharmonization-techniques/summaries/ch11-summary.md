# Chapter 11 — Line Clichés

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Line clichés are techniques that sustain a particular harmonic chord while adding forward momentum to a progression, typically achieved through stepwise motion (usually half-steps, though whole-step motion is also possible) between the fifth and root of the embellished chord. They are most commonly applied to the tonic (I) chord, though they may be used on any diatonic chord in shorter forms; however, overuse on non-tonic chords risks obscuring the sense of key by causing listeners to hear the embellished chord as a new harmonic entity. The harmonic rhythm of a line cliché should align with the performance tempo, requiring sparser, extended rhythms for very fast tempos. While whole-step motion may be employed to prevent melody-harmony clashes, the technique traditionally relies on half-step increments. Line clichés extend beyond minor keys to reharmonize melodies in major keys, and related passing-sevenths techniques can serve similar functions by increasing movement within a chord progression, always with root-position chords preceding seventh-over-bass voicings.
