# Chapter 12 — Diminished Seventh Chords

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch12.md`._

Diminished seventh chords serve as versatile reharmonization tools because four dominant seventh chords, each containing one of the tritones found in the original diminished seventh, can substitute for them. These dominant chords are located either a major third below each chord tone or a minor second below, providing multiple equivalent derivation methods. The choice among these four options depends primarily on the melody-harmony relationship and root motion into the target chord: ascending half-step motion typically favors dominants a perfect fifth above or minor second above the target, while descending root motion requires different selections. When no obvious dominant cadence exists to the target chord, the fallback rule is to choose the dominant that shares the most chord tones with the original diminished seventh while remaining compatible with the melody. This three-tiered decision hierarchy—prioritizing shared tritones, then dominant cadences, then chord-tone relationships—makes diminished sevenths powerful reharmonization devices that can smoothly connect to various targets through chromatic voice leading. The chapter also defines the chromatic II-V7 pattern, in which a minor seventh chord is followed by a dominant seventh a half step below.
