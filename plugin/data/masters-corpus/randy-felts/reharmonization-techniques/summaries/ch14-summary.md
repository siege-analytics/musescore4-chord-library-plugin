# Chapter 14 — Hybrid Chord Voicings

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch14.md`._

Hybrid chord voicings create an altered dominant sound by layering a triad or seventh chord above a fixed bass note, producing ambiguous textures useful for phrase endings and contrast. The construction follows two key rules: retain the root as the bass note, and choose an upper-layer structure that is a major or minor triad, or a major, minor, or dominant seventh chord—avoiding augmented, diminished, or minor 7(b5) structures. The technique fails if the melody contains a major or minor third above the bass, which destroys the characteristic ambiguous texture and creates undesirable harmonic errors. Hybrid voicings work most effectively at phrase endpoints rather than throughout an entire passage, serving as deliberate tonal tools. The upper layer may be voiced flexibly as either a triad or seventh chord, and a single phrase can mix both forms, offering composers adaptability within the hybrid framework. The altered dominant scale (1, b9, #9, 3, #4/b5, b6, b7, 1) provides the theoretical basis for why hybrid voicings produce their distinctive altered sound.
