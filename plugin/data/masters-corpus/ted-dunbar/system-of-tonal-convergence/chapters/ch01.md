## Scales as raw material for chords

> Each scale may be used as raw material for convergent ideas
> within a piece of music as well as many chordal sounds extracted
> from them,

**Significance (p. 4).** This is the core methodological rule.

## Combining scales in rotas

> These scales & their chords may be combined (several of them
> together) in several rotas varying their order and making more
> interesting musical usuage of color (A,—»; +, 0, 7th).

**Significance (p. 4).** Rotating multiple scales together distinguishes advanced convergence usage.

## Goal: expanding ear to chromatic resources

> The
> important factor is the expanding of hearing tolerance increasing
> the musicians ear awareness to the entire tonal resources of the
> chromatic scale.

**Significance (p. 4).** States the pedagogical objective.

## Scales in turnbacks, cadences, cycles

> The usuage of these scales in turnbacks, cadences and cycles
> (which comprise all music) in Jazz yields a wider source of ideas
>

**Significance (p. 4).** Locates where convergence scales are applied.

## Tonal gravity law

> A tonal gravity law is created once you start to play music
> (in a key). The key of the music exerts a gravity pull on all
> other chords no matter what they may be.

**Significance (p. 4).** Defines the theoretical underpinning of convergence.

## Convergence toward key center

> The energy amassed in
> a jazz improvisors solo has to be climatically released by
> arrival "Home" to the overall key center.

**Significance (p. 4).** Defines convergence as directional motion toward a tonic.

## Chromatic scale tonal relationships

> A single note removed from the chromatic scale has a relation-
> ship to every remaining tone. If the tone represents a tonality
> or key center, all remaining intervals chords and scales have a
> certain type of converginal relationship in a cadential motion
> sense to this key center.

**Significance (p. 4).** Opening axiom: every chromatic pitch has cadential relationship to any key center.

## 7th chord as most flexible chord type

> The 7th chord having the all important tritone host the
> largest and most flexible of all the chord types. (7th bs, 7b 9,
> 7th #5, 7th #9, 7th 9s, ren bg bs ect).

**Significance (p. 4).** Defines the dominant seventh chord as the pivot of the convergence system.

## System conceived at II-V cadence

> It was at
> this final juncture (II-V) where my convergence system was conceived,
> noticing all the chord possibilities on the final 5th chord in the
> cadence.

**Significance (p. 4).** Locates the generative moment of the system at the II-V cadence.

## Scale color types as harmonic spectrum

> Major, minor, augmented, dominant & diminish became
> like the green, blue, black, red & yellow of the color spectrum
> showing a manifestation and parallelism of two art forms.

**Significance (p. 4).** Defines the color metaphor that organizes the system's taxonomy.

## Using many scale colors on one chord

> This,
> from listening to John Coltrane, Yusef Lateef and others caused
> me to try using many different scale "color" types on one chord
> (7th).

**Significance (p. 4).** Key practical rule: multiple scales over a single dominant seventh chord.
