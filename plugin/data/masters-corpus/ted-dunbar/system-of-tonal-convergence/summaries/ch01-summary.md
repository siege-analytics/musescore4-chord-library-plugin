# Chapter 1 — Conceiving this Convergence System (scales & their chords)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

This chapter introduces the foundational concepts of the Convergence System, which treats scales as raw material for extracting chordal sounds and organizing them by harmonic color—major, minor, augmented, dominant, and diminished functioning like a color spectrum in music. The core pedagogical objective is expanding the musician's ear awareness to the entire chromatic scale by understanding that every pitch has a cadential relationship to any key center, governed by a "tonal gravity law" that creates directional harmonic motion toward the tonic. The system was conceived at the II-V cadence, where the author discovered the wealth of chord possibilities available on the dominant (V) chord, particularly the seventh chord with its tritone, identified as the most flexible chord type hosting variations like 7b9, 7#5, and 7#9. The characteristic move is combining multiple scales (rotating them in different orders) over a single chord, especially the dominant seventh, and applying these convergent scales in turnbacks, cadences, and cycles—the structural elements that comprise all music in jazz. Key terminology includes "convergence" (directional harmonic arrival), "tonal gravity law," "scale color," and "convergent chord," with Bb7 serving as the first concrete example of a convergent chord to Eb minor and major tonalities.
