## Chord formula definition

> These
> tones may be derived from the major scale with the same root as the chord name. For instance, any
> minor chord always has the root, 3rd and 5th tones of the major scale with the same letter name.

**Significance (p. 10).** Foundational principle: chord formulas built relative to major scale.

## Formula and triad defined

> The
> particular group of tones that is used for the construction of a particular kind of chord is called the
> FORMULA of that chord; any chord that has 3 tones in the formula is called a TRIAD.

**Significance (p. 10).** Defines FORMULA and TRIAD.

## Three chord families

> The MAJOR, MINOR, and DOMINANT 7TH chords are the “fathers” of the
> 3 families, and all the other chords, with a few exceptions, are derived from them.

**Significance (p. 10).** Three-family taxonomy organizing the chord vocabulary.

## Extensions, altered, suspended defined

> When tones are
> ADDED to any of the 3 basic chords, the resulting chords are called EXTENSIONS. For example:
> Cm7 is an extension of Cm because the bath (BD) is added to the basic Cm chord to make the Cm7. In
> other words, 1, 63, 5, becomes 1, 63, 5,67, Any chord that has a STH, #5TH, DTH or #9TH in the
> formula is called an ALTERED chord; any chord that has the 3RD replaced by a 4TH is called a
> SUSPENDED chord.

**Significance (p. 10).** Three structural categories defined.

## Inversions and voicings defined

> Any chord may be played with different arrangements of the tones in the formulas; these different
> ways to play the same chord are called INVERSIONS or VOICINGS.

**Significance (p. 10).** Defines inversions/voicings.

## 9th/11th/13th vs. 2nd/4th/6th naming

> The terms 9th, 11th, and 13th (they are the same letter names as 2nd, 4th and 6th) are a carry
> over from piano theory.

**Significance (p. 10).** Clarifies compound interval naming convention.

## #9 and +11 voice placement rule

> when using the #9, it is usually put above the
> 3rd in a chord, not below (such as in chords like 7 #9, 7#94#5, etc.), and the +11 is usually put
> above the 5th in a chord (assuming there is a Sth being played) as in chords like the +11, 13411,
> etc

**Significance (p. 10).** Voicing rule for altered extensions.

## Same-string inversion method

> starting with a 3 or 4 note chord, RAISE each tone in the chord ON THE SAME STRING up
> to the next higher tone IN THE CHORD.

**Significance (p. 10).** Core mechanical rule of the book's inversion system.

## Triad has 3 forms rule

> a rule can be derived from this exercise; that is, that any
>
> 3 note chord (triad) has only 3 different FORMS on the same set of strings.

**Significance (p. 10).** Memorable takeaway: 3 forms per triad per string set.

## 4-note chord has 4 forms

> Using the same logic for a 4 note chord, you will find there are 4 forms of any 4
> note chord on the same set of strings.

**Significance (p. 11).** Extends triad rule to seventh chords.

## Omitting root or 5th in 5-note chords

> actually in any chord with 5
> tones in the formula, the root OR Sth is often omitted and
>
> possibly one of the remaining tones DOUBLED

**Significance (p. 11).** Practical voicing rule for 5-note chords on guitar.

## Altered 5th must be kept

> if the
> Sth is altered, that is, sharped or flatted, you may not leave it out as it is essential to the chord. You
> could still leave out the root though.

**Significance (p. 11).** Distinguishes optional vs essential tones.

## Omitting 3rd in 11th chords

> In chords that have the 11th tone, the 3rd is often omitted, particularly in the 11th chord itself.
> Also you may try leaving out the 3rd in ANY CHORD (and possibly replace it with a 2nd or 4th)

**Significance (p. 11).** Widely applicable voicing guideline for 11th chords.
