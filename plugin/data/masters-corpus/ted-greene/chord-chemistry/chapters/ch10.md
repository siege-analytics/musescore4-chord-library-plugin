## Experiment with chord moving principle

> Experiment with this principle using other chords. It will really help to give you insight into the
> nature of the guitar.

**Significance (p. 57).** Closing prescription for the move-by-4th technique.

## Substitutions are guidelines not rules

> These are just guidelines or suggestions (not rigid rules) for some of the most common substitutions.

**Significance (p. 58).** Frames Section 11 as a heuristic system, not a prescriptive ruleset.

## m7 5th-higher substitution

> dominant 7th chord, you may play a m7 type chord whose root is a 5th higher than the
> root of the dominant chord, but you do not only play this m7 type chord; you play it for part of
> the duration of the dominant chord and then play the dominant chord (or extension).

**Significance (p. 58).** States the ii-V partial substitution as a duration-splitting rule.

## Altered dominant target contexts

> Dominant chords with altered tones (remember this means sth, #5th, both, #9th) can be used
> effectively for dominant chords when the next chord is: 1-one whose root is a 4th higher, 2-one
> whose root is a % step lower, and 3-a minor type chord with the same root.

**Significance (p. 58).** Catalogs the three resolution contexts where altered dominants work.

## 7#9 as bluesy direct substitution

> However, the 7#9
> chord can also be used as a direct replacement for the 7th chord in other cases too, when a
> “bluesy” sound is desired.

**Significance (p. 58).** Exception: 7#9 substitutes anywhere for blues color.

## Don't start a song with altered I7

> One important point is that you couldn’t always replace a 17 at the beginning of a song with an al-
> tered chord (except for a 7* 9); you might first play the 17 (or extension) and then you could play the
> altered chord.

**Significance (p. 58).** Restricts altered substitution at song openings.

## Top note is critical in altered chords

> The most critical factor in an altered tone is often its top note (highest pitch).

**Significance (p. 58).** Voicing rule: the soprano voice of an altered chord drives its acceptability.

## Improvising soloist context

> If you are playing chords behind
> an improvising soloist, you need not worry, if he is competent, as he will be able to hear your chords
> as you hit them, and play his solo accordingly

**Significance (p. 59).** Ensemble-context permission: competent soloists adapt to substitutions.

## Tritone-sub / half-bar dominant interpolation

> Whenever a major or minor type chord is followed by a major, minor, or dominant type chord
> whose root is a 4th higher, you may divide the duration of the Ist chord in % and play a
> dominant chord with the same root for the 2nd ¥% of the allotted time.

**Significance (p. 59).** Defines a duration-splitting substitution adding a dominant before resolution.

## Dominant fifth-higher substitution

> For a dominant type chord, you may play another dominant type chord whose root is a 5th
> higher.

**Significance (p. 59).** States the V-of-V-style dominant substitution rule.
