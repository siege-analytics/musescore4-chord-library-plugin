## Dominants a 5th apart relation

> all dominant chords a Sth apart are related.

**Significance (p. 61).** Tritone-substitution principle stated for dominant chord families.

## m6 + dominant-4th-higher conflict

> Minor 6th type chords usually should not be used for a minor chord that is followed by a
> dominant chord a 4th higher, because the m6 type chord will sound too much like the next
>
> dominant chord.

**Significance (p. 61).** Specific avoidance rule for m6 in ii-V contexts.

## m7b5 sole altered minor

> The only altered minor chord that is widely used is the m75 type.

**Significance (p. 61).** Restricts the practical altered-minor vocabulary.

## Dominant 11 (no 3rd) as V

> The dominant 11th chords (especially with no 3rd) are some of the prettiest chords when used
> properly.

**Significance (p. 61).** Highlights 11(no3) as a primary V-function substitution.

## +11 dominant substitution

> Augmented 11th type chords can be used in place of dominant chords that are followed by a
> chord a 4th higher (just like other altered dominants).

**Significance (p. 61).** Rule for +11 chord as altered-dominant substitute.

## +11 cannot start a song

> the +11 does not assume a tonic
> function; that is, you wouldn’t REPLACE a I7 at the beginning of a song with it.

**Significance (p. 61).** Restricts altered dominants at tonic openings.

## 9b5 preferred over +11

> It is actually more
> common to play a 9b5 than a +11.

**Significance (p. 61).** Practical preference between two altered dominants.

## Common tones smooth resolutions

> Notice the use of common tones on top of some of these chords. This is one aid to smooth chord
> resolutions.

**Significance (p. 61).** Voice-leading principle for chord-to-chord smoothness.

## Dom sus partial replacement

**Significance (p. 62).** Rule for using sus dominants as duration-shared substitutions.

## Unresolved sus substitution

> Sometimes suspended chords are left unresolved; in other words, they may directly replace the
> dominant 7th chord.

**Significance (p. 62).** Allows sus chord as standalone replacement.

## Diminished 7th uniqueness

> The diminished 7th chord, usually referred to as the diminished chord, is a unique chord.

**Significance (p. 62).** Frames diminished 7 as a special-case chord category.

## Diminished repeats every 3 frets

> any form of the diminished chord repeats itself every 3 frets.

**Significance (p. 62).** Symmetry property; gives diminished chords 4 names.

## Diminished as passing chords

> Diminished chords are used as “passing” chords usually; that is, they go IN BETWEEN other
> chords.

**Significance (p. 62).** Functional role of diminished as connector.

## Tonic monotony substitutions

**Significance (p. 62).** Opens rule XII: how to vary an extended tonic.

## Omit 3rd for ancient/modern sound

> Try leaving out the 3rd in major type chords for a different type of sound (expecially if you
> replace the 3rd with a 2nd).

**Significance (p. 62).** Voicing rule for sus-flavored major textures.

## Major-7/9 as passing chord

> Major 7ths and major 9ths that have the root on top or 7th in the bass can be used as good
> passing chords to a major type chord a 4th higher.

**Significance (p. 62).** Specific use of M7/M9 voicings as passing chords.
