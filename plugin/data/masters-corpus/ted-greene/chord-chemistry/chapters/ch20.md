## Lowering 3rd and b7th yields IV9

> if you take any 7th chord and lower the 3rd and 7th 1 fret each, you will
> get a 9th chord built on the IV.

**Significance (p. 106).** States the dominant→IV9 voice-leading transformation rule.

## 3rd-b7th lowering general rule

> You actually can take the 3rd and 57th of ANY dominant chord, and by lowering them | fret each,
> you will get some form of a IV dominant chord

**Significance (p. 106).** Generalizes the lowering transformation to any dominant.

## Progressive lowering through cycle

> In any progression that has a series of dominant chords based on the cycle, you may progressively
> lower the 3rd and 57th, one fret at a time.

**Significance (p. 106).** Voice-leading rule through cycle-based dominant series.

## 3rd+b7 + melody as fragment

> you may just use the 3rd and b7th of any dominant chord and play melodies on top for an
> interesting effect.

**Significance (p. 106).** Defines the 3rd-b7-shell-voicing technique.

## Resolving diminished construction

> For an interesting sounding resolution of V7 to I, you may try putting together any 3 notes of a
> diminished 7th chord whose root is one fret higher than the root of the V7 chord, with the root
> of the I chord.

**Significance (p. 106).** Constructs the resolving diminished chord for V-I.

## Resolving Diminished defined

> This chord will now replace the V7 chord, and will be called a RESOLVING
> DIMINISHED CHORD.

**Significance (p. 106).** Names the technique central to Section 19.
