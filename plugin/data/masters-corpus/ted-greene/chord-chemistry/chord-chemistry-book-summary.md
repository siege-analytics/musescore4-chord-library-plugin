# Book-level distillation — chord-chemistry

_Aggregated from per-chapter summaries under `summaries/`._

## Overall Arc and Pedagogical Structure

This guitar method book proceeds in three broad phases. The first phase (Chapters 1–8) builds the complete theoretical and referential infrastructure: fingerboard geography and notation conventions (Chapters 1–2), right-hand technique taxonomy (Chapter 3), scale and interval theory (Chapter 4), chord formulas and families (Chapters 5–6), and comprehensive chord synonym and reference charts (Chapters 7–8). The second phase (Chapters 9–16) moves from reference to application, developing ear training (Chapter 9), harmonic motion through the cycle of 4ths (Chapters 10–12), voice leading as a systematic discipline (Chapter 13), and the practical forms of chord melody, triads, and diatonic scale harmonies (Chapters 14–16). The third phase (Chapters 17–20) applies all prior systems to idiomatic contexts — modal and non-diatonic scales (Chapter 17), blues (Chapters 18–19), and rock (Chapter 20). Throughout, the book consistently subordinates mechanical pattern-learning to formula-based reasoning: students are taught *why* a chord or substitution works before they are expected to play it.

## Characteristic Systems and Terminology

The book's organizing system is the **chord formula**, defined in Chapter 4 as a combination of scale-degree numbers drawn from the major scale built on the same root. Three foundational **chord families** — MAJOR, MINOR, and DOMINANT 7TH — are established in Chapter 5 as the parents from which all other chords derive. **Extensions** (9th, 11th, 13th, treated as compound intervals interchangeable with 2nd, 4th, 6th per Chapters 5–6), **altered chords** (containing ♯5, ♭5, ♭9, or ♯9), and **suspended chords** (3rd replaced by 4th) complete the taxonomic vocabulary. The book introduces the **same-string inversion method** in Chapters 4–5 as its signature voicing tool: raising each chord tone on its string to the next higher tone in the chord, which produces exactly N distinct forms for any N-note chord on a fixed string set. Chapter 2 establishes two sets of neck **reference points** (5th fret and 12th fret) and the 1st/6th string symmetry rule that halves fingerboard memorization. Chapter 6 codifies **polytonal chord naming** — parsing a symbol like C7♭9♭5 mechanically to its formula (1, 3, ♭5, ♭7, ♭9) — so that no chord requires rote lookup. The **cycle of 4ths/5ths** (Chapter 12) is the macro-structural backbone, with dominant 7th chords generating harmonic momentum through cyclical motion.

## Rules and Prescriptions

The book establishes a dense set of explicit rules. On voicing: altered 5ths must be retained in any voicing (Chapter 5); roots or 5ths may be omitted in five-note chords; 3rds are frequently omitted in 11th chords; the bottom triad of a polychord may omit its 5th (Chapter 7); when playing with a bassist, upper voicings must be kept high to avoid muddiness (Chapter 7). On substitution: the **tritone substitution** principle (Chapters 11–12) establishes that dominants a 5th apart are functionally related; altered minors are restricted to m7♭5 (Chapter 11); m6 chords must not be used where they conflict with the following dominant a 4th higher (Chapter 11); augmented chords repeat symmetrically every 4 frets and diminished 7th chords every 3 frets — both usable as passing chords (Chapters 8, 11). On blues and turnarounds: turnarounds must cadence on V7 or its ♭5 voicing (Chapter 19); in fragment substitutions, the ending chord matters more than the starting chord (Chapter 19). On altered dominants: altered I7 chords should not open a song, except 7♯9 (Chapter 10); the 11♭9 chord is reserved for authentic V7 function (Chapter 12). On chord melody: the melody must always sit above the chord voicing (Chapter 14); chords are typically played on the first note of each measure rather than every note (Chapter 14). On technique: certain chord voicings demand finger technique rather than flat pick alone, with specific string-count rules governing pick-plus-fingers assignments (Chapter 3).

## Explicit Methodology and Pedagogical Moves

The book's explicit methodology is **systematic formula-based thinking**. Chapter 13 names it directly: break every chord into its individual tones, treat each tone as a separate voice, and analyze how each voice moves to the next chord. This "systematic thinking" is asserted as the reason every chord in the reference charts earns its place, and as the mechanism that makes any chord usable when properly voice-led. Chapter 6 applies the same logic to chord construction: students parse symbols mechanically rather than memorizing isolated shapes. Chapter 7 counters the temptation to accumulate chords without application, prescribing that memorization be organized by **physical proximity on the fingerboard** rather than by arbitrary collection, and insisting that knowing many chords without knowing where to use them is senseless.

**Back-cycling** (introduced in Chapters 14–15) is a recurring compositional tool: preceding any chord with its own V7 creates voice-leading momentum, and the two-chord back-cycling rule (moving back two chords yields V and V-of-V) extends this systematically. **Voice leading** is formalized in Chapter 13 with V7–I as the primary laboratory, establishing leading-tone resolution (the 3rd of V7 moving up to the root of I) and permitting 5th omission when voice leading is strong. Chapter 14 introduces the m(maj7)–m7 voice-leading rule (the 7 tone moving one fret up or down into a root or 6/7 tone) as a concrete application of these principles.

**Ear training** (Chapter 9) is structured as categorical triage: any heard chord is first classified into one of three aural categories — major, minor, or dominant — before finer identification. The chapter balances mental categorization with the explicit prescription that experience is the primary teacher for progression recognition. This empirical-plus-analytical balance recurs throughout: Chapter 10 frames substitution guidelines as non-rigid rules that competent improvisers adapt in real time, and Chapter 17 frames modal harmony in terms of **tonal center** thinking rather than exhaustive scale-chord labeling, explicitly warning against "dry" or "stiff" execution.

The book's modal vocabulary (Chapter 17) establishes **scale equivalence** — C major = G mixolydian = A natural minor = D dorian minor — as the conceptual bridge between key-based and mode-based thinking. Blues (Chapters 18–19) is defined structurally by the 12-bar form with I in bar 1 and IV in bar 5, harmonically dominated by the dominant 7th family, and extensible through turnarounds, chromatic voice-leading (stepwise motion without skipping), and reinterpreted IV minor chords. Rock (Chapter 20) adds the characteristic transformation of lowering the 3rd and ♭7th of a dominant by one fret to generate IV9 chords, the resolving diminished technique, and the I–IV–I sandwich pattern — all presented as formula-derivable rather than memorized idioms, consistent with the book's overarching commitment to systematic harmonic reasoning over pattern accumulation.
