# Chapter 4 — The Major Scale

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 introduces the foundational vocabulary and construction rules for understanding scales and chords. The chapter defines scale (synonymous with mode) as a group of notes with intervals between them, then establishes two core intervals: whole step (2 frets apart) and half step (adjacent frets). The major scale follows a characteristic interval formula of W-W-H-W-W-W-H from any starting note (the root or tonic), with notes numbered 1–8 in a scale-degree system used for chord formulas and interval naming (inclusive counting). The chapter introduces Roman numeral notation for naming chords within a major scale key, then explains that every chord has a formula—a specific combination of scale tones—distinguishing triads (3-tone chords) from extensions (added tones like 7ths), altered chords (with raised or lowered 5ths or 9ths), and suspended chords (3rd replaced by 4th). Finally, the chapter defines inversions or voicings as different arrangements of chord tones, with the book's characteristic method for generating them: raising each tone on the same string to the next higher tone in the chord.
