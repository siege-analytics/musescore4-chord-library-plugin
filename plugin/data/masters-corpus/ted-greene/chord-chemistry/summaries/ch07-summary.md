# Chapter 7 — Essential Chords, Synonyms

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch07.md`._

The chapter introduces polychords as an alternative analytical viewpoint for understanding chord construction, defining them as two superimposed triads (with the practical voicing rule that the bottom triad may omit its 5th). Polychord thinking is positioned for ensemble contexts — particularly when playing with bass players or other harmonic contributors — where a guitarist can superimpose upper triads over a bassist's root note, keeping those upper voicings high to avoid muddiness. The chapter establishes that the "essential chords" (major, minor, major 6th, minor 6th, major 7th, minor 7th, dominant 7th, and extensions up to 13th and 11th) are sufficient for nearly any musical situation and prescribes a learning order progressing from simple to complex. A core pedagogical principle emerges: knowing many chords without knowing where to apply them is senseless, and memorization is accelerated by grouping physically related chords together on the fingerboard rather than collecting voicings arbitrarily. The master's characteristic move here is pragmatic realism — framing theory (polychords) as useful only within ensemble application and prioritizing functional application over exhaustive harmonic knowledge.
