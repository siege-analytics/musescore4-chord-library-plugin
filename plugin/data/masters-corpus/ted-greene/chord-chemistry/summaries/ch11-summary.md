# Chapter 11 — Chord Substitutions and General Information

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Chapter 11 covers chord substitutions and harmonic devices for expanding comping vocabulary and smoothing voice-leading. The master establishes that dominant chords a 5th apart are related (tritone-substitution principle), restricts altered minors to m7b5, and demonstrates that m6 chords should avoid contexts where they conflict with the following dominant a 4th higher. Key substitutions include dominant 11th chords (especially with no 3rd) and augmented 11th chords as altered-dominant replacements, though +11 cannot function as tonic; diminished 7th chords repeat enharmonically every 3 frets and serve as passing chords between other harmonies. The chapter teaches practical techniques like using common tones to smooth resolutions, employing dominant sus chords as partial or complete replacements for dominant 7ths, omitting the 3rd in major chords for sus-flavored textures, and deploying major 7th and major 9th voicings as passing chords. The load-bearing concepts are tritone substitution, altered dominants (particularly the +11 and 9b5), voice-leading through common tones and passing chords, and the enharmonic symmetry of diminished 7th chords.
