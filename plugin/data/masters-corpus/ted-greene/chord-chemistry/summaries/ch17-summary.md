# Chapter 17 — Other Chords Built from Scales

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch17.md`._

Chapter 17 extends harmonic understanding by teaching that chords can be derived from any scale — not just the fundamental diatonic chords — provided their notes belong to that scale, and that any such chord can serve as the seed for building a chord scale using the same diatonic-step procedures. Key load-bearing terms introduced are tonal center (the scale-based key context, synonymous with key) and modal scale equivalence (the principle that C major = G mixolydian = A natural minor = D dorian minor, underpinning modal harmony). The chapter establishes stylistic conventions: natural minor progressions favor 3-tone chords (triads) over 4-tone extensions for their ease in modulation, while mixolydian harmonies lend themselves to blues and rock idioms; practical substitutions include using a plain minor chord in place of Im(maj7) in harmonic minor contexts. Rather than labeling every chord in these scales, working musicians think of them in terms of their tonal center, and expressive playing — avoiding "dry" or "stiff" execution — is essential to making these progressions sound musical.
