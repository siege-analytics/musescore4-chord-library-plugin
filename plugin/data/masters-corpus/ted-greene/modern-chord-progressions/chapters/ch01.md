## fingerstyle vs. flat pick

> the progressions in this book were geared to be played without a flat pick, that is, they are
> meant to be played using only the thumb and fingers of the right hand (however, if you are accustomed to using a
> thumb pick or finger picks or both, this book is also right up your alley).

**Significance (p. 6).** This establishes the method's foundational right-hand technique choice, which governs every exercise and chord voicing in the book.

## right-hand finger assignment

> Your thumb (T) should strike the lowest (thickest) string involved in the chord; your other fingers just get
> added on “‘in order” (starting with the Ist finger) according to how many other notes are in the chord.

**Significance (p. 6).** This is the core rule for right-hand fingering that students must apply to every chord in the method.

## plucking technique / squeezing

> As far as the actual plucking of the strings goes, all you need to do is “squeeze” gently to produce a sound.
> Exceptions to this arise when dealing with a chord that has six notes init. In such a case, one recommended approach
> is to just use your thumb to strum all the strings.

**Significance (p. 6).** This defines the default right-hand attack mechanic and its one exception, giving beginners a complete plucking rule set.

## nail length for fingerstyle

## double stop technique defined

> we will call the double stop. We can define the double stop as a technique where you come
> down with the tip of your finger right between two strings, so as to sound them both.

**Significance (p. 7).** This is the book's formal definition of double stop, a named left-hand technique that recurs throughout the method's chord voicings.

## double stop benefits

> some of the most important benefits of the double stop technique
> are: 1) the ability to play chords that could not be played any other way, 2) the ability to play some chords on higher
