# Chapter 1 — How to Interpret the Diagrams

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch01.md`._

Chapter 1 teaches how to read the book's diagrams and chord notations, establishing the foundational right-hand and left-hand techniques that govern all subsequent exercises. The master's characteristic approach emphasizes fingerstyle playing (thumb and fingers without a flat pick), precise wrist positioning for tone and efficiency, and a deliberate system for notating complex left-hand techniques like the double stop and the 5th finger principle. Key load-bearing terms introduced include: double stop (sounding two adjacent strings with a single finger), flattening (the alternative technique for adjacent notes at the chord's top), cleanliness (the quality standard that all intended notes must sound), arpeggiation (the diagnostic practice of plucking chord notes individually to verify accuracy), and the 5th finger principle (George Van Eps's technique for fingering one finger on two different frets). Notation conventions are explicitly defined—sustain ties between diagrams, connecting lines for double stops, and a symbol for added melodic notes over sustained chords—making the diagrams readable and the chord voicings reproducible. The chapter also addresses physical prerequisites: nail length for fingerstyle tone quality, right wrist height (2–3 inches), and the squeeze plucking mechanic, establishing that technique and notation are inseparable prerequisites for executing the method's harmonic-melodic approach.
