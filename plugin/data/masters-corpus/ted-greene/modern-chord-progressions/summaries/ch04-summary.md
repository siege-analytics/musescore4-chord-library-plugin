# Chapter 4 — I iii IV V (I)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 teaches the I iii IV V (I) harmonic formula — one of the foundational chord progressions used throughout the method — by first establishing the diatonic triads and 7th chords in C, then introducing the concept of chord extensions (added diatonic notes layered onto basic triads or 7th chords) with detailed voicing options for the I and IV chords. The master's characteristic move is prioritizing ear-appeal and good-sounding chord forms over theoretical completeness; the chapter explicitly states that missing chord tones (typically the 5th or root) are acceptable and that register/transposition should be guided by ear judgment to avoid muddy low voicings. Load-bearing terminology introduced includes harmonic formulas (progressions repeatedly used as the basis for improvisation and composition), extensions (additional diatonic notes added to basic triads or 7th chords), and the convention that harmonic formulas use triad symbols regardless of the specific extensions employed (so C/9 Em7 F/9 G7sus C7 is still labeled I iii IV V I in general terms). The chapter also situates voicing choices within musical history, contrasting Baroque usage (primarily triads and some 7th chords) with modern practice (heavy use of 7ths and extensions).
