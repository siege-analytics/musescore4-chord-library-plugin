# Chapter 5 — Diatonic Chord Scales (Major Keys) and String Transference

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches the construction and application of diatonic chord scales in major keys — chords built exclusively from major-scale notes and progressed through stepwise voice leading where each note moves up to the next scale degree of the same name. The chapter introduces string transference, a technique that transposes the same chord voicing across string groups (moving to higher strings by shifting notes, retaining shape except for 2nd-string adjustments, then moving the whole chord down five frets; conversely for lower strings with 3rd-string corrections) to avoid running out of playable frets and maintain tone quality. Key load-bearing terms include diatonic chord scale (the ordered sequence of triads built from a major scale), string transference (the cross-string voicing shift), and decoration (breaking up chord scales through note delays or moving lines derived from diatonic tones). The author emphasizes the pedagogical rule that players must mentally track both chord names and Roman numeral functions during practice, and notes that 1st-inversion voicings carry stylistic weight for 4-note triads. George Van Eps's team concept — alternating two interesting intervals with separate left-hand finger groups — is named as a specific embellishment approach, and patterns may flexibly begin on any scale degree rather than always on tonic.
