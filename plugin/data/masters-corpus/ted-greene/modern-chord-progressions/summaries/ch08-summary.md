# Chapter 8 — The Diatonic Cycle of 4ths in Major Keys

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch08.md`._

This chapter teaches the diatonic cycle of 4ths, a harmonic progression where each chord root sits a 4th higher than the previous one, forming patterns like I IV vii° iii vi ii V I that can begin on any diatonic chord as long as the interval motion is maintained. The master's central technique reduces the cycle to three steps: secure a good I-to-IV connection, identify the two descending chord scales embedded in the pattern, and alternate between them in two-chord sequence units, adjusting fingerings as needed for playability. Key terminology includes the cycle of 4ths (alternately called cycle of 5ths), sequence (exact repetition of a musical phrase at different pitch levels), and chord scales — the load-bearing concepts that allow players to construct and navigate cycles across all octaves and string sets. The chapter stresses thinking chord names while playing and warns against overuse, encouraging experimentation with combining different cycle patterns to avoid monotony.
