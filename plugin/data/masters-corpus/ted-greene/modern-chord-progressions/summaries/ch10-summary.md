# Chapter 10 — iii7 vi7 II7 V7 (and I vi7 II7) V7

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch10.md`._

Chapter 10 teaches the iii7 vi7 II7 V7 harmonic progression, with the core technique of deriving II7 voicings by raising the 3rd (one fret higher) in ii7 shapes to convert them to dominant sevenths. The chapter establishes a specific fingering rule for cases with two 3rds: raise the lower-pitched 3rd while leaving the other alone, often producing a II7#9 chord. The same conversion mechanism applies to vi7, which can be transformed into VI7 by raising its 3rd, though the author notes most vi7-to-VI7 derivations in practice employ other methods to achieve more desirable sounds. Key load-bearing terminology includes II7#9, iim7/11, and the concept of soprano exceptions — situations (such as iim7/11 with its 11th in the soprano) where the basic conversion method does not apply effectively. The chapter includes a boundary condition that limits the method's applicability and directs students to practice V7 resolutions to I as a standing rule.
