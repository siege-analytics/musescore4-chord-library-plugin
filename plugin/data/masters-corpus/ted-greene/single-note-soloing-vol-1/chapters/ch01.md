## Single-Note Soloing defined

> ‘Single-Note Soloing’ is another way of saying ’Single-Note Playing’,
> which means playing one note at a time or, more accurately, playing successions of single notes, which form
> melodies.

**Significance (p. 6).** Foundational definition of the method's central concept.

## Three core questions of soloing

> What notes can you play, especially when someone else is playing the chords behind you and you
> are doing the soloing, in other words, WHAT NOTES OR SCALES SOUND GOOD OVER ANY
> GIVEN CHORD PROGRESSIONS?
>
> 2 WHERE ARE THESE NOTES ON THE INSTRUMENT? and
>
> 3 HOW CAN YOU MAKE THESE NOTES SOUND INTERESTING? (This mainly involves being
> able to play these notes in many beautiful, flowing combinations.)

**Significance (p. 6).** Three-question framework structural spine of the method.

## Scale defined

> A scale is a fixed group of notes, usually built in an ascending direction from a fixed starting note.

**Significance (p. 7).** Book's working definition of 'scale.'

## Major scale as foundation

> In jazz, as in many types of music, the scale known as the MAJOR SCALE is used as a foundation
> from which we can build all other scales.

**Significance (p. 7).** Establishes major scale as reference system for all derivations.

## Half-step and whole-step intervals

> The most basic type of interval is known as the HALF-STEP. This refers to
> the distance between two adjacent notes... forinstance: A to Bb, Bd to B, B to C, C# to D, Eto F, and Gto Ad,
> are all examples of the half-step interval. The other basic type of interval is the WHOLE-STEP, which is
> simply a distance of two half-steps . . . some examples of whole-step intervals are: A to B, Bb to C, B to C#,
> D to E, E to F#, and Go to Ab.

**Significance (p. 8).** Atomic interval units from which all formulas derive.

## Scale degrees as numbers

> The different notes in a major scale are often referred to by number, for instance, if you see the statement
> "G is the 4th of D”, this means ’G is the 4th note in the D major scale.” This number business also applies
> to intervals.

**Significance (p. 8).** Establishes numeric notation system for chord formulas.

## Chord tones defined

> chord
> tones are simply the notes in a chord (the words "tone,” and “note” are often used interchangeably . . .
> also the word “degree”... Examples: "the tones in a chord,” “the notes in a chord,” and "the degrees of
> a chord” all mean the same thing).

**Significance (p. 9).** Defines chord tones and interchangeable terminology.

## Chord formula system

> All chords
> have a certain group of tones called a formula, that is derived from the major scale. For example, major
> 7th chords have the following formula: 1, 3, 5,7... this means that the chord tones of an A Major 7th
> chord would be the Ist, 3rd, Sth and 7th tones of the A Major scale, or: !A, 3C#, SE and 7G#. And the notes
> in an Eb Major 7th chord would be 'Eb, 3G, 5Bb and 7D (which are the Ist, 3rd, Sth and 7th tones of the
> Eb Major scale). Make sure you understand this before going any further.

**Significance (p. 9).** Introduces chord formula system — core analytical method of the book.

## Minor 7th chord formula

> Minor 7th chord has the following formula: 1, 63, 5, 67. (Note: the symbol ”b”
> placed before any tone in a formula means you Jower that tone by a half-step. The symbol "#” placed before
> any tone in a formula means you raise that tone by a half-step).
>
> So aC Minor 7th chord would contain the following tones: 1C,'3Es, 5G and *7Bé, which are the Ist,
> flatted 3rd, Sth and flatted 7th tones of the C Major scale.

**Significance (p. 9).** Worked example of formula system with altered tones.

## Triads — four types and formulas

> most basic kind of chord in music is called a triad; it is a chord that almost always has some type
> of 1, 3 and 5 in its formula. For instance, the Major triad’s formula is 1, 3, 5; the Minor triad’s is 1, 63, 5;
> the Diminished triad’s is 1, 63, 65; and the Augmented triad’s is 1, 3, #5.

**Significance (p. 9).** Catalogues all four basic triad types.

## Upper partials: 9th, 11th, 13th chords

> expanded to 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 and 11, you are talking about some type of eleventh
> chord. And when you expand it to 1, 3,5,7,9, 11 and 13, you are talking about some type of thirteenth chord.
>
> Notice that an I 1th is the same letter name as the 4th, and the 13th tone is the same letter name as the
> 6th. Once again the difference between the lower and upper partials is 7 (4 from 11 is 7, 6 from 13 is 7).

**Significance (p. 10).** Establishes upper-partial extension system.

## Chord takes a scale — terminology

> certain kind of chord “takes” a certain scale, what they mean is that that particular scale
> sounds good when played with that particular chord.)

**Significance (p. 10).** Defines the chord-scale pairing operational concept.

## Major Group 1 chords and scales

> group 1 major chords most commonly take one of two scales, either the MAJOR
> or the LYDIAN. As to which of the two to use, it is almost always a matter of personal preference . . . they
> both sound good in most cases

**Significance (p. 10).** First concrete chord-scale rule in the book.

> The D Major scale has the following note: —————————>= !D ?E 3F# ‘G 5A 6B 7C# &(D)

**Significance (p. 10).** Defines Lydian via single difference from major scale.
