## Hammer-on scope rule

> NOTE: This higher note, for now, will be
> found in the scale you are working with at

**Significance (p. 41).** Constrains hammer-on targets to scale tones at this stage of study.

## Hammer-ons in jazz context

> With the exception of a special useage known as the grace note, (to be covered in Volume II), hammer-ons,
> by themselves, have not been commonly used by most jazz guitarists. But they are commonly used as
> part of the device known as the frill, which will be discussed next.

**Significance (p. 41).** Positions hammer-ons as a building block for the trill rather than a standalone jazz device.

## Trill as decoration

> Actually, another way to look at this, and a way that I prefer, is that A is not really being
> replaced, but instead, is just being decorated, you know, like we're kind of playing around with the A note,
> but not getting rid of it, just enhancing it.

**Significance (p. 41).** Conceptual reframe of the trill as ornamentation rather than substitution, shaping student intent.

## Trill placement

> The trill is more commonly used on the strong part of the beat than the weak one, but experiment and see
> what you like.

**Significance (p. 41).** Stylistic rule for where in the beat the trill lands while leaving room for personal taste.

## 8th note as jazz unit

> At medium and fast tempos, which most of the runs in this book are intended for, the normal unit of
> rhythm in jazz is the 8th note. Great players often create a very fluid sound by running long streams of 8th
> notes together in measure after measure during one of their good solos.

**Significance (p. 39).** Establishes the eighth note as the fundamental rhythmic building block for jazz soloing.

## Swing 8ths definition

> In the swing feel, each group of two 8th notes is actually played as if it were a group of three triplets,
>
> with the middle one tied or missing. The ear then only hears two notes, which it interprets as kind of funny
> 8th notes.

**Significance (p. 39).** Core definition of swing feel: written straight 8ths transformed via triplet subdivision in performance.

## Practice both feels

> all the 8th notes in this book should be played as jazz 8ths and straight 8ths,
> because both feels are necessary to today’s music.

**Significance (p. 39).** Standing practice rule requiring every run be rehearsed in both swing and straight feels.

## Double-timing defined

> Players who possess great technique often love to break into 16th notes at medium (or even fast!)
> tempos; this is commonly referred to as double-timing.

**Significance (p. 40).** Names and defines double-timing as a specific advanced rhythmic technique.

## Speed comes from clean play

> The speed that is required here comes of course, as a result of constantly pushing yourself a little,
> gradually increasing the tempos on all the runs you are practicing; but also by making sure you are playing
> cleanly at any tempo before moving on to a faster one.

**Significance (p. 40).** Method's rule that cleanliness must precede tempo increases when developing technique.

## Dominant 7th scale defined

> The Dominant 7th scale is simply a Major scale
> with a lowered 7th tone (this tone will be symbolized by the following: 57).

**Significance (p. 41).** Foundational definition of the dominant 7th scale as a major scale with a lowered 7.

## b7 notation note

> Notice that the term ”57” means that the 7th tone is lowered one-half-step, NOT that the lowered 7th
> tone always ends up being a “flat” note.

**Significance (p. 41).** Prevents misreading of chord formula notation by clarifying what 'lowered' means in spelling.

## Scale-chord compatibility rule

> Any chord containing some combination of any of (but only) these chord tones, can take the Dominant
> 7th scale.

**Significance (p. 41).** Core scale-to-chord matching rule defining the dominant chord types compatible with the scale.

## Avoid relative-major shortcut

> Don’t fall for
> the “oh, dom. 7th scales, they're really just major scales up a 4th-trap” — it will only tend to make things
>
> harder later if you do.

**Significance (p. 43).** Pedagogical warning against the relative-major shortcut; dominant 7th must be internalized from its own root.
