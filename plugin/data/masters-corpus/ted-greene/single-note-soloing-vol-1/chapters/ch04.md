## Run/chord group sync

> for maximum synchronization of runs and chords, you could generalize and say that if a run
> contains the 3rd, use it over any of the group-1 dominants, and if it contains the 11th, use it over any of the
> group-2 dominants. And if a run contains both (or neither) the 3rd and 11th, use it over any chord in
> either group of chords. But watch out for ending or lingering tones.

**Significance (p. 59).** Core rule for matching runs to dominant chord types via scale content.

## Register importance for b7 and 9

> If you said REGISTER, you were on the money. 67 and 9 really start to come alive in this “higher” register (it's still a fairly low
> register, but high enough so that these two tones don't sound like big, weighty bass tones, but rather, more like spicy lower mid-
> range notes).

**Significance (p. 58).** Teaches that register governs how chord extensions sound — foundational for voice leading.

## Combining scales in one position

> On the following pages you will see examples of runs that combine the Major and Dom. 7th scales, in
> one position at a time. This type of guitar playing is a little preview to what happens when you have to solo
> over a lot of chords in one song or piece. You will want to know separate scales for the different chords in
> the song, but if you know enough positions of each scale, you will be able to stay in one little 5 or 6 fret area
> and play through the whole song

**Significance (p. 60).** Defines the method's position-based approach to multi-chord soloing.

## Visualize fingerboard

> Remember to
> VISUALIZE the notes on the fingerboard as discussed earlier; at the risk of boring you, let me say it again:
> THIS IS VERY IMPORTANT! It is one of the best things you can do for yourself.

**Significance (p. 67).** Primary technique rule for internalizing scale positions — visualization over rote patterns.

## Minor 7th scale defined

> The Minor 7th scale is simply a Major Scale with
> LOWERED 3rd (63) and LOWERED 7th (67) tones.

**Significance (p. 66).** Core definition of the minor 7th scale.

## Warning: don't think m7 as relative major

> You will probably notice that each m7 scale has the same notes as a major scale whose root is a whole
> step lower. But, as cautioned before on dom. 7 scales, for your own good, don’t think of the m7 scale in
> this way now.

**Significance (p. 69).** Pedagogical rule against the relative-major reframe — keeps focus on root-based chord-tone thinking.

## ii-V-I context for m7 runs

> This particular series of sounds is one of the most common in jazz (it is known as “ii - V - I’ if
> that means anything to you now; if not that’s O.K.., it will be covered later in Volume II), and is a practical
> way, at this stage of the game, for you to become acquainted with m7 runs.

**Significance (p. 67).** Situates m7 exercises within the ii-V-I progression — the most structurally important sequence in jazz.

## Overtone Dominant scale defined

> The Overtone Dominant scale is simply a Dominant 7th scale with a #4th tone instead of the regular 4th.

**Significance (p. 82).** Concisely defines Overtone Dominant by its single distinguishing feature.

## Jazz Waltz feel

> 3/4 time can be very attractive in jazz, creating the feel known as "Jazz Waltz". You may find it highly stimulating for variety's sake, to make up some runs in 3/4 time, as above, or even just using the Major (or Lydian) scale, or just the Dom. 7 scale.

**Significance (p. 71).** Introduces Jazz Waltz as a creative-application rule for learned scales across meters.
