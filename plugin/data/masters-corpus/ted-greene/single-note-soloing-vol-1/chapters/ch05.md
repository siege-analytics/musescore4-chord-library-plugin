## Lydian Dominant naming

> Because it contains the #4th (#11th), the Overtone Dominant scale is also called the LYDIAN
> Dominant scale.

**Significance (p. 83).** Defines the dual name of a key scale and the structural reason behind it.

## Upper-structure triad

> An interesting feature of this scale and also the Lydian scale
> is that the three “hippest” notes (namely, the 9, #11 and 13) form a major triad whose root is a whole step
> above the root of the scale.

**Significance (p. 83).** Teaches the upper-structure triad — foundational for polytonal color over Lydian Dominant.

## Polytonality defined

> This type of thinking and sound is commonly known as POLYTONALITY, on which there will be
> a whole chapter in Volume II.

**Significance (p. 83).** Names polytonality as a deliberate improvisational device.

## Altered dominant scale defined

> What is an altered dominant scale? Simply, any dominant 7th scale with one or
> more of the following tones: 59, #9, 65 or #5 (613).

**Significance (p. 94).** Definitive rule for what qualifies as an altered dominant scale — gateway to the section.

## Group 4 dominants

> Any Dom. 7th type chord that contains any one or more of the four altered tones (69, #9, 65 and
> #5 or 613) will be considered as being part of what we call the GROUP 4 DOMINANT CHORDS.

**Significance (p. 94).** Establishes taxonomy linking altered-tone chords to Group 4, connecting chord selection to scale choice.

## Package-deal learning rationale

> the three types of A.D. scales are all so
> similar that your brain, while noting the differences, can absorb them all in a kind of ‘package deal’, one
> position at a time. Also because they are all juxtaposed in the same position, the subtle differences betwen
> them will be more evident to you, thereby increasing your sensitivity to these slightly different colors, and
> helping you to learn the sounds faster.

**Significance (p. 96).** States the pedagogical method of presenting all three altered types in the same position simultaneously.

## Visualizing via chord forms

> If you have trouble visualizing any of these runs, then please, for your own good, go back and learn the chord forms (group 4) for this position. This should straighten it out.

**Significance (p. 99).** Remedial rule: when runs are hard to visualize, return to chord forms first.

## Overtone Dom not altered

> The Overtone Dominant scale is not considered to be an altered dominant scale — that #11 is not
> really a b5th, even though we may call it that — check the Overtone Series diagram again if you are in
> doubt

**Significance (p. 94).** Critical categorical boundary between Overtone Dominant and altered dominants.
