# Chapter 3 — SLURRING and DECORATION - Part 1: Half-step Slides; Scale-Tone Slides; Pull-offs; Hammer-ons; Trills / TEMPO: 8th Notes, Triplets, 16th Notes and Double-timing

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch03.md`._

Chapter 3 teaches slurring and decoration techniques—hammer-ons (constrained to scale tones in this stage of study), pull-offs, trills (reframed as ornamentation of a held note rather than substitution of one note for another), and their rhythmic contexts—alongside foundational tempo concepts including the eighth note as jazz's primary rhythmic unit, the swing feel (straight 8ths played as triplet subdivisions with the middle note tied or missing), and double-timing (running 16th notes at medium to fast tempos). Greene's characteristic moves visible here are the pedagogical reframe of the trill as decoration rather than substitution and the insistence on clean playing before tempo increases. Key terminology introduced: the Dominant 7th scale (a major scale with a lowered 7th tone), the scale-to-chord compatibility rule (any chord built from a scale's chord tones can take that scale), and a standing rule that all 8ths in the book be practiced both swing and straight. The chapter explicitly warns against the relative-major shortcut for dominant 7th scales, anchoring future learning to root-based thinking.
