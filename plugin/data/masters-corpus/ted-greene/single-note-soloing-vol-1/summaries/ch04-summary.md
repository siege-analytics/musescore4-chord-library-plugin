# Chapter 4 — THE DOMINANT 7th SCALE: Group-1 Dominants; Group-2 Dominants; Chord Forms; Dominant 7th Type Arpeggios and Runs; Melody Notes; Combining Scales in One Position

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 teaches the Dominant 7th scale and its application through Group-1 and Group-2 dominant chord families, establishing the synchronization rule that runs containing the 3rd suit Group-1 dominants while runs containing the 11th suit Group-2 dominants, with both/neither working over either group. Register is identified as a primary variable governing whether extensions like b7 and 9 sound like weighty bass tones or "spicy lower mid-range notes," a characteristic Greene move that subordinates note choice to voicing context. The chapter introduces the Minor 7th scale (Major scale with lowered 3rd and 7th) and warns again against thinking of it as a relative major a whole step lower, situating m7 practice within the ii-V-I progression. The Overtone Dominant scale is introduced concisely as a Dominant 7th with a #4. The chapter's organizing technique is combining Major and Dominant 7th scales within one position so the player can stay in a 5-6 fret area across multiple chords, with fingerboard visualization called out as the most important internalization practice.
