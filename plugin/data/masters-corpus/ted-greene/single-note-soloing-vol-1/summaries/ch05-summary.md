# Chapter 5 — THE MINOR 7th SCALE: Minor 7th Chord Types; Chord Forms; Minor 7th Type Arpeggios and Runs; Melody Notes; Combining Scales in One Position

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch05.md`._

Chapter 5 teaches the Overtone/Lydian Dominant scale and the three types of Altered Dominant scales as a unified package, defining an altered dominant as any dominant 7th scale containing one or more of the tones b9, #9, b5, or #5(b13), and establishing the taxonomy of GROUP 4 DOMINANT CHORDS as any dominant chord built from those altered tones. The chapter introduces POLYTONALITY as a named device — the upper-structure triad formed by the 9, #11, and 13 of the Lydian Dominant scale, rooted a whole step above the chord — flagging a whole later chapter on the subject in Vol. II. Greene's characteristic pedagogical move here is the "package deal": presenting all three altered types juxtaposed in the same position so the brain can absorb their subtle color differences side by side rather than in isolation, with an explicit remedial rule that students who struggle to visualize the runs should return to the chord forms first. A critical categorical boundary is drawn: the Overtone Dominant, despite its #11, is NOT an altered dominant — the raised fourth reflects the overtone series rather than a chromatic alteration.
