# Book-level distillation — 1939-method

_Aggregated from per-chapter summaries under `summaries/`._

Chapter 1 establishes the biomechanical and conceptual foundation that every subsequent chapter builds upon. The method's first and most persistent commitment is to reduce guitar technique to explicit mechanical laws, each stated as a near-absolute rule with justification drawn from physics and anatomy rather than from feel or tradition. Posture, seated position, and guitar angle are specified so that the hands remain free of stabilizing duty. Right-hand mechanics center on pick oscillation (as opposed to bending the wrist), a wrist axis positioned over the highest note being played (the dynamic-axis rule), and a 2:1 hand-to-elbow speed ratio that keeps hand orientation consistent as the player traverses the neck. Left-hand mechanics are equally legislated: thumb one-third around the neck, fingers arched and suspended half an inch above the strings at all times, and the wrist held straight as an inviolable principle. Chapter 1 also introduces the Solfeggio orientation that runs through the entire method — every key's tonic is treated as "do," equalizing all twelve keys and preventing any single key from receiving privileged practice attention.

The method's core execution doctrines are introduced in Chapter 1 and enforced throughout Chapters 2 and 3. Attack-and-exit timing requires that the pick strike and fretting pressure land simultaneously, eliminating the common habit of fretting before or after picking. Legato execution is defined precisely: every note sustains its full value, and the next hand position is pre-formed during the motion between positions, not after arrival. Slow-practice tempo is a structural rule rather than a suggestion — all exercises are internalized at whatever pace permits perfect execution, and the method explicitly prohibits stopping mid-exercise regardless of how slow that tempo must be. String-set notation is established as the spatial vocabulary for describing where exercises live on the neck, and this vocabulary carries forward into the harmonized scale systems of Chapters 2 and 3.

Chapters 2 and 3 apply the Chapter 1 mechanics to harmonized scale studies — major triads in Chapter 2, minor triads in Chapter 3 — in each case organized into six distinct fingering forms. These six forms are not interchangeable shortcuts; the method requires that all six be practiced equally, in long, medium, and condensed variants across their assigned string sets, in order to build what Chapter 2 calls "balanced technique." The sequence form is the structural pattern for this work: three steps up and back, repeated through the harmonized scale. Chapter 2 introduces arpeggio picking as the standard right-hand approach for these studies, defined specifically as a pulsation technique in which downstrokes stop against the adjacent string rather than forcibly passing through, and Chapter 3 adds alternate picking as the prescription for second and fourth triads in minor contexts.

The left-hand vocabulary grows substantially across Chapters 2 and 3. Legato shift — quick, accurate position changes in which all notes receive equal value and no sliding occurs at modulation points — is introduced in Chapter 2 as a cardinal requirement for moving between forms. Finger flattening, described as the method's most advanced left-hand principle, requires a finger to sustain adjacent notes simultaneously during a barre or flat maneuver. Chapter 3 contributes the rocker-arm technique, an alternating motion between adjacent fingers used specifically to achieve legato on diminished triads without sliding. Finger suspension above strings — fingers hovering rather than resting — is stated as the means of preventing unintentional slides and ensuring clean voice separation throughout both chapters.

The method is also a theory-integrated technique curriculum. Harmonized scale studies are not purely physical exercises; they are the vehicle through which the player learns chord structure, voice leading, and key relationship simultaneously. Chapter 2's instruction to sustain inner voices while the melody moves encodes counterpoint awareness directly into the physical drill. Chapter 3 introduces contrary motion (voices moving in opposite directions) and exploits diminished symmetry — the property that diminished voicings repeat every four frets — as both a fingering economy and a music-theory observation. Open voicing and broken string sets in Chapter 3 extend the spatial vocabulary introduced in Chapter 1's string-set notation.

The pedagogical architecture across all three chapters follows a consistent master pattern: state the mechanical rule, name the error it prevents, specify the practice prescription, and then embed the exercise in a key-equalized transposition regimen via Solfeggio. Common mistakes are preempted by name — rushing the final note of each measure (Chapter 2), using conventional fingerings that produce slides where legato is required (Chapter 3), and forcing picks through strings rather than pulsing (Chapter 2). The etude composition method introduced in Chapter 3 — weekly construction of original studies under fixed fingering constraints — is the method's explicit prescription for internalizing its principles beyond the printed exercises. Taken together, the three chapters constitute a unified system in which posture and grip laws from Chapter 1 are the constant substrate, the six-form harmonized scale is the primary technical vehicle, and arpeggio picking, legato shift, finger flattening, and rocker-arm technique are the named moves through which that vehicle is mastered.
