## Harmonized minor scale study

> Starting a harmonized minor scale study in six different forms. Pay close attention to the markings
>
> in these forms as they are very confusing and difficult. Watch the movement of all the fingers closely.
>
> Give this exercise the same treatment as Ex. 1, such as arpeggio picking, etc. Practice in, all keys.

**Significance (p. 18).** This sets the governing practice method for the entire opening section of minor scale exercises, establishing arpeggio picking and all-keys transposition as the standard treatment.

## Legato practice principle

> Always bear in mind the legato practice and the use of the six different fingerings. Practice in all
>
> keys.

**Significance (p. 19).** This is the method's explicit rule that legato must be a constant goal across all six fingering forms, not just a stylistic option.

## Lower harmonized minor scale

> This is a lower form (in pitch) of the harmonized minor scale. Practice very carefully as some of
>
> the fingerings, though correct, are confusing. Practice this also with the arpeggio picking and in all keys.

**Significance (p. 20).** Explicitly warns the student that unconventional but correct fingerings exist, preempting the common mistake of correcting them away.

## Tonic-only harmonized minor

> The harmonized minor scale built on the tonic chord. To explain this more clearly in case of doubt, it is written in the key of D minor (using accidentals), using the tonic triad only, as harmony. Practice each form carefully as each fingering presents different hazards. This exercise is to be practiced in all keys.

**Significance (p. 22).** Defines a specific harmonic reduction technique — using only the tonic triad — and alerts the student that each fingering form carries its own technical danger.

## Seventh arpeggio fingerboard range

> Built on the seventh arpeggio which takes in the entire fingerboard. These exercises cannot be played
> in all the keys in their original form but can be moved up and down the fingerboard by leaving out the
> first measure when descending (if too low) and leaving out the fifth measure when ascending (if too high).

**Significance (p. 24).** Gives a specific structural rule for adapting full-fingerboard arpeggio exercises to avoid range limits, which is a practical transposition method unique to this style.

## Alternate picking / up-strokes

> Pay special attention to the markings as this exercise uses alternate picking, which means that
>
> the second and fourth triads in the measure are played with up-strokes.

**Significance (p. 25).** Defines alternate picking operationally within the context of triads, specifying exactly which beats take up-strokes — a foundational right-hand rule.

## Broken set of three strings

> This is the first of a series of stretching exercises. You will notice that the middle note remains the
> same while the other two voices move around the middle voice in chromatic tenths. This is the first exercise
> using a broken set of three strings. (See page 6). Pay close attention to the set markings under the staff
> in all chese exercises.. Practice in all keys in straight down strokes, then with arpeggio picking. Be careful
> in the latter 2s you have to jump over a string with the pick. This must be done smoothly.

**Significance (p. 26).** Introduces the concept of the broken string set as a distinct technical category and warns about the pick-jumping challenge that makes it harder than closed-set exercises.

## Contrary motion / hand gymnastics

> Contrary motion is introduced in this stretching exercise. This comes more under the heading of hand gymnastics. Constant practice of these two forms will definitely increase the spread of the fingers, particularly in the first form. Though dissonant in spots, it is correct. Practice in all keys which is made possible by the two forms.

**Significance (p. 27).** Categorizes contrary-motion exercises as "hand gymnastics" and reassures the student that resulting dissonances are structurally correct, preventing abandonment of a difficult exercise.

## Diminished chord repeats every four frets

> As you know the diminished chord repeats itself every four frets. Therefore it is necessary
> to have a good foundation in order to judge the distance between triads. You will notice the fingering re-
> mains the same throughout this exercise. The jumps should be fast and accurate with the fingers solidly im-
> planted each time. In other words, look out for slides. The first finger should rest just above the fourth
>
> string, not riding it, but suspended a little above the string. Practice slowly.

**Significance (p. 28).** States the defining property of diminished symmetry, then gives a precise left-hand rule — finger suspension above the string — to keep jumps clean and avoid unintentional slides.

## Idle finger suspension (reversed)

> The same principles apply in this exercise as in Ex. 48 except for the finger suspension, which is re-
> versed. This time the first, second and third fingers do the work, and the fourth finger is poised just above
> the first string. You may have to spend a lot of time on these first two diminished exercises to get the
> two idle fingers to lie in readiness. This is important and must be practiced until perfect.

**Significance (p. 28).** Establishes idle-finger readiness as a non-negotiable technique requiring dedicated practice until perfect, not merely a desirable habit.

## Rocker-arm finger alternation

> The first and fourth fingers should alternate like the
> ends of a rocker arm. In other words the first finger should remain down until the fourth comes down, but
> make sure there are never four notes sounding not even for a fraction of a second. With this fingering
> the exercise can be played very legato. Down strokes should be used throughout until thoroughly acquain-
> ted with the exercise, after which time alternate picking should be used, with the up-strokes falling on the
>
> second and fourth triads.

**Significance (p. 28).** Describes the rocker-arm technique as the mechanism for achieving legato in diminished triad sequences while simultaneously specifying the correct pick-stroke progression.

## Unusual second-finger fingering

> A rather unusual fingering is introduced in this exercise which will have to be practiced rationally as it
>
> Make sure that all the fingers except the second are arched. Don’t be alarmed if the first
>
> joint of the second finger aches slightly while it is learning to bear the strain. The fingering remains the
>
> same ascending until the octave is reached, then reverses and remains the same descending until the starting
>
> point is reached.

**Significance (p. 30).** Warns the student about physical discomfort from a non-standard finger position and normalizes the pain, preventing early abandonment of a technically important fingering.

## Two notes with one finger

> The chromatic diminished scale in this form is one of the most difficult exercises so far, inasmuch as you
> have to play two notes in succession with the first finger. The difficulty here is in making these two notes
> sound as though they were fingered with two fingers instead of one. This can be done by making the move-
>
> ment very fast without sliding or slurring. Practice slowly.

**Significance (p. 31).** Defines the specific technical challenge of playing two successive notes with one finger and gives the only solution — speed of movement — to avoid audible slides.

## Deadening a string (open voicing)

> This is the first diminished chord exercise in open voicing. It is necessary to *‘‘deaden” a string. This
> is taken care of by the fingering. For instance, in the first form the D string (IV) is stopped from vi-
> brating with the second finger while that finger is used for the note on the A (V) string. Practice slow-
> ly and observe all markings.

**Significance (p. 31).** Introduces the technique of using a fretting finger simultaneously to mute an adjacent string, a critical left-hand skill for open-voicing chord work.

## Up-stroke arc around upper string

> Make sure you do not
> deaden either of the sustaining voices with the up-stroke. This is accomplished as follows: After the down
> -stroke the pick should travel in a small returning arc around the upper string so you will not touch it
> and stop its vibration. Then with the descending backward motion the pick will strike the middle note
>
> safely. Use a complete wrist action.

**Significance (p. 32).** Gives a precise right-hand pick-path prescription — a small arc around the upper string — to isolate the middle voice in a three-voice texture, a technique with no obvious substitute.

## Hammer-down left-hand snap

> In other words, have the fingers
> formed before the two fingers drop and make sure they come down together firmly. Do not merely Jay them
> down but snap them down hammer-fashion simultaneously with the pick stroke. Give the notes their full
>
> value and practice slowly.

**Significance (p. 34).** Prescribes a specific left-hand attack — simultaneous hammer snap coordinated with the pick stroke — as a rule for clarity and rhythmic accuracy.

## Sustaining bass under moving fingers

> You may have diffi-
> culty in sustaining the bass note while the other fingers are performing. This is overcome by concen-
>
> tration and a little more pressure on that one note. Practice slowly.

**Significance (p. 35).** Identifies the common failure mode of bass-note drop-out and gives the specific remedy — increased pressure plus concentration — rather than a generic "practice more."

## Fourth finger arch rule

> This exercise increases the reach of the fourth finger. This finger will have a tendency to
>
> lay flat when it should be arched. This may be a strain at first but it can be developed through work.

**Significance (p. 30).** States the governing left-hand rule for the fourth finger and acknowledges physical resistance, framing correct arch as a developmental goal rather than an innate ability.

## Lower strings require more pressure

> More pressure has to be applied when playing on the lower strings in order to obtain a clear tone. Practice carefully.

**Significance (p. 37).** Establishes a physical rule about string tension and fretting pressure that applies across the entire method whenever exercises are transposed to lower string sets.

## Up-stroke revision drill

> Go back through the book now, taking all exercises in which down-strokes only were used and practice
>
> them using up-strokes. Then practice them using up and down strokes alternately. WARNING: In
>
> crossing the strings with the pick, make sure the up-strokes are played just as quickly as the down strokes.
>
> Try to make them sound alike.

**Significance (p. 39).** Issues a method-wide retrospective drill requiring up-stroke and alternate-stroke revision of all prior exercises, with an explicit warning that up-strokes must match down-stroke speed and tone.

## Etude composition method

> You should try writing one of these every week, applying the following rules. Limit yourself to a
> certain number of exercises (five or six for example), varying the value of the notes any way you wish
> without changing the fingering. You can use any part of the exercises selected, from the smallest part to
> the whole exercise. Try to vary each etude from the rest. Remember you can not milk any of: the ex-
> ercises dry, as there is always a new way to twist them around. Keep them as simple as possible at first, for
> if they become too complicated it will be difficult to resolve them properly.

**Significance (p. 40).** Lays out the method's compositional pedagogy — weekly etude construction with a fixed fingering constraint — as a structured creative practice integral to the method's approach.

## Benefit from uninterrupted practice

> Benefit is derived
>
> from an exercise only when you can go through it without stopping, no matter how slowly you have to go
>
> in order to do so.

**Significance (p. 38).** States a universal practice principle — continuity over speed — that governs how all exercises in the method should be approached.
