## Independent finger and multi-thought control

> These studies help to build discipline, independent finger
> control, multi-thought control, and independent harmonic
> chromatic notational selectivity. These, I believe, are the
> foremost objectives in order to play an instrument well.

**Significance (p. 6).** States Van Eps's four foundational training objectives that the entire seven-string method is engineered around.

## No single-voice studies

> My books contain no single voice studies as such. All of
> the studies employ two or more yoices. however, single
> voices will stand out in most of the various harmonic
> structures.

**Significance (p. 6).** Declares the polyphonic-only constraint that defines this method as a study of voice motion inside chordal structures rather than melodic lines.

## Voice trajectory in composition

> In creating any musical composition, harmony must begin
> somewhere, it must go somewhere, and it must end
> somewhere; therefore, it is of utmost importance to know
> where the voices have been, where they are at the moment,
> where they are going logically, and where they can go by
> creative free choice and surprise.

**Significance (p. 6).** Codifies the past/present/logical-future/surprise framework Van Eps uses to evaluate every voice in every chord throughout the book.

## Similar is not identical

> Some of the studies may appear to be redundant and
> identical at first glance. but careful scrutiny will show that
> they are not identical, they are different—bear in mind that
> “similar” is not “identical”. The study of subtle mechanical
> and notational differences is more than just desirable, it is
> absolutely necessary.

**Significance (p. 6).** Justifies the book's deliberate near-duplicate exercise structure as a study of micro-differences in fingering and notation, not redundancy.

## Thumb placement on neck

> Keep fingers above and close to the fingerboard where they
> will be ready to function when needed. Keep the thumb of the
> left hand logically placed on the back of the neck (not around
> it) so that it doesn’t have to change attitude every time the
> hand moves.

**Significance (p. 8).** Specifies the classical thumb-behind-neck posture that the seven-string four-part voicings physically require.

## Fatigue factor

> Don't depress more strings than are being used because of
> the “fatigue factor.” When three strings are depressed, but
> only two sounding, the result endurancewise is a 1/3 waste of
> energy, and over the long haul this becomes a sizable loss—
> when the hand is fatigued it makes mistakes.

**Significance (p. 8).** Establishes Van Eps's rule of pressing only the strings that sound, an economy principle critical to sustaining seven-string textures.

## Walking action / alternation principle

> “*‘Alternation”’»—common sense, therefore, tells us that we
> should copy this walking action with our fingers as much as
> possible; shifting the balance from one finger to the other just
> as in walking. But in doing this, we must think four instead of
> two because we have four fingers.

**Significance (p. 8).** Defines the four-finger alternation principle that underlies every multiple-alternate-fingering exercise in the book.

## Don't repeat fingers

> Don’t repeat fingers on either hand unless you must do so.

**Significance (p. 8).** Compresses Van Eps's alternation doctrine into a single hard rule applied to both hands.

## Standing on notes

> Using the arched Finger technique (finger tips) and the barre in combination is necessary for
> overall technique -It's also necessary to be able to (lock down) sustain notes while other
> Fingers are in motion- I refer to this as "standing on notes".

**Significance (p. 9).** Names and defines the sustain-while-others-move technique that enables Van Eps's signature polyphonic independence on a single hand.

## Tonal chemistry / register density

> Because of this tonal chemistry a bass line can be above a light harmonic structure and stand out with
> authority if it has enough weight/density, not louder, but richer in "Timbre''- Sound color.

**Significance (p. 10).** Articulates the timbre-density rationale that justifies the seven-string low-bass voicings against light upper triads (the lap-piano texture).

## Arpeggios are frozen chords

> Arpeggios are broken chords—chords are frozen ar-
> peggios.

**Significance (p. 11).** Compresses Van Eps's scale→arpeggio→chord chemistry into the aphorism that drives every reduction and expansion exercise that follows.

## Selectivity defined

> Good i rt selective, chr
> control of the instr It having chromatic har-
> monic knowledge of the fingerboard plus the mechanisms and
> agility to be able to cope with any harmonic situation within the
> physical limits of the hands. It means not having to stay within
> the “well wom” comfortable boundaries of old habits and
> paths. It means being able to venture into other harmonic
> areas. It means having the mind dictate to the hands, not the
> hands to the mind.

**Significance (p. 14).** Defines 'selectivity', the central organizing concept of the book — chromatic fingerboard control with mind-leading-hands as the goal.

## Inner voices need room

> Moving lines must have space. In order for an inner voice to
>
> be able to stand out in any harmonic situation it must be given
>
> room to be heard. If all the notes on an organ were sounded
>
> simultaneously, all motion would be smothered—buried.

**Significance (p. 17).** States the spacing-for-audibility rule that motivates the broken/divided string sets and open voicings throughout the volume.
