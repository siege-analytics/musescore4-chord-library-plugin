## Chromatics as life line

> The very life line of music is chromatics, for without it you can't have complete selectivity.

**Significance (p. 18).** Ties chromatic motion directly to the book's central concept of selectivity, defining why every triad scale is studied chromatically.

## Descending cycle of fifths preferred

> Most of the studies in these books go through the keys
> employing the descending cycle of fifths (up a fourth or down
> a fifth) in preference to changing keys chromatically. The
> reason being that it provides more variety and change of
> fingering then just moving everything up or down a half tone
> at a time.

**Significance (p. 18).** Justifies the book's organizing principle for transposition — cycle of fifths over chromatic — because it forces fingering variety.

## Gravitational descending harmony

> Another very important reason is that our whole system of
> harmonics is heavily based on gravitational/descending
> harmony in fifths. Therefore, it is very practical to study the
> cycle by working with it in practice.

**Significance (p. 18).** Grounds the cycle-of-fifths organization in a theory claim about Western harmony's gravitational pull downward by fifths.

## Triad as central unit

> One must try to evaluate the many possibilities they afford,
> for they are endless. Most of the studies in these books are
> based on, and relate to the “triad” in one form or another.
> Closed and open voicings in all stages of separation are
> interesting and informative.

**Significance (p. 19).** Declares the triad as the universal substrate of the entire book — every subsequent study is a permutation of triadic voicing.

## Compounding triads / deletion rule

> Careful deletion of notes of least importance to the
> structure also helps in reducing the size of chords, The upper
> and lower notes of first inversion triads are major and minor
> sixth intervals apart. The sixth makes a very powerful two
> note chord and because of this power, the middle notes can be
> deleted without spoiling the chordal result, (in many in-
> stances, therefore, reducing the number of notes from six to
> four to achieve the effect of compounding two triads).

**Significance (p. 19).** States the outer-voice-anchor doctrine: the strong sixth interval between top and bottom of a first-inversion triad lets you delete the middle to fit four notes on the hand.

## Hint / suggestion of a tone

> A very useful tool in harmonic voicing (for all instruments) is: the “hint/suggestion™ of a tone. A note can be inferred but not actually
> sounded, yet its presence is felt to the point where it’s almost heard.

**Significance (p. 20).** Introduces the inferred-note device that Van Eps uses to imply harmonies beyond what the hand can physically grip.

## Overtones in voicing

> * Overtones” also play a very important part in harmonic voicing. Every note contains and excites its primary overtones, which are the
> fifth—third—and unison to a greater or lesser degree, depending on the register of the tone.

**Significance (p. 20).** Builds the overtone-aware voicing rationale that justifies the heavy-bass / light-upper-triad textures throughout the volume.

## Delayed coupling

> Another effective method to Experiment with in the illusionary art of compounding triads or
> any chords that contain more notes than can be sounded Simultaneously is by a delayed type
> of coupling - To achieve this effect the chords must be struck fairly close together timewise-
>
> The ear will remember the first notes sounded and automatically couple them to the next
> notes thus forming an overall harmonic picture.

**Significance (p. 21).** Names the temporal compounding technique — fast successive partial chords the ear stitches into a single larger harmony — central to Van Eps's lap-piano illusion.

## Triad names defined

> In the 1st inversion the tonic is on top, in other wordsitis first from the top down - in the 2nd inversion
> the tonic is second - in the root voicing the tonic is the lowest note - “the root’ - hence the terms ist -2nd
> root.

**Significance (p. 23).** Defines the inversion nomenclature (1st = tonic on top, 2nd = tonic in middle, root = tonic on bottom) used in every triad-scale exercise that follows.

## Satellite key relationships

> Become aware of these relationships for they never
> change, and keep in mind the fact that these satellite
> keys also have their own satellite keys that automat-
> ically includes their own triad relationships, etc.

**Significance (p. 25).** Codifies the invariant satellite-key duplication pattern (two keys above + two below share triads on fixed scale steps) as a transposition shortcut.

## Intentionally awkward fingerings

> Some of the fingerings are intentionally awkward in these and other studies throughout these books. They are good practice for agility
> and finger discipline/manipulation. There are smoother more ligical fingerings also shown that quite naturally should be used for more
> flowing continuity.

**Significance (p. 26).** Tells the reader that uncomfortable fingerings are pedagogical features, not errors — a critical interpretive key for the rest of the book.
