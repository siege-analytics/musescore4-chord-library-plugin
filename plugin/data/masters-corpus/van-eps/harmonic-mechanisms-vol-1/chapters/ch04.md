## Reductions defined

> REDUCTIONS
> 6 TO 3—3 TO 6—MAJOR AND MINOR—
>
> 3rd Up to the 6th-
> 3rd Down to the 6th-
> 6th Up to the 3rd-
> 6th Down to the 3rd-

**Significance (p. 70).** Lays out the four reduction directions (3↔6, up/down) that organize the entire chapter and define the reduction concept.

## Scales within scales

> REDUCTIONS
> SCALES WITHIN SCALES
> THIRD TO SIXTH—SIXTH TO THIRD INTERVALS—

**Significance (p. 70).** Names Van Eps's central reduction concept: third-to-sixth intervals are themselves scales hidden inside triad scales.

## Analyze hand shapes

> Analyze these shapes; become familiar with the subtle differences between long angles—slight angles—parallels—squares etc.

**Significance (p. 70).** Tells the student that hand-shape geometry (angles, parallels, squares) is itself a study object — Van Eps's visual-fingerboard approach.

## Hold the whole note while voice moves

> Run the moving voice up and down r ly in each station (bar) while making sure to jin the whole note.

**Significance (p. 70).** Codifies the anchor-and-move drill (sustain the whole note while the other voice runs the scale) — the physical foundation of polyphonic independence.

## Mistakes are habit forming

> Practice all of these studies very slowly at first to avoid building up bad habits—mistakes are habit forming.

**Significance (p. 70).** States Van Eps's slow-practice discipline as protection against muscle-memory errors — a recurring directive in this method.

## Third and fourth fingers as main stays

> In many instances the third and fourth fingers are the main stays of the structures. With practice, a good long relaxed reach will result,
> but don’t overdo at first.

**Significance (p. 70).** Identifies fingers 3 and 4 as the load-bearing anchors of reduction shapes — guidance for the stretch-building strategy unique to these voicings.

## Resolution chord for orientation only

> 3 to 6 up—Repeat the fingering until changed—resolution chord at end of each measure need not be sounded—it is there for orientation—1st inversion—the fourth set of three is marked in the fourth measure because that is the first cross over point to the third set of three. In other words, stay on the 4th set until changed.

**Significance (p. 71).** Explains the notation convention: resolution chords are visual anchors not played notes, and string-set markings persist until changed — without this the entire chapter is unreadable.

## Open strings only when necessary

> Open strings are used in the lower register only where absolutely necessary

**Significance (p. 71).** Codifies Van Eps's preference for fretted notes over open strings — open notes break the fingering-pattern transposability that the method depends on.

## First finger replaces open strings

> Notice that the 1st finger is ready to take the place of the open strings in the Ist bar of the "A" scale -
>
> this is a necessary mechanism because it can be raised a half tone without change-this mechanism will
> appear often - check back

**Significance (p. 72).** Names the recurring open-to-1st-finger substitution that converts open-string voicings into chromatically-transposable shapes.

## Compound and mix the stations

> 6 To 3 - 3 To 6 Variation examples - play all 1st stations - 2nd stations etc.
> Then 1st stations & 3rd stations - compound the stations both Major & Minor - mix the forms

**Significance (p. 114).** Closing directive that the printed reductions are starter material — the student must combine stations across major/minor to generate the full vocabulary.
