## Hand attack coordination

> Do not place fingers in position in advance - left and right hand attack must be together for best results
>
> in articulated situations - velocity demands it

**Significance (p. 133).** Sets the core articulation rule that left-hand placement and right-hand attack must be simultaneous, which underwrites every articulated passage in the book.

## Sixths upper line study

> The following study is the same pattern as the others in this chapter with this difference:
>
> The overall spread of notes is one octave instead of a tenth

**Significance (p. 134).** Defines the chapter's compressed sixths-with-upper-line variant by contrasting its one-octave spread against the tenth-spread default that governs the surrounding studies.

## Sustain pressure while articulating

> The whole and half notes must be sustained full value; keep the pressure applied firmly while the quarter notes are in
>
> motion, The left hand must be trained to apply and hold pressure with any one of the fingers while the others articulate with relaxed control,
>
> power, and agility.

**Significance (p. 134).** Codifies the lap-piano texture demand: sustained outer voices held under full pressure while remaining fingers articulate the inner line independently.

## Ears see, eyes hear

> THE SHAPES OF FINGERBOARD SOUNDS—THE EARS SEE
> THE SOUNDS OF FINGERBOARD SHAPES—THE EYES HEAR

**Significance (p. 135).** States Van Eps's chiastic doctrine that physical shape and sound are mutually predictive—the cognitive foundation of his fingerboard-visualization method.

## Visualize fingerboard from above

> For this concept the fingerboard must be visualized from above.

**Significance (p. 135).** Prescribes the specific mental viewpoint required for the shape-transfer studies, distinguishing Van Eps's geometric approach from standard player-perspective notation.

## Shape mutation across sets

> A and B are same shape but different notation - B and C are same notation but different shape - C and
> D are same shape but different notation etc. It is a form of mutation

**Significance (p. 136).** Names the central phenomenon—'mutation'—that arises when voicings shift between string sets, which the entire string-set chart system is designed to track.

## Cause of notational variety

> This principle applies to any notation or Voicing that involves the B and G string because of the Major
> third in the tuning - If the tuning was all fourths, this difference would not exist - It is this difference
> that allows the great variety of notational and fingering choices - what is imposible in one location is
>
> possible in another

**Significance (p. 136).** Traces the existence of Van Eps's broken/divided string-set vocabulary to a single physical cause—the major third between B and G—which justifies the whole transfer-across-sets methodology.

## Chromatics emphasis

> ALL THROUGH MY BOOKS THE EMPHASIS WILL BE ON “CHROMATICS”—
> CHROMATICS, CHROMATICS, AND MORE CHROMATICS——

**Significance (p. 137).** Van Eps's own declaration that chromatic inner motion is the defining preoccupation of the entire Harmonic Mechanisms series.

## String set symbol system

> A vertical or angled line thus— / —means “*Set"’—
> The number in front of it is the set number—
>
> The number after it indicates the number of strings employed—

**Significance (p. 138).** Defines the notational grammar (e.g., 1/3, 2/B3) that every fingering callout in the book uses to specify which strings carry a voicing.

## Broken and divided sets

> When the Ist-2nd-4th-strings are employed (skipping the 3rd string) it is termed a broken set —the symbol is:
>
> 1/B3—The first set of broken three—etc.

**Significance (p. 138).** Introduces the 'broken' set concept that lets Van Eps notate non-adjacent three-string voicings, a prerequisite for his open-voicing triad work in later chapters.
