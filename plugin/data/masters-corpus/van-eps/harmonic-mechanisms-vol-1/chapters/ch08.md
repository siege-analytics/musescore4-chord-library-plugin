## Super and sub defined

> When any one of the voices in a triad is raised one step of the
> scale it is placed in super position.
>
> When it is lowered one step of the scale it is placed in sub
> position.

**Significance (p. 160).** The core definitional move of the chapter—establishes 'super' and 'sub' as the diatonic-step displacements that generate every voicing in the series.

## Super/sub motion as voice motion

> When the voice motion in a triad (regardless of direction,)
> moves one step above normal, or, returns from above normal
> to normal position, it is termed “super” motion—
>
> When the voice motion moves one step below normal, or,
> returns from below normal to normal position, it is termed
> “sub” motion.

**Significance (p. 160).** Reframes super/sub as motion rather than static position, the conceptual key to reading the chromatic inner-voice exercises as melodic events.

## Inversion membership shift

> A first inversion triad with the middle voice super automat-
> ically becomes a member of the second inversion family—
> A first inversion with the upper voice sub becomes a member
> of the root position triads—
>
> Asecond inversion triad with the lower voice super becomes
> a root position triad—
>
> A root position triad with the lower voice sub becomes a
> second inversion triad, and so on—

**Significance (p. 160).** States the mutation rule that makes the super/sub series mathematically self-contained: every voice motion lands on another named inversion in the family.

## Original inversion is the boss

> When working with the super and sub principle it is
> necessary to keep the named inversion in each study in mind
> at all times because “it” is the “origin”; it is the original
> voicing that is going to produce others. Therefore, it becomes
> the “boss” — it is the dominating factor regardless of the other
> inversions produced.

**Significance (p. 160).** Establishes the conceptual-vs-notational distinction that lets identical notations be read differently depending on which inversion 'caused' them.

## All chords are centers

> SUPER AND SUB CONCEPT - All chords are centers - When voices in a chord are moved one
> step up or down in any scale the resulting change becomes another center.

**Significance (p. 161).** Articulates the generative axiom of Van Eps's harmonic system: every voicing is both a destination and a launchpad for new centers.

## Distinction without a difference

> There is a distinction without a difference which is very similar to the enharmonic difference in notes
> by the use of accidentals.

**Significance (p. 161).** Frames the conceptual identity of super/sub products with enharmonic spelling—same sound, different functional meaning—a central interpretive lens for the series.

## Wrong notes plant bad habits

> Every wrong note plants the germ of a bad habit because the ear retains the sound of the mistake. and therefore it becomes subconsciously
> influential; there is a very strong chance that the mistake will be repeated, and that is why any new study should be gone over very slowly and
> correctly.

**Significance (p. 163).** Justifies the slow-practice doctrine on ear-memory grounds, defining the discipline required by the multi-key repetition format that follows.

## Repetition in fingering necessary

> Repetition in fingering is necessary in all studies that are shown just descending or ascending, because they must be read in reverse to return.

**Significance (p. 163).** Explains the bidirectional reading requirement that the chapter's one-direction notation implicitly demands.

## Alternating string sets purpose

> Alternating string sets as shown above and else where in these studies is necessary practise
> for building hand shift accuracy plus the ability to change sets either up or down any time or
> place within range

**Significance (p. 168).** Names the pedagogical purpose of the chapter's set-jumping notation: hand-shift accuracy and on-demand set-changing across the fingerboard.
