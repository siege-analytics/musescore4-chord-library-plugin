## Sub duplicates root super

> First inversion triads with the upper voice motion sub are the exact same notation as root triads with the upper voice motion
> super—but of course the tonic first step triad will appear on a different numbered scale step as the examples show:

**Significance (p. 175).** Establishes the duplication principle that lets Van Eps later omit root-position upper-voice-super studies, a structural pruning rule for the whole series.

## Sub upper voice produces root

> As was mentioned earlier, notice how 1st inversion triads with the upper voice sub produce
> Root triads on the 1st and 3rd beats of each measure

**Significance (p. 176).** Points out the audible by-product of the upper-voice-sub motion—root triads on strong beats—that ties the abstract voice motion to a perceivable harmonic event.

## Four-string spread mechanics

> The two notes in the moving upper line of each step move from the tonic to the next diatonic step of the scale
> notationwise as usual, except that they are now sounded on two strings instead of the usual one, which makes a four string
> overall spread. In other words, the single step melodic interval is sounded on two strings—one note per string.

**Significance (p. 185).** Defines the four-string articulated triad geometry that distinguishes this section's voicings—a single melodic step distributed across two strings.

## Articulation timing precision

> The timing of the articulating fingers must be very precise because the two notes in the moving line must not be heard ringing
> together, they must be very closely linked but not ring over each other. When properly executed just a trio of tones is heard,
> never four, unless a hold-over effect is desired.

**Significance (p. 185).** Specifies the audible test for correct four-string-spread execution: three sounding tones, never four—the listener-side criterion for the technique.

## Second-finger hammer mechanic

> The first joint of the second finger plays the double stop in flattened position (just the first joint flat, not the whole finger).
> and at the precise moment must be arched into hammer position while making certain that the middle note “A” on the D
> string is sustained—the note must be held full value.

**Significance (p. 185).** Gives the exact joint-level mechanic—flatten-then-arch the second finger's first joint—that produces the lap-piano sustain under articulated upper voices.

## Mechanical linkage thought line

> The action of raising the flattened first joint of the second finger to arched position
> and lowering the first finger must be simultaneous and smooth as though they were mechanically linked together and
> therefore unable to be either up or down at the same time.

**Significance (p. 185).** Codifies the 'mechanical linkage' image as the mental model for coordinated articulating fingers, applicable across all four-string-spread triads.

## Mixing fingerings for smoothness

> A smoothness is gained by a mixture of both the 4 string articulation and the 3 string fingering in
> various places as this example shows

**Significance (p. 186).** Authorizes mixing the four-string and three-string spreads within a single passage, a key flexibility rule for real-world application of the articulated-triad concept.

## Articulating fingers as team

> The articulating fingers must work as a precision team

**Significance (p. 186).** Distills the chapter's articulation discipline into a single rule—finger coordination, not finger independence, is the operative virtue.
