## Step loss compensated at range edges

> ‘When a step is lost on the low end because of running out of
> fingerboard a step is always added on the high end, and vice
> versa.

**Significance (p. 223).** States the range-compensation rule that lets the reader reconstruct full-scale studies even when fingerboard limits truncate an end of the written exercise.

## Notation identity across inversions

> The 2nd inversion lower voice super is the same notation as root triads lower voice sub, but the tonic step is in a different place-take the above G scale as an example: When applying it as a root scale the 3rd step is the tonic chord=D-B-G- therefore, 3 becomes 1-this applies to all keys Major or Minor

**Significance (p. 226).** Demonstrates how identical written notation must be re-labeled based on which inversion is the conceptual 'origin'—the practical instance of the cause/effect rule from chapter 8.

## Voice motion mandates inclusion

> In root triads most of the reaches are quite great. However,
> these voicings must be included no matter how impractical
> they may appear, because by design they belong to the family
> of basic triads. In other words, the voice motion cannot be
> ignored just because the reaches become extended. The same
> treatment for all the triads is necessary in order to keep the
> continuity of the concept—what is done to one must be done
> to the others.

**Significance (p. 239).** Justifies including impractical-reach root voicings on conceptual-symmetry grounds, defending the completeness of the super/sub system over playability.

## Compromise vs original notation

> The difference between an original notation and a compro-
> mise fingering is noticeable harmonically when the tones are
> sustained, but when the moving voice is detached and
> sounded separately there is no notation difference.

**Significance (p. 239).** Defines the audible test for when compromise fingerings are acceptable substitutes—detached articulation hides the difference, sustained voicings reveal it.

## Transpose by cycle of fifths

> Remember to always transpose slowly, correctly. Also, when transposing these studies adhere to the cycle of fifths to
> keep the continuity.

**Significance (p. 239).** Prescribes the cycle-of-fifths transposition order that preserves the chapter's pedagogical continuity across the twelve keys not explicitly written.

## Station-to-station scale practice

> Practice all scale forms, major and minor, first station to first staion, second station to second station, third station to
> third station, etc. Both directions, up, and down.

**Significance (p. 239).** Defines the station-matched practice pattern that drills positional consistency across the scale, distinct from straight ascending/descending runs.

## Octave transposition as mental drill

> This pattern helps to build mental agility while building physical dexterity. The ability to be able to pick up a
> phrase or a progression (etc.) an octave higher or lower is a golden asset

**Significance (p. 239).** Names the high-value skill—on-the-fly octave displacement—that the station-to-station practice format is designed to develop.

## Open voicing fingering practice

> Many times individual fingers are used where double stops and small barres could be employed; when voice motion continues, double stops wont work - practise both ways-mix fingerings and sets - change cross over points etc.

**Significance (p. 245).** Explains why individual-finger fingerings are preferred over barres in open-voicing contexts where voice motion is ongoing—barres lock voices that need to move.
