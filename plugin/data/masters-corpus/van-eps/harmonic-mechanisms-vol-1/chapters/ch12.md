## Long reaches require relaxed power

> ‘The art of accomplishing long reaches is based on relaxed
> power and control—not a large hand.

**Significance (p. 257).** Codifies the central left-hand philosophy of the Van Eps method: reach comes from relaxation and control, not anatomy, refuting the common excuse of hand size.

## Visualizing harmonic situations

> Every harmonic/melodic situation has form, shape, a partic-
> ular geographic appearance, as was mentioned in the
> foreword.
>
> It is very important to recognize these shapes—use the
> eyes, train the mind’s eye to distinguish the subtle
> differences in harmonic schematics, the vast variety of
> plans, the layout of the total fingerboard.

**Significance (p. 257).** Defines the geographic/shape-based fingerboard cognition that underpins Van Eps's entire visualization pedagogy.

## Layered harmonic modes

> Many different harmonic modes occupy the same space, one
> on top of the other, in layers. Learn to see and hear them as
> though they were laid out in individual colors and tones

**Significance (p. 257).** Introduces the layered-fingerboard mental model that the rest of Vol 1's multi-voice work depends on for analysis.

## Chromatic scale as universal source

> The chromatic scale contains every known scale, arpeggio,
> chord, harmonic situation, theme, etc. in all twelve keys.
>
> This is why I believe the chromatic scale is to music what the
> alphabet is to literature, and the colors to art.
>
> Chromatics will be stressed frequently throughout my
> books because that is where the complete control of the
> instrument lies.

**Significance (p. 257).** Names the chromatic scale as Van Eps's foundational organizing principle, justifying why chromatic drills dominate Vols 1 and 2.

## Accidentals vs key signatures

> For the sake of clarity, many of the studies in these books are
> written using accidentals instead of key signatures because
> they don’t belong to any particular key.
>
> All accidentals, except key signatures, are canceled by a bar
> line, unless tied over. And of course, accidentals of one
> register do not apply to another register.

**Significance (p. 257).** Establishes the specific notational convention used throughout the book—per-bar/per-register accidentals—which is required to read the chromatic studies correctly.

## String set symbol system

> A vertical or angled line thus means “Set”
> The number in front of it is the set number
> The number after it indicates the number of strings employed—

**Significance (p. 258).** Defines the core syntax (e.g., 1/3) of Van Eps's string-set notation, which annotates virtually every exercise in the book.

## Broken and divided sets defined

> When the Ist-2nd-4th-strings are employed (skipping the 3rd string) it is termed a broken set —the symbol is:
> 1/B3—The first set of broken three—etc
>
> When the order is reversed, (skipping the 2nd instead of the 3rd string) the word “*broken” is used first, thus.
> B1/3—The broken first set of three—-etc.
>
> When the I st-2nd-4th-Sth-strings are employed it is termed a divided set—the symbol is:
> D1/4—The divided first set of four—ete
>
> When the Ist-3rd-Sth-strings are employed the symbol is:
>
> 1/D3—The first set of divided three—and so on

**Significance (p. 258).** Defines the broken-vs-divided string-set vocabulary that distinguishes Van Eps's 7-string method from standard guitar notation and is required to parse all subsequent voicings.

## Fifth Finger Principle definition

> THE FIFTH FINGER PRINCIPLE
> is the knack of sounding two notes simultaneously that are not located on the same fret, with
> one finger.
>
> This is accomplished by angling the finger slightly across the fingerboard, so that the upper
> inside part of the third joint of the first finger sounds the upper note while the tip of the finger
> sounds the lower note.
>
> The first finger must be arched slightly so that the fleshy inner part of the third joint can be
> gently pressed downward to depress the Ist. string.
>
> With practise the third joint of the first finger will be able to sound notes on the second string
> also.

**Significance (p. 263).** Defines the signature 'Fifth Finger Principle' technique—one finger sounding two non-collinear notes—that is uniquely Van Eps's and unlocks his open-voicing arsenal.
