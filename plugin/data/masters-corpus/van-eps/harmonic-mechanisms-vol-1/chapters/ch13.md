## Nontraditional configurations must be practiced

> Traditional configurations are well known; nontraditional configurations must be found, explored, analyzed, and well practiced.

**Significance (p. 269).** States the chapter's rationale for drilling non-idiomatic triad/bass alignments: traditional voicings need no extra work, so practice time must target the unusual.

## Don't shy from dissonance

> It is not good to shy away from dissonances. Let tones clash at times:

**Significance (p. 269).** Codifies the dissonance-tolerance rule that licenses Van Eps's chromatic contrary-motion exercises, where clashes are pedagogical features not bugs.

## Mechanical motion regardless of harmonic result

> This type of chordal movement is based on mathematical
> mechanical motion without regard for the harmonic result,
> which produces many interesting relationships, and provides
> very useful fingerboard knowledge. Notice the very interest-
> ing dissonances and progressions.

**Significance (p. 273).** Defines the chapter's generative method: drive triads and bass by independent mechanical rules and harvest the harmonic byproducts.

## Discipline of strict format

> Extend all of the examples to cover all of the 12 keys.
>
> Make up other patterns, select other keys, change the order of
> the triads, change the bass line also. Then, for the sake of
> discipline, don’t deviate from that format. Stick to it, no
> matter how dissonant it sounds
>
> Later on, when the principle is understood more clearly,
> deviation should take place.

**Significance (p. 273).** Establishes the two-stage learning rule—strict adherence first, deviation only after mastery—that governs how the student practices every chromatic-triad pattern.

## Bass as independent fourth voice

> The bass lines are completely independent of the triads. The
> bass line is an independent fourth voice, therefore it will use
> its own accidentals and because of this, there will be a
> mixture of accidentals. This situation occurs when working
> with more than one key simultaneously because, one can't
> use multiple signatures.

**Significance (p. 273).** Defines the load-bearing premise that the bass and triad are notationally independent voices in two keys at once, explaining the chapter's mixed-accidental enharmonics.

## Triad and bass as separate identities

> Always think of the triad and bass as being separate
> isolated lines in an overall four note harmonic group of
> tones. It is very important that they keep their identities at all
> time while still becoming one overall sound.

**Significance (p. 273).** Encodes the analytical-listening doctrine the student must hold while practicing: two simultaneous monophonic lines that incidentally form four-note chords.

## Major makes minor, minor makes major

> The minor seventh chord has to contain the notes (open or closed) of one of the three pure major triads.
> The major seventh chord has to contain the notes of one of the pure minor triads—in other words, major makes minor, and minor
> makes major.

**Significance (p. 276).** States the harmonic identity rule the chapter exploits: seventh chords decompose into a triad-of-the-opposite-modality, which justifies the entire Major/Minor Series cross-mapping.

## Chord symbol bass-note suffix

> The letter *
>
> the end of a chord symbol designates the bass note in the chord. Example:
>
> If the bass is the third of the chord the last two figures would be the number three and the letter “B” =
>
> 3B etc.

**Significance (p. 274).** Defines the '3B' bass-position symbol used throughout the Major/Minor Series, without which the chord labels in the chapter are unreadable.

## Inversion arpeggios reveal hidden chords

> The inversion arpeggios help in finding the recognizable overall chords.

**Significance (p. 275).** States the analytical use of inversion arpeggios: rearranging triad voices against a bass exposes which familiar 4-note chord is actually present.

## Cycle of fifths bass is neutral

> A bass line descending through the cycle of fifths is neutral—neither major or minor—until other voices are introduced.

**Significance (p. 277).** Identifies why the circle-5 bass is Van Eps's preferred generator: its modal neutrality allows it to host both major and minor triad streams without bias.

## Chameleon chords

> All small chords have second, third, fourth (etc) callings under varied harmonic conditions, positions and situations.

**Significance (p. 276).** Encodes the multi-naming doctrine—the same notes belong to many chord names depending on context—that the chapter trains via its dual-key triad/bass labels.

## Dissonance is natural in contrary motion

> Dissonances are natural when working with chromatic contrary motion involving a chord line
> going one way and the bass line going another.
>
> Also, in moving a chord chromatically up or down against a pedal bass note.

**Significance (p. 285).** Names the inevitable dissonance of contrary-motion and pedal-bass material so the student doesn't 'correct' the exercises out of existence.
