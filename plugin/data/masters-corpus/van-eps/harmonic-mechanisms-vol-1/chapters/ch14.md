## Blockout definition

> A “blockout” is a graphic chart that shows a series of
> chords on the upper line of each cycle and an independent
> unrelated bass on the lower line.
>
> Triads, both major and minor are employed in the follow-
> ing blockouts; root, first and second inversions are used.
> Their order of appearance may be consecutive majors,
> consecutive minors, mixed majors, mixed minors, or mixed
> majors and minors.

**Significance (p. 288).** Defines the term 'blockout'—the chapter's central organizing artifact—and lists the allowed triad-order permutations the student will work through.

## Dissonance is intentional in blockouts

> Blockouts by their very nature will of course contain
> harmonic clashes and dissonances of many kinds because of
> the recurring chord and bass misalignment, but these dis-
> sonances are very helpful and necessary at times. It is useful
> information,

**Significance (p. 288).** Pre-empts the student's instinct to 'fix' clashes—the misalignment is the curriculum.

## When to break the strict form

> After the basic thought line of the blockout principal is clearly
> understood, the unwanted dissonances may be altered or
> omitted by breaking away from the strict mathematical form
> of the principal. This can be achieved many different ways,
> such as: having just the chord repeat while the bass continues,
> having the bass note repeat while the chords continue, by
> having one or both skip a step or two in the cycles or by
> joining the more pleasing sections of the different cycles and
> forms together.

**Significance (p. 288).** Lists the specific deviation techniques (repeat, skip, splice) that turn the rigid blockout drill into musical material.

## Bass and triad as independent voices

> The bass line is one voice action
>
> The three notes in the triads are independent voice actions
> Sounding all four voices together produces various chords
> (singly)
>
> When they are in motion as in the blockout cycles,
> action is intoduced which is, progressional motion

**Significance (p. 288).** Restates the four-voice independence rule as the analytical lens for reading every blockout chart.

## Enharmonic spelling isolates lines

> Flats are used in the chromatic descending bass lines while the triads use their respective key
> accidentals which naturally will produce some enharmonic spellings which helps to keep the
> triad and bass lines separate and independent of each other - but at times will cause some of the
> intervals to appear wrong, however, the notes are correct even tho a third interval may look
> like a second etc.

**Significance (p. 289).** Warns the reader that the chapter's odd-looking intervals are deliberate enharmonic isolation, not errors—critical for correct transcription.

## Strict adherence in learning stage

> Rules and principles must be understood before they may be employed intelligently, in other
> words, strict adherance to the mathematical form is very important in the learning stage.

**Significance (p. 289).** Codifies the discipline-before-deviation pedagogy that governs the blockout exercises.

## Repeat order vs mixed order

> When the three triad inversions are placed in a fixed uniform group that appears in exactly the same order over and over again,
>
> forming a pattern each measure or so thru the cycle, it is quite naturally termed a “‘repeat order.”
>
> It makes no difference which inversion is first, middle, or last in the group. The location of each inversion in the pattern does not
> change. The keys may change of course, but the inversions maintain their positions in the pattern,

**Significance (p. 290).** Defines 'repeat order'—the chapter's primary blockout taxonomy term—and distinguishes it from the mixed-order variant.

## Mixed-order patterns

> The mixed order is a scrambling of the three inversions which generally makes a longer overall pattern that includes a whole cycle or
> more, but they eventually repeat. Some will take more than a hundred steps before repeating.

**Significance (p. 290).** Defines mixed-order blockouts and their large cycle lengths, justifying why some studies span dozens of measures before repeating.

## Purpose of blockouts

> One of the main purposes of the blockouts is to be able to systematically investigate the
> progressional possibilities by following and studying the three basic Major and Minor triads
>
> in sequence, going thru a sequence, while locked in a set pattern against a completely independent.
> bass line.

**Significance (p. 290).** States the chapter's analytical mission: systematic enumeration of progression possibilities under controlled triad/bass independence.

## Why fingerings are omitted

> The fingering and string set markings have been omitted in these blockout studies because all of the triad/bass relationships have
> been shown with both markings in previous material.
>
> Another reason for the deletions is:
> Blockouts are meant to be fingered and played in as many different locations and positions as possible.

**Significance (p. 298).** Explains the absence of fingerings as a deliberate pedagogical move: the student must invent and explore multiple fingerings per blockout.

> HOW TO BLOCK OUT THREE KEYS: (see chart)
>
> For example the keys of:
> C-Ab-E-Major- (only for the first cycle of course)
>
> They are major thirds apart descending—(any keys may be
> used). The order for the triad inversions will be: (repeat
> order). First=1-Second=2-Root=R- (any order may be
> used)
>
> The bass will start on Ab descending chromatically (any
> step of the chromatic scale may be used to start because all of
> the bass-triad relationships will occur anyway, regardless of

**Significance (p. 293).** Provides the explicit construction recipe (three keys a major third apart, repeat-order inversions, chromatic bass) that lets the student build their own blockouts.

## Avoiding unwanted dissonance

> Two lines opposing each other working chromatically will
> produce dissonances that may be avoided either by moving
> one of the lines a whole tone or more, or, by having one of
> them remain on a step for more than one beat. Later on,
> unwanted dissonances can be avoided when desired and
> wherever desired.

**Significance (p. 293).** Gives the two concrete deviation techniques—whole-tone shift or sustain—for taming dissonance once the strict form is mastered.
