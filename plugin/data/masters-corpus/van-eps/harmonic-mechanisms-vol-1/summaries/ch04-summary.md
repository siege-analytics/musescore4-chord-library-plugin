# Chapter 4 — Reductions: 3-to-6 and 6-to-3 Triad Reductions (Major, Harmonic Minor, Melodic Minor)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch04.md`._

Chapter 4 is the volume's first deep mechanical chapter and teaches 'reductions' — the systematic conversion between triads and the third-to-sixth intervals embedded inside them — worked in all four directions (3rd up to 6th, 3rd down to 6th, 6th up to 3rd, 6th down to 3rd) through major, harmonic-minor, and melodic-minor systems (Reductions defined). The central conceptual move is 'scales within scales': the third-to-sixth intervals are themselves scales hidden inside the triad scales of chapter 2 (Scales within scales). The defining physical drill is the anchor-and-move pattern — sustain the whole note while the other voice runs the scale up and down — which Van Eps presents as the foundation of polyphonic independence (Hold the whole note while voice moves), with fingers 3 and 4 explicitly named as the load-bearing anchors of the reduction shapes (Third and fourth fingers as main stays) and the student told to analyze hand-shape geometry (long angles, slight angles, parallels, squares) as a study object in its own right (Analyze hand shapes). Several notational and practical rules are made load-bearing for the rest of the volume: the resolution chord at the end of each measure is a visual orientation marker not a played note, and string-set markings persist until explicitly changed — without grasping this the chapter is unreadable (Resolution chord for orientation only); open strings are avoided except where absolutely necessary because they break the fingering-pattern transposability that the method depends on (Open strings only when necessary); and the recurring 'first finger replaces open strings' substitution converts open-string voicings into chromatically-transposable shapes (First finger replaces open strings). Van Eps frames the slow-practice discipline as protection against muscle-memory errors with the aphorism 'mistakes are habit forming' (Mistakes are habit forming), and the chapter closes by directing the student to compound and mix the stations across major and minor to generate the full vocabulary from the printed seeds (Compound and mix the stations).
