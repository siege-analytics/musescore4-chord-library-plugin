# Chapter 6 — Chromatic Triads and Chromatic Scales in Intervals

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch06.md`._

Chapter 6 introduces the notational and cognitive infrastructure that the rest of Van Eps's chromatic-triad work depends on, anchored by the lap-piano demand that sustained whole and half notes be held under full left-hand pressure while other fingers articulate the inner line independently (Sustain pressure while articulating). Articulation is governed by a strict simultaneity rule—left-hand placement and right-hand attack must arrive together, never with fingers pre-planted (Hand attack coordination)—and the sixths upper-line study is differentiated from surrounding material by its compressed one-octave spread versus the default tenth (Sixths upper line study). The load-bearing terminology arrives in three layers: Van Eps's chiastic doctrine that shape and sound are mutually predictive (Ears see, eyes hear) plus the prescription to visualize the fingerboard from above (Visualize fingerboard from above); the 'mutation' phenomenon naming why identical voicings change notation across string sets, traced to the single physical cause of the major third between B and G (Shape mutation across sets; Cause of notational variety); and the string-set symbol grammar itself—the slash-line notation with set number before and string count after, including the 'broken' set marker (e.g., 1/B3) that lets non-adjacent three-string voicings be specified (String set symbol system; Broken and divided sets). The chapter closes by declaring the series' guiding obsession—'CHROMATICS, CHROMATICS, AND MORE CHROMATICS' (Chromatics emphasis)—which frames every subsequent exercise as a study in chromatic inner motion.
