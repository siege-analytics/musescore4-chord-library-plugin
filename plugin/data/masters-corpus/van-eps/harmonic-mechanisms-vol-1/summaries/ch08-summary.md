# Chapter 8 — Super and Sub Series: First-Inversion Triads (Major, Harmonic Minor, Melodic Minor)

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch08.md`._

Chapter 8 introduces the 'super' and 'sub' vocabulary that powers the rest of Volume 1, defining them as diatonic-step displacements—a voice raised one scale step is in super position, lowered one step is in sub position (Super and sub defined)—then immediately reframing them as voice motion rather than static placement, so each super or sub event is read as a melodic occurrence in the chromatic inner line (Super/sub motion as voice motion). The load-bearing structural rule is the inversion-membership shift: every super or sub motion lands the triad on another named inversion in the family (first-inversion middle-voice super becomes second-inversion; first-inversion upper-voice sub becomes root, etc.), making the series mathematically self-contained (Inversion membership shift). To keep the system legible, the 'original inversion is the boss' doctrine requires the student to hold the named source inversion in mind at all times because identical notations can carry different functional meaning depending on which inversion caused them—a 'distinction without a difference' analogous to enharmonic spelling (Original inversion is the boss; Distinction without a difference). The generative axiom that 'all chords are centers' frames every voicing as both a destination and a launchpad for new centers (All chords are centers). Practice discipline is enforced by the ear-memory argument that wrong notes plant subconscious habits, mandating slow, correct first readings (Wrong notes plant bad habits) and bidirectional reading of one-direction notation (Repetition in fingering necessary); the chapter's set-jumping notation is justified as hand-shift-accuracy training (Alternating string sets purpose), and three-finger triads are preferred over barres in contexts where upcoming voice motion would be locked by a barre (Three-finger triads rationale).
