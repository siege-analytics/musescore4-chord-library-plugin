# Chapter 10 — Super and Sub Series: Root-Position Triads and Open-Voicing Alternatives

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch10.md`._

Chapter 10 is the long capstone on root-position triads and open-voicing alternatives within the super/sub series, and it opens with two decoding rules the reader must internalize to access the compressed notation: voice changes are written every four keys of the cycle of fifths rather than all twelve, leaving the remaining fingerings to be reconstructed by the student (Four-key voice change shorthand), and a range-compensation rule that adds a step at the high end whenever one is lost at the low end (and vice versa) when the fingerboard runs out (Step loss compensated at range edges). The chapter restates and applies the cause/effect labeling rule from chapter 8—second-inversion lower-voice super shares notation with root-triad lower-voice sub but must be re-labeled depending on which inversion is the conceptual origin (Notation identity across inversions)—and defends including impractical-reach root voicings on conceptual-symmetry grounds, since 'what is done to one must be done to the others' even when reaches become extended (Voice motion mandates inclusion). A practical audible test distinguishes original notations from compromise fingerings: the difference is heard when tones are sustained, but vanishes when the moving voice is detached (Compromise vs original notation). Practice is governed by cycle-of-fifths transposition order to preserve continuity (Transpose by cycle of fifths), station-to-station drilling across all scale forms in both directions (Station-to-station scale practice), and octave-displacement work targeted at the 'golden asset' of picking up a phrase an octave higher or lower on demand (Octave transposition as mental drill). For the open-voicing material, individual-finger fingerings are preferred over barres and small double stops precisely because ongoing voice motion would be locked by a barre (Open voicing fingering practice), and the variation exercises are flagged as independent-finger-control drills rather than mere voicing catalogs (Independent finger control variation).
