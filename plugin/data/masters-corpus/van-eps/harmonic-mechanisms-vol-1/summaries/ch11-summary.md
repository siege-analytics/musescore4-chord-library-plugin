# Chapter 11 — Super and Sub Series: Open Voicings, Multi-Voice Motion and Harmonic Variation

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch11.md`._

Chapter 11 trains the Super and Sub Series of open voicings as a multi-voice motion discipline: while two voices move, a third must be physically sustained full value, with release of the held voice permitted only as a last resort (Sustaining voices through long reaches). Van Eps's lap-piano texture is enforced through specific 7-string left-hand mechanics—most notably the arched-2nd-finger sustain that holds the middle voice through open-voicing motion (Arched-finger sustain technique)—and through the cross (+) notational convention marking the deleted middle voice of the first-inversion triad that begins each station (Finger-team articulation marking). The chapter frames these drills not as isolated chord-shape practice but as the analytical foundation for improvisation, insisting that hearing and analyzing the voice motion is co-equal with executing it cleanly (Hearing voice motion beats physical execution; Voice motion mastery as improvisation prerequisite). Load-bearing standing instructions recur: every closed-to-open voicing study must be transposed across major and both minor forms and run in both directions (Transpose to all keys, both minors); single steps should be looped as finger-control drills (Repeating steps for finger control); finger-team articulation must transfer from harmonic minor to melodic minor, mixed minor, and major (Apply team principles to all scales); and the student must remain alert that identical fingerings carry different harmonic and notational meaning across the 7-string string-sets (Subtle differences across sets).
