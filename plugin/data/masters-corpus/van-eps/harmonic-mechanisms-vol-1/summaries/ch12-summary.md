# Chapter 12 — Visualizing Harmonic Situations / The Magic of the Chromatic Scale, Contrary Motion Chromatic Triads, 5th Finger Principle

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch12.md`._

Chapter 12 lays down Van Eps's visualization and notation infrastructure and introduces his signature one-finger-two-notes left-hand technique. The chapter opens with the method's core left-hand philosophy—long reaches come from relaxed power and control, not anatomy (Long reaches require relaxed power)—then defines fingerboard cognition as geographic shape-recognition with multiple harmonic modes stacked in layers on the same physical space (Visualizing harmonic situations; Layered harmonic modes). The chromatic scale is named as the universal source containing every scale, arpeggio, and chord in all twelve keys, justifying why chromatic drills dominate the volume (Chromatic scale as universal source). Critical reading conventions are codified: per-bar/per-register accidentals are used instead of key signatures because the studies belong to no key (Accidentals vs key signatures), and the 7-string-specific string-set syntax is defined in full, including the broken (1/B3, B1/3) and divided (D1/4, 1/D3) set vocabulary required to parse every subsequent voicing (String set symbol system; Broken and divided sets defined). Practice doctrine is explicit—hard shots first so easy shots take care of themselves (Practice hard shots, easy shots follow), intentionally awkward long shifts as duplicate-location pedagogy (Intentional long shifts), and cross-exercise generalization of every principle (Principles transfer across exercises)—culminating in the chapter's signature contribution, the Fifth Finger Principle: angling and arching the first finger so its tip and the upper inside of its third joint sound two notes on non-adjacent frets simultaneously, unlocking the open-voicing arsenal (Fifth Finger Principle definition).
