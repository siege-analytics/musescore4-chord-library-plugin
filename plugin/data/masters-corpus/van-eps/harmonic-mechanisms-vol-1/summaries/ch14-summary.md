# Chapter 14 — Chromatics — Triads — Blockouts: Explanations, Charts and Blockout Patterns

_Distillation; backed by `chapters/ch14.md`._

Chapter 14 introduces and exhaustively charts the 'blockout,' Van Eps's term for a graphic two-line study in which a series of major and minor triads in root, first, and second inversions runs on the upper line against a completely independent bass on the lower line (Blockout definition; Bass and triad as independent voices). The chapter's central premise is that the recurring chord/bass misalignment is the curriculum: the resulting harmonic clashes are intentional and useful, and the student must not 'fix' them during the learning stage (Dissonance is intentional in blockouts; Strict adherence in learning stage). Two organizing taxonomies are defined—'repeat order,' in which the three inversions hold fixed positions in a per-measure pattern as keys change (Repeat order vs mixed order), and 'mixed order,' a scrambled inversion sequence whose cycles can run more than a hundred steps before repeating (Mixed-order patterns)—serving the chapter's analytical mission of systematically enumerating progression possibilities under controlled triad/bass independence (Purpose of blockouts). An explicit construction recipe is provided (three keys a major third apart, repeat-order inversions, chromatic bass from any starting step) so the student can build blockouts of their own (How to block out three keys). Reading conventions are flagged as load-bearing: descending chromatic bass lines are spelled with flats while triads carry their own key accidentals, producing enharmonic spellings that may make a third look like a second but keep the two lines visually isolated (Enharmonic spelling isolates lines). Fingerings and string-set markings are deliberately omitted so the student must finger every blockout in as many positions as possible (Why fingerings are omitted), and once the strict mathematical form is mastered, the chapter authorizes specific deviation techniques—repeat the chord while the bass continues, repeat the bass while the chords continue, skip steps in either cycle, splice the more pleasing sections of different cycles, or move one line by a whole tone or sustain it across a beat to tame an unwanted clash (When to break the strict form; Avoiding unwanted dissonance).
